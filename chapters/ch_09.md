# Chapter Nine: The Trust Business

The first tour started at nine-fifteen.

Andrea had already been at the facility since six-forty, had already done the morning intake — three dogs through the door before seven, including a new boarder named Hank who had pressed himself against the entry wall and panted in the shallow, rapid way that meant he was not going to hurt anyone but needed five minutes before he could be convinced of that. She'd given him the five minutes. She'd crouched at his level, not reaching, not talking, just being a person in the room who wasn't demanding anything. By the time the third dog came in, Hank had moved six feet from the wall and was watching the floor with his ears at half-mast, which was not calm but was the direction of it.

Jim read the room and took over. Andrea went to wash her hands and get ready for the tour.

---

The Delacroix family came in at nine-fifteen with a four-year-old golden retriever named Biscuit and the particular energy of people who had done their research and were still nervous anyway. The husband, Marcus, had a list of questions on his phone. His wife, Priya, kept her hand on Biscuit's head even as she was looking around the room, the kind of touch that was for her own reassurance as much as the dog's. Biscuit himself was interested in everything — nose going, tail going, no anxiety in him at all, just curiosity, which told Andrea something useful about what kind of household he came from.

"So this is the main floor," Andrea said. "This is where the dogs spend most of their time during the day. The whole thing is cage-free — there are no crates, no kennels. So what you're seeing is the actual space."

Marcus was already typing something. "And for a dog Biscuit's size—"

"He'd be in the large-dog group. Right now we've got about fourteen regulars in that group, mix of breeds, mostly between fifty and ninety pounds. We separate by size and temperament, not just size — if a dog is small but confident, sometimes they do better with the bigger group. Depends on the dog." She looked at Biscuit, who was currently attempting to investigate a corner of the room with the focused attention of a detective. "He looks pretty confident."

"Oh, he's fine with other dogs," Priya said. "It's more — we're worried about him. Being away from us."

Andrea nodded. "That's the thing people worry about most. And it's worth worrying about, honestly — some dogs don't do well in daycare, and if Biscuit turns out to be one of them, I'll tell you." She said it plainly, no softening. "What I usually see with dogs his age, good socialization history, is that the first day is a little disorienting and then by the third day they're walking in ahead of their owners. But I'll take notes on how he does and I'll text you."

Priya looked at her. "You'll actually text us?"

"I'll text you a photo," Andrea said. "Because that's what you actually want."

The laugh that came out of Priya was small and a little surprised, and Marcus stopped typing. Biscuit chose that moment to sit down squarely in the middle of the floor and look up at Andrea with the open, uncomplicated expression of a dog who had decided she was fine.

The tour took twenty-two minutes. The Delacroix family signed up for two days a week starting the following Monday.

---

The second tour was at ten, a woman named Carol who had a rescue shepherd mix named Pepper and who asked, before Andrea had said anything beyond hello, whether the facility used any aversive training methods.

"No," Andrea said. "Nothing like that."

Carol looked at her. "What does that mean specifically."

"It means no shock collars, no prong collars, no physical corrections. Our training philosophy is reward-based. If a dog is struggling behaviorally, we work on it or we refer out — we don't escalate to punishment." She paused. "Does Pepper have a history with aversive methods?"

Carol's jaw moved slightly. "Previous facility. She came to us from a shelter and she was — they said she needed firm handling. She doesn't need firm handling. She needs to not be scared."

"Okay," Andrea said. "Can I ask — how does she do with new spaces? New people?"

"She's cautious. She'll take a while to warm up. She's not aggressive, she just — she needs time."

Andrea thought about the intake process, the way Jim had sat on the floor this morning without approaching, the way that had worked with Hank. "We can work with that," she said. "When you bring her in for a trial day, I'd want to do a slow introduction — let her see the space without pressure, let her set the pace. Some dogs need thirty minutes before they're comfortable enough to actually be here. We've got the time."

Carol was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "The last place said she was 'difficult.'"

"She sounds like a dog who had something bad happen to her and is being appropriately careful about whether it happens again," Andrea said. "That's not difficult. That's just — that's sense."

The tour took thirty-five minutes. Carol scheduled a trial day for Pepper for the following week and asked twice whether Andrea was sure about the slow introduction, and Andrea said yes, twice, and meant it both times.

---

The third tour was at eleven and was a father named Derek with his ten-year-old son, Caleb, and a corgi named Wellington. Caleb had clearly been the one to lobby for the dog and was treating this tour with the gravity of someone whose entire argument was about to be proven right or definitively wrong. He asked more questions than his father. He wanted to know specifically where Wellington would sleep during boarding, whether the dogs had blankets, whether anyone stayed overnight.

"Someone is here until ten," Andrea said. "And then we have a camera system and an alarm, and the dogs who are boarding have their own area that's separate from the daycare space. It's quiet at night. Dogs sleep well here, usually."

"But nobody stays with them," Caleb said.

"No. Not overnight."

Caleb looked at Wellington, who was sitting on Derek's foot. "He doesn't like being alone."

"Most dogs don't," Andrea said. "But dogs are also pretty adaptable. When there are other dogs around and the space feels safe, the alone is different from being alone at home." She looked at Caleb directly, not at his father. "I'm not going to tell you he won't miss you. He probably will. But he'll be okay."

Caleb thought about this with the serious consideration of someone who had not yet learned to pretend he wasn't thinking. "Can you tell us if he seems sad?"

"I'll text your dad a photo," Andrea said. "You can judge for yourself."

Derek put his hand briefly on Caleb's shoulder. "That works," he said.

The corgi tour took forty minutes and ended with Caleb shaking Andrea's hand, which she received without remarking on it, because it was the right thing to do.

---

By eleven-fifty she had eaten half a granola bar standing at the front desk and was making notes in the scheduling software about the three new clients — their dogs' names, the things each family had said that she wanted to remember. Biscuit, large-dog group, confident, owner anxiety moderate, photo text on first day. Pepper, slow introduction, history of aversive handling, trial day next week, owner will need more communication than average. Wellington, corgi, separation-sensitive, boarding client, Caleb is the primary relationship, text photos to Derek so Caleb can see them.

This was not in any manual. There was no protocol that said to note the ten-year-old's name and the fact that he was the one who needed to know the dog was okay. It was just what the situation required, and Andrea wrote it down because she would not always remember and the situation would always require it.

---

The Hartmann family came in at two o'clock, not for a tour, but because their dog was struggling.

Obie was a three-year-old beagle who had been coming to daycare for six weeks and had, in the last two, started refusing to come through the door. Not aggression — refusal. He'd plant his feet at the entry and look up at his owners with an expression that was, by their account, heartbreaking. They'd been bringing him anyway, assuming he'd adjust. He hadn't.

Tom and Rachel Hartmann sat across from Andrea at the small table near the front desk. Tom had the look of a man who had come prepared to be told something was fine. Rachel had the look of a woman who already knew it wasn't.

"Tell me about the last few weeks," Andrea said. "Not just with daycare — generally."

Tom started to say something about Obie's routine, but Rachel said, "We moved him to a different crate at home. About three weeks ago. The old one was falling apart."

Andrea looked at her. "Is the new one the same size?"

"It's — a little smaller. We thought it would feel more den-like."

"How's he sleeping?"

Rachel glanced at Tom. "Not great."

Andrea set down her pen. "Here's what I think is happening. I don't think he's struggling with daycare specifically. I think he's in a period of general anxiety — the crate change disrupted something, and now everything that involves a transition is harder. Coming here is a transition. So he's stacking the anxiety on top of the existing anxiety." She paused. "The good news is that's fixable. The bad news is the fix isn't here, it's at home."

Tom leaned back slightly. "So it's the crate."

"Probably. I'd go back to the old crate size if you can, or get him something closer to what he had. Give it two weeks. If he's still planting his feet after that, then we talk about whether daycare is the right fit for him right now." She looked at both of them. "I'm not telling you to pull him. I'm telling you what I see and what I think it means. You decide what to do with it."

Rachel was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "The other place we tried, they just said he needed more socialization."

"He might," Andrea said. "But more socialization won't help if he's already overwhelmed. You have to fix the baseline first."

Tom said, "You're pretty direct."

"I find it saves time," Andrea said.

She didn't say: *I find it saves the dog.* That was in the sentence already. Tom heard it; she could tell by the way he stopped leaning back.

Rachel pulled out her phone and made a note. Obie's crate. Two weeks. "Okay," she said. "We'll try it."

They left with Obie on the leash between them, and Andrea watched through the glass door as the beagle walked to their car without planting his feet, because leaving was a different thing from arriving, and that distinction mattered, and she filed it in the same place she filed everything: the part of her that was always paying attention.

---

She photographed eleven dogs that afternoon.

Not for the website, not for the social media account that she updated when she had time, which was not often enough. She photographed them because she knew the owners were thinking about them. This was not a complicated theory — it was just true. The woman who'd dropped off Mango, the five-year-old lab mix, at seven-fifteen that morning had said *he gets anxious when I'm gone too long* and Andrea had heard what was underneath it: *I get anxious when he's gone too long.* So at two-thirty, when Mango was flopped on his side near the window in a posture of complete physical abandon, Andrea took a photo and sent it with the text: *He is having a very stressful day.*

The reply came back in forty seconds: a string of heart emojis and then: *I needed that thank you.*

Eleven dogs. Eleven different compositions, eleven different moments — Buster mid-shake after the water bowl, ears flying; the Lab sisters sleeping in opposite directions like bookends; a shepherd named Rosie who had found her corner under the east window and was sitting in it with the settled authority of someone who had done the work of finding the right place and was not moving. Andrea crouched to get the angle right on Rosie's photo, the particular quality of afternoon light coming through the glass and catching the fur along her back.

It took time. It was not billable. It was, as best she could measure, the single most effective thing she did for client retention, because what it said — what it communicated beyond the image itself — was: *your dog is a specific dog and I see her specifically and she is fine.*

That was the product. Not the daycare. The confidence that the daycare was being done with love.

---

By seven she was alone in the building.

The last pickup had been at six-forty — Wellington the corgi, collected by Derek while Caleb waited in the car and then immediately rolled down the window to watch Andrea hand over the dog. She'd waved at Caleb. He'd waved back with the dignity of someone trying not to look like he'd been watching.

Now the floor was clean. The water bowls were washed. The dogs were home.

Andrea sat at the front desk with the scheduling software open and worked through the rest of the week's bookings — a new boarding request that needed confirmation, a grooming appointment that had to be moved because the groomer had a conflict on Thursday, a note from a client asking whether they could add a third daycare day starting in January. She sent the confirmation, moved the appointment, replied to the note. The work was invisible. It would be invisible tomorrow when the families came in and everything ran smoothly, and the smoothness was the product of this, the seven o'clock at the front desk, the maintenance of the system that made the smoothness possible.

She pulled up the billing.

The numbers were good. Better than good — the fall had been steady, the word-of-mouth was working, three new clients in a single morning was not nothing. She looked at the revenue line for October and thought about what it had taken to get there: the tours, the photos, the honest conversations about crates and anxiety and what *cage-free* actually meant in practice. The specific work of making each family feel that their dog was known.

The building was quiet around her. The fluorescent light above the desk hummed at a frequency she'd stopped hearing weeks ago.

She thought: *if I keep doing this right, it will keep working.*

She believed it. She had good reason to believe it. The evidence was in the October revenue line and in the Delacroix family signing up this morning and in Caleb's handshake, which had been solemn and genuine and had made her want to laugh in the best possible way.

She believed it, and she went on believing it, and she did not examine it too closely because it was the belief that made it possible to be here at seven o'clock, alone, doing the invisible work. You had to believe the work was sufficient. You had to believe that doing it correctly was a kind of protection.

Outside, Fayette Street was quiet. A truck went by on Route 15, its sound rising and then diminishing. Somewhere a dog was barking — a neighbor's dog, not one of hers, a sound she'd learned to distinguish without deciding to learn it.

She closed the billing tab and opened the scheduling for next week. The Hartmann family had Obie on Wednesday. She added a note: *slow entry, watch baseline, follow up on crate.* She added a note for Pepper's trial day: *Jim for intake, slow introduction, thirty minutes minimum, no pressure.* She added a note for Wellington's first boarding stay in December, which was still six weeks away but which she was already thinking about, because Caleb would be thinking about it from the moment it was scheduled and she would need to have a plan.

The notes were for her. No one would see them. They were the infrastructure beneath the infrastructure — the thinking that made the doing possible.

She saved the file and sat for a moment in the quiet of the building.

Eleven dogs today. Three new families. One honest conversation about a beagle and a crate. A ten-year-old's handshake.

She had done what she knew how to do, and it had worked, and tomorrow she would do it again.

She reached over and turned off the desk lamp, and for a moment the only light in the room came from the computer screen, blue-white against the dark, and she sat in it and felt the specific weight of the day without naming what it was — just the weight, just the fact of it, all the families she was carrying now and would carry tomorrow and the day after, all the dogs whose names she knew and whose corners she'd noted and whose photos she'd taken and sent. It was not a burden exactly. It was more like what you felt at the end of a day of physical work: the satisfying ache of having used yourself fully.

She closed the laptop.

The building went dark.

She sat there for another few seconds in the complete dark, just breathing, before she stood and got her coat and locked the door behind her.
