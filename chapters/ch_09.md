# Chapter Nine

The workshop light was already on.

Cass stopped at the corner of his grandfather's street and looked at it — the yellow rectangle of the window in the gray morning, 

He walked up the front path, went around the side of the house, and knocked on the workshop door.

"It's open," his grandfather said.

---

The smell was always the first thing. Flux and metal and something older underneath, the particular damp of a stone floor that had absorbed decades of work. The workshop was a converted outbuilding, low-ceilinged, with exposed beams that his grandfather had hung tools on rather than the walls. Chisels and files and burnishing rods, each one in its place. The lathe stood against the far wall. The casting bench ran along the right side, and on it, in a row, were three bells in various stages of completion — one rough from the mold, one filed down to its final profile, one waiting for its crown to be fitted.

His grandfather was at the bench with a loupe to his eye, running a thumb along the rim of the middle bell.

"Sit," he said, without looking up.

Cass sat on the stool by the door. He put his notebook on his knee and held his pen and waited.

His grandfather set the loupe down. He was a tall man gone narrow with age, with hands that still looked like a younger man's hands — the knuckles large, the skin tight over the tendons, the palms calloused in specific places that corresponded to specific tools. He turned and looked at Cass.

"You brought it," he said.

"The notebook?"

"I meant your ears." He picked up a cloth and wiped his hands. "But the notebook is fine."

"Why'd you cross it out?"

"Too neat," Cass said.

His grandfather grunted.  

"Your father called me," Cass said.

"I know. I called him first." He set the cloth on the bench. "Tell me what you heard."

---

 

"The pain," his grandfather said, when Cass stopped. "Left eye specifically?"

"Right behind it."

"Not the right."

"No."

He was quiet for a moment. Then he got up and went to the shelf above the casting bench, where he kept things that weren't tools — a small framed photograph, a jar of river sand from somewhere, a bell Cass had never seen him ring. He took down the bell. 

He held it out.

Cass took it. It was heavier than it looked.

"Ring it," his grandfather said.

"With what?"

He handed him a small brass rod from the bench. Cass held the bell by the crown and struck the rim, gently, the way you'd do anything for the first time.

The note was a G. Clean, centered. And then, under it, something else.

The needle went in behind his left eye.

"How long have you known?" Cass said.

"That it does that to you?" His grandfather took the bell back. "Since you were nine. You made a face. You never said anything, so I didn't."

"I didn't know what it was."

"Neither did I. Not exactly." He set the bell on the bench, not back on the shelf. "I had a great-great-uncle who went deaf in his left ear. Did your father tell you that?"

"He told me about the ledger."

"The ledger is how I know about the ear."  "He kept working. He documented everything he heard before the ear went. Some of it I've never been able to reproduce — sounds he described that don't match any bell I've cast. But the under-note." He looked at Cass. "He described it exactly."

"What did he say it was?"

"He said it was the bell's second intention."

Cass wrote that down. *Second intention.* His grandfather watched him write it.

"He didn't mean it spiritually," his grandfather said. "He meant it technically. A bell is cast with an intended note. But the shape, the alloy, the thickness at the rim — they produce other frequencies. Most of them are harmonics. Predictable. The under-note is something else. It's not a harmonic. It doesn't follow the math."

"Then what does it follow?"

"That's what Aldric was trying to figure out when he lost the ear." He set it down. "He thought it had to do with the cooling. How fast the bronze sets. There's a moment, right at the transition — liquid to solid — where the crystal structure locks in. He thought that moment left a kind of record in the metal. Something the bell remembered about becoming itself."

Cass looked at the bell on the bench. The fog outside had lightened slightly, the gray going less uniform, and through the window the outlines of his grandfather's garden were starting to separate from the general murk.

"And the pain?" Cass said.

"He had a theory about that too." His grandfather sat back down on the stool. "He thought the people who could hear the under-note were hearing something the ear wasn't designed to process. Not the sound — the structure underneath the sound. And the eye—" He stopped.

"What?"

"He wrote that the left eye is where the body sends things it doesn't have a category for."  

"The bell on the shelf," he said. "The one from the shop. It has an under-note."

"I know."

"You heard it?"

"I heard something. Not the way you do." He looked at his hands. "I hear the note. You hear what's underneath it. That's the difference."

 

"What happened to his bells?" Cass said.

"Some of them are in churches. One is in a museum in Ghent. I have three." He nodded toward the shelf where the old bell sat. "That one's his."

Cass looked at it.

"The one I just rang."

"Yes."

"How did you get it?"

"It came with the workshop."  "The man I apprenticed with had it. He said it was a teaching bell. He said you learned more from it than from anything you could cast yourself." He picked up the cloth and actually used it this time, wiping the rim of the bell on the bench — the middle one, the one he'd been examining with the loupe. "I think he was right. I also think he couldn't hear what you hear."

"Could you hear it? When you were young?"

His grandfather didn't answer right away. He kept wiping the rim, the same slow circular motion.

"A little," he said finally. "Less now. The ear changes." He set the cloth down. "You have it more than I did. More than Aldric did, maybe, from what he wrote. I don't know what that means."

"Do you think it means something?"

His grandfather looked at him. It was the kind of look that was measuring something, not unkindly.

"I think it means you should keep the notebook," he said.

---

They worked for two hours after that. Or his grandfather worked and Cass observed, 

His grandfather was fitting the crown to the third bell. It was a small bell, intended for a private collector, with a profile that was slightly unusual — wider at the shoulder than standard, which was what the collector had asked for and what his grandfather had argued against and then made anyway, because the collector was paying and also because his grandfather believed that bells should be made and argued with and that the arguing was part of the process.

"The map," Cass said, after a while. "The one I've been drawing."

His grandfather didn't look up from the crown fitting. "What about it."

"I've been treating it like a diagram. Like there's a center I haven't found yet."

"And?"

"I think maybe the under-note is the center," he said. "Not something that explains it. Just — the thing itself."

His grandfather set down the crown piece. He looked at Cass.

"Say that again."

"I think the map isn't pointing at something. I think it is something." Cass turned his pen in his fingers. "The bell makes a sound. The sound has something underneath it that doesn't follow the math. And the only way to know what's there is to be the person who can hear it."

His grandfather picked up the crown piece again. He held it over the bell's shoulder, not setting it down yet.

"Aldric wrote something in the last pages of the ledger," he said. "Not the technical notes. After those." He fitted the crown into place, checked the angle, lifted it again. "He wrote that he had spent forty years trying to understand the under-note and had come to believe that understanding wasn't the right word for what you did with it. He said the right word was *attend*."

Cass wrote it down.

*Attend.*

"He meant it the old way," his grandfather said. "To be present to. To keep your attention on." He set the crown down and reached for the flux brush. "He thought the under-note was the bell trying to be known. Not understood. Known."

The fog had burned off while they'd been working. The window showed a pale blue sky now, the kind of morning that arrived late in the season and felt like a concession. Cass looked at his notebook, at the word he'd just written, and thought about the night before — lying in the dark, the bell on the shelf, the under-note moving when he moved, or seeming to. The bell trying to be known.

He wasn't sure he believed it. He also wasn't sure he didn't.

"The under-note changed," he said. "Or I thought it did. When I moved closer, it shifted."

His grandfather looked up from the flux brush.

"Shifted how."

"The interval changed. Not the note — the relationship between the note and the under-note." Cass found the page where he'd written it down. "It was a minor second when I was across the room. When I moved to within a foot of it, it went to a major seventh."

His grandfather set the flux brush down. He was very still.

"You measured it."

"I sang the under-note and then the note and figured out the interval."

"You can sing the under-note."

"Yes." Cass looked up. "Can't you?"

"No," his grandfather said. "I can hear a suggestion of it. I can't produce it." He picked up the flux brush, then set it down again. "Aldric couldn't either. He wrote that he'd tried. He said it was like trying to hum a color."

"The interval changing," his grandfather said. "Distance-dependent."

"Yes."

"That's not in the ledger."

"Write it down," his grandfather said. "Everything you just told me. Put the date on it."

"I already have most of it."

"Put the date on it." He picked up the flux brush for the third time and this time used it. "Someone's going to come after you. Write it down for them."

Cass opened to a clean page. He wrote the date in the top corner, the same small even handwriting he'd had since he was twelve, and then he started from the beginning — the first day in the shop, the C-sharp, the under-note, the needle behind the left eye. 

He was three pages in when his grandfather said, without looking up from the crown fitting, "You know your father wanted to hear it."

Cass stopped writing.

"The under-note," his grandfather said. "When he was your age. He asked me about it. I told him what I knew, which wasn't much then. He tried for two years to hear it." He set the crown in place. "He couldn't. It didn't skip him — he just doesn't have it. Some people don't."

"He never told me that."

"He wouldn't." His grandfather picked up the flux brush and touched it to the joint. "He's proud of you. He doesn't always know how to say it without making it about him, so he says other things instead."

He decided now that it was.

"He's been in the shop every morning," Cass said.

"He comes in when he needs to think." His grandfather examined the crown joint, tilted the bell to check the angle. "He's been coming in more this week."

"Since I started."

"Yes."

He turned back to the clean page. He found his place in the notes and kept writing.

His grandfather worked. The flux brush moved along the joint. The bells sat in their row on the bench — rough, finished, waiting — 

When Cass got to the part about the interval changing with distance, he wrote it twice. Once in plain language, once as a measurement. He put the date on both.

Then he wrote, at the bottom of the page, in smaller letters: *Aldric Voss went deaf in his left ear and kept working. He wrote everything down. He did not know who would read it.*

He looked at it for a moment.

He did not cross it out.
