# Chapter Nine: The Trust Business

The first tour started at nine-fifteen.

Andrea had been there since six-forty. Morning intake: three dogs before seven, including a new boarder named Hank who pressed himself against the entry wall and panted in the shallow, rapid way that meant he wasn't going to hurt anyone but needed five minutes before he could be convinced of that.  By the time the third dog came through, Hank had moved six feet from the wall, ears at half-mast, watching the floor. 

Jim read the room and took over. She went to wash her hands.

---

The Delacroix family arrived with a four-year-old golden retriever named Biscuit and the particular energy of people who had done their research and were still nervous anyway. The husband had a list of questions on his phone. His wife kept her hand on Biscuit's head while she looked around — . Biscuit himself was interested in everything, nose going, tail going, no anxiety in him at all, .

"This is the main floor," Andrea said. "The whole thing is cage-free — no crates, no kennels. What you're seeing is the actual space."

Marcus was already typing. "And for a dog Biscuit's size—"

"He'd be in the large-dog group. About fourteen regulars, mostly fifty to ninety pounds. We separate by size and temperament — if a dog is small but confident, sometimes they do better with the bigger group. Depends on the dog." She watched Biscuit investigate a corner with the focused attention of a detective. "He looks pretty confident."

"He's fine with other dogs," Priya said. "It's more — we're worried about him. Being away from us."

"That's the thing people worry about most. And it's worth worrying about — some dogs don't do well in daycare, and if Biscuit turns out to be one of them, I'll tell you."  "What I usually see with dogs his age, good socialization, is that the first day is disorienting and by the third day they're walking in ahead of their owners. But I'll text you."

Priya looked at her. "You'll actually text us?"

"I'll text you a photo," Andrea said. "Because that's what you actually want."

The Delacroix family signed up for two days a week starting Monday.

The second tour was at ten — a woman named Carol with a rescue shepherd mix named Pepper, who asked, before Andrea had said anything beyond hello, whether the facility used aversive training methods.

"No," Andrea said.

Carol looked at her. "What does that mean specifically."

"No shock collars, no prong collars, no physical corrections. Reward-based. If a dog is struggling behaviorally, we work on it or we refer out." She paused. "Does Pepper have a history with aversive methods?"

Carol's jaw moved. "Previous facility. They said she needed firm handling. She doesn't need firm handling. She needs to not be scared."

"How does she do with new spaces?"

"She's cautious. She'll take a while. She's not aggressive, she just — she needs time."

Andrea thought about Jim on the floor this morning, eight feet from Hank, doing nothing. "When you bring her in for a trial day, I'd want to do a slow introduction. Let her see the space without pressure, let her set the pace. Some dogs need thirty minutes before they're comfortable enough to actually be here. We've got the time."

Carol was quiet. Then: "The last place said she was 'difficult.'"

"She sounds like a dog who had something bad happen to her and is being appropriately careful about whether it happens again," Andrea said. "That's not difficult. That's just — that's sense."

Carol scheduled a trial day for the following week.

---

The Hartmann family came in at two, not for a tour. Their dog was struggling.

Obie was a three-year-old beagle who had been coming to daycare for six weeks and had, in the last two, started refusing to come through the door. Not aggression — refusal. He'd plant his feet at the entry and look up at his owners with an expression that was, by their account, heartbreaking. They'd been bringing him anyway, assuming he'd adjust.

Tom and Rachel Hartmann sat across from Andrea at the small table near the front desk. Tom had the look of a man who had come prepared to be told something was fine. Rachel had the look of a woman who already knew it wasn't.

"Tell me about the last few weeks," Andrea said. "Not just with daycare — generally."

Tom started in on Obie's routine, but Rachel said, "We moved him to a different crate at home. About three weeks ago. The old one was falling apart."

"Is the new one the same size?"

"It's — a little smaller. We thought it would feel more den-like."

"How's he sleeping?"

Rachel glanced at Tom. "Not great."

Andrea set down her pen. "I don't think he's struggling with daycare specifically. I think he's in a period of general anxiety — the crate change disrupted something, and now every transition is harder. Coming here is a transition. He's stacking the anxiety." She paused. "The fix isn't here. It's at home."

Tom leaned back slightly. "So it's the crate."

"Probably. Go back to the old size if you can, or as close to it as possible. Give it two weeks. If he's still planting his feet after that, we talk about whether daycare is the right fit for him right now." She looked at both of them. "I'm not telling you to pull him. I'm telling you what I see. You decide what to do with it."

Rachel was quiet. Then: "The other place we tried just said he needed more socialization."

"He might. But more socialization won't help if he's already overwhelmed. You fix the baseline first."

Tom said, "You're pretty direct."

"I find it saves time," Andrea said.

She didn't say: *I find it saves the dog.* 

Rachel pulled out her phone and made a note. "Okay," she said. "We'll try it."

They left with Obie between them, and Andrea watched through the glass as the beagle walked to their car without planting his feet — .

---

She photographed eleven dogs that afternoon.

 The woman who'd dropped off Mango at seven-fifteen had said *he gets anxious when I'm gone too long*, and Andrea had heard what was underneath it. So at two-thirty, when Mango was flopped on his side near the window in a posture of complete physical abandon, she took a photo and sent it: *He is having a very stressful day.*

The reply came back in forty seconds. Heart emojis, then: *I needed that thank you.*

Eleven dogs. Eleven different moments — a shepherd named Rosie who had found her corner under the east window and was sitting in it with the settled authority of someone who had done the work of finding the right place and was not moving. Andrea crouched to get the angle right, the afternoon light catching the fur along Rosie's back.

It took time. It was not billable. It was, as best she could measure, the most effective thing she did for client retention — because what it communicated beyond the image was: *your dog is a specific dog and I see her specifically and she is fine.*

---

By seven she was alone in the building.

She sat at the front desk and worked through the week's bookings — a boarding confirmation, a grooming appointment that needed moving, a note from a client asking about a third daycare day in January. She sent the confirmation, moved the appointment, replied to the note. 

She pulled up the billing. The numbers were good — fall had been steady, the word-of-mouth was working. She looked at the October revenue line and thought about what it had taken to get there. The tours. The photos. The honest conversation about a beagle and a crate. The specific work of making each family feel that their dog was known.

She opened the scheduling for next week and added notes: *Obie — slow entry, watch baseline, follow up on crate. Pepper — Jim for intake, slow introduction, thirty minutes minimum, no pressure.* Wellington the corgi had a boarding stay in December, still six weeks out, but Caleb would be thinking about it from the moment it was scheduled, so she made a note for that too.

She saved the file.

Eleven dogs today. Three new families. One honest conversation about a beagle and a crate. She had done what she knew how to do, and it had worked, and tomorrow she would do it again.

She reached over and turned off the desk lamp. For a moment the only light came from the computer screen, blue-white against the dark.  She closed the laptop.

The building went dark. Outside, a truck moved along Route 15, its sound rising and then gone. Somewhere down the block, a dog she didn't know barked once and was quiet.

She got her coat, locked the door, and walked out into the cold.
