# Chapter Eleven

The under-note came back on a Tuesday.

Cass was in the workshop alone, his grandfather at the hardware store for flux wire, and he had set the Voss bell at the far end of the bench and was working through the C-major sequence on the practice bell — a smaller casting, rougher, that his grandfather used for demonstrations. He was doing the thing his grandfather had taught him: listening past the fundamental, past the first overtone, down into the hum beneath the hum where the bell's real character lived.

He struck the bell again. Listened again. Tuned himself to it the way you tune a radio, past the static, into the signal.

The needle went in behind his left eye.

He dropped the mallet. It hit the bench and rolled and he caught it by reflex, and for a moment he just stood there with his hand pressed to the side of his head, heel of his palm against his temple, and waited for the pain to pass. It didn't pass. 

The needle eased. Didn't leave, but eased.

He set the mallet down carefully and pulled the stool over and sat.  The Voss bell on the shelf caught a stripe of afternoon light through the high window and held it.

Cass looked at his hands.

 And Cass had not mentioned it to anyone, partly because he couldn't explain it 

He picked up the notebook.  He opened it to the last entry and read what he'd written four days ago, after the session with the Voss bell.

*Under-note present. Duration approx. 4 seconds after strike. Fundamental C. Under-note F below. Pain: yes. Same location. Wondering if it's the interval or the bell.*

He uncapped the pen. Wrote the date.

*Today: practice bell, not Voss. Under-note F below C. Pain: yes. Same location. Same intensity. So it's not the bell.*

He stared at that last sentence for a moment. Then added: *It's the interval.*

---

His grandfather came back at four-fifteen, the hardware store bag in one hand and a white paper bag from the bakery two doors down in the other. He set both on the workbench and pulled out a wax paper package and put it in front of Cass without looking at him.

"Cheese danish," he said. "Don't tell your mother I bought it before dinner."

"She'll smell it on your breath."

"Pastry doesn't have a smell."

"It has powdered sugar on it."

His grandfather looked at the danish. Looked at Cass. "Fine. We'll tell her it was a gift."

"From who?"

"From a man at the hardware store who owed me a favor."

"Do you know anyone at the hardware store?"

"I know the man who owns it."

"Does he owe you a favor?"

"He might," his grandfather said. "By the time we finish the story, he will." He pulled up the other stool and sat and opened his own package, which was a plain croissant, and bit into it. He chewed. He looked at the practice bell on the bench. He looked at the mallet, which Cass had placed at an angle he didn't usually use. "You had a session."

"Yes."

"How was it?"

Cass looked at the danish.  He picked it up and took a bite and thought about how to say it.

"The under-note came back," he said.

His grandfather nodded. He was looking at the practice bell.

"It's not the Voss bell," Cass said. "I thought maybe it was something specific to that casting. But I got it on the practice bell today. Same interval, same—" He paused. "Same everything."

His grandfather was quiet for long enough that Cass heard the refrigerator hum and the distant sound of a car on the road outside. Then his grandfather said, "What interval?"

"F below C."

"A fifth."

"Yeah."

His grandfather set the croissant down. He turned on the stool and looked at Cass directly,  "Does it hurt?"

"Yes," Cass said.

 

"Where?"

Cass touched the side of his head. Just below the temple, slightly behind the eye. His grandfather watched his hand.

"How long has this been happening?"

"Since—" Cass thought. "The fourth session, I think. Maybe the fifth. I didn't write it down right away."

"Why not?"

"Because I didn't know what it was yet."

 

"It's not common," his grandfather said finally. "But it's not unheard of."

Cass set the danish down. "You know what it is."

"I have a guess." His grandfather turned to look at him again. "There's a sensitivity. Some people who work with bells — who really work with them, not just ring them — develop an acute response to certain intervals. The minor seventh. Sometimes the perfect fifth. The auditory system is doing something, processing the harmonic relationship, and in some people that processing has a—" He paused. "A cost."

"A headache."

"More precise than a headache. A specific point. Behind the eye, usually the left."

Cass felt something move in his chest. Not quite relief. "You have it too."

His grandfather looked at the Voss bell on its shelf. "Had it. For about three years, when I was first learning to hear the under-note. Then it stopped."

"Why did it stop?"

"I don't know. It just did. One day it wasn't there anymore." He picked up a piece of croissant and looked at it. "I should have told you earlier. I was waiting to see if you'd mention it."

"Why were you waiting?"

His grandfather glanced at him sideways, the brief look that meant he was about to say something he'd thought about more than he was going to let on. "Because the people it happens to are the ones who actually hear it. Not everyone can. I wanted to know if you were one of them before I said anything. If I'd told you at the start, you might have expected the pain and found it anyway."

Cass thought about this. "So you weren't sure if it was real."

"I wasn't sure if *yours* was real. Mine was." He put the croissant down. "Yours is."

The afternoon light had shifted while they talked and the stripe on the Voss bell had moved, was now catching the bell at its shoulder rather than its waist. Cass watched it. The bell was a dark bronze, almost brown in the low light, and where the light hit it the metal went warm and deep, a color that had no good name in English — amber wasn't right, copper wasn't right, it was something between and below both of them.

"Voss," Cass said. "Did he have it?"

"The journals don't say. But he wrote about the fifth interval specifically — the way it sat differently in the ear than other intervals. He called it *die lastende Quinte*. The burdening fifth." His grandfather stood up and carried his croissant to the bench and stood looking at the practice bell. "He might have meant acoustically. He might have meant something else."

"The burdening fifth," Cass said.

"Yes."

"That's a strange way to describe a sound."

His grandfather looked at him. Something moved across his face — not quite a smile, but close to one. "Is it?"

Cass thought about the needle behind his eye. The way it arrived not as pain first but as a kind of sharpness, an almost-clarity, and then clenched into pain. "No," he said. "I guess not."

---

His grandfather showed him the passage that evening, after dinner, while Cass's mother was in the kitchen and his father was reading in the other room and the house had settled into its after-dinner quiet. The Voss journal was a reproduction — his grandfather had told him the original was in a library in Cologne — and the pages were the faint gray of old documents photographed and printed again. His grandfather translated slowly, pausing over words, occasionally writing an alternative in the margin.

"Here," his grandfather said, and read: "*The fifth interval between fundamental and under-note creates in the attentive listener a sensation of weight. Not unpleasant, but not nothing. The bell is asking something of the body as well as the ear. I have come to believe this is how it should be. The bell that costs nothing to hear is not fully heard.*"

Cass read it again. His grandfather's handwriting in the margin said: *asking something of the body* — underlined.

"He knew," Cass said.

"He wrote it down, anyway."

 

"When he went deaf," Cass said. "After. Did he still feel the fifth?"

His grandfather was quiet for a moment. "The journals don't go there."

"But you think he did."

"I think," his grandfather said carefully, "that some things, once the body learns them, don't require the original signal. The ear is a translator. What it translates into — the sensation, the response — that can persist." He paused. "But I'm speculating."

"That's okay."

"Is it?"

"You're speculating from fifty years of knowing these things," Cass said. "That's different from just guessing."

His grandfather looked at him for a moment with that same quality of attention, the focused stillness, and 

"Yes," his grandfather said. "It is different." He took the journal back. "Go to bed. We have a long session tomorrow."

Cass stood. He picked up his jacket from the hook. He stood there for a second with the journal still on the workbench between them, the Voss bell on its shelf, the tools on their pegs. The needle behind his eye had been gone since dinner. 

"Grandfather."

His grandfather looked up.

"The pain," Cass said. "When it stopped for you. Did you miss it?"

 

"Yes," his grandfather said. "I did."

He didn't explain it. Cass didn't ask him to.

He went upstairs, and lay in the dark, and listened to the house settle around him — the particular creak of the third step that his grandfather had never repaired, the refrigerator cycling on in the kitchen below, the sound of his parents' voices through the wall, low and indistinct and familiar. 

He thought about Voss writing in that cramped hand: *The bell that costs nothing to hear is not fully heard.*

He thought about his grandfather saying: *Yes. I did.*

He lay there for a while longer with his hand against his temple, feeling nothing, which was its own kind of information.
