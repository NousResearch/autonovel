# Chapter Eleven: The First Year

The shepherd mix arrived on a Tuesday in November, second month of operations, and she came in low with her ears flat and her whole body saying *no*. Her name was Bella, which was the name of half the dogs in Northern Virginia, and her owner was a woman named Patricia Cho who worked in Tysons and had been driving forty minutes each way since September and felt, she said, terrible about it.

Jim sat down on the floor near the door. Not close to Bella. Just in the same general region. By the time Patricia signed the last form, Bella was sitting eighteen inches from Jim's left knee, not looking at him, but not looking at the wall either.

By December she was sleeping in the corner near the south wall. By January she was crossing the floor to him when he came in the morning — not running, not performing, just walking over with the purposefulness of a dog who has decided something.

---

There was a golden retriever named Captain who belonged to the Okonkwos, a family from Gainesville with three kids and a schedule that ran from six in the morning until seven at night. Captain had no fear and no guile and no concept of personal space. There was a French bulldog named Pretzel whose owner, Marcus Webb, dropped him off at seven-fifteen every morning without fail and always asked the same question: *Was he good?* The answer was always yes. Marcus always looked briefly relieved.

Andrea kept track of things that didn't appear on any form. Captain got overstimulated after lunch if there were more than twelve dogs on the floor. Pretzel's ears needed checking every other week because Marcus, for all his punctuality, did not notice ears. A lab mix named Scout had a thing about the drain cover near the washing station and needed to approach it on his own terms. His family, the Delgados, had moved from Alexandria in the spring and were still deciding whether they'd made a mistake.

"It's just — we want to make sure it's the same quality," Mrs. Delgado said.

"It's not the same," Andrea said. "It's different. Let me show you why."

The Delgados became regulars by February. Mrs. Delgado texted Andrea directly now, not the main number.

One morning in late January, Andrea's phone showed a text from Patricia Cho at seven-fifteen — Bella had been dropped off ten minutes earlier. *Just checking in. How is she today?*

Andrea went to the floor, found Bella in her corner with her chin on her paws, watching the room with the calm authority of a dog who owned the place, and sent the photo with a caption: *She's good. Already in her spot.*

Patricia's response came back in under a minute. *Thank you. I needed that.*

---

By end of year, they had forty-three regular dogs.

Jim sat at the kitchen table on a Thursday evening in late December with the laptop open and the numbers in front of him. The numbers were good. Not comfortable — good was not the same as comfortable, not yet — but good in the way a first year could be good. Cassie was under the table. From the living room came the sound of Gracie settling in for the night.

"Captain's family wants to add a boarding night," Andrea said. "February. Myrtle Beach."

"We can do that."

"I told them we could do that."

A car went by on Route 15, headlights sweeping across the kitchen window and gone. Cassie shifted and put her chin on Jim's foot instead, redistributing her allegiance with the perfect arbitrariness of dogs.

"The Delgados paid three months ahead," Andrea said.

"That's good."

"That's someone who's decided to trust you."

A moment passed.

"She'll want to know about the numbers," Jim said. Dottie. He'd been meaning to call.

"She'll want to know about the dogs."

Andrea considered this. "Both," she said. "She'll want to know both."

---

Patricia texted at six on a Friday morning in early January. Andrea saw it when she came downstairs.

"Her mother died," she said.

Jim set down his coffee.

"She's not bringing Bella today." Andrea put her phone down. "I'm going to send flowers."

"From the business or from us?"

She thought about it. "Both. Separately."

Cassie came and sat on his foot. He put his hand on her head without looking down and she leaned into it slightly.

"Bella's what she has right now," Andrea said. Not to Jim, exactly. To the room.

"I know."

Patricia brought Bella back the second week of January, thinner in the face, quieter than usual. She handed over the leash and Jim took it and Bella went immediately to her corner, and Patricia watched that happen and her face did something that was not quite crying, and then she said thank you and left.

Andrea stood at the window and watched her go.

The year turned. The families from the commuter subdivisions kept finding them, kept calling, kept showing up on trial days with anxious dogs and guilty feelings and the specific hope of people who had decided to trust strangers with something they loved. Jim learned the new dogs the way he'd learned the first ones: sitting with them, letting them decide. By February they had fifty-one dogs. By March, fifty-eight.

The awards came in the spring. Jim put the certificates in the office where the staff could see them. Andrea mentioned them only when families asked.

It was early 2020. The schedule was full. Bella was in her corner. Captain was in the middle of everything. The floor was loud with the specific sound of dogs who knew each other, who had been in this room enough times to know its rhythms, who had decided this place was safe.

Andrea was on the floor with a new dog, a nervous terrier mix in for her first trial day.
