# Chapter Eleven: The First Year

The shepherd mix arrived on a Tuesday in November, second month of operations, and she came in low with her ears flat and her whole body saying *no*. Her name was Bella, which was the name of half the dogs in Northern Virginia, and her owner was a woman named Patricia Cho who worked in Tysons and had been driving forty minutes each way since September and felt, she said, terrible about it.

Jim sat down on the floor near the door, not close to Bella, just in the same general region. Patricia watched him with an expression somewhere between confused and grateful, and then Andrea redirected her toward the front desk — forms, pickup times, food sensitivities. By the time Patricia signed the last form, Bella was sitting eighteen inches from Jim's left knee, not looking at him, but not looking at the wall either.

Patricia said goodbye three times. The third time, she was already at the door.

By December Bella was sleeping in the corner near the south wall. By January she was crossing the floor to Jim when he came in the morning — not running, not performing, just walking over with the purposefulness of a dog who has decided something.

---

There was a golden retriever named Captain who belonged to the Okonkwos, a family from Gainesville with three kids and a schedule that ran from six in the morning until seven at night. Captain had no fear and no guile and no concept of personal space, and the staff loved him immediately because it was impossible to be near Captain without feeling like something good was happening.

There was a French bulldog named Pretzel who belonged to Marcus Webb, a contractor from Manassas who dropped Pretzel off at seven-fifteen every morning without fail and always asked the same question: *Was he good?* The answer was always yes. Marcus always looked briefly relieved.

There was a lab mix named Scout whose family, the Delgados, had moved from Alexandria in the spring and were still deciding whether they'd made a mistake. Andrea figured this out from the first tour — from the way Mrs. Delgado kept referencing things they'd had in Alexandria, the trainer, the daycare they'd loved.

"It's just — we want to make sure it's the same quality," Mrs. Delgado said.

"It's not the same," Andrea said. "It's different. Let me show you why."

The Delgados became regulars by February. Mrs. Delgado texted Andrea directly now, not the main number.

Andrea kept track of things that didn't appear on any form. She knew Captain got overstimulated after lunch if there were more than twelve dogs on the floor. She knew Pretzel's ears needed checking every other week because Marcus, for all his punctuality, did not notice ears. She knew Scout had a thing about the drain cover near the washing station and needed to approach it on his own terms.

One morning in late January, her phone showed a text from Patricia Cho: *Just checking in. How is she today?*

It was seven-fifteen. Patricia had dropped Bella off at seven-oh-five.

Andrea went to the floor, found Bella in her corner — chin on her paws, watching the room with the calm authority of a dog who owned the place — and sent the photo with a caption: *She's good. Already in her spot.*

Patricia's response came back in under a minute. *Thank you. I needed that.*

Andrea put her phone in her pocket. She picked up her phone again and typed a note to herself. *Call her Wednesday.*

---

By the end of the first year, they had forty-three regular dogs.

Jim sat at the kitchen table on a Thursday evening in late December with the laptop open and the numbers in front of him. The numbers were good. Not comfortable — good was not the same as comfortable, not yet — but good in the way a first year could be good.

Andrea sat across from him with a mug of tea and the week's schedule. Cassie was under the table with her head on Andrea's foot. From the living room came the sound of Gracie settling in for the night.

"Captain's family wants to add a boarding night," Andrea said.

"When?"

"February. Myrtle Beach."

"We can do that."

"I told them we could do that."

He made a note. A car went by on Route 15, headlights sweeping across the kitchen window and gone. Cassie shifted and put her chin on Jim's foot instead, redistributing her allegiance with the perfect arbitrariness of dogs.

"Bella's coming every day now," Jim said.

"I know."

"Patricia asked me last week if she could do five days instead of three."

"I know. I already adjusted the rate."

Jim looked up. Andrea was looking at the schedule, not at him, but there was something in the set of her face that was not quite a smile and not quite not one.

"The Delgados paid three months ahead," she said.

"That's good."

"That's someone who's decided to trust you."

A moment passed. The refrigerator made its sound.

"We're going to be okay," Andrea said. Not a question. The thing you said when you'd been too afraid to say it for twelve months and now it was December and you could.

Jim closed the laptop. "We're going to be okay."

Cassie's tail hit the floor once and stopped.

"Andy wants to know if we can get a third dog," Andrea said.

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him to ask you."

Jim stood and rinsed his glass and looked out at the dark backyard, the fence just visible in the neighbor's light. He was thinking about the spring. The families from the new subdivisions in Gainesville were still finding them — he'd had three calls this week from people who'd heard about them from other people, which was the best possible way to get a call.

He turned off the kitchen light. Andrea was already up, folding the schedule, and Cassie had relocated to the living room where Gracie had made room on the couch without being asked.

"We should call Dottie," Andrea said.

"I'll call her tomorrow."

"She'll want to know about the numbers."

"She'll want to know about the dogs."

Andrea considered this. "Both," she said. "She'll want to know both."

---

Patricia texted at six on a Friday morning in early January. Andrea saw it when she came downstairs.

"Her mother died," she said.

Jim set down his coffee.

"She's not bringing Bella today." Andrea looked at her phone, put it down. "I'm going to send flowers."

"From the business or from us?"

She thought about it. "Both. Separately."

Outside the kitchen window the sky had gone from gray to the pale blue of a cold clear morning — the kind that meant the Blue Ridge would be sharp on the horizon, fully visible, the way it was when the air had been rinsed clean by cold. Jim couldn't see it from where he stood. He knew it was there.

Cassie came and sat on his foot. He put his hand on her head without looking at her and she leaned into it slightly.

"She'll bring Bella back," Andrea said. Not to Jim, exactly. To the room.

"I know she will."

"Bella's what she has right now." Andrea stopped. Started again. "Bella's what she has."

Jim nodded. He'd understood it before they opened, before the permits, before the name was the name — that this was what they were building. The thing the dogs gave to the people who loved them. Continuity. Something in the room that needed you and didn't know about the rest of it.

He put on his coat. He had forty-three dogs to get to.

Patricia brought Bella back the second week of January, thinner in the face, quieter than usual. She handed over the leash and Jim took it and Bella went immediately to her corner, and Patricia watched that happen and her face did something that was not quite crying, and then she said thank you and left.

Andrea stood at the window and watched her go.

The year turned. The families from the commuter subdivisions kept finding them, kept calling, kept showing up on trial days with dogs who needed somewhere to be. Jim learned the new dogs the way he'd learned the first ones: sitting with them, letting them decide. By February they had fifty-one dogs. By March, fifty-eight.

The awards came in the spring — Best of Prince William first, then the Virginia recognition. Jim put the certificates in the office where the staff could see them. Andrea mentioned them only when families asked.

It was early 2020. The schedule was full. The world was about to change and none of them knew it. The Okonkwos didn't know it. Marcus Webb didn't know it. The Delgados, still paying ahead, didn't know it.

Bella was in her corner. Captain was in the middle of everything. The floor was loud with the specific sound of dogs who knew each other, who had been in this room enough times to know its rhythms, who had decided this place was safe.

Andrea was on the floor with a new dog, a nervous terrier mix in for her first trial day. She was sitting cross-legged about six feet away, not touching her, not reaching. Just being there. Waiting.
