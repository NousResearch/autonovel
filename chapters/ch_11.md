# Chapter Eleven: The First Year

The shepherd mix arrived on a Tuesday in November, second month of operations, and she came in low to the ground with her ears flat and her whole body saying *no* in the clearest possible language. Her name was Bella, which was the name of half the dogs in Northern Virginia, and her owner was a woman named Patricia Cho who worked in Tysons and had been driving forty minutes each way since September and felt, she said, terrible about it.

Jim had heard the terrible about it before. He would hear it again. It was what people said when they handed over the leash and then stood there another three minutes because they couldn't quite make themselves leave.

"She's going to be fine," Andrea said, which was what Andrea said, and which was almost always true.

Jim didn't say anything. He sat down on the floor near the door, not close to Bella, just in the same general region of the room, and he waited. Patricia Cho watched him do this with an expression that was somewhere between confused and grateful, and then Andrea gently redirected her toward the front desk, toward the forms and the discussion of pickup times and the question of whether Bella had food sensitivities. By the time Patricia signed the last form, Bella was sitting eighteen inches from Jim's left knee, not looking at him, but not looking at the wall either.

Jim didn't touch her. He let her have the distance.

Patricia said goodbye three times. The third time, she was already at the door.

By the end of that first week, Bella had found a spot she liked near the south wall, a corner where she could see the whole room without being in the middle of it. By December she was sleeping there. By January she was crossing the floor to Jim when he came in the morning, not running, not performing, just walking over with the specific purposefulness of a dog who has decided something.

Andrea noticed. She noticed everything.

---

There was a golden retriever named Captain who belonged to the Okonkwos, a family from Gainesville with three kids and a schedule that ran from six in the morning until seven at night and a dog who needed somewhere to put his energy or he would put it into the baseboards. Captain had no fear and no guile and no concept of personal space, and he was beloved by everyone on staff within a week because it was impossible to be near Captain without feeling like something good was happening.

There was a French bulldog named Pretzel who belonged to Marcus Webb, a contractor who worked out of Manassas and dropped Pretzel off at seven-fifteen every morning without fail and picked him up at five-thirty without fail and always asked the same question: *Was he good?* The answer was always yes, because Pretzel was a profoundly agreeable dog, and Marcus Webb always looked briefly relieved, as though he'd been worried the answer would be different.

There was a young lab mix named Scout whose family, the Delgados, had moved from Alexandria in the spring and were still figuring out what Haymarket was and whether they'd made a mistake. Andrea figured this out from the first tour — from the way Mrs. Delgado kept referencing things they'd had access to in Alexandria, the trainer they'd used, the daycare they'd loved. She gave the tour and answered the questions and when Mrs. Delgado said, *It's just — we want to make sure it's the same quality*, Andrea said, *It's not the same. It's different. Let me show you why.*

The Delgados became regulars by February. Mrs. Delgado texted Andrea directly now, not the main number.

This was the work. This was what the work looked like from the inside.

Andrea kept track of things that didn't appear on any form. She knew that Captain got overstimulated after lunch if there were more than twelve dogs on the floor and needed twenty minutes in the quieter back area. She knew that Pretzel's ears needed checking every other week because Marcus Webb, for all his punctuality, was not a man who noticed ears. She knew that Scout had a thing about the drain cover near the washing station and needed to approach it on his own terms. She wrote none of this down in a place anyone else could find it. She carried it.

She also kept track of the families. She knew that Patricia Cho's mother had been sick in October and that Patricia had mentioned it once, briefly, while signing Bella in, and that Patricia had not mentioned it again since, which probably meant one of two things. She knew that the Okonkwos' oldest was applying to colleges and that Mrs. Okonkwo was trying not to talk about it constantly and mostly succeeding. She knew that Marcus Webb was going through something — she didn't know what, but she knew from the way he asked *was he good* that the question was doing more work than it appeared to.

One morning in late January, she was in the office at seven-fifteen when her phone showed her a text from Patricia Cho: *Just checking in. How is she today?* 

It was seven-fifteen. Patricia had dropped Bella off at seven-oh-five.

Andrea looked at the text for a moment. Then she went to the floor, found Bella in her corner, took a photo — Bella with her chin on her paws, watching the room with the calm authority of a dog who owned the place — and sent it with a caption: *She's good. Already in her spot.*

Patricia's response came back in under a minute. *Thank you. I needed that.*

Andrea put her phone in her pocket and went back to work. She thought about Patricia's mother. She picked up her phone and typed a note to herself. *Call her Wednesday.*

---

By the end of the first year, they had forty-three regular dogs.

Jim sat at the kitchen table on a Thursday evening in late December with the laptop open and the numbers in front of him, and the numbers were good. Not comfortable — good was not the same as comfortable, not yet — but good in the way that a first year could be good, which meant they had built the thing they said they would build and it was holding its shape.

Andrea sat across from him with a mug of tea and the week's schedule, which she'd brought home out of habit, and Cassie was under the table with her head on Andrea's foot, and Gracie was on the couch in the next room making the specific sound of a dog who has decided she's done for the evening.

"Captain's family wants to add a boarding night," Andrea said.

"When?"

"February. They're going to Myrtle Beach."

Jim looked at the schedule. "We can do that."

"I told them we could do that."

He made a note. Outside, a car went by on Route 15, its headlights sweeping briefly across the kitchen window and then gone. The refrigerator made its refrigerator sound. Cassie shifted and put her chin on Jim's foot instead, redistributing her allegiance with the perfect arbitrariness of dogs.

"Bella's coming every day now," Jim said.

"I know."

"Patricia asked me last week if she could do five days instead of three."

"I know that too. I already adjusted the rate."

Jim looked up. Andrea was looking at the schedule, not at him, but there was something in the set of her face that was not quite a smile and was not quite not one. She had known before he said it. She usually knew before he said it.

"The Delgados paid three months ahead," she said.

"That's good."

"That's very good. That's someone who's decided to trust you."

He nodded. He understood what she meant. In this business, paying three months ahead was not a financial transaction. It was a statement. The Delgados had looked at what they'd found and decided it was worth betting on, and they'd put that bet down in the specific language of prepaid invoices.

"We're going to be okay," Andrea said. It was not a question. It was not quite a declaration either. It was the thing you said when you had been looking at the numbers and the numbers were good and you'd been too afraid to say it for twelve months and now it was December and you could finally say it.

Jim closed the laptop. "We're going to be okay."

Cassie's tail moved once against the floor, a single thump, and then stopped.

They were tired in the way that people were tired when they had been doing something real. Not the tired of exhaustion or defeat. The tired of having spent themselves on something worth spending on, and knowing it, and sitting in the kitchen at the end of the year with the numbers good and the dogs accounted for and the schedule full of names they knew.

Andrea set down the mug. "Andy wants to know if we can get a third dog."

"What did you tell him?"

"I told him to ask you."

"Smart."

"I thought so."

Jim stood and rinsed his glass at the sink and looked out at the dark backyard where the fence they'd put in three years ago was just barely visible in the light from the neighbor's porch. He was thinking about the spring. The families from the new subdivisions in Gainesville were still finding them — he'd had three calls this week from people who'd heard about them from other people, which was the best possible way to get a call. Word of mouth in a community like this was not marketing. It was testimony.

He turned off the kitchen light. Andrea was already up, folding the schedule, and Cassie had relocated to the living room where Gracie had made room on the couch without being asked, the way dogs made room for each other when they'd been sharing a space long enough to know the geometry of it.

"We should call Dottie," Andrea said.

"I'll call her tomorrow."

"She'll want to know about the numbers."

"She'll want to know about the dogs."

Andrea considered this. "Both. She'll want to know both."

---

Cassie woke Jim at six-fifteen on a Friday morning in early January by sitting next to the bed and breathing at him until he opened his eyes. This was her method. It had a hundred percent success rate.

He got up. He fed both dogs, which required managing the fact that Gracie ate quickly and Cassie ate slowly and Gracie had opinions about this discrepancy. He stood in the kitchen in the gray January light and drank his coffee and listened to the sound of the dogs eating, which was one of the more reliable sounds in his life, and watched the sky go from dark to the particular pale gray of a Virginia winter morning.

Andrea came down twenty minutes later, dressed, her hair still damp. She poured coffee. She looked at her phone. She put her phone face-down on the counter.

"Patricia texted," she said.

"This early?"

"She texted at six. Her mother died."

Jim set down his coffee.

"She's not bringing Bella today. She said she'll be in touch next week." Andrea picked up her phone again, looked at it, put it down. "I'm going to send flowers."

"From the business or from us?"

Andrea thought about this for a moment. "Both," she said. "Separately."

Jim picked up his coffee. Outside, the sky had gone from gray to the specific pale blue of a cold clear morning, the kind of blue that meant the Blue Ridge would be sharp on the horizon today, fully visible, the way it was on the days when the air had been rinsed clean by cold. He couldn't see it from the kitchen window. He knew it was there.

Cassie came and sat on his foot. He reached down and put his hand on her head without looking at her, and she leaned into it slightly, and they stood like that for a moment, the two of them, while Andrea typed something on her phone.

"She'll bring Bella back," Andrea said. Not to Jim, exactly. To the room.

"I know she will."

"Bella's her — " Andrea stopped. Started again. "Bella's what she has right now."

Jim nodded. He understood what that meant. He had understood it before they opened, before the permits, before the name was the name — he had understood that this was what they were building, not just a place for dogs to go, but the thing the dogs gave to the people who loved them, which was continuity, presence, something in the room that needed you and didn't know about the rest of it.

He put on his coat. He had forty-three dogs to get to.

---

The year turned. January became February. The families from the commuter subdivisions kept finding them, kept calling, kept showing up on trial days with dogs who needed somewhere to be and people who needed somewhere to put their trust. Jim learned the new dogs the way he'd learned the first ones: by sitting with them, by letting them decide, by paying the specific and patient attention that could not be performed, only given.

By February they had fifty-one dogs. By March, fifty-eight.

Andrea gave tours three times a week now, sometimes four. She had a rhythm to it — the entry, the floor, the grooming area, the explanation of what cage-free meant in practice, not just in principle. She had gotten good at reading what each family needed from the tour. Some needed the facts: the ratio of staff to dogs, the cleaning protocols, the emergency procedures. Some needed to see the dogs themselves, to watch how they moved in the space, to look at Bella in her corner and Captain in the middle of everything and understand from the dogs' own bodies that this was a place that worked.

Some just needed Andrea to look them in the eye and mean it when she said: *Your dog is going to be happy here.*

She always meant it.

The awards came in the spring. Best of Prince William first, then the Virginia recognition, and Jim put the certificates in the office where the staff could see them and Andrea told the families about them only when families asked, which some did and some didn't, and either way the certificates were not the point. The point was Bella walking across the floor every morning. The point was Pretzel's agreeable face and Scout's negotiation with the drain cover and Captain's complete conviction that every single person who walked through the door had arrived specifically to see him.

The point was the numbers in December and Andrea saying *we're going to be okay* and meaning it.

The point was this: they had built what they said they would build. In the town they'd chosen. With the name Andy had given them from the back seat of the car. It was on the building on Fayette Street and it was what it said it was and the dogs knew it, which was the only review that mattered.

---

It was early 2020.

Cassie and Gracie were home. The schedule was full. Patricia Cho had brought Bella back in the second week of January, thinner in the face, quieter than usual, and she'd handed over the leash and Jim had taken it and Bella had gone immediately to her corner, and Patricia had watched that happen and her face had done something that was not quite crying, and then she'd said thank you and left.

Andrea had stood at the window and watched her go.

The world was about to change. The families coming through the door in February didn't know this. The Okonkwos didn't know it. Marcus Webb didn't know it. The Delgados, still pleased with their decision, still paying ahead, didn't know it. Jim didn't know it. Andrea didn't know it.

Bella was in her corner. Captain was in the middle of everything, as always. The floor was full and loud with the specific sound of dogs who knew each other, who had been in this room together enough times to know its rhythms and its rules, who had decided, in the way that dogs decided things, that this place was safe.

Outside on Route 15, the morning traffic was moving toward Gainesville, toward the I-66 on-ramp, toward Washington. The Blue Ridge sat on the western horizon, doing what it always did: being there, being visible on clear days and invisible on overcast ones, always present whether you could see it or not.

Andrea was on the floor with a new dog, a nervous terrier mix who'd come in that morning for her first trial day. She was sitting cross-legged about six feet away from it, not touching it, not reaching for it, just being there. Waiting.

Jim watched her from across the room and thought: *she learned that from me.*

He was wrong. She'd always known it.
