# Chapter Twenty

The apple was almost gone.

Cass turned the core in his hands, the last of it — just the pale fibrous column and the seeds tucked in their chambers, the smell of it sharp and fading. His father had taken the last real bite. Cass had taken the one before that. Now there was nothing left worth eating and he held it anyway, looking at the way the seeds sat in there, brown-tipped, orderly, like something that had been arranged.

He set it in the cupholder. His father glanced at it but didn't say anything.

Outside, the light was doing what it had been doing for the last forty miles — coming and going, the bare trees catching it and then blocking it as the road bent through the low hills, so that the car moved through alternating bands of pale gold and gray. Cass watched it. The fourth movement was still running in the back of his skull, the cello line specifically, the way it came in under the violins and held everything up from underneath. He hadn't asked it to be there. It was just there.

The needle behind his left eye had been there since Grafton.

Not bad. Not a spike. Just present, like a tuning fork held an inch from the bone — a single frequency, steady, not going anywhere. He'd had worse. He'd had much worse, the kind that made him put his head against the cold window glass and breathe through his mouth and wait for it to pass. This was just the background hum of it. He could work around it.

He shifted in the seat and looked at the road ahead.

Eighty miles on the last sign. That was — he did the math without meaning to — maybe an hour and fifteen. The sky had gone from that early non-color to something that was almost blue now, cloud-streaked, the orange at the horizon fully established and working its way up through the layers. It was going to be a real morning. The kind his grandfather would have pointed out if he'd been awake to see it.

His grandfather had been asleep when they left.

---

Cass had stood in the doorway of the guest room for a moment before they went. He'd meant to just check — make sure the man was breathing, which was a thing he'd started doing without deciding to, sometime in the last year. His grandfather was on his back with one arm across his chest and the other at his side, and his breathing was slow and audible, not labored, just the sound of an old man sleeping deeply. The canvas bag was on the chair by the window, already packed. His grandfather had packed it the night before, while Cass was practicing in the living room. Cass had come out and found it there and understood that his grandfather had known they'd be leaving early and had done the thing he could do, which was make sure Cass had apples and the sandwiches he liked and — he'd checked — the small score of the Brahms intermezzo that Cass had been working on, tucked in the side pocket.

He hadn't asked for it. His grandfather had just put it there.

Standing in the doorway, Cass had wanted to say something. He didn't know what. His grandfather's face in sleep looked different from his face awake — softer, or maybe just more still, without the slight watchfulness that was always there when he was conscious, the quality of a man who had spent sixty years listening for wrong notes and never quite turned it off. Asleep, he just looked old. Cass had stood there for maybe thirty seconds and then gone to find his father.

In the car, pulling out of the driveway, Cass had looked back at the house. The guest room window was dark.

---

"You know what quintet he used?" his father said.

Cass came back to the car. "What?"

"When Brahms premiered it. The quintet. You know what players?"

"No." He thought about it. "Do you?"

His father made a slight sound. "No. I was just wondering."

This was a thing his father did — asked questions he didn't have the answers to, not rhetorically, just because the question had arrived in his head and he'd said it out loud. Cass used to find it strange. Now he understood it was a kind of thinking.

"I could look it up," Cass said.

"Later."

The road straightened and the light came back, and Cass watched his father's hands on the wheel. His father drove the way he did most things — without waste. Both hands, steady, not gripping. He'd been driving since before Cass was born and it showed in the total absence of performance. He wasn't demonstrating that he knew how to drive. He just drove.

"How's the head?" his father said.

"Fine."

His father looked at him for a second, then back at the road. He didn't push it.

The thing about the needle behind Cass's eye was that it wasn't always a warning. Sometimes it was just the residue of a long evening, or the particular frequency of the car engine sitting at highway speed, or the fact that he'd slept at a bad angle in the back seat somewhere around two in the morning. His father knew the difference between the needle that meant something was wrong and the needle that meant Cass needed water and an hour of quiet. He'd gotten good at reading which was which. Right now he seemed to have read it correctly, because he didn't ask again.

Cass got his water bottle from the bag and drank half of it.

The cello line moved through the back of his skull. He let it.

He'd told his father he was going to learn the Brahms — the whole quintet, not just the cello part, the whole five-voice architecture of it — and his father had said *okay* in that particular way that meant he was taking it seriously. Not *that's a big project* or *are you sure* or any of the cautionary things an adult might say. Just *okay*, and then the small sound that wasn't quite a laugh, and *you sound like him*. Meaning his grandfather. Meaning the man asleep in the guest room with the canvas bag already packed.

Cass had known which *him* immediately. He hadn't needed to ask.

He turned the water bottle in his hands. The plastic was cold from sitting in the bag overnight, and the condensation on the outside was exactly the temperature of the air in the car, which was still slightly cool from the night. His father had turned the heat on low but not all the way. Cass's feet were warm. His hands were almost warm.

Outside, another green sign. Sixty-three miles.

He'd gained seventeen miles while he was thinking about his grandfather.

---

The thing about the Brahms quintet — the thing that had settled into him over the last few days, listening to it on his headphones while his grandfather slept in the next room, running the cello line on the instrument in the evenings when the house was quiet enough — was that it wasn't one voice carrying the others. It was five voices that needed each other. The cello could carry the harmonic foundation for twelve bars and then drop away and the viola would take it, and the first violin would be doing something completely different up in the register, and none of it was decoration. All of it was load-bearing.

He'd never played in a quintet.

He'd played in the school orchestra, which was a hundred and twelve people and a different thing entirely — the sense of the individual voice subsumed into the mass, your particular sound just one strand of something much larger. He'd played in a trio once, at a competition, and that had been closer — three people listening to each other so hard that the listening was as much of the work as the playing. But five voices was different from three. Five was complex enough that you could lose the thread if you stopped paying attention for even a bar, and simple enough that every absence showed.

You'd need a quartet to play it with.

His father had said it not as a discouragement but as a fact, the way his father stated facts — plainly, without softening, because softening would be a kind of condescension. Cass knew he didn't have a quartet. He also knew that knowing the piece — the whole of it, all five voices, the way they fit and where they needed each other — was the thing that came before finding the people to play it with. You couldn't hear what you were missing until you understood what was supposed to be there.

The needle pulsed once, sharply, then settled.

Cass put the water bottle down and pressed two fingers to the side of his head, just above the temple. The skin there was slightly warm. He held the pressure for a moment, not because it helped exactly but because it gave the pain something to push against, which was different from just letting it sit there unacknowledged.

His father saw it. Cass could tell by the slight change in his father's posture — the almost imperceptible shift of attention — but his father kept his eyes on the road and said nothing, which was the right thing.

"I'm okay," Cass said anyway.

"I know."

The light came through the windshield at a low angle now, the sun getting itself properly above the horizon, and it caught the dust on the dashboard and the small crack in the lower right corner of the windshield that had been there since August and that his father kept meaning to get fixed. In the light, the crack looked like a musical staff that someone had started and abandoned. Cass had noticed this before and never said it out loud. He noticed it again now.

---

They stopped for gas outside of Woodbridge.

Cass got out while his father worked the pump, and the cold hit him — not the car-cold of the last several hours but the real outside cold, the kind that had weight to it, that pressed against his face and the backs of his hands and got into his collar. He stood by the passenger door and breathed it in. The gas station smelled like exhaust and the particular sweetness of the fuel itself, and underneath that the smell of a fast food place on the other side of the parking lot that was already open, its exhaust fans running, pushing out the smell of oil and coffee.

His father was doing something with the pump.

"It's not reading the card," his father said.

"Do you want me to go in?"

"No, I've got it." His father went inside, and Cass stood by the car.

A truck pulled into the next lane and the driver got out — a large man in a canvas jacket, moving quickly, with the purposeful efficiency of someone who had done this stop a thousand times and had no intention of spending more time on it than necessary. He nodded at Cass. Cass nodded back. The man started his pump and got his phone out and looked at it with the focused expression of a person checking something specific, not scrolling, and then put it away and looked at the sky.

"Going to be a nice day," the man said. Not to Cass specifically. Just out loud.

"Yeah," Cass said.

The man looked at him for a second — not unkindly, just noticing — and then looked back at his pump.

Cass looked at the sky too. It was fully light now, the blue coming up through the cloud cover, the orange burned off into a thin line at the horizon that was losing ground by the minute. His grandfather would have said something about the light. His grandfather noticed weather the way he noticed music — not as backdrop, but as a thing happening, a thing worth paying attention to. He'd said once that the best musicians were the ones who couldn't stop noticing things, who were constitutionally incapable of going through the world on autopilot, and that this was both the gift and the cost of it. You heard everything. You couldn't un-hear it. The needle behind Cass's eye was, in some version of this, the price of the same quality — a nervous system calibrated too finely, catching frequencies that most people walked right past.

His grandfather had not said this second part. Cass had worked it out himself.

His father came back with a receipt and a cup of coffee.

"You want anything?"

"I'm good."

They got back in the car. His father peeled back the lid on the coffee and the smell of it filled the car immediately, dark and slightly bitter, the gas station version of the thing rather than the real thing. His father drank it without complaint. He had the quality of a man who could take what was available and not spend energy grieving what wasn't.

Forty-one miles.

---

The radio was still doing what it had been doing all night — the classical station out of DC, the signal strengthening as they came back into range. It had gone to static somewhere around Grafton and his father had turned it down to almost nothing, just enough to tell when it came back. Now it was back, a string quartet Cass didn't recognize, something spare and modal, the kind of thing that sounded simple and wasn't.

"Do you know this?" Cass said.

His father listened for a moment. "Shostakovich, maybe. Eight?"

Cass listened. The cello was doing something underneath the upper voices that was almost too quiet to hear over the engine noise, a sustained note that didn't move while everything above it did. "Maybe," he said.

"Or seven." His father paused. "I don't actually know."

"I thought you knew all of them."

"Nobody knows all of them." His father drank his coffee. "Your grandfather knows more than I do."

This was probably true. His grandfather's knowledge of the repertoire was the kind that came from sixty years of playing and teaching, from having had the actual scores under his hands, from knowing not just the sound of a piece but the physical memory of how it felt to play it. Cass's knowledge was mostly from listening. He was still building the other kind.

The spare modal thing on the radio resolved into something unexpected — a sudden shift, the first violin going somewhere the piece hadn't suggested it was going, and Cass felt it land in the place behind his sternum where music landed when it was doing something right. The needle behind his eye pulsed once, sympathetically, then settled.

"There," his father said.

"Yeah."

They listened until the movement ended.

---

The last twenty miles went quickly. The road widened, the exits came closer together, the trees thinned and gave way to the familiar geometry of Northern Virginia — the overpasses, the sound barriers, the long stretches of commercial development running parallel to the highway. Cass watched it come. He knew this approach, had made it hundreds of times, but it looked different at this hour, in this light, after the particular kind of night they'd had. The ordinary made strange by context. His grandfather had a word for it in German that Cass could never remember, a word for the experience of seeing a familiar thing as if for the first time.

His father took Exit 40.

Route 15 south, and then the turn onto Fayette Street, and then the house. The street was quiet. A neighbor's car was in the driveway with frost on the windshield. The maple at the corner had dropped most of its leaves, and they'd blown into a drift against the fence that Cass would probably be asked to rake. The house looked the way it always looked — ordinary, specific, exactly itself.

His father pulled into the driveway and turned off the engine.

Neither of them moved for a moment.

The quiet after a long drive had a particular quality — the absence of road noise, the engine ticking as it cooled, the sense of the world being still after hours of movement. Cass sat in it. His father sat in it. The coffee cup was in the holder between them, empty. The apple core was in the other holder.

"You should sleep," his father said.

"Probably."

"A few hours, at least."

Cass looked at the house. The window of the room where he practiced was on the second floor, facing the street, and the glass caught the morning light and gave it back slightly warmer. He thought about the Brahms score on his stand. He thought about the cello in its case in the back seat, which his father had driven three hours to bring back to him. He thought about his grandfather's hands packing the canvas bag in the dark, putting the score in the side pocket without being asked.

"Dad," he said.

"Yeah."

"When he — " Cass stopped. Started again. "When he was my age. Was he already — did he already know?"

His father was quiet for a moment. Not the quiet of not knowing. The quiet of choosing.

"He knew he wanted it," his father said finally. "Whether he knew he'd get it — " He shook his head slightly. "I don't think it works that way. I don't think you know that part."

"But he kept going anyway."

"He kept going anyway."

Cass looked at the window. The light in the glass shifted as a cloud moved somewhere above them, and the warmth in it went briefly, then came back.

His father picked up the empty coffee cup, looked at it, put it back. "Go sleep," he said. "The Brahms will still be there."

"I know."

"Cass."

He looked at his father.

"It's a good piece to learn." His father said it simply, without ceremony. "It's a good one."

Cass got out of the car. The cold came back, different from the gas station cold — this was his street, his cold, the specific temperature of Fayette Street at this hour in this season. He got the cello case from the back seat, the canvas bag from next to it, and stood in the driveway for a moment with both of them.

His father was still in the car, not moving yet, looking at something through the windshield that Cass couldn't see from where he stood.

The needle behind his eye was still there. Quiet, steady, its single frequency.

He carried the cello inside.
