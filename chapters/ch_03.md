# Chapter Three

The house smelled like dinner and he wasn't hungry.

Cass set his bag down inside the door and stood in the hallway with his coat still on. His mother in the kitchen — the low percussion of a pot lid. His father's workroom at the far end of the hall, silent. The silence meant his father was home.

He hung up his coat.

"Cass." His mother appeared in the kitchen doorway. Flour on her forearm. That full attention he could never quite meet for long. "How was it?"

"Fine."

"Maret?"

"Fine." He picked up his bag. "I'm going to wash up."

"Dinner in twenty."

---

He sat on the edge of his bed and pressed the heel of his hand against his left eye.

The ache had faded on the walk home. Sitting down brought it back.

*You heard the note itself.*

He moved his hand and looked at the ceiling.

His grandfather had made that bell. Had died before he could ring it. His father knew this and had sent Cass to Maret's forge without explaining why. The bell had looked clean when he'd seen it — barely marked, the surface still holding its original character, as if someone had been tending it through all those years of silence.

*His father might have been the one maintaining it.*

He stood, because sitting was making the room feel like it was pressing in, and went to the window. A woman walking a dog he didn't recognize. The elm at the corner, which had been losing branches for three years and would probably have to come down eventually. The grey afternoon light that meant rain before morning.

His eye ached.

---

His father was already at the table when Cass came down. He generally arrived last, from the workroom, with the distracted quality of a man whose mind was still in whatever he'd been doing. Tonight he was already seated, hands around a glass of water, looking at the table.

Cass sat.

His mother brought the pot and served without ceremony, and his father looked up.

"She told you," he said.

"About Grandfather's bell."

His father nodded once, slowly, and picked up his fork.

"I was going to tell you myself," his father said. "I wanted you to hear it from her first."

"Why?"

His father set the fork down. "Because I'm too close to it. I would have told you what it means before you'd had a chance to hear what it is."

Cass looked at his plate.

"What does it mean?" he asked.

"I don't know yet."

He looked up. His father was watching him with the kind of attention that felt like measurement, not unkindness.

"I've been going to that forge for twenty years," his father said. "Your grandfather built that bell and then he died, and it's been sitting in Maret's back room since before you were born, and in twenty years I have never once been able to get a sound out of it." He paused. "Not a real sound. Not what it should give."

Cass was quiet.

"She said you heard the under-note," his father said.

"It hurt."

"I know."

"You could have warned me."

"I didn't know it would happen. I hoped." He looked at Cass steadily. "I hoped it might, and I didn't want to tell you that in case it didn't, and I didn't want to tell you in case it changed how you went in."

"That's a lot of things you didn't want to do."

His father almost smiled. "Yes."

Cass pushed the food around on his plate. Outside a car passed, its headlights briefly sweeping the kitchen wall, and then the room was dim again.

"What was he making it for?" Cass asked.

"I don't know that either."

"Does Maret know?"

"She knows more than she's said to me." His father's voice was even, no accusation in it. "She's careful about what she tells and when."

"She told me about Grandfather tonight. First time I met her."

"She told you what she thought you were ready for."

Cass put his fork down. "How does she decide that?"

His father looked at him for a long moment.

"She listens," his father said. "To the person. The same way she listens to the bells." He picked up his fork. "She says everyone has a fundamental pitch. You just have to find it."

"She said Grandfather made it for something specific. Something he didn't finish."

"Yes."

"Do you know what?"

His father was quiet for long enough that Cass counted four seconds in his head.

"He was trying to do something with the under-note," his father said. "Something past just hearing it. He thought — " He stopped. Tried again. "He thought if you could hear it clearly enough, in the right bell, you might be able to use it."

"Use it how?"

"That's what he didn't finish finding out."

Cass sat back. The kitchen was warm and smelled like the meal and like the wood of the old table. He had never thought much about that smell before.

"Why me?" Cass asked.

His father looked up.

"Why did you send me? You could have kept going yourself. You've been going for twenty years."

"Yes."

"So why —"

"Because I can't hear it." His father said it plainly, no performance in it. "I've tried. I have my father's hands and I know the ratios and I've stood in that room with that bell for twenty years and I cannot get the under-note out of it. Not once." He looked at Cass. "You did it in ten seconds."

The kitchen was quiet.

"That doesn't seem fair," Cass said.

His father almost laughed — a short sound, surprised out of him. "No. It doesn't."

His mother reached across and refilled his water glass without being asked.

Cass looked at her. Her expression was something between worry and something that might have been pride but was too careful to be pride yet.

"Am I going back?" Cass asked.

"That's up to Maret," his father said. "And up to you."

"What do you want?"

His father was quiet. Then: "I want you to be careful. And I want you to go back."

Cass nodded. He picked up his fork.

They finished dinner.

He helped his mother with the dishes. She washed, he dried — and he noticed almost immediately that she was moving wrong. A quarter-beat off her usual rhythm, setting things on the rack before they were quite ready, her wrists doing small corrections. He watched her hands and said nothing. Outside the kitchen window the yard had gone dark. The water ran loud and then she turned it off and the kitchen went quiet.

She handed him the first bowl and held it a half-second too long before she let go.

"Your eye," she said.

He looked at her.

"You've been holding your hand near it. Since you came in."

"It's fine."

"Does it still hurt?"

"Not much." He dried the bowl and set it on the stack. "It was worse at the forge. When I rang the bell."

She handed him another bowl without speaking. Then she stopped, hands still in the water, and something shifted in her jaw — a tightening, then a release, the face of a woman deciding to say a thing she had been deciding for longer than tonight.

"Your grandfather had that," she said. "The pain behind the eye."

He went still.

"Your father doesn't know I know." She handed him the bowl. "Maret told me, years ago. Before you were born." A pause. "She said it was the under-note. That it costs something to hear it. That the cost is different for different people, and she didn't know what it meant that for your grandfather it was pain."

"What did it mean for him?"

His mother pulled the drain. She dried her hands on the dish towel and turned to look at him directly, and the full weight of it was in her face — not grief, something older than grief, something that had been sitting in her for thirteen years waiting for this conversation.

"I don't know," she said. "He died before anyone found out."

She folded the towel, hung it on the rack, and left the kitchen.

He stood with the last bowl in his hands and looked at the place where she had been.

His grandfather had felt this same point of light behind his eye. Had stood somewhere with that bell and died before he found out what it meant.

He put the bowl away.

In the hallway he stopped outside his father's workroom. Light at the bottom of the door, and from inside a small sound — something being set on the workbench, the careful sound of a man handling something fragile, and then silence, and then the sound again. Picking it up. Setting it down. Not working. Something else.

He raised his hand and knocked.

The sound stopped.

A long pause. Then footsteps, and the door opened — not all the way, his father's body filling the gap — and Cass could see behind him on the workbench: papers, diagrams, something spread flat under the low lamp. His father's hand was on the door frame and his face in the half-light was tired in a way that had nothing to do with the hour. He looked at Cass. He didn't speak, which was its own kind of question.

"I didn't know what you were teaching me," Cass said. "The ratios. All of it. I didn't know what it was for."

His father looked at him. "I didn't either," he said. "Not for certain."

"But you thought."

"Yes."

They stood there. The warmth came out of the workroom — oil and old paper and something metallic underneath.

"He didn't know I'd exist," Cass said. "Grandfather. He made the bell before I was born."

"Yes."

"So he wasn't making it for me."

His father was quiet for a moment. Then he opened the door a little wider — not an invitation exactly, more like a concession — and Cass could see the papers more clearly: diagrams of bells in cross-section, handwritten annotations in a script he didn't recognize. His father picked up one of the pages and looked at it, and then looked at Cass, and something in his face shifted — a small, precise grief, there and then managed.

He held the page out.

Cass took it. His grandfather's handwriting, he understood without being told. Dense and slanted, the letters pressing forward as if in a hurry. The diagram beneath showed a bell's profile with measurements annotated at each curve. At the bottom, in a different ink, a single line: *the note is not in the metal.*

"He was making it for whoever could hear it," his father said. He took the page back, carefully, and set it in its place. "It just turned out to be you."

Cass looked at the diagrams a moment longer. His father let him.

Then his father stepped back, and the door closed, gently, and Cass stood in the hallway listening to the sound of the page being lifted again on the workbench.

He didn't know if that was love or something more complicated than love, something that looked like love from most angles but had a different name when you got close enough to see the whole shape.

Upstairs, he lay in the dark with his left eye against the pillow. The rain had started — he could hear it against the east window. The room felt sealed.

His grandfather had been working toward something that didn't have a name yet, and that was why he was making the bell in the first place, because you make the instrument before you understand what it will let you play.

The rain came harder. He could hear it in the gutters now, the sound of water moving in volume.

*The bell has been waiting longer than I've been alive.* Then: *it can wait until morning.*

He closed his eyes.

At the edge of sleep, a single tone arrived — low and specific, gone before he could locate it. His eye flared once, bright and sharp. Then the pain settled, and the rain kept on, and he lay very still.
