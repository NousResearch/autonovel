# Chapter Three

The house smelled like dinner and he wasn't hungry.

Cass set his bag down inside the door and stood in the hallway with his coat still on. His mother in the kitchen — the particular rhythm of her moving between the counter and the stove, the low percussion of a pot lid. His father's workroom at the far end of the hall, silent. The silence meant his father was home.

He hung up his coat.

"Cass." His mother appeared in the kitchen doorway. She had flour on her forearm and she looked at him the way she had been looking at him since he was small, that full attention he could never quite meet for long. "How was it?"

"Fine."

"Maret?"

"Fine." He picked up his bag. "I'm going to wash up."

"Dinner in twenty."

He went upstairs.

---

He sat on the edge of his bed and pressed the heel of his hand against his left eye.

The ache had faded to something distant on the walk home, but sitting down seemed to bring it back.

*You heard the note itself.*

He moved his hand away and looked at the ceiling.

His grandfather had made that bell. He had died before he could ring it. His father knew this and had sent Cass to Maret's forge without explaining why, which meant his father had made a decision about what Cass was ready to know and when, and Cass had no idea if that decision was right.

He put his hand back over his eye.

The bell had been sitting in that back room for — how long? Maret had not said. He hadn't asked. He should have asked.

His grandfather had been dead since before Cass was born. Fourteen years, at least. The metal had looked clean, barely marked, as if someone had been tending it. Someone had. The thought settled in his chest before it arrived as a fact: *his father might have been the one maintaining it.*

He stood up, because sitting was making him feel like the room was pressing in, and he went to the window and looked out at the street below. A woman walking a dog he didn't recognize. The elm at the corner, which had been losing branches for three years and would probably have to come down eventually. The grey afternoon light that meant rain before morning.

His eye ached.

---

His father was already at the table when Cass came into the kitchen, which was unusual. His father generally came to the table last, from the workroom, with the particular distracted quality of a man whose mind was still in whatever he'd been doing. Tonight he was already seated, his hands around a glass of water, looking at the table.

Cass sat down.

His mother brought the pot and served without ceremony, and his father looked up.

"She told you," he said.

"About Grandfather's bell," Cass said.

His father nodded once, slowly, and picked up his fork.

"I was going to tell you myself," his father said. "I wanted you to hear it from her first."

"Why?"

His father set the fork down. "Because I'm too close to it. I would have told you what it means before you'd had a chance to hear what it is."

Cass looked at his plate.

"What does it mean?" Cass asked.

"I don't know yet."

He looked up. His father was watching him with the kind of attention that felt like measurement, not unkindness.

"I've been going to that forge for twenty years," his father said. "Your grandfather built that bell and then he died, and it's been sitting in Maret's back room since before you were born, and in twenty years I have never once been able to get a sound out of it." He paused. "Not a real sound. Not what it should give."

Cass was quiet.

"She said you heard the under-note," his father said.

"It hurt."

"I know."

"You could have warned me."

His father picked up his fork again. "I didn't know it would happen. I hoped." He looked at Cass steadily. "I hoped it might, and I didn't want to tell you that in case it didn't, and I didn't want to tell you in case it changed how you went in."

"That's a lot of things you didn't want to do."

His father almost smiled. "Yes."

Cass pushed the food around on his plate. Outside a car passed, its headlights briefly sweeping the kitchen wall, and then the room was dim again.

"What was he making it for?" Cass asked.

"I don't know that either."

"Does Maret know?"

"She knows more than she's said to me." His father's voice was even, no accusation in it. "She's careful about what she tells and when. You'll learn that about her."

"She told me about Grandfather tonight. First time I met her."

"She told you what she thought you were ready for."

Cass put his fork down. "How does she decide that?"

His father looked at him for a long moment.

"She listens," his father said. "To the person. The same way she listens to the bells." He picked up his fork. "She says everyone has a fundamental pitch. You just have to find it."

"She said Grandfather made it for something specific. Something he didn't finish."

"Yes."

"Do you know what?"

His father was quiet for long enough that Cass counted four seconds in his head.

"He was trying to do something with the under-note," his father said. "Something past just hearing it. He thought — " He stopped. Tried again. "He thought if you could hear it clearly enough, in the right bell, you might be able to use it."

"Use it how?"

"That's what he didn't finish finding out."

Cass sat back. The kitchen was warm and smelled like the meal and like the wood of the old table. He had never thought much about that smell before. He thought about it now.

"Why me?" Cass asked.

His father looked up.

"Why did you send me? You could have kept going yourself. You've been going for twenty years."

"Yes."

"So why —"

"Because I can't hear it." His father said it plainly, no performance in it. "I've tried. I have my father's hands and I know the ratios and I've stood in that room with that bell for twenty years and I cannot get the under-note out of it. Not once." He looked at Cass. "You did it in ten seconds."

The kitchen was quiet.

"That doesn't seem fair," Cass said.

His father almost laughed — a short sound, surprised out of him. "No. It doesn't."

His mother reached across and refilled his water glass without being asked.

Cass looked at her. Her expression said something he couldn't quite read, something between worry and something that might have been pride but was too careful to be pride yet.

"Am I going back?" Cass asked.

"That's up to Maret," his father said. "And up to you."

"What do you want?"

His father was quiet for a moment. Then: "I want you to be careful. And I want you to go back."

Cass nodded. He picked up his fork.

They finished dinner.

---

He helped his mother with the dishes, his habit on weekday nights. She washed, he dried. The water ran loud and then she turned it off and handed him the first bowl, and he could feel something in her rhythm that was different — a half-beat of hesitation before each handoff, the set of her shoulders not quite relaxed.

"Your eye," she said.

He looked at her.

"You've been holding your hand near it. Since you came in."

"It's fine."

"Does it still hurt?"

"Not much." He dried the bowl and set it on the stack. "It was worse at the forge. When I rang the bell."

She handed him another bowl and didn't say anything. Then:

"Your grandfather had that. The pain behind the eye."

He went still.

"Your father doesn't know I know," she said. "Maret told me, years ago. Before you were born." She handed him the last bowl. "She said it was the under-note. That it costs something to hear it. That the cost is different for different people, and she didn't know what it meant that for your grandfather it was pain."

"What did it mean for him?"

His mother pulled the drain. She dried her hands on the dish towel and turned to look at him directly, which she had not done yet, and he understood that she had been working up to this, that the whole dish-washing had been working up to this.

"I don't know," she said. "He died before anyone found out."

She folded the towel and hung it on the rack and left the kitchen.

He stood with the last bowl in his hands and looked at the place where she had been standing.

His grandfather had felt this same point of light behind his eye. Had stood somewhere with that bell and died before he found out what it meant.

He put the bowl away.

He went to the hallway and stood outside his father's workroom. Light showed at the bottom of the door. He raised his hand to knock — and then he heard it: the small sound of something being set down on the workbench, the particular careful sound of a man handling something fragile, and then silence, and then the sound again, as if his father was picking it up and setting it down and picking it up. Not working. Something else.

He knocked.

The sound stopped.

A long pause — long enough that Cass thought he wasn't going to answer. Then the door opened, not all the way, and his father looked out at him. Behind him on the workbench Cass could see papers, diagrams, something he couldn't quite make out in the low light. His father's hand was on the door frame.

"I didn't know what you were teaching me," Cass said. "The ratios. All of it. I didn't know what it was for."

His father looked at him. His face in the half-light was tired in a way that had nothing to do with the hour.

"I didn't either," his father said. "Not for certain."

"But you thought."

"Yes."

They stood there. Cass could feel the warmth coming out of the workroom, the particular smell of his father's work — oil and old paper and something metallic underneath.

"He didn't know I'd exist," Cass said. "Grandfather. He made the bell before I was born."

"Yes."

"So he wasn't making it for me."

His father was quiet for a moment. "He was making it for whoever could hear it." He looked at Cass steadily. "It just turned out to be you."

Cass nodded. His father nodded. Neither of them said anything else, and after a moment his father stepped back and the door closed, gently, and Cass stood in the hallway listening to the sound of something being picked up again on the workbench.

He didn't know if that was love or something more complicated than love, something that looked like love from most angles but had a different name when you got close enough to see the whole shape.

He went upstairs to bed.

He lay in the dark with his left eye against the pillow, which was cooler than the air in the room. The rain had started — he could hear it against the east window, the window he'd chosen at seven for the morning light. Now it just made the room feel sealed.

His grandfather had been working toward something that didn't have a name yet, and that was why he was making the bell in the first place, because you make the instrument before you understand what it will let you play.

The rain came harder. He could hear it in the gutters now, the sound of water moving in volume.

He thought: *the bell has been waiting longer than I've been alive.* Then: *it can wait until morning.*

He closed his eyes.

At the edge of sleep, a single tone arrived — low and specific, gone before he could locate it. His eye flared once, bright and sharp, and then the pain settled back and he lay very still and listened to the rain and did not hear it again.
