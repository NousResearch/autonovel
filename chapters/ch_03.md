# Chapter Three

The house smelled like dinner and he wasn't hungry.

Cass set his bag down inside the door and stood in the hallway with his coat still on, listening. His mother in the kitchen — the particular rhythm of her moving between the counter and the stove, the low percussion of a pot lid. His father's workroom at the far end of the hall, silent. The silence meant his father was home.

He hung up his coat.

"Cass." His mother appeared in the kitchen doorway. She had flour on her forearm and she looked at him the way she had been looking at him since he was small, that full attention he could never quite meet for long. "How was it?"

"Fine."

"Maret?"

"Fine." He picked up his bag. "I'm going to wash up."

"Dinner in twenty."

He went upstairs.

---

He sat on the edge of his bed and pressed the heel of his hand against his left eye.

The ache had faded to something distant on the walk home, but sitting down seemed to bring it back — 

*You heard the note itself.*

He moved his hand away and looked at the ceiling.

His grandfather had made that bell. He had died before he could ring it. His father knew this and had sent Cass to Maret's forge without explaining why, which meant his father had made a decision about what Cass was ready to know and when, and Cass had no idea if that decision was right.

He put his hand back over his eye.

The bell had been sitting in that back room for — how long? Maret had not said how long. He hadn't asked. He should have asked. 

How long had it been waiting?

His grandfather had been dead since before Cass was born. So at least that long. At least fourteen years.

He sat with that for a moment.

Fourteen years was not a long time for bronze. Bronze lasted centuries if you kept it from the worst of the moisture and the acid, which Maret clearly did — her forge was dry and carefully managed, and the bell in the back room had looked clean, the metal barely oxidized, the surface still holding its original character. Someone had been maintaining it. Either Maret herself or someone before her.

The thought arrived without announcement: *his father might have been the one maintaining it.*

He took his hand away from his eye and stood up, because sitting was making him feel like the room was pressing in, and he went to the window and looked out at the street below. A woman walking a dog he didn't recognize. The elm at the corner, which had been losing branches for three years and would probably have to come down eventually. The grey afternoon light that meant rain before morning.

His eye ached.

He went downstairs to wash his hands.

---

His father was already at the table when Cass came into the kitchen, which was unusual. His father generally came to the table last, from the workroom, with the particular distracted quality of a man whose mind was still in whatever he'd been doing. Tonight he was already seated, his hands around a glass of water, looking at the table.

Cass sat down.

His mother brought the pot and served without ceremony, the way she always did, and 

His father looked up.

"She told you," he said.

It wasn't a question. Cass looked at him.

"About Grandfather's bell," Cass said.

His father nodded once, slowly, and picked up his fork. He turned it over in his hand — not using it, just holding it, which was something he did when he was deciding how to say something. Cass had watched him do it his whole life.

"I was going to tell you myself," his father said. "I wanted you to hear it from her first."

"Why?"

His father set the fork down. "Because I'm too close to it. I would have told you what it means before you'd had a chance to hear what it is."

Cass looked at his plate. 

"What does it mean?" Cass asked.

"I don't know yet."

He looked up. His father was watching him with the kind of attention that felt like measurement, not unkindness.

"I've been going to that forge for twenty years," his father said. "Your grandfather built that bell and then he died, and it's been sitting in Maret's back room since before you were born, and in twenty years I have never once been able to get a sound out of it." He paused. "Not a real sound. Not what it should give."

Cass was quiet.

"She said you heard the under-note," his father said.

"It hurt."

"I know."

"You could have warned me."

His father picked up his fork again. "I didn't know it would happen. I hoped." He looked at Cass steadily. "I hoped it might, and I didn't want to tell you that in case it didn't, and I didn't want to tell you in case it changed how you went in."

"That's a lot of things you didn't want to do."

His father almost smiled. "Yes."

His mother reached across and refilled his water glass without being asked, which was her way of saying: *keep going, I'm here, this matters.*

Cass ate a few bites. The food was good — it was always good — but he was eating by rote, the fork moving because he'd been raised to eat what was in front of him.

"What was he making it for?" Cass asked.

"I don't know that either."

"Does Maret know?"

"She knows more than she's said to me." His father's voice was even, no accusation in it. "She's careful about what she tells and when. You'll learn that about her."

"She told me about Grandfather tonight. First time I met her."

"She told you what she thought you were ready for."

Cass put his fork down. "How does she decide that?"

His father looked at him for a long moment. Outside, a car passed, its headlights briefly sweeping the kitchen wall.

"She listens," his father said. "To the person. The same way she listens to the bells." He picked up his fork. "She says everyone has a fundamental pitch. You just have to find it."

"She said Grandfather made it for something specific," Cass said. "Something he didn't finish."

"Yes."

"Do you know what?"

His father was quiet for long enough that Cass counted four seconds in his head.

"He was trying to do something with the under-note," his father said. "Something past just hearing it. He thought — " He stopped. Tried again. "He thought if you could hear it clearly enough, in the right bell, you might be able to use it."

"Use it how?"

"That's what he didn't finish finding out."

Cass sat back in his chair. The kitchen was warm and smelled like the meal and like the wood of the old table and like the particular smell of his parents' house, which he would not have been able to describe to someone who hadn't grown up in it. He had never thought much about that smell before. 

"Why me?" Cass asked.

His father looked up.

"Why did you send me? You could have kept going yourself. You've been going for twenty years."

"Yes."

"So why —"

"Because I can't hear it." His father said it plainly, no performance in it. "I've tried. I have my father's hands and I know the ratios and I've stood in that room with that bell for twenty years and I cannot get the under-note out of it. Not once." He looked at Cass. "You did it in ten seconds."

The kitchen was quiet.

"That doesn't seem fair," Cass said.

His father almost laughed — a short sound, surprised out of him. "No. It doesn't."

His mother looked at Cass across the table and her expression said something he couldn't quite read, something between worry and something else, something that might have been pride but was too careful to be pride yet.

"Am I going back?" Cass asked.

"That's up to Maret," his father said. "And up to you."

"What do you want?"

His father was quiet for a moment. Then: "I want you to be careful. And I want you to go back."

Cass nodded. He picked up his fork.

They finished dinner.

---

He helped his mother with the dishes, which was his habit on weekday nights, and she washed and he dried and they stood at the sink together in the comfortable silence of people who had done this a thousand times.

"Your eye," she said.

He looked at her.

"You've been holding your hand near it. Since you came in."

He hadn't realized he'd been doing that. "It's fine."

"Does it still hurt?"

"Not much." He dried a bowl and set it on the stack. "It was worse at the forge. When I rang the bell."

She handed him another bowl. She was looking at the sink, not at him. "Your grandfather had that. The pain behind the eye."

He went still.

"Your father doesn't know I know," she said. "Maret told me, years ago. Before you were born." She handed him the last bowl. "She said it was the under-note. That it costs something to hear it. That the cost is different for different people and she didn't know what it meant that for your grandfather it was pain."

"What did it mean for him?"

His mother pulled the drain and dried her hands on the dish towel. She looked at him.

"I don't know," she said. "He died before anyone found out."

She folded the towel and hung it on the rack and left the kitchen. He stood with the last bowl in his hands and looked at the place where she had been standing.

He put the bowl away.

His grandfather had felt this same point of light behind his eye. Had stood in some room — maybe Maret's back room, maybe somewhere else — and felt the under-note come up through his hands and into his skull, and had thought: *this is something, this means something, I need to find out what.* And then he had died before he could.

Cass went to the hallway and stood outside his father's workroom.

The light was on under the door. He could hear his father moving inside — the particular sounds of the workroom at night, the soft settling of tools, the occasional low note of metal on metal, careful and deliberate. His father working through whatever he was thinking about, the way he always did. The work was how his father processed things. Cass understood this because he was the same.

He raised his hand to knock. Stopped.

His father had sent him to Maret because he couldn't hear the under-note himself. Had been going to that forge for twenty years. Had stood in that room and tried and failed and then waited until his son was old enough and sent him instead, without explaining, because he hadn't wanted to change how Cass went in.

Twenty years of trying. And then: *here, you go.*

He lowered his hand and went upstairs to bed.

He lay in the dark with his left eye against the pillow, which was cooler than the air in the room and pressed gently against the ache in a way that helped. The rain had started — he could hear it against the east window, the window he'd chosen at seven for the morning light. Right now it just made the room feel sealed, the sound of water on glass like the room had been wrapped in something.

He thought about the bell.

Not the sound. The bell itself — the shape of it, the small and ordinary-looking thing on the bench in Maret's back room. 

*Your grandfather made it before you were born and died before he could ring it and it waited fourteen years for someone to ring it and that someone was you.*

He pressed his eye harder against the pillow.

The bell had not felt like an inheritance. It had felt like a question. The kind that doesn't wait for you to decide if you want to answer — it just arrives, and you either engage with it or you don't, and if you don't, it doesn't go away.

What he didn't know was what he was going back for. Maret would show him what she showed him. His grandfather had been trying to do something with the under-note, something past hearing it, and hadn't finished, and now the bell was Cass's problem — or his opportunity, or his inheritance, he didn't have the right word yet. 

The rain came harder. He could hear it in the gutters now, the particular sound of water moving in volume.

His eye ached.

He thought: *the bell has been waiting longer than I've been alive.* Then he thought: *it can wait until morning.*

He closed his eyes.

In the dark, at the edge of sleep, he heard something — not with his ears, but in the same way he'd heard the under-note, the way sound sometimes bypasses the usual channels and arrives somewhere deeper. A single tone, low and specific. Gone before he could locate it.

His eye flared once, bright and sharp, and then the pain settled back to its baseline and he lay very still and listened to the rain and did not hear it again.
