# Chapter Seven: What Dottie Built

The sign had been up for three days.

Jim noticed it every time he passed,  *A Pup's Valley. Dog Daycare & Boarding.* The letters were clean against the dark background. The address underneath. Nothing more than it needed to be.

He was on his way to Dottie Leonard's office.

---

Washington Street ran parallel to Fayette, one block removed, and the difference between them was the difference between a town's face and its working back. Fayette had the light industrial uses, the practical buildings, the parking lots that didn't pretend to be anything. Washington had the brick sidewalks and the lamp posts and the deliberate accumulation of decisions made over decades by people who believed that a town was worth the trouble of looking like one.

It was late October 2019. The oaks along Washington Street had gone copper and the morning air had enough cold in it that Jim could see his breath for the first step out of the house, then lost it by the time he reached the corner. The Blue Ridge was visible to the west, the particular blue-gray of a clear fall day, close enough that you could convince yourself you could walk there by afternoon.

 The door was unlocked when Jim arrived at eight-forty.

---

"Jim." She looked up from her desk. 

"Morning, Dottie."

"Sit down."  "I saw the sign went up."

"Three days ago."

"Looks good. The lettering is clear."  "How's Andrea?"

"Tired. Good tired."

Dottie nodded once. She understood the distinction. "The tours are going well?"

"She gave four last week. Two families are confirmed for November. The Kowalski family, and a couple from Gainesville with a shepherd mix. He's never been in daycare before."

"First-time daycare families are the best clients," Dottie said. "If you do it right they're yours for the life of the dog." She finally closed the folder. "What do you need from me this morning?"

 "I wanted to ask you something. About the Disney fight."

"What do you want to know?"

"Everything you'll tell me," Jim said.

She looked at him steadily. 

"All right," she said. "Let me get some coffee first."

---

She talked for forty minutes.

Dottie described the proposal the way she would describe a listing — specific, factual, without drama. Disney had announced in November 1993 that they wanted to build a 3,000-acre history-themed park. Disney's America, they called it. The site was just outside Haymarket, near the Manassas battlefield — 

"They came in very confident," Dottie said. "The way large things come in. They had the land optioned, they had the design renderings, they had the economic impact studies." She paused. "The economic impact studies were very attractive. That's always how it starts."

The opposition built from both directions — nationally, historians and preservationists and editorial boards who understood that what was being proposed was a conversion, the transformation of a place that was what it was into a backdrop for something else. And locally, the people who lived within sight of what was being proposed and who had a different relationship to the word *here* than any national organization did.

"We weren't against Disney," Dottie said,  "We weren't against economic development. We were against this specific thing in this specific place. The battlefield is not a backdrop. It's a place where something happened that should not be made into an attraction."

 

"September 1994," Jim said.

"They withdrew in September 1994." Dottie picked up her coffee cup and looked at it rather than at Jim. "I won't tell you it wasn't satisfying. It was. But I'll tell you what was more satisfying."

"The battlefield," Jim said.

She looked up. "You've been out there."

"Last spring. Andrea and I walked it."

"Then you know." She set the cup down. "It's still there. It's exactly what it was before they decided it would make a good backdrop. That's the outcome. That's the only outcome that matters."

Jim was quiet for a moment. Outside, Washington Street was doing its morning routine — a truck going through, the sound of someone unlocking a door two buildings down, a car slowing at the intersection and then continuing.

"Why'd you get behind us?" Jim asked. "The business. The approval process."

Dottie tilted her head slightly. It was not quite a smile.

"Because you were worth getting behind," she said. "And because I've watched enough people come through this town to know the difference between someone who wants to build something here and someone who wants to build something and happens to be here. You're not the second kind."

Jim sat with that.

"Also," Dottie said, "the cage-free model is the right model. I've seen the alternative. The alternative smells like the alternative." She stood up, which was the signal that the meeting was over. "Tell Andrea I'll stop by Thursday. I want to see the floor."

"She'll want to show you the whole thing."

"Good," Dottie said. "I want to see the whole thing."

---

Jim walked home the long way.

 

He turned east on a side street and came back north toward Fayette.

 

He came around the corner onto Fayette Street.

6680 was halfway down the block. The building sat the way it had always sat, undemonstrative, a commercial building doing what commercial buildings do, except now it had a sign and the sign had a name on it and the name had come from the back seat of his car, from a ten-year-old who had heard the problem and solved it in five seconds.

Jim stopped in front of it.

The sign was readable from here, clearly, which was what Dottie had said and which was the right criterion. *A Pup's Valley.*  He thought about bringing it home to Andrea, saying it in the kitchen, watching her face go still for a moment and then settle into the expression she got when something was right.

The building was locked and dark. The dogs would start coming in a few weeks. The Kowalski family was confirmed, and the couple from Gainesville with the shepherd mix, and there were two more families Andrea was talking to, and by January there would be enough dogs on the floor to make the specific sound that a room full of dogs makes when they know each other and feel safe — 

Jim looked at the sign for another moment.

A truck went past on Fayette Street. Jim watched it go.

He pulled out his phone and typed a text to Andrea: *Dottie wants to come Thursday. She wants to see the whole thing.*

The reply came back in forty seconds: *Tell her I'll have coffee.*
