# Chapter Seven: What Dottie Built

The sign had been up for three days.

Jim noticed it every time he passed, which was twice a day at minimum, and each time he noticed it he did the same thing: slowed his pace, read it once, kept walking. *A Pup's Valley. Dog Daycare & Boarding.* The letters were clean against the dark background. The address underneath. Nothing more than it needed to be.

He was on his way to Dottie Leonard's office.

---

Washington Street ran parallel to Fayette, one block removed, and the difference between them was the difference between a town's face and its working back. Fayette had the light industrial uses, the practical buildings, the parking lots that didn't pretend to be anything. Washington had the brick sidewalks and the lamp posts and the deliberate accumulation of decisions made over decades by people who believed that a town was worth the trouble of looking like one.

It was late October 2019. The oaks along Washington Street had gone copper and the morning air had enough cold in it that Jim could see his breath for the first step out of the house, then lost it by the time he reached the corner. The Blue Ridge was visible to the west, the particular blue-gray of a clear fall day, close enough that you could convince yourself you could walk there by afternoon.

Dottie's office was on Washington Street. It had been in various locations over the years, but it had always been on Washington Street, because that was where Dottie was. The door was unlocked when Jim arrived at eight-forty.

---

"Jim." She looked up from her desk. She had a folder open and a pen in her hand and she set the pen down but kept a finger in the folder, marking her place. She was not a woman who lost her place.

"Morning, Dottie."

"Sit down." She gestured at the chair across from her desk with the kind of efficiency that was not unfriendly but was also not wasting time. "I saw the sign went up."

"Three days ago."

"Looks good. The lettering is clear." She said this the way she said most things — as a fact, not a compliment, though it functioned as both. "How's Andrea?"

"Tired. Good tired."

Dottie nodded once. She understood the distinction. "The tours are going well?"

"She gave four last week. Two families are confirmed for November. The Kowalski family, and a couple from Gainesville with a shepherd mix. He's never been in daycare before."

"First-time daycare families are the best clients," Dottie said. "If you do it right they're yours for the life of the dog." She finally closed the folder. "What do you need from me this morning?"

Jim leaned forward with his elbows on his knees. This was the posture he used when he was about to say the practical thing and wanted the other person to know he meant it. "I wanted to ask you something. About the Disney fight."

Dottie was quiet for a moment. Not hesitant — just letting the subject settle before she picked it up.

"What do you want to know?"

"Everything you'll tell me," Jim said.

She looked at him steadily. She had been looked at by a lot of people across a lot of desks and she was not a woman who flinched from being looked at.

"All right," she said. "Let me get some coffee first."

---

She talked for forty minutes.

Jim listened the way he listened to dogs: completely. Not waiting for his turn to speak, not organizing his response, not cataloging the information for later use. Just listening, with the full attention of someone who understands that the person in front of them knows something he doesn't.

Dottie described the proposal the way she would describe a listing — specific, factual, without drama. Disney had announced in November 1993 that they wanted to build a 3,000-acre history-themed park. Disney's America, they called it. The site was just outside Haymarket, near the Manassas battlefield — the ground where two of the Civil War's largest engagements had been fought, where the soil had been soaked through with something that did not belong to a theme park.

"They came in very confident," Dottie said. "The way large things come in. They had the land optioned, they had the design renderings, they had the economic impact studies." She paused. "The economic impact studies were very attractive. That's always how it starts."

The opposition built from both directions — nationally, historians and preservationists and editorial boards who understood that what was being proposed was a conversion, the transformation of a place that was what it was into a backdrop for something else. And locally, the people who lived within sight of what was being proposed and who had a different relationship to the word *here* than any national organization did.

"We weren't against Disney," Dottie said, and she said it carefully, the way she said things she had said many times before and wanted to say correctly. "We weren't against economic development. We were against this specific thing in this specific place. The battlefield is not a backdrop. It's a place where something happened that should not be made into an attraction."

She had been in the rooms. The town meetings, the county hearings, the conversations with people who needed to understand what was actually being proposed underneath the language about history and education and family entertainment. She had been the person who got on the phone and called the people who needed to be called, who showed up to the meetings that needed bodies in the chairs, who said the same thing clearly and without losing her temper when it needed to be said again.

"September 1994," Jim said.

"They withdrew in September 1994." Dottie picked up her coffee cup and looked at it rather than at Jim. "I won't tell you it wasn't satisfying. It was. But I'll tell you what was more satisfying."

"The battlefield," Jim said.

She looked up. "You've been out there."

"Last spring. Andrea and I walked it."

"Then you know." She set the cup down. "It's still there. It's exactly what it was before they decided it would make a good backdrop. That's the outcome. That's the only outcome that matters."

Jim was quiet for a moment. Outside, Washington Street was doing its morning routine — a truck going through, the sound of someone unlocking a door two buildings down, a car slowing at the intersection and then continuing.

"Why'd you get behind us?" Jim asked. "The business. The approval process."

Dottie tilted her head slightly. It was not quite a smile.

"Because you were worth getting behind," she said. "And because I've watched enough people come through this town to know the difference between someone who wants to build something here and someone who wants to build something and happens to be here. You're not the second kind."

Jim sat with that.

"Also," Dottie said, "the cage-free model is the right model. I've seen the alternative. The alternative smells like the alternative." She stood up, which was the signal that the meeting was over. "Tell Andrea I'll stop by Thursday. I want to see the floor."

"She'll want to show you the whole thing."

"Good," Dottie said. "I want to see the whole thing."

---

Jim walked home the long way.

He went south on Washington Street first, past the brick facades and the lamp posts that had been fought for in the same manner and by some of the same people who had fought for the battlefield — not with the same stakes, not with the same drama, but with the same underlying argument: that a place was worth being a specific place, and that someone had to say so, and that saying so required showing up.

The historical overlay on Washington Street had not happened by accident. Jim knew this in the general way you know things about a town you've chosen to live in, but Dottie's name was attached to enough of it that the general knowledge had specific weight. The brick sidewalks. The lamp posts. The zoning decisions that had kept the fast food from colonizing the downtown the way it had colonized everything east of Route 15. These things had required someone to be in the room, repeatedly, over years, saying the same thing with the patience of a person who understands that institutions move slowly and the only answer to that is to keep showing up.

He turned east on a side street and came back north toward Fayette.

The morning had warmed enough that the Blue Ridge had gone from blue-gray to a softer color, the haze of midday starting to build. October in Northern Virginia had a particular quality of light — the angle of it, the way it hit the brick buildings on Washington Street and turned them the color of the leaves — and Jim had been walking through it for three years now and still looked up at it. That was the thing about choosing a place deliberately: you kept noticing it.

He came around the corner onto Fayette Street.

6680 was halfway down the block. The building sat the way it had always sat, undemonstrative, a commercial building doing what commercial buildings do, except now it had a sign and the sign had a name on it and the name had come from the back seat of his car, from a ten-year-old who had heard the problem and solved it in five seconds.

Jim stopped in front of it.

The sign was readable from here, clearly, which was what Dottie had said and which was the right criterion. *A Pup's Valley.* He thought about Andy saying it, the flat declarative certainty of a kid who doesn't know he's supposed to be strategic about naming things. He thought about bringing it home to Andrea, saying it in the kitchen, watching her face go still for a moment and then settle into the expression she got when something was right.

The building was locked and dark. The dogs would start coming in a few weeks. The Kowalski family was confirmed, and the couple from Gainesville with the shepherd mix, and there were two more families Andrea was talking to, and by January there would be enough dogs on the floor to make the specific sound that a room full of dogs makes when they know each other and feel safe — not the sound of a kennel, which was a different sound entirely, but the sound of a community.

Jim looked at the sign for another moment.

Dottie had spent fifty years making Haymarket worth coming to. She had fought Disney with the patient, specific stubbornness of someone who understood that some things should remain what they are. She had sold real estate in this town for thirty years, which meant she had the institutional memory of every property, every family, every transaction — the history of a place stored not in a document but in a person, which was the only form that history actually mattered in.

And she had gotten behind the Paynes. Not with fanfare. Not with a public endorsement or a press release. With the specific, practical support of someone who knew who needed to talk to whom, who knew what needed to be said and to whom, who had been in rooms with these people for decades and whose word meant something that didn't show up on any permit application but was present in every room where a decision got made.

Jim thought about what it cost to do that — to give fifty years to a place, to fight for it when it needed fighting for, to sell its houses and attend its meetings and make its decisions and show up for it, over and over, in the unglamorous daily work of making a community legible to itself.

He thought about Andrea saying *I said it like it was a promise.*

It was a promise. That was what they were doing. And the promise had weight because of everything Dottie had done before they arrived — because the town they were promising to be part of was a town someone had already spent fifty years making worth the promise.

A truck went past on Fayette Street. Jim watched it go.

He pulled out his phone and typed a text to Andrea: *Dottie wants to come Thursday. She wants to see the whole thing.*

The reply came back in forty seconds: *Tell her I'll have coffee.*

Jim put his phone in his pocket. He looked at the sign one more time — *A Pup's Valley*, the letters clean and readable from the road — and then he walked the rest of the way home.

The question that stayed with him, the one he didn't have an answer to yet, was not whether they could build what they'd said they would build. He was fairly confident about that. The question was whether they were worthy of the place they were building it in — whether the people who came after fifty years of Dottie Leonard were the right people to be coming after.

He didn't have an answer. What he had was the sign, and the families confirmed for November, and Andrea's text about the coffee, and the knowledge that Dottie had looked at them and made a judgment.

She did not make that judgment lightly.

He was almost to the door of the house when Gracie appeared in the window, nose against the glass, tail already going. The motion of it was visible even from the sidewalk — the whole back half of her moving with the specific joy of a dog who has spotted the person she was waiting for and cannot contain the fact of it.

Jim went inside.
