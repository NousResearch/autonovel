# Chapter Seven

The note was still in him when he stopped counting.

Not the sound itself — that had faded in the usual way, the air in the workshop reclaiming its ordinary thickness — but the frequency, the thing underneath the note, the wolf-interval that his great-grandfather had written about in the ledger as *the voice beneath the voice.* It sat behind Cass's left eye like a splinter of something very thin and very precise, and he breathed through it the way his grandfather had shown him, the slow exhale that didn't fight the pain but acknowledged it and moved past it.

"Forty-three," he said.

His grandfather's pen scratched.

"Say it again."

"Forty-three beats per second. Maybe forty-four. I lost it near the end when the — when the upper partial came in and I couldn't separate them."

"You're sure about forty-three."

"I'm sure about forty-three."

His grandfather wrote. The scratch of the pen on paper was the only sound in the workshop for a moment, and Cass stood with his arm still half-extended from the strike, the striker loose in his fingers, and let the pain behind his eye do what it was going to do. It peaked. It began, very slowly, to back off. 

"Your great-grandfather's number was forty-one," his grandfather said, without looking up.

"The crack's changed it."

"Yes."

"Or the temperature. Or both." Cass set the striker down on the bench. "What's the humidity today?"

His grandfather glanced at the small gauge on the wall near the door, the one that had been there since before Cass was born, its face yellowed and its needle reliable. "Sixty-two percent."

"He doesn't list humidity in the ledger entries. Not for this one."

"He didn't know it mattered yet."

Cass looked at the bell. It hung from its bracket on the same chain his great-grandfather had used, the iron links dark with age, and the crack ran from the sound bow upward in its familiar path — not straight, not perfectly curved, but the specific wandering line that a fracture takes when it follows the grain of the metal rather than being imposed on it. He had spent enough time looking at it that he had stopped seeing it as damage. It was more like a signature now. The bell's own handwriting.

"If I hit it again," he said, "I can try to isolate the lower partial before the upper one comes in. There's a window — maybe two seconds — where I can hear them separately."

His grandfather put the pen down. He had a way of putting things down that was not quite setting them aside, more like pausing them, as if the object would wait for him and knew it. He looked at Cass with the expression that Cass had come to understand meant he was deciding something.

"How's the eye."

"Fine."

"Cass."

"It's manageable. It's always manageable after the first thirty seconds."

"Your great-grandfather went deaf in his left ear. Partially. By the time he was sixty."

Cass had not known this. It arrived in him the way facts about the dead sometimes arrived — not as information exactly, more as a sudden awareness of depth, the way you step onto what you think is solid ground and feel it give slightly beneath your foot.

"The ledger doesn't say that."

"The ledger doesn't say everything." His grandfather picked up the pen again. "He documented the bell. He didn't document himself." He looked at the page in front of him. "That was his choice. I'm not sure it was the right one."

The workshop smelled of oil and metal and the particular dry-dust smell of old wood that had absorbed decades of work and never quite released it. Through the high window, the light had moved since they started — it was later than Cass had thought, the angle changed, the thin line of it no longer catching the crack at the specific angle that made it look almost beautiful. Now the bell just looked broken.

"Did he know?" Cass asked. "About the hearing."

"He knew something was happening. Whether he knew what it was—" His grandfather made a small gesture that was not quite a shrug. "He kept ringing it."

"Because the data was worth it."

"Because he couldn't leave it alone. Those are different things." His grandfather looked at him steadily. "I want you to understand they're different things."

Cass turned this over. Outside, somewhere down the street, a car passed, its sound arriving and receding in the ordinary way of sounds that had nothing to do with what was happening in this room. He thought about his great-grandfather standing where he was standing, striker in hand, choosing to ring a bell that was hurting him, and writing down the numbers, and not writing down the cost.

"If I stop now," he said, "I have forty-three beats per second at sixty-two percent humidity. That's one data point."

"Yes."

"It's not enough to map the pattern."

"No."

"So I need to ring it again."

His grandfather waited. 

"But I should write down what it costs," Cass said. "Not just the frequency. What the pain is doing. Where it is, how long it lasts." He paused. "So the next person has the full picture."

Something shifted in his grandfather's face, very slightly. Not surprise — more like recognition, the particular look of someone who has seen a thing arrive that they were hoping for but not counting on.

"Yes," his grandfather said. "That's what he should have done."

Cass picked up the striker.

The second strike was different in the way that second strikes were always different — the body already braced, the ear already oriented, the anticipatory tension that was itself a kind of interference in the listening. He had learned to account for this. He breathed out before the moment of impact, tried to go loose, tried to be the kind of listener who received rather than reached.

The note opened.

The wolf came.

The needle arrived behind his left eye at the usual address, precise and immediate, the same location every time, as if it had mapped a route and was following it. He breathed through it. He counted.

*One. Two. Three.* The beats were distinct at first, the interference pattern clear — two frequencies close enough to create a pulse, the way two singers slightly off-pitch create a wobble in the air between them. *Four. Five.* The upper partial was building, the bell's other voice rising underneath the fundamental, and he had maybe two seconds before it complicated everything. *Six. Seven. Eight.* He was counting the beats of the pulse, not the seconds, and the pulse was — he did the math behind his counting — approximately forty-three and a half, which meant his first number had been low by half a beat, which was significant.

Then the upper partial arrived and the sound became a room with too many people in it, all talking, and he couldn't separate the voices anymore.

He let the sound run out.

"Forty-three and a half," he said. "I was low."

"By how much."

"Half a beat. Maybe a little more."

His grandfather made a note. His pen had a specific sound on this paper, slightly coarser than the ledger's pages, a rougher scratch. "The eye."

"Worse on the second one. The anticipation doesn't help." Cass pressed two fingers lightly against the bone above his left eye, not pressing hard, just acknowledging. "It peaks around fifteen seconds and then starts backing off. I'd say by forty-five seconds it's down to — I don't know. A third of the peak intensity. Something like that."

"And now?"

"Now it's a low-level thing. Like a string still vibrating."

His grandfather wrote this down, all of it, and Cass watched him write and felt something that was not quite pride and not quite relief but somewhere in that territory — the specific satisfaction of having made something useful exist that hadn't existed before. A data point about pain, recorded in a notebook, so that whoever came next would know the full cost and not just the numbers.

"Grandfather."

"Mm."

"Did you ever ring it?"

The pen stopped.

His grandfather was quiet for long enough that Cass started to think he'd asked something wrong, crossed some line he hadn't known was there. Then his grandfather set the pen down in the pause-not-set-aside way, and turned to look at the bell, and said: "Once."

"And?"

"And I understood why your great-grandfather kept doing it." He was quiet again. "I also understood why I was not going to."

"Because of the hearing."

"Because I'm a coward about pain in ways he wasn't." He said this completely without self-pity, the way someone states a fact about themselves they've had a long time to accept. "He had something I don't. A kind of — he could separate himself from his body's complaints. I can't do that. The pain arrives and I'm entirely inside it." He looked back at Cass. "You're more like him."

Cass didn't know what to do with this. He looked at the bell instead.

"I don't feel like I'm separating from it," he said. "It still hurts."

"I know. But you're counting while it hurts. That's the difference."

The light through the high window had gone the color of late afternoon, flatter and more diffuse, the workshop's shadows deepening at the edges. Cass became aware that he was hungry, that they had been in here for a long time, that the world outside had continued without them in the ordinary way the world did.

"Can I ask you something else."

"You can ask me anything."

"Why did he build it broken? The original casting — why didn't he just cast it without the flaw?"

His grandfather looked at him for a moment. "What makes you think he did?"

Cass opened his mouth and closed it.

He turned to look at the bell again, and this time he looked at it differently — looked at the crack, the specific path of it, the way it ran from the sound bow upward in that wandering line, and he thought about what he knew about casting, about how metal cooled, about how flaws formed. A casting flaw from cooling would look different. It would be internal first, or it would be at a point of thermal stress, or it would — he didn't know enough. He didn't know enough to say for certain.

"You're saying the crack happened later."

"I'm saying I don't know when the crack happened. Neither do you. The ledger doesn't say." His grandfather picked up the pen again. "What I'm saying is: don't assume you know the history of a thing just because you've been living with it."

"Okay," he said. "Okay, so — if the crack happened later, then the original bell had a different frequency. A cleaner one."

"Almost certainly."

"And your great-grandfather — he heard both versions. The clean one and this one."

"He did."

"And he kept the broken one."

"He kept the broken one."

Cass stood with this. 

"Because the clean one didn't tell him anything," Cass said slowly. "Or — not nothing. But less. The broken one was telling him something the clean one couldn't."

"That is one interpretation."

"What's another one."

His grandfather smiled, small and brief, the kind of smile that meant *you're doing fine, keep going.* "That he simply couldn't bring himself to destroy it. Even broken. Even painful." He paused. "Attachment is not always rational. Sometimes the reason you keep a thing is just that it's yours."

Cass thought about this. He thought about the ledger, the forty-seven pages of entries, the careful handwriting that had documented the bell's voice over decades. He thought about a man going slightly deaf in his left ear and continuing to write down numbers, and whether that was data collection or something else, something less easily categorized.

"Both can be true," he said.

"Yes. Both can be true."

He picked up the striker a third time, and his grandfather, without being asked, uncapped the pen.

This strike Cass didn't count immediately. He let the note arrive and he let the wolf arrive and he let the needle go in behind his eye and he just listened, for the first two seconds, without trying to do anything with what he heard. Just received it. The way his grandfather had described it once, the difference between looking at something and seeing it — the looking was active, the seeing was what happened when you stopped trying so hard.

The pulse was there. He could feel it in his chest as much as hear it, the beating of two close frequencies against each other, the same principle as the shimmer in a mistuned piano string, the same principle as the wobble in a voice that's reaching for a note just outside its range. He counted.

Forty-three and a half. Maybe forty-four. The humidity was the variable. If he could ring it tomorrow at a different humidity, and the day after at a different temperature, he could start to build a picture of how the crack's effect changed with conditions. A map, not a single point.

The upper partial rose. He tracked it this time, tried to hold both voices simultaneously — the fundamental and the partial, the bell's intended note and the extra voice the crack had given it. They were almost a minor third apart. Almost. Not quite. The interval was wrong in the specific way that made something feel unresolved, like a sentence ending on the wrong word, like a step that expected solid ground and found it a half-inch lower than anticipated.

The sound ran out.

The pain peaked and began its recession.

"A minor third," he said. "Between the fundamental and the upper partial. Slightly flat."

"How flat."

"I can't measure that by ear. I'd need the frequency counter." He looked at his grandfather. "Do we have one?"

"We have one. It's not calibrated."

"Can we calibrate it?"

"Against what reference pitch?"

Cass thought. "The tuning fork. The A-440. If we know where that sits on the counter, we can calculate the offset and correct for it."

His grandfather looked at him for a moment with the expression that Cass had learned to read as *I know this and I want to know if you've worked it out yourself.* "Yes," he said. "We can do that."

"Then tomorrow we can get an actual number for the interval."

"Tomorrow."

"Unless you want to do it now."

His grandfather glanced at the window, at the late-afternoon light. "Your mother will want you home for dinner."

"She'll hold it."

"She'll hold it once." He began capping the pen. "Tomorrow. We do it properly, with the calibrated counter and a full set of conditions recorded — temperature, humidity, time of day. If we're building a map, we build it right."

Cass accepted this. He set the striker down on the bench, next to the ledger, and looked at what they had: three data points, a pain log, a hypothesis about the interval, and a plan for tomorrow. It was not very much. It was more than there had been this morning.

His grandfather closed the notebook — not the ledger, the new notebook he'd pulled from the drawer, the one that was accumulating Cass's observations — and set it beside the ledger on the bench. The two notebooks sat together, the old one and the new one, and Cass looked at them and felt the thing he couldn't name again, the not-quite-pride, the awareness of depth.

"Grandfather."

"Mm."

"What happened to his hearing. At the end. Was it — did it get worse?"

His grandfather was quiet for a moment. He put his hand on the older ledger, not opening it, just resting his hand on the cover. "By the time he was seventy, he couldn't hear the bell at all. Not the fundamental. He could still feel the vibration if he touched the bronze." He paused. "He still came to the workshop. He still wrote in the ledger. He recorded the conditions — temperature, humidity — even though he couldn't hear what the bell was doing."

Cass looked at the ledger. He thought about the later entries, the ones he'd read in the first days, the handwriting that had changed in the final years, less steady, the numbers still precise.

"He was recording for someone else," Cass said.

"Yes."

"He knew he couldn't use it anymore. He was recording for whoever came next."

His grandfather lifted his hand from the ledger. He looked at Cass with something that was not the small brief smile but something older and less easy to read. "He wrote in the last entry — you haven't read it yet, it's near the back — he wrote: *I can no longer verify these observations directly. I record them in the faith that they will be useful to someone who can.* That's the whole entry. One sentence."

Cass stood very still.

"Can I read it," he said. "The last entry."

"Not today." His grandfather said it gently, without apology. "Today you've done enough. Read it when you're ready for it."

 But he looked at his grandfather and he understood, in the particular way that you sometimes understand things from the way a person holds themselves rather than from what they say, that this was not a rule being enforced. It was a gift being timed.

He nodded.

He picked up his jacket from the hook by the door and shrugged into it, and his grandfather began the small rituals of closing the workshop — capping the ink, covering the bench, turning off the lamp over the work surface so that the only light left was the ambient gray of the late afternoon coming through the high window. The bell hung in that light, and the crack was invisible now, the angle wrong for it, and from here the bell just looked like a bell.

Cass stopped with his hand on the door.

"The last entry," he said. "When do you think he wrote it?"

His grandfather was moving the ledger to the shelf, its usual place, the place where it had lived for longer than Cass had been alive. He settled it carefully, spine out, and turned to look at Cass.

"I think he wrote it the last time he came to the workshop," he said. "I think he knew it was the last time."

"And he didn't write about the bell. He wrote about the next person."

"Yes."

Cass held this. Outside, the street was doing its late-afternoon things — a neighbor's car, a dog somewhere, the ordinary sounds of a day that was ending. He stood in the doorway between the workshop and the rest of the world and he felt the low steady throb behind his left eye, fainter now, the string winding down, and he thought about an old man in a workshop not very different from this one, deaf in his left ear, writing a single sentence in a ledger he knew he'd never add to again.

*In the faith that they will be useful to someone who can.*

He had been the someone who could. He hadn't known it yet when he'd first opened the ledger, hadn't known what he was being handed. He was starting to know now.

"I'll be here at eight," he said. "If that's not too early."

His grandfather made the small sound that meant it was fine, it was always fine, he would be here.

Cass stepped out into the early evening. The door closed behind him, and he stood on the step for a moment, not listening for anything in particular, just standing. The pain behind his eye was almost gone. What was left was something else — not pain exactly, more like the memory of it, the body's record of where the sound had been.

He started toward home.
