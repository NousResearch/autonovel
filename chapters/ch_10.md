# Chapter Ten: Haymarket in Winter

The Town Council chambers held maybe sixty people when full, and on a Tuesday night in late November 2019 they held thirty-seven, which Jim Payne had counted twice from his seat in the fourth row because there was nothing else to do while Councilman Peters finished his remarks about the dump trucks.

The dump trucks were the issue. Specifically, the dump trucks on Fayette Street — the number of them, the hours they ran, the noise they made, and what that noise did to the character of the street. A woman named Elaine Marsh had spoken first during Citizens Time, standing at the microphone with a printed sheet of notes and reading from it with the focused energy of someone who had been waiting for this meeting for three weeks. She had counted the trucks herself. She had the times. She had a neighbor who worked night shifts and slept days and had not slept through a morning in six weeks. She said this last part without looking at her notes, which was when Jim understood she was not performing.

He shifted in his chair. The fluorescent lights overhead ran at a frequency that was not quite a sound, more a pressure, and the room smelled like old carpet and the particular staleness of a public building that never quite opened its windows all the way. 

Councilman Peters was speaking now about traffic patterns on Fayette Street, and he was doing it with the gravity of a man discussing a foreign policy crisis. Jim found this completely reasonable. The trucks were real. The noise was real. Elaine Marsh's neighbor was real and sleep-deprived and had asked someone to do something about it, and Elaine Marsh had shown up tonight to make sure that someone did. This was the whole system working exactly as it was supposed to work.

He had his own stake in Fayette Street, obviously. The building at 6680 was his, or would be his in the sense that mattered — he had signed for it, he was paying for it, he had spent months turning it into something. A Pup's Valley had been open for six weeks. He had not come tonight to speak about the trucks. He had come because this was the meeting and he lived here and he showed up.

Councilman Shannon leaned into his microphone. "Before we move to the next item, I want to thank everyone who came out tonight." 

The woman behind Jim made a small sound of acknowledgment. Someone in the front row shifted in their chair. The meeting moved on.

---

He found Dottie near the back of the room afterward, in the shuffle of people putting on coats and retrieving bags from under chairs. She was talking to a man Jim didn't recognize — something about a survey, a property line question — and she finished the conversation with a precision that left no room for follow-up before turning to Jim.

"How's the business," she said. Not quite a question.

"It's going." He pulled his coat from the back of his chair. "Six weeks. We've got about thirty regular dogs now. More coming in January, we think — people travel for the holidays and they need someone."

Dottie nodded. She had the particular stillness of someone who had heard many people describe their businesses to her and had learned to distinguish between the ones who were reporting facts and the ones who were selling themselves on their own story. Jim knew she was doing this. He didn't mind. He was reporting facts.

"Cage-free," she said.

"Cage-free."

She nodded again, differently this time. "People will pay for that if you do it right."

"We're doing it right."

"I know." She picked up her bag. "I've heard from people. The Hartmanns, the Okafor family. They like what they found." She paused, not for effect but because she was deciding something. "There are businesses that open in this town and you can tell in the first month whether they'll last. Some of them are busy and they still won't last. You know what I mean."

Jim said he did.

"You'll last," she said, and she said it the way she said everything — not as encouragement, not as a prediction exactly, but as a fact she had assessed and found to be true. Then she said good night and walked toward the door, stopping once to say something to Councilman Peters, and Jim stood for a moment in the emptying room with the fluorescent lights still running their almost-sound overhead, and felt the specific weight of being told the truth by someone who had no reason to say anything else.

---

The I-66 corridor had been growing since before Jim moved to Haymarket, and the growth had a particular character: it moved east to west, following the highway, filling in the land between Washington and the mountains with subdivisions that were identical in the way that things built quickly and for volume were identical. Dominion Valley. Piedmont. Names that gestured toward what had been there before the grading crews arrived. The families who bought in those subdivisions were not abstractions — they were people with commutes and children and, in very large numbers, dogs.

 The families understood this about their dogs, and they were looking for somewhere to take them, and the market within a twenty-minute drive of Haymarket was larger than the town itself by a factor Jim had calculated before he signed the lease.

He and Andrea had done this before, in a different place, and they had won awards doing it. They had not come to Haymarket guessing. They had come knowing, which was a different thing entirely.

---

Washington Street on a Saturday morning in December had a quality that was hard to name without sounding like you were trying to sell something. The brick sidewalks were empty at seven-thirty, the lamp posts throwing circles of yellow light that the pale winter sun was slowly making redundant. The Blue Ridge showed clearly on the western horizon — cold air, low humidity, the mountains more present in winter than in summer, the blue-gray line of them sharper against the sky. Jim walked from the parking lot to the building and stood for a moment on the sidewalk looking west before he unlocked the door.

Inside, the smell hit first: clean concrete and the faint warm scent of dog, the good version of that smell, the version that meant the place had been properly maintained and the animals in it were comfortable.  You could tell a dog daycare's standards by its smell on a Saturday morning before the dogs arrived. This one smelled right.

He turned on the lights. The room opened up — the daycare floor, the grooming area, the boarding spaces in the back. He walked the perimeter the way he did every morning, checking corners, checking the water stations, checking the temperature. The building had its own sounds in the early morning: the HVAC cycling, a faint creak from somewhere in the back wall when the heat came on. He had learned these sounds the way you learned the sounds of any building you spent enough time in — not consciously, but until the day one of them changed and you noticed the change before you understood why.

Andrea arrived at eight-fifteen with two coffees and a bag from the place on Route 15. She set his on the desk without asking and sat down in the chair across from him, pulling her coat off one shoulder.

"Hartmann's bringing Obie at nine," she said. "And I got a call yesterday — family in Gainesville, they have a shepherd mix, two years old, they want to do a trial day."

"What's the dog's name?"

"Pepper." She opened her coffee. "The mom said she's been in a kennel situation before and it wasn't — she said it wasn't good. She was a little vague about what that meant."

Jim considered this. "What did she sound like? The mom."

"Worried," Andrea said. "The specific kind of worried where they've already decided they're going to trust you and they're scared you'll give them a reason not to."

He understood that kind. "Slow intake. I'll do it myself."

"I already put that in the notes." She pulled her laptop toward her and opened it. "Oh, and Wellington's boarding stay in December — Caleb's family confirmed. First full overnight."

"How's Caleb doing with that?"

Andrea looked at him over the top of her laptop screen. "His mom said he's been talking about it since they scheduled it. She didn't say whether that was excited or anxious."

"Probably both."

"Probably both," she agreed.

The first car pulled into the lot at eight-fifty — not the Hartmanns, someone else, a woman Jim recognized from the week before with a small terrier mix whose name he was pulling from memory: Biscuit, no, Biscotti, that was it, the woman's daughter had named her. He went to the door.

---

By noon the floor held eleven dogs and the sound had shifted into the frequency Jim associated with a room that had reached equilibrium — not quiet, never quiet, but a specific register of movement and communication that meant everyone present had made their assessments and decided the situation was acceptable. Two dogs were doing the loose, bouncing thing at the far end of the room that was either play or the beginning of something that needed managing; he watched for another three seconds and decided it was play. The beagle whose name was Franklin had found his corner by the water station and was doing what Franklin always did, which was observe the room with the systematic attention of an animal conducting a census. The shepherd mix from last week — not Pepper, a different one, a dog belonging to a family from Gainesville whose name Jim kept in the same internal register where he kept all of them — was moving more freely than she had on her first day. On her first day she had pressed herself against the far wall and watched the room with the focused stillness of an animal running threat calculations. Jim had not approached her. He had sat down on the floor about eight feet away and waited, and ten minutes later she was sitting next to him, and now here she was, moving through the room with her tail at a neutral height, comfortable enough to stop thinking about the room and just be in it.

That was three weeks of work, that tail height. Three weeks of slow mornings and consistent handling and the accumulated evidence the dog had gathered that this place was not going to hurt her. 

Andrea appeared in the doorway from the office. "The Gainesville family called. They want to confirm the trial day for Thursday."

"Confirmed," Jim said, watching the shepherd mix navigate around a golden who had stopped in the middle of the room for no apparent reason.

"She's doing well," Andrea said, meaning the shepherd.

"She is." He watched her for another moment. "She was a smart dog the whole time. She just needed the data."

---

The regional market was a fact, and facts had a way of making themselves visible over time. The families started coming from Gainesville in October, two or three at first, then more as the word spread through the particular channels by which these things spread — a conversation at a school pickup line, a recommendation in one of the neighborhood Facebook groups, someone mentioning it to someone else at the Dominion Valley community center. The Paynes had not advertised heavily. They had opened a good facility and done the work well and trusted that the work would speak for itself, which was either confidence or stubbornness or both, and which was turning out to be correct.

 

By the end of November, Jim knew forty-three dogs by name. He knew which ones needed a slow introduction and which ones came through the door already at ease. He knew which ones were watching him for permission and which ones were going to test him until he made himself clear. He knew the ones who had been in bad situations before, because they carried it in their bodies — in the low tail, the wide eye, the particular quality of stillness that was not calm but its opposite.

He knew Franklin the beagle's habit of conducting his census from the corner. He knew the shepherd mix's three-week arc from the wall to the middle of the room. He knew Obie Hartmann's baseline energy level and the specific way Obie announced his arrival, which was loudly and at the front door and with a level of enthusiasm that the Hartmann family found embarrassing and Jim found completely legible.

Forty-three dogs. Three months open. The business was working.

---

The Saturday before Christmas, Andrea stayed late finishing the billing while Jim loaded the last of the clean bedding into the storage closet. The building was empty of dogs — the last pickup had been at five-thirty, a family from the Piedmont subdivision with a standard poodle named Duchess who was, in Jim's assessment, the most composed dog he had ever met and who was probably smarter than several people he knew. He had told the family this. They had laughed, and then the father had said, seriously, "She really is, though," and Jim had agreed.

He could hear Andrea in the office, the faint sound of the keyboard, the occasional click of the mouse. Outside, Washington Street was quiet in the way it was quiet on winter evenings — the specific quiet of a small town after the shops had closed, the lamp posts doing their work, the occasional car on Route 15 audible as a distant sound that rose and faded.

He put the last of the bedding away and stood in the storage closet for a moment, looking at the shelves. Organized. Everything where it belonged. The smell of clean laundry and the faint underlying smell of the building itself, which he had learned so thoroughly by now that it registered not as a smell but as a baseline, the sensory equivalent of silence.

Three months. Forty-three dogs. A room full of people on a Tuesday night arguing about dump trucks on his street, which was their street too, which was the whole point. Dottie telling him he'd last in the tone of voice she used for facts.

He turned off the storage closet light and went back to the office.

Andrea was closing the laptop. She looked up when he came in, and something in her face was doing the thing it did at the end of a good day — not quite a smile, more a settling, the particular expression of someone whose work had gone the way it was supposed to go.

"Pepper's family confirmed again," she said. "The mom texted. She said Pepper's been anxious lately and she's hoping the trial day will help."

"Anxious how?"

"She said she's been pacing at night. Since they moved."

Jim sat down in the chair across from the desk. "When did they move?"

"August. New construction in Gainesville. She said the dog was fine in the old house." Andrea paused. "She said she feels guilty about it."

He thought about the shepherd mix, three weeks ago, pressed against the far wall with her threat calculations running. He thought about what it took to convince an anxious animal that a new place was safe — not persuasion, not performance, just the patient accumulation of evidence. You could not rush it. You could only show up the same way every day until the evidence was sufficient.

"She doesn't need to feel guilty," he said. "She needs to give the dog somewhere to go where the routine is consistent. That's what we're for."

Andrea looked at him for a moment. "I'll tell her that."

"Not like that. Just — tell her what to expect on Thursday. What the intake looks like, what we'll watch for, what we'll tell her at pickup." He shifted in the chair. "The guilt isn't the problem. The dog is the problem. Focus her on the dog."

"I know," Andrea said. She did know. She had been doing this longer than he had, in some ways — the relational part of it, the management of what the families brought through the door alongside their dogs. She picked up her phone and typed a note to herself. "I'll call her Wednesday."

Outside, a truck went by on Route 15, its sound rising through the quiet and then diminishing. Jim sat in the chair and looked at the wall where Andrea had tacked the week's schedule — the dogs' names, the families, the notes she kept for herself that no one else would read but that were the thinking beneath the doing, the infrastructure that made the whole thing possible.

Forty-three dogs. He was going to know forty-three more by February. The families from the commuter subdivisions were still finding them, still calling, still showing up on trial days with anxious dogs and guilty feelings and the specific hope of people who had decided to trust strangers with something they loved.

He was not going to let them down. This was not a promise he had made to anyone. 

Andrea stood, pulling her coat from the hook by the door. "You want to get food on the way home? Andy texted that he's hungry."

"Andy's always hungry."

"He said specifically that he's *extremely* hungry, which apparently is different."

Jim stood. "There's a difference."

They turned off the office light. The building went quiet behind them — the HVAC, the faint creak from the back wall, the particular settled silence of a place that had been full of life all day and was now resting. Jim locked the door and stood for a moment on the sidewalk, his breath visible in the cold air, the Blue Ridge invisible now in the dark but present the way things were present when you knew they were there.

The lamp posts were doing their work on Washington Street. Somewhere down the block a door opened and closed, and voices carried briefly in the cold air, and then the street was quiet again.

He got in the car.
