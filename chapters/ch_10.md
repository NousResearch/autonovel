# Chapter Ten

The copper came in a coil the diameter of a dinner plate, and Cass's grandfather set it on the workbench without ceremony and told him to cut twelve inches.

Cass picked up the snips. The copper was soft and gave easily, 

"Straight," his grandfather said, not looking.

Cass straightened the cut end with the flat of his thumb. The metal was cool against his skin, almost waxy, and left a faint green-brown smell on his fingers that he'd stopped trying to wash off.

He put the snips down. His grandfather was at the bench across the room, working the flux brush along the lip of a bell that was maybe eight inches tall — a small one, the kind that went on a door somewhere, in a shop or a church anteroom. The bell was still rough from casting, its surface pitted and dull. In two hours it would look different. Cass had watched this enough times now to know the sequence: flux, heat, solder, quench, polish. The same every time, and different every time, because the metal was never exactly the same twice.

"What's this for?" Cass asked, holding up the copper strip.

"Crown repair on the Hendricks job. Crack in the old one." His grandfather set the flux brush down and came over, took the copper strip, sighted down it. "Little long. Cut a quarter inch."

Cass cut a quarter inch.

"Good. Now put it on the bench and don't touch it."

He put it on the bench. Outside, the afternoon had gone gray and close, the kind of October sky that couldn't decide between rain and just looking like rain. The light through the workshop window was flat and even, no shadows, which made the bench look different than it did in morning light — the tools more ordinary, the bells less alive.

He pulled his stool up to the notebook.

He'd been keeping the notes for three weeks now. Forty-one pages in a composition book, the kind with the black-and-white marbled cover that his grandfather had handed him without explanation on the first day. The notes were uneven — some pages were careful and organized, with headings and measurements; some were just fragments, words he'd written fast to catch something before it went. *Pitch changes with wall thickness, not diameter alone.* That was from the second week. *The sound a bell makes when it's wrong is not the same as silence — it's something that makes you want to stop.* He'd written that one at the bottom of a page and then stared at it, unsure if it was useful or just true.

His grandfather came back to the bench with the crown piece and a length of solder wire. He held the copper strip against the bell's crown and examined the fit. Then he made a small sound in his throat — not approval, not disapproval, just acknowledgment — and set the solder wire down.

"Tell me what you wrote yesterday," he said.

"About the quench?"

"About the interval."

Cass flipped back two pages. "The interval between a bell's fundamental and its hum note changes with distance. Closer in, the hum dominates. Further out, the strike note comes forward. So the bell sounds like a different bell depending on where you're standing."

"And why does that matter."

It wasn't a question. Cass knew by now that when his grandfather said it without the inflection, he meant: *finish the thought.*

"Because if you tune a bell for someone standing next to it, it'll be wrong for someone standing across the room."

"So you tune it for—"

"The room," Cass said. "You tune it for where it's going to live."

His grandfather picked up the flux brush again. "Write that down if you haven't."

Cass checked. He had. He'd written it twice, actually — once in plain language, once as a note about the Aldric Voss bell, the one on the shelf with the crack in the lip. 

"Can I ask you something?" he said.

His grandfather was heating the crown piece with the small torch, moving the flame in slow circles. The copper went through its colors quickly — dull, then faintly rose, then the beginning of orange at the edges. "You're going to ask it either way."

"The Voss bell. The crack in the lip." Cass paused. "Did he know it was cracked when he made it, or did it crack later?"

The torch kept moving. His grandfather didn't answer immediately, which meant he was thinking, not ignoring. After a moment: "Casting flaw. You can see it in the grain if you know what to look for. He knew."

"And he used it anyway."

"He used it for forty years." His grandfather pulled the torch back, examined the copper. "Then it went to a church in Fredericksburg. Then it came here. It's been here longer than you've been alive."

Cass looked at the bell on the shelf. From where he sat, the crack was invisible. He knew it was there because his grandfather had shown him — a hairline fracture running from the lip up about three inches, not structural, not dangerous, just a flaw that affected the resonance. The bell's hum note was slightly flat because of it, his grandfather said. Not enough that most people would notice. Enough that Cass noticed now, every time, which meant he'd been changed by knowing.

"Does it bother the sound?" he asked. "The crack?"

"Changes it."

"But does it bother it."

His grandfather looked at him then, the torch still in his hand, flame off. He had the look he sometimes got when Cass said something that landed in an unexpected place. Not surprised, exactly. More like — recalibrating. "No," he said finally. "It doesn't bother it. It's part of it."

He went back to the work.

Cass wrote: *A flaw that changes the sound is not the same as a flaw that ruins it.* He looked at it. Then he wrote underneath: *How do you know which is which?* He left that one without an answer, because he didn't have one, and he'd learned that his grandfather didn't like it when he filled in answers he hadn't earned yet.

The torch came back on. The smell of flux was sharp and sweet at once, the kind of smell that Cass associated now with concentration — with the particular quality of attention that his grandfather brought to every joint, every seam, every place where two pieces of metal had to become one. He'd asked about that once, whether it ever got boring. His grandfather had looked at him like he'd said something in a language that didn't translate.

"Hand me the solder."

Cass handed it across. His grandfather touched the wire to the joint and the solder flowed — that was the only word for it, *flowed*, like water finding a level, filling the seam between copper and bronze with a line of silver that would hold as long as the bell held. Cass watched it happen and felt the thing he always felt watching it, which was not quite awe and not quite envy but something in between, the recognition of a skill so practiced it had become invisible to the person doing it.

The door from the house opened. His father came in.

He was in his work clothes still, which meant he'd come straight from somewhere — the hardware store, or the Castellano job, which had been running over schedule for two weeks. 

"Hey," his father said.

"Hey," Cass said.

His grandfather didn't turn around. "Coffee's from this morning."

"I'm fine." His father came over and stood near the bench, looking at the work without quite looking at it — the peripheral attention of someone who wasn't there for the bells. He picked up the copper strip that was left over from the cut and turned it in his fingers. Set it down. "How's it going?"

"Good," Cass said.

"He's starting to hear the interval," his grandfather said, still working. "Took me longer than that."

His father went still.  He set the copper strip down carefully. "Yeah?"

"Wrote it down yesterday. Without being told."

His father looked at Cass then, and Cass looked back, and neither of them said anything for a moment. The torch was off. The workshop was quiet except for the refrigerator in the corner that cycled on and off with a low hum that Cass had stopped hearing weeks ago.

"That's good," his father said. His voice was doing the thing it did sometimes, where it was trying to be neutral and wasn't quite making it. "That's — yeah."

Cass didn't know what to do with this. He looked down at the notebook. He could feel his father still standing there, the particular weight of his presence, which was different from his grandfather's presence — his grandfather occupied space quietly, like a piece of furniture that had been in the room long enough to belong; his father occupied it with a kind of held energy, like he was always about to say the next thing.

"The Castellano job," his father said to his grandfather. "They want to add the cupola."

"They always want to add something."

"I know. I told them it'd cost." He pulled a folded paper from his jacket pocket and spread it on the end of the bench, away from the work. "But if we're going to do it, I want to get the bell placement right. There's a wind issue on the east side."

His grandfather came over and looked at the paper. It was a rough sketch — Cass could see from where he sat that it was a floor plan with elevation notes in his father's handwriting, which was larger and less precise than his grandfather's. They stood together over it, and Cass watched them from the stool and thought about what his grandfather had told him three weeks ago, about his father not having the hearing. About how his father was proud and didn't always know how to say it without making it about himself.

He thought about that and tried to decide if it made this moment easier or harder.

His grandfather pointed at something on the sketch. "Move it six inches west. The wind'll carry the sound down the slope instead of across."

"I thought about that. The beam placement makes it complicated."

"Everything's complicated. Move the bell."

His father made a sound that was almost a laugh, not quite. He folded the paper and put it back in his pocket. He looked over at Cass. "You eat?"

"Not yet."

"Mom's making the soup. The one with the—" He gestured vaguely. "The white beans."

"Okay."

"Twenty minutes, probably." His father looked at the bench, at the bell with its new crown piece, at the notebook open on the stool. He looked at it for a moment too long, the way people look at things when they're actually looking at something else. Then: "What are you writing about?"

Cass looked at the open page. "Right now? The interval thing. How you tune for the room, not for the bell."

His father nodded slowly. "Your grandfather taught me that. I just—" He stopped. Started again. "I understood it in theory. I never quite got it in practice."

"Because you couldn't hear the hum note," Cass said.

His father looked at him. Not hurt, exactly. Something more complicated than hurt, which was worse.

"Dad. I didn't mean—"

"No, you're right." His father said it quietly. "That's exactly it." He was quiet for a moment. "Your grandfather told you."

"Yeah."

"Good." He said it like he meant it, which was the surprising thing — Cass had expected deflection, or the careful neutrality his father used when he was managing something. Instead it just landed flat and real. "Good. You should know."

His grandfather had gone back to the bell. He was examining the crown joint with the magnifying loupe, turning the bell in the light. He didn't look up. This was also something Cass had learned about him: he knew when to be very busy.

"I can hear some things," his father said. He wasn't talking to anyone in particular, or he was talking to Cass, or he was talking to the room. "I can hear when something's wrong. I just can't always hear what's wrong with it. There's a difference."

Cass thought about that. "Like how you can tell a beam is settling wrong but you have to get the level to know how much."

His father looked at him. Then he laughed — a real one, short. "Yeah. Like that." He shook his head slightly. "God, you're a carpenter's kid."

"I'm a bell-maker's grandson."

"That too." His father put his hand on Cass's shoulder, briefly, the way he did sometimes — not a hug, just contact, just the weight of the hand saying something the voice wasn't going to. Then he straightened up. "Soup in twenty."

"I'll be there."

His father went back through the door to the house. The workshop settled back into itself — the refrigerator hum, the faint sound of the street outside, the smell of flux and hot metal and the particular dusty warmth of a room that had been heated by work for decades.

His grandfather set the bell down. He picked up the polishing cloth.

"You handled that well," he said.

Cass looked at the notebook. "I said the wrong thing."

"You said a true thing. That's not the same as the wrong thing." His grandfather worked the cloth along the bell's shoulder, the bronze starting to come up. "He's known about his hearing his whole life. You didn't tell him anything he didn't know."

"It felt—" Cass stopped. Tried again. "It felt like I was pointing at something he'd rather not look at."

"Probably." His grandfather turned the bell. "He came in anyway. That's what matters."

Cass wrote the date at the top of the new page. October 14th. He wrote it and looked at it and thought about what it meant that his father came in every morning, and had been coming in more since Cass started, and never once picked up a tool or asked to try anything. Just came in. Just stood there for a while. Just looked at the work.

He'd been thinking about this for three weeks and hadn't figured out what it meant, and now he thought maybe he had it: his father came in because the workshop was the place where the thing he couldn't do was being done, and that was not comfortable, and he came in anyway.

That was its own kind of hearing.

He wrote: *Some people learn the interval. Some people learn to work without it. Both are real.* He looked at it. It felt like something his grandfather might say, which made him uncertain whether he'd thought it or remembered it.

"The Voss bell," he said. "You said he wrote everything down."

"For thirty years after he went deaf." His grandfather set the bell down and examined it. The bronze was coming up warm now, the surface catching the light differently — still not finished, but closer to itself. "He couldn't hear the bells anymore, but he remembered what they sounded like. He worked from memory and from measurement. His students said you couldn't tell the difference."

"Between the bells he made before and after?"

"Between the bells he made before and after."

Cass sat with that for a moment. Outside, a car went by slowly, its tires on the wet street making a sound like someone drawing a finger across a drumhead. The refrigerator cycled off. In the quiet, the workshop felt very still.

"He must have been—" Cass started, and then stopped, because he didn't know how to finish it. Terrified? Determined? Both? The words felt too small for what he was trying to say.

His grandfather looked at him. "He must have been what?"

"I don't know." Cass looked at the Voss bell on the shelf. The crack was still invisible from here. "I was going to say *brave*, but that's not quite it."

"No," his grandfather agreed. "That's not quite it."

"What is it, then?"

His grandfather picked up the polishing cloth again. He worked for a moment in silence, the cloth moving in the small circles Cass had watched him make a hundred times. Then: "He had a job. He kept doing it." He turned the bell to catch the light. "That's not bravery. That's just — what you do when the work matters more than the condition you're doing it in."

Cass wrote it down word for word. He wrote it down and then sat there with the pen still in his hand, looking at the words on the page.

The bell on the shelf held its crack and its flat hum note and its forty years in a Fredericksburg church and however many years before that in Aldric Voss's workshop, where a man had stood at a bench and made bells by memory and measurement after the sound had gone out of the world for him. 

He turned to a fresh page.

At the top he wrote: *What you do when the work matters more than the condition you're doing it in.* He put a box around it. Not because he was certain it was the most important thing he'd written — he wasn't certain of that. But because it was the kind of sentence that would mean something different in a year, and he wanted to be able to find it.

His grandfather finished the polish and set the bell down. He looked at it for a long moment, the way he always did at the end — not admiring, exactly, more like taking inventory. Making sure the thing was what it was supposed to be.

"Soup in twenty," his grandfather said.

"I know."

"So go in ten." He picked up the flux brush and began cleaning it. "You've been here since two."

Cass looked at the window. The gray light had shifted; it was later than he'd thought, the flat October afternoon having compressed itself somehow while he wasn't watching. He looked at the notebook — forty-three pages now, he'd filled two more without noticing.

He closed it.

He sat for a moment on the stool with the closed notebook on his knee and looked around the workshop. The bells in their row on the bench. The Voss bell on the shelf. The tools hung on their pegs in his grandfather's specific order, which Cass had learned well enough now that a displaced tool bothered him the way a wrong note bothered him, a small wrongness that sat in the back of his awareness until it was corrected. The smell of flux and bronze and the wool of his grandfather's work sweater. The refrigerator hum.

He stood up. He put the notebook on its shelf — his grandfather had cleared a space for it on the third week, without comment, which was how his grandfather gave things.

"Grandfather," he said.

His grandfather looked up from the flux brush.

"When Voss went deaf." Cass paused. He was trying to get it right. "Did anyone know? That he'd lost it. Did his students know?"

His grandfather considered this. "His journals don't say." He set the brush down. "But I think they knew. You can't hide that kind of loss for long. The people who work close to you figure it out."

"And they kept working with him anyway."

"They kept working with him anyway."

Cass nodded. He picked up his jacket from the hook by the door. He put it on and zipped it, and he stood there for a moment with his hand on the door to the house, feeling the warmth from the other side of it — the soup smell was coming through now, faint and good, white beans and something green.

"The notes," he said. "Voss's notes. You said he wrote them down and didn't know who would read them."

"That's right."

"But someone did."

"Someone did," his grandfather said. "Or we wouldn't know any of this."

Cass opened the door. The soup smell came forward, warm and specific, his mother's kitchen on the other side of the house making itself known. Behind him, his grandfather picked up the polishing cloth and went back to the bell, the bronze catching the incandescent light and giving it back changed — warmer, more itself, the way bronze always did when you gave it enough attention.

He went in.
