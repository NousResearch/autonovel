# Chapter Five

The bell was on his desk when he woke.

He'd set it there the night before, on the corner nearest the window, and in the morning light it looked smaller than it had felt in his hands — a practice bell, bronze gone dark at the crown where the metal had been worked too many times, the casting seam still visible along the skirt where his grandfather had not quite finished it. Cass lay in bed and looked at it. Outside, a car went past on the wet street. The starlings were already in the yard.

He'd slept badly. Not because of the eye, which had settled through the night into a low, specific throb, but because every time he'd come close to sleep the under-note had seemed to be there at the edge of things, not arriving, just waiting. Like the moment before a bell is struck when the hammer is already moving and the sound is already inevitable and you are already bracing.

It hadn't come. He'd slept eventually. He woke with it still not there and the absence felt, somehow, worse.

He got up and dressed and carried the bell downstairs.

---

His mother was at the kitchen table with her tea, the morning paper folded to the crossword she never finished. She looked up when he came in. She had the particular expression she'd been wearing for three days now, since the night he'd come home from the forge with his eye swollen and a story she hadn't quite believed — the expression of someone holding a question they've decided not to ask yet.

"How's the eye," she said.

"Fine."

"It doesn't look fine."

He set the bell on the counter and got down a bowl. The box of oats was behind the kettle. He measured without looking. "It's better than yesterday."

"Your grandfather called."

Cass turned. "When?"

"Last night, after you were asleep. He said to tell you—" She paused and looked at the crossword. "He said to tell you that he'd been thinking about the pitch. That there's a crack in the core, possibly. He said you'd understand what that meant."

Cass poured water over the oats and stood at the counter while it steeped. A crack in the core. He turned the bell in his hands, tilting it, looking up through the mouth at the inside of the crown. The light wasn't good enough to see anything. He got his phone and turned on the torch and looked again.

There. A hairline, starting at the strike point and running up toward the crown. Not wide enough to show from outside. But a crack was a crack.

"Cass," his mother said.

"Yeah."

"What is it?"

He set the phone down. "A crack in the casting. It changes the pitch. Makes it—" He tried to think how to explain it to someone who hadn't grown up in a forge. "It makes the bell ring two notes at once. One it's supposed to ring, and one it's not."

His mother looked at the bell. "Is that why your eye—"

"I don't know," he said. Which was true, and also not the whole truth, and she could probably tell the difference.

She went back to the crossword. He ate his oats standing at the counter with the bell beside him, and neither of them said anything for a while, and the kitchen was warm and smelled like tea and toast and the particular morning smell of a house where someone had been up for an hour already, and Cass thought about what his grandfather had said at the gate: *Some bells ring true and some don't. You learn to hear which is which.*

He'd said it like it was only about bells.

---

School was ordinary in the way that school was always ordinary when something else was happening — the corridors loud, the lessons proceeding, Mr. Farrant writing equations on the board in his cramped left-handed script. 

He wrote *crack in the core — strike point to crown* in the margin of his notebook, then crossed it out, then wrote it again.

At lunch, Petra Voss sat across from him and said, "You look terrible."

"Thanks."

"Your eye, I mean. It's all—" She gestured at her own eye, squinting slightly.

"I know what it is."

"Did you go to the doctor?"

"My dad said to keep an eye on it."

Petra looked at him with the expression she used when she thought he was being deliberately obtuse, which was an expression he'd been on the receiving end of since they were nine. "Your dad the blacksmith."

"He's a bell-maker."

"Your dad who makes things out of metal said to keep watching the eye injury you got from making things out of metal."

"He said *take care of it*," Cass said. "That's different."

"How is that different?"

He opened his mouth and closed it. "It just is."

Petra stole one of his chips and ate it and looked out the window at the playing field. "My mum would make you go to A&E."

"Your mum makes you go to A&E for splinters."

"That was once, and it was a very deep splinter." She paused. "Is it the bell? The one you were telling me about?"

"The pitch is wrong. There's a crack in the casting."

"Can you fix it?"

Cass looked at his tray. "I don't know yet. I need to try something first."

She waited. That was one of the things about Petra — she had patience in the way that people who are genuinely curious have patience, not performing it, just actually willing to wait for the answer. He'd told her about the under-note two days ago, sitting on the low wall behind the science block, and she hadn't laughed and she hadn't immediately explained it away. She'd said, *what does it feel like*, and he'd tried to describe it, and she'd said, *like a radio frequency you can only hear in one ear*, and he'd said, *yes, exactly like that*, and she'd nodded and said, *that's interesting*, and that had been that.

"I think I need to ring it," he said. "Properly. Find out what the second pitch is. The one it's not supposed to have."

"Will that hurt your eye?"

"Probably."

Petra looked at him for a moment. Then she said, "Okay, but can I come? I want to hear it."

"You won't hear the under-note."

"I know. I want to hear the bell."

---

His grandfather was at the workbench when they came through the gate, not at the forge itself — the fire was banked low, the bellows still. He was doing something with a file and a small iron fitting, the careful work of the late afternoon when the heavy casting was done. He looked up.

"Cass," he said. Then, looking at Petra: "And who's this."

"Petra Voss. She's—" Cass stopped, because he wasn't sure how to finish that sentence in a way that explained why she was here. "She wanted to hear the bell."

 Petra held still. She was good at that.

"Voss," his grandfather said. "Your mother's the one on the parish council."

"Yes," Petra said.

"She argued against the new drainage scheme last spring. Was she right?"

Petra blinked. "The field at the bottom of Church Lane is still flooding, so. Probably yes."

His grandfather made a sound that might have been approval and went back to his fitting. "Get the bell," he said, which Cass took to be directed at him.

The bell was where he'd left it that morning — he'd brought it back before school, set it on the shelf beside the other practice castings. He lifted it carefully. The crack wasn't visible in the forge's dim light, but he could feel it if he ran his thumb up the inside of the skirt, a faint interruption in the smooth curve of the bronze.

"What are you going to do?" Petra asked, quietly, because his grandfather was three feet away.

"Ring it," Cass said. "And listen."

He carried the bell to the ringing post, the iron stand his grandfather used for testing finished castings, and hung it by the crown-loop. The clapper hung inside. He steadied the bell with one hand and looked at it for a moment — the dark bronze, the unfinished seam, the crack he couldn't see but knew was there.

He struck it.

The note went out into the forge, clean at first and then not — the upper note rising and the second pitch underneath it, not quite a harmony, not quite a dissonance, something that lived in the interval between the two. Cass stood with his hand still raised and listened. The needle arrived behind his left eye, sharp and immediate, and he pressed his lips together and breathed through it.

The bell went on ringing. The note decayed and the under-note did not decay at the same rate, which meant for a few seconds they were at different volumes, the intended pitch falling away while the second one held, and in that gap Cass heard it clearly for the first time.

Not a pitch. An interval. The bell was ringing a specific relationship between two notes, and that relationship had a character — not good or bad, but particular, . He'd heard it before. Not from this bell. From somewhere else.

"That's a strange sound," Petra said.

The needle was still there, lower now, the bell's vibration fading. "Which part?"

"Both. But the low part especially. It sounds like—" She frowned. "It sounds like the tone they play at the train station. The one before the announcement."

Cass turned and looked at her.

She shrugged. "I know that's not helpful."

"No," he said slowly. "No, it might be."

His grandfather had set down the file. He was standing at the workbench looking at the bell with the focused expression he wore when something had his full attention. "Ring it again," he said.

Cass struck it again. The needle again, hard and specific. He kept his face still.

His grandfather listened. He walked to the bell, slowly, and stood beside it while the note died, and then he put out one hand and rested two fingers against the skirt of the bell, just touching, the way you'd touch a surface to feel for heat. His eyes were closed.

"There," he said.

"What is it?"

"The crack is acting as a second resonator. The bell is ringing its own fault." He opened his eyes. He looked at Cass. "You knew there was a crack before I told you."

"I found it this morning. The torch."

"But you heard something last night. Before you found it."

Cass said nothing.

His grandfather looked at him for a long moment. Then he said, to Petra, "Do you know what a wolf tone is?"

Petra shook her head.

"Stringed instruments. When a string vibrates at the same frequency as the body of the instrument, you get an unstable note. A feedback. The instrument is resonating with itself in a way it's not supposed to." He looked back at Cass. "Some people are sensitive to it. They feel it before they can explain what they're hearing."

"In their eye?" Petra said.

"That's new," his grandfather said. He said it matter-of-factly, like he was noting a variation in a material's behavior. Not alarmed. Just observant. "Your father didn't have that."

The forge was quiet. Outside, a car went past on the street, and then it was quiet again.

"My father," Cass said.

"He had the ear. Not the eye." His grandfather picked up the file again and turned the fitting over in his hand. "He used to get headaches. From certain bells. He stopped coming into the forge after a while." He ran the file along the fitting's edge, a single deliberate stroke. "He never said it was the bells. But I knew."

Cass looked at the bell hanging on the ringing post. The bronze had gone still. "He never told me that."

"No." His grandfather kept filing. "He wouldn't."

---

Petra walked home with him as far as the corner of his street and then stood there with her bag over one shoulder and the low afternoon light on her face. She'd been quiet for most of the walk, which wasn't unusual — she thought in long uninterrupted stretches and you learned to give her room for it.

"The wolf tone thing," she said.

"Yeah."

"That's real. I looked it up on my phone while your grandfather was talking." She held out the phone. He looked at the article — a paragraph about cello resonance, a diagram. "It says some players can feel it in the bow arm before they hear it. Like a vibration that travels up through the instrument."

"Through the instrument," Cass said.

"Through the player, I mean. The instrument is the medium." She put the phone away. "I'm not saying you're an instrument."

"I know."

"I'm saying maybe you're—" She stopped. Started again. "The bell has a crack, right. And the crack makes it resonate at a frequency it's not supposed to. And you can hear that frequency before you can hear it."

"That doesn't make sense."

"I know it doesn't make sense. That's why I'm saying it like a question."

He looked at her. "You didn't say it like a question."

"I was going to." She shifted her bag. "Is your eye okay?"

"It'll be fine."

"That's not what I asked."

The needle was still there, low and steady, the way it had been all day — the background throb that his body had organized itself around. He'd stopped noticing it by mid-afternoon, 

"It's manageable," he said.

Petra looked at him for a moment with the expression that meant she knew he was not telling her everything and had decided, for now, to accept it. "Okay," she said. "Tell me what you figure out. About the interval."

"The wolf tone."

"Whatever it is." She turned to go. Then, over her shoulder: "Cass. Go to the actual doctor. Not just your dad."

He watched her walk away. Then he turned and went up his street toward his house, and the needle pulsed once, sharp, at the base of the thought that was forming — not a complete thought yet, not something he had words for, just the shape of a question: if the crack made the bell ring its own fault, what did it mean that he could hear it? Was he hearing the bell, or was he hearing something the bell had found?

The question didn't have an answer. He walked home with it anyway, and the evening came on gray and cool around him, and the street was wet from a brief rain he'd missed while they were in the forge, the puddles reflecting the orange of the streetlights just coming on.

He stopped outside his house.

The bell. The interval. The fact that his father had the same ear and had stopped coming to the forge.

*He never said it was the bells. But I knew.*

He stood on the front step for a moment with his key in his hand. Then he went inside, dropped his bag in the hall, and went to find his father.

His father was in the kitchen, reading, the kind of end-of-day reading that was less about the book than about having something to hold while the day finished. He looked up.

"Dad," Cass said. "Did Grandad ever ring a bell for you that hurt your head?"

His father went still. Not tense — just still, the way he went still sometimes before he said something he'd been thinking about for a long time. "Why."

"Because he told me today that you had the ear. That some bells gave you headaches. And you stopped coming to the forge."

His father set the book down. He looked at the table for a moment, then at Cass.

"Yeah," he said. "Some of them did."

"Which ones?"

"The ones with something wrong in them." He paused. "Not always a visible fault. Sometimes it was—" He stopped. Started over. "There was a bell once, a commission piece, looked perfect. Rang perfect to most ears. But every time I was near it I got sick. Took me three days to figure out it was the bell." He picked up the book again, then set it back down. "Your grandfather thought it was a flaw in the alloy. Something in the tin ratio. Said the proportion was off by just enough to create a frequency below the audible range."

"Infrasound," Cass said.

"That's what he called it, yeah." His father looked at him. "Your eye."

"It's the bell. The practice casting. There's a crack in it."

His father was quiet for a moment. Then he said, "Does your grandfather know?"

"He's the one who told me about the crack. About the wolf tone."

"But does he know about your eye."

Cass said nothing.

"He knows something's happening," Cass said finally. "He said it was new. That you didn't have the eye thing, just headaches."

His father nodded slowly. "That's true." He looked at the table again. "Cass. How bad is it?"

"It's manageable."

"That's not—"

"I know. It's—" He thought about how to say it. "When the bell rings, it's a needle. Sharp, specific. Left eye. When it's not ringing it's just there. Low. Like a—" He looked for the right comparison and found it from somewhere he hadn't expected. "Like a string that hasn't been touched but is still vibrating from the last note. Does that make sense?"

His father looked at him for a long moment. Something moved across his face that Cass couldn't quite read — not worry exactly, or not only worry. Something older than worry.

"Yeah," his father said quietly. "That makes sense."

He didn't say anything else. Cass didn't ask him to. He sat down at the kitchen table across from his father and they stayed there in the warm kitchen while the light outside went from gray to dark, and his father didn't pick the book back up, and the needle behind Cass's eye held its low and steady note, and neither of them spoke,
