# Chapter Five: A Pup's Valley

The car smelled like French fries. Andy had left a bag from yesterday in the back seat, and Jim had meant to throw it out twice already and forgotten both times, and now the smell was just part of the car, the same as the dog hair on the passenger seat and the crack in the dashboard that had been there since 2017 and was never going to get fixed.

Jim pulled out of the Fayette Street lot and turned south on Washington Street. Two-forty in the afternoon. School let out at three-fifteen, which gave him enough time to make the turn onto Route 15 without hitting the backup at the McDonald's, assuming the backup wasn't already there, which it sometimes was by two-thirty on a Tuesday.

He drove past the hardware store, past the insurance office, past the spot where the sidewalk buckled slightly from a tree root that the town had been discussing for two years. The Blue Ridge was up ahead and to the right, just visible over the tree line, the same faint blue-gray it always was on clear October days. 

The name of the business was still wrong.

 

He turned onto Route 15. The McDonald's backup was already there, six cars deep, but it was contained to the turn lane and he got through clean.

*A Dog's Day Out.* He said it in his head for maybe the hundredth time and waited to feel something. Nothing happened. The name sat there, adequate and lifeless.

---

Andy came out the side door of the school with his backpack hanging off one shoulder and his jacket unzipped despite the October cold, 

"How was it," Jim said, when Andy got in.

"Fine." Andy dropped his backpack on the seat beside him. "We did fractions again."

"You like fractions."

"I liked them the first time. We've done them like four times."

Jim pulled out of the school lot and back onto the road. 

They drove for a while without talking.  The radio was off. The heater was making the faint clicking noise it made when it first came on, and then it stopped clicking and just ran.

"Dad," Andy said.

"Yeah."

"What's the name going to be? For the dog place?"

Jim glanced in the rearview mirror. Andy was looking out the window, not at him, watching the fields go past on the eastern side of Route 15 — the ones that hadn't been developed yet, the ones that still looked like Piedmont farmland instead of subdivision.

"Working on it," Jim said.

"You've been working on it for a long time."

"I know."

Andy was quiet for a moment. Outside, a hawk was sitting on a fence post, and then it wasn't — it dropped off the post and banked low over the field and was gone.

"Why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?" Andy said. The same tone he'd used to say *we did fractions again*. Not an announcement. Just a thing that seemed true, offered because it seemed true.

Jim heard it.

*A Pup's Valley.*

 He drove, and the name was in the car with him, and it was right. 

"Yeah," he said finally.

Andy looked up from the window. "Yeah what?"

"That's it. That's the name."

Andy seemed to consider this for a moment, then nodded, and looked back out the window. The hawk or another hawk was up again, circling something in the field. Andy watched it until it was behind them.

"Can we get a snack when we get home?" he said.

"There's stuff in the pantry."

"I mean a real snack."

"There's stuff in the pantry," Jim said again, and Andy accepted this, and they drove the rest of the way home with the name sitting in the car between them like something that had always been there.

---

Andrea was in the kitchen when they got back, her laptop open on the counter and a legal pad beside it covered in notes. 

She looked up when they came in. "How were fractions?"

"We've done them four times," Andy said, and went to the pantry.

Jim set his keys on the counter. Andrea was already looking back at her laptop, reading something, her pen moving in small circles on the edge of the legal pad the way it did when she was processing. He waited until she looked up again.

"I've got the name," he said.

She put the pen down. "Okay."

"Andy got it. In the car."

She looked past Jim toward the pantry, where Andy was audibly rearranging things in search of something specific. Then she looked back at Jim.

"Tell me."

"A Pup's Valley."

 

"That's it," she said.

"Yeah."

"That's the name."  

Andrea looked toward the pantry again. Andy emerged with a sleeve of crackers and a jar of peanut butter and took them to the table without looking at either of his parents, already focused on the task of getting the peanut butter open.

"Andy," Andrea said.

"Mm."

"Good name."

Andy looked up. He seemed momentarily uncertain what she was referring to, and then it clicked. "Oh," he said. "Yeah, it just seemed like — I don't know. It's a valley. And there are pups." He worked the lid off the peanut butter. "It seemed right."

Andrea looked at Jim. Jim looked at Andrea.

"It seemed right," Jim said.

"Yep," Andrea said.

Andy spread peanut butter on a cracker with the focused attention of someone who was no longer interested in the conversation, and that was the end of it. 

---

Jim called Mark Donahue's office that evening about the HVAC question, and while he had Donahue on the line he mentioned that they'd settled on the name. Donahue said *A Pup's Valley*, said it back to make sure he had it, and said it sounded good. Jim said they'd need to update the permit applications and the signage plans. Donahue said he'd make a note. Jim said thanks and hung up.

He sat for a moment at the kitchen table with the phone in his hand. The house was quiet — Andrea had moved to the back room with her laptop, and Andy was upstairs, and Cassie was asleep under the table with her head on Jim's foot, which she had done since she was eight weeks old and had not stopped doing in the years since. Gracie was somewhere in the back of the house, probably with Andrea, because Gracie had a preference and she exercised it.

Cassie shifted in her sleep, adjusted her head, settled again.

Jim thought about the sign. Not the process of getting the sign made, not the permit for the sign, not the dimensions or the font or the color — just the sign itself, the words on it. *A Pup's Valley.* On a building on Fayette Street. In Haymarket, Virginia. Visible from the road.

 

Jim set the phone down. He had two more calls to make before he could reasonably stop for the night, and he was going to make them. But he sat for another minute with Cassie's weight on his foot and the name in his head, and he let it be what it was — .

Then he picked the phone back up and made the first call.

---

The permit applications were updated the following week. The signage plans went to the fabricator in early November, and the fabricator said six weeks, and Jim said fine. Andrea updated the name everywhere it needed updating — the business filings, the website they were building, the accounts she'd been setting up, the conversation she'd been having for months with the families who'd already said they were interested, the ones who had dogs and commutes and were waiting for somewhere to bring the dogs while they were gone.

 *A Pup's Valley.* Oh, that's nice. Where is it? Fayette Street, just off Washington. Do you know Haymarket? Yes. Good. Here's what we're building.

The sign went up in January. White letters on a dark background, clean and readable from the road. Jim drove past it the first morning it was up and did not stop to look at it, 

Andy never mentioned it again.  

Cassie and Gracie were at home, waiting.
