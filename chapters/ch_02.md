# Chapter Two: The Under-Note

The second morning Cass came in early.

Not before Maret — she was already there, had been there long enough that the forge was breathing steadily and the smell of heated metal had settled into the room's low places like something that lived there. But early enough that the two boys hadn't arrived, and Brennan hadn't arrived, and the small girl in the corner, whose name he still didn't know, was only just unwrapping her coat.

He set his bag down at his bench and stood for a moment, listening to the forge. It had a sound distinct from the one his father's forge made — lower in pitch by what he estimated was a minor third, and steadier, without the slight rhythmic variation his father's produced when the bellows needed adjustment. His father's forge talked to itself. This one kept its own counsel.

"You're staring at it."

He turned. Maret was at the back of the room, not looking at him, doing something with a set of tongs he couldn't see clearly.

"I was listening," he said.

"To what?"

"The pitch is different from my father's."

She set the tongs down. "How different."

"About a minor third lower." He paused. "Maybe a little more."

She turned then, and looked at him with the same expression she'd had the previous afternoon — that quality of listening directed outward, toward him. "Your father's forge runs hot," she said. "He always has. The pitch goes up with temperature."

"I know." His father had told him this. What his father hadn't told him was that it was worth paying attention to, which was perhaps the same thing as not telling him at all.

"Sit down," Maret said, which was apparently the beginning of the workday.

---

The morning's task was filing. Not creative filing — corrective filing, the removal of excess material from a set of small bronze fittings that had come out of the mold slightly oversized. The work required a specific kind of attention: enough to keep the file angle consistent, not so much that you started second-guessing and overcorrected. His father had given him filing work before and he understood the rhythm of it. He settled in.

Brennan arrived twenty minutes later, took the bench two down from his, and began working without acknowledging anyone. The two boys — Cass had learned their names were Pell and Oren, brothers, both of them broad-shouldered in the way of people who had been doing physical work since they were young — came in together and immediately began arguing in low voices about something that had happened on their walk. The small girl in the corner, whose name he still hadn't learned, was already at her work.

He filed.

The filing had its own sound, which layered over the forge sound, which layered over the low conversation between Pell and Oren and Maret's periodic movements through the room. He had always been able to hear layers in sound the way some people could see layers in paint — not something he'd been taught, just something his ears did. His father's workshop was a chord. This one was a different chord, stranger, with intervals he hadn't learned yet.

An hour in, he noticed the girl in the corner had stopped working.

She was sitting very still, her hands flat on the bench in front of her. Not resting. The posture of someone waiting for something to pass.

He filed another stroke, then stopped.

Underneath the forge sound, underneath the ambient noise of the room — there was something. Not quite a sound. More like the memory of one, or the anticipation. A pressure at the low end of what he could hear, like a note that hadn't decided yet whether to exist. His left eye began to ache, just behind the socket, a thin needle of sensation that had nothing to do with the light.

He set his file down.

The girl in the corner had her eyes closed.

The pressure built for three or four seconds and then resolved into something that was almost a pitch — a low, subsonic drone that he felt more in his chest than heard with his ears, and then a harmonic above it, brief and clear, like a finger run along the rim of a glass. The needle behind his eye sharpened and then withdrew.

Then it was gone.

He picked up his file. His hands were steady. The girl in the corner opened her eyes and went back to work.

He wanted to ask her. He did not ask her. He filed another stroke and listened to the room settle back into itself, and thought: *twice now.*

---

At midday they stopped for the meal break, which in Maret's workshop meant twenty minutes and a bench cleared of metal shavings. Pell and Oren produced food from their bags and ate standing up, still arguing. Brennan ate alone at her bench with a book open beside her food, which she read without appearing to actually read it. The girl in the corner ate slowly and looked at nothing.

Cass sat at his bench and unwrapped the bread his aunt had sent him with and tried to decide whether to say anything.

The girl spoke first.

"You heard it," she said.

He looked up. She was watching him, direct and unguarded in a way that seemed younger than her face, which was twelve or thirteen, he thought, though her manner was older.

"I heard something," he said. Which was what he'd told Maret the day before, and was still true, and was also not the whole truth.

"The under-note," she said. "That's what I call it. The thing that comes before a bell sounds." She looked at her food. "The bells here do it sometimes. The big one in the back mostly."

"Does it hurt you?"

She considered this. "Not exactly. It feels like—" She stopped. "Like when you've been in a loud room too long and then it goes quiet. That feeling. Except it happens in reverse."

He understood this. The needle behind his eye had been real, specific, nothing like the vague pressure she was describing, but he recognized the shape of what she meant — the body registering something the ears alone couldn't process.

"Does Maret know?" he asked.

"I don't know what Maret knows." She said it without bitterness, just as a fact about the limits of her information.

He looked across the room to where Maret was standing at the forge, eating something without looking at it, her attention on the coals. "What's your name?" he asked.

"Sona."

"Cass," he said.

She nodded once, as if she'd known this, and went back to her food.

---

In the afternoon Maret came to his bench.

She watched him file for a moment without speaking. He kept his angle consistent and did not look up, because she was watching his work and not him, and he understood this kind of attention.

"Stop," she said.

He stopped.

She picked up the fitting he'd been working on and turned it in her fingers. Then she set it down and picked up the one he'd finished an hour earlier, and turned that one too. She placed them side by side.

"The second one is better," she said.

"I know."

"Why."

He looked at them. "I was overcorrecting on the first one. I kept checking the angle and then adjusting and then checking again. By the second one I stopped checking."

"You trusted your hands."

"Yes."

She was quiet for a moment. "Your father's hands do that. Stop thinking about what they're doing." She set both fittings back down. "Most people never learn it. They think the skill is in the checking."

He looked at the two fittings. The difference was subtle — a slightly uneven surface on the first, a cleaner finish on the second. Anyone who wasn't looking for it would have passed both as acceptable.

"Maret," he said. "The under-note."

She stilled. Not visibly — she was already still — but something in the quality of her stillness changed.

"This morning," he said. "And yesterday, with the bell in the back room."

"What about it."

"Sona hears it too." He paused. "She said it comes before a bell sounds. That it's like—" He stopped, because he was about to describe what she'd said and that wasn't what he meant. "It hurt," he said instead. "Yesterday, when I touched the bell. This morning, when it happened again. Behind my left eye. Like a needle."

Maret turned and looked at him fully. It was the first time she'd looked at him that way, without the assessment being aimed at his work.

"Show me your left hand," she said.

He held it out. She took it and turned it palm up and looked at it for a long moment, then set it down.

"Your father has this too," she said.

"The pain?"

"The hearing." She picked up her own file and turned it in her hands, not looking at it. "He never told you."

"No."

"He wouldn't." She set the file down. "It's not something the Guild recognizes. They don't have a word for it, which means officially it doesn't exist, which means the people who have it don't discuss it in company." She looked at him steadily. "I'm telling you this once. You can ask me questions now or you can ask me later. If you ask me in front of Pell and Oren, I'll answer as if you asked about something else."

He thought about this. "What is it?"

"I don't have a name for it either. The Guild would call it sensitivity to resonance and then write it off as subjective variance in perception." She paused. "What it actually is — what I think it actually is — is that some people hear the potential of a bell before it's rung. The harmonic that the metal is already holding. The note it wants to make."

The forge breathed behind her.

"The bells here," he said. "The ones in the back room."

"Some of them are holding notes they've held for a long time," she said. "The old ones especially."

"And the under-note. The pain."

"The pain means the note is wrong." She said it simply, without drama. "Or not wrong — incomplete. Unresolved. A bell that hasn't found its pitch yet. The dissonance is physical, for people who can hear it." She picked the file back up. "Your father doesn't work on bells. Did you ever wonder why?"

He had. He'd wondered it for years and not asked, the way you don't ask about the door in the house that's always closed. "He said he preferred smaller work."

"He does prefer smaller work." She turned back to the forge. "He also can't be in the same room as a bell with an unresolved harmonic for more than a few minutes without a headache that lasts three days."

He stared at the back of her head.

"Go back to your filing," she said.

---

He went back to his filing.

Pell and Oren were still arguing, quietly now, the argument having apparently exhausted its energy and settled into a kind of ritual continuation. Brennan worked with the focused blankness of someone who had learned to be somewhere else while her hands did things. Sona was at her bench, and he couldn't see what she was working on from where he sat, but her posture had changed since the morning — less braced, more settled.

He filed, and listened, and thought about his father's hands.

His father had the largest hands of anyone Cass had known well, and they were also, when the work required it, the most precise. He had watched his father hold a piece of silver at the exact angle required for a specific cut and hold it there, without trembling, for as long as the cut took. He had assumed this was practice, repetition, the forty years of it. He had not considered that it might also be a kind of listening — that his father's hands were positioned not just by eye but by the sound the metal was making, the note it was holding, the harmonic it was ready to give up.

The under-note.

*He never told you.*

*He wouldn't.*

He filed another stroke. The bronze gave up a thin curl of metal that fell to the bench and caught the light.

He thought about the bell in the back room. Ten seconds of contact and a needle behind his eye and something that might have been a note. He thought about Sona's description: *like when you've been in a loud room too long and then it goes quiet. Except in reverse.* He thought about what Maret had said: *the note it wants to make.*

A bell that hadn't found its pitch yet.

He wondered how long it had been waiting.

---

Near the end of the day, Maret called them all to the front of the room.

This was apparently unusual. Pell and Oren exchanged a look. Brennan closed her book with her finger still holding the page. Sona came from her corner without hurrying.

They stood in a rough half-circle in front of Maret's workbench. On it she had placed five bells, small ones, each roughly the size of a fist. They were finished work — clean surfaces, proper proportions, each with a small clapper suspended inside.

"Tell me what you hear," she said.

She picked up the first bell and rang it once, cleanly, letting the clapper strike at the midpoint of the body.

The note was clear and round, a mid-range tone that decayed evenly and left behind a silence that felt like the bell was still present in it. Nothing behind Cass's eye. He waited, but there was nothing.

"Good bell," Pell said. Oren nodded.

Maret set it down and rang the second.

This one was higher, brighter. The decay was faster. Cass heard a slight roughness at the top of the note, a burr in the upper harmonic that smoothed out after a second but left a residue of — not quite discomfort. Attention. The way a slightly sharp note made you want to adjust it.

"Higher," Brennan said. "And something in the upper register. An overtone that's not quite—" She stopped. "It's fine. It's close."

"It's not fine," Maret said, without cruelty. "You heard the problem. Name it."

Brennan looked at the bell. "The overtone is sharp. By a little. Maybe a quarter tone."

"More like an eighth," Maret said. "But yes." She set it down.

The third bell she rang twice, then set down without comment.

Cass heard nothing unusual from it. The note was even, the decay clean. He watched the others' faces. Pell and Oren showed nothing. Brennan tilted her head slightly. Sona had gone still again.

"Sona," Maret said.

Sona looked at the bell. "There's something underneath it," she said. "Not in the note. Below it." She pressed her lips together. "Like the bell is arguing with itself."

"Yes," Maret said.

"I don't hear that," Oren said. Pell shook his head.

"You will," Maret said, "or you won't. Both are fine. You'll work with the ones you can hear and you'll learn to trust the ones who hear what you can't." She looked at Cass. "Cass."

He looked at the third bell. The argument Sona had described — he could hear it now that she'd named it, or he thought he could, the faint internal friction of a note that was fighting its own harmonics. No pain. Just the quality of something not yet settled.

"She's right," he said. "It's — the fundamental and the first harmonic aren't quite agreeing."

"What would you do to fix it?"

He thought. "File the inside of the lip. Thin it a little, raise the fundamental slightly." He paused. "Or add mass at the top of the crown to lower the harmonic. Depending on which one is wrong."

Maret looked at him for a moment. "Which one is wrong?"

"The harmonic," he said. "The fundamental sounds right for the size. The harmonic is too low."

"Yes." She moved to the fourth bell.

She rang it and the needle went through his left eye so sharply that he put a hand to his face before he could stop himself, and then lowered it, and stood straight, and breathed. The note was ordinary to look at — a clean strike, a reasonable tone. But underneath it was a dissonance that felt like a frequency his skull was trying to reject, a pressure that built for three seconds and then held, waiting.

Sona made a small sound beside him.

"Leave it," Maret said. She set the bell down and the pressure withdrew. "That one is waiting for someone. I haven't found them yet." She said it the way you'd say a room was unfurnished. Matter-of-fact. Temporary.

The fifth bell she didn't ring.

She picked it up and held it, and looked at them. "This one is silent," she said. "Has been silent for eleven years. I made it in a bad year and I haven't been able to hear what's wrong with it since." She set it down. "That's not a lesson. That's a fact about the work. Some things you make will be wrong in ways you can't find and can't fix. That happens. You carry it." She looked at the bell. "You don't discard it."

The room was quiet.

"Go home," she said.

---

He was the last one out, because he took longer than the others to clean his bench, partly because he was careful and partly because he wanted the moment after they left — the room almost empty, just Maret at the forge and the five bells still on her workbench and the afternoon light coming in at a low angle through the high windows.

He put his tools in their roll and tied it and picked up his bag.

"The fourth bell," he said.

Maret didn't turn around. "Yes."

"The one that's waiting. What does that mean?"

"Some bells need a specific hand to find their pitch. A specific resonance in the person ringing them." She adjusted something in the forge. "You put the wrong person at the wrong bell and you get noise. You put the right person at it—" She paused. "The bell tells you what it's been holding."

He stood with his bag over his shoulder and looked at the fourth bell, small and clean and ordinary on the bench. He thought about the one in the back room. Eleven years of silence, and then his ten seconds, and then Maret's voice: *you heard the note itself.*

"Is the one in the back room the same?" he asked. "Waiting for someone?"

Maret was quiet for long enough that he thought, again, she wasn't going to answer. Then she turned around and looked at him.

"That bell," she said, "was made by your grandfather."

He stood very still.

"He died before he could ring it," she said. "Before he could finish whatever he was making it for." She looked at him steadily, with that quality of outward attention. "Your father knows this. It's why he sent you here."

The forge breathed.

He had nothing to say to this. He found he had no words at all, which was unusual for him, and he stood in the low afternoon light with his bag over his shoulder and the smell of heated bronze in his nose and the faint residue of the needle behind his eye, and he looked at Maret, and she looked back at him, and neither of them said anything for a long moment.

Then she turned back to the forge.

He walked to the door and opened it and went out into the street, where the evening was beginning and the cold air smelled like stone and distant rain, and he stood on the step for a moment before he started walking.

He had eleven years of silence to account for. He had a grandfather he had never met. He had a bell that had been waiting, and hands that were still becoming what they would be, and a father who had taught him the old ratios and sent him here without explaining why.

He started walking.

His left eye ached, faintly, all the way home.
