# Chapter Two: What Haymarket Is Trying to Be

Haymarket was incorporated in 1799, which means it existed before there was a Virginia to incorporate it into, and the town has never quite let anyone forget this.

The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 is the whole argument. Stand at that corner on a Tuesday morning and watch what crosses it: the flatbeds hauling pipe fittings east toward Gainesville, the commuter sedans headed north to catch the I-66 ramp, the occasional horse trailer moving at a pace that the flatbeds find intolerable. Route 15 wants to go somewhere. Route 55 cuts across and makes it wait. The intersection is unglamorous, signal-timed, and flanked on one corner by a McDonald's whose drive-through backs traffic onto the road when the line is long, which is often. None of this sounds like the stuff of civic identity, and yet here it is: the place where two roads disagree about direction, and the town that grew up around the argument.

What the town decided to be, over a long time and through the effort of specific people, is visible on Washington Street. The brick sidewalks are not original — nothing about a small Virginia town in the twenty-first century is original in the sense of unchanged — but they are deliberate. Someone chose them. Someone else agreed. Someone after that fought to keep them when a county engineer suggested concrete would be cheaper and easier to maintain, which it would have been, and that is precisely why the bricks are still there. The lamp posts on Washington Street have the look of something that belongs, which is a quality that requires as much work as anything structural. Belonging doesn't happen to a streetscape. It is made.

Haymarket has the particular self-consciousness of a small place that knows it is being watched. The county wants to absorb it. The developers want to replicate it. The tourists want to photograph it. The Town Council wants to preserve it, though they argue constantly about what preservation actually means — whether it means keeping the old buildings or keeping the old feel, and whether those are the same thing or very different things dressed in the same word. The incorporated town limits are small. The county presses in from all sides, with its subdivisions named Dominion Valley and Piedmont, its chain restaurants, its logic of commuter convenience. Haymarket is what the county surrounds but has not yet consumed. This is not an accident. It is a condition that has required maintenance.

Dorothy Watts came to Haymarket sometime in the early 1970s, young, and she looked at the place and decided to stay. This decision sounds simple stated that way, and it was not. Staying in a small town is a choice that compounds over time, the way interest compounds — each year you stay, you have more invested, and more to lose, and more reason to care about what happens to the place. Dottie Leonard, which is what she became after she married, understood this arithmetic. She did not stay in Haymarket the way people stay somewhere because they never got around to leaving. She stayed the way you commit to a position: with intention, with the full knowledge of what you were committing to.

She got her real estate license. She served on the Town Council. She sold houses in Haymarket for thirty years — not as a career that happened to be set in Haymarket, but as a career that was Haymarket, in the way that knowing every property in a town and every family that has moved through those properties is a form of knowing the town more completely than almost anyone else can. She knew who had sold the Fayette Street corner lot in 1987 and why. She knew which families had come through town and stayed, and which had come through and kept going, and occasionally she could tell you in the first meeting which kind they were.

This is institutional memory. It is not romantic. It is the specific, accumulated knowledge of someone who showed up for decades and paid attention.

The brick sidewalks on Washington Street — the lamp posts, the deliberate effort to make Haymarket look like somewhere with a past worth preserving — did not happen by accident, and Dottie's fingerprints are on them, literally and otherwise. She was in the rooms where those decisions got made. She was at the dais when the county wanted something different and the town said no, not here, not this. She understood that saying no to the wrong thing is how you protect the right thing, and she was not afraid to say it.

In 1993, Disney announced plans for a 3,000-acre history-themed park called Disney's America. The proposed site was just outside Haymarket, near the Manassas battlefield. The corporation was confident. It had the land optioned and the renderings prepared and the economic projections ready, and it arrived in Prince William County with the momentum of something that had already decided it was going to happen.

The opposition was national — historians, preservationists, editorial boards that found the idea of a Disney park adjacent to a Civil War battlefield aesthetically and morally objectionable in ways they were happy to explain at length. But the local fight was local, and Dottie was in it. She confirmed this to Jim Payne personally, in conversations they had before she died. She looked at what Disney was proposing and what it would do to the battlefield and to the town, and she said no. Not complicated. Just requiring the willingness to say it to a corporation that was not accustomed to hearing it from a small Virginia town.

Disney withdrew in September 1994. The park was never built. The battlefield is still there.

What this data point tells you about Dottie Leonard is this: she was someone who could assess a very large force moving in a very confident direction and say *not here, not this*, and mean it, and win. She did not win by matching the force. She won by being more certain about the place than the corporation was certain about the profit. That is a specific kind of strength. It does not advertise itself. It shows up when it is needed.

---

The Town Council chambers are a room that seats maybe sixty people. Fluorescent lighting. A dais that makes whoever sits behind it look either important or uncomfortable, depending on the meeting. Most of the meetings are about things that sound small from the outside — dump trucks on Fayette Street, the timing of traffic signals, the question of what kind of signage is permitted on Washington Street and whether the existing ordinance is being enforced. The people on the dais are your neighbors. You see them at the post office. The stakes of speaking to them, in both directions, are different from what they'd be in Arlington or Alexandria, where the Council is an abstraction and the citizens are constituents. In Haymarket, everyone in that room knows everyone else in that room, and they all know Dottie Leonard, and they know what she thinks about the brick sidewalks.

She served on that Council. She sat behind that dais and made decisions about this town for years. When she was no longer on the Council, she was still in the room — as a citizen, as a realtor, as the person who had been in Haymarket longer than most of the people currently arguing about dump trucks on Fayette Street had been alive. Her presence in a room was a form of institutional gravity. She did not have to say anything to be heard.

Fifty years of that. Fifty years of showing up, paying attention, fighting the right fights, selling the houses, knowing the families, keeping track.

What fifty years of that amounts to is a town that is still itself. A town with brick sidewalks that someone fought for. A town with lamp posts that belong. A town where the intersection of two roads is still an argument about what the place is and what it's trying to be, rather than a solved problem paved over by the county's answer.

Dottie Leonard made Haymarket worth coming to. This is not a small thing. Most places do not have someone who does that work, or they have someone who starts it and doesn't finish, or they have several someones who fight each other about what worth coming to means. Haymarket had Dottie, for fifty years, and the evidence is in the bricks under your feet and the lamp posts overhead and the fact that the battlefield is still a battlefield.

The question the town would eventually have to answer — the question that Jim and Andrea Payne would eventually make concrete by opening a business on Fayette Street — is whether the people who come to a place made worth coming to are worthy of it. Whether they understand what they've been given. Whether they will take care of it the way it was taken care of for them.

Dottie would have had opinions about this question. She had opinions about everything that touched Haymarket. But the question was not hers to answer alone, and she knew it. She knew it the way someone knows, after fifty years, that the work is larger than any one person and longer than any one life, and that the best you can do is hand it forward to people who understand what they're receiving.

She was still looking for those people when Jim and Andrea Payne came to town with a plan to open a cage-free dog daycare on Fayette Street, and she found them, and she got behind them, and she was right about them. This is what the next fifty years of Haymarket will know about Dottie Leonard, among other things: she was right about them.

But that part of the story belongs to Fayette Street, and to the Paynes, and to the dogs, and to the April night in 2020 when Andrea stood at the podium in the Council chambers under the fluorescent lights and told the truth about what was happening to the business. It belongs to the thirty days the town could offer, and to what the Paynes did with those thirty days.

What this chapter belongs to is the ground beneath all of that: the place itself, and the woman who spent her life making it mean something.

Dottie's real estate career was not separate from her civic work. It was the same work by another name. When you know every property in a town and have helped families buy and sell them for three decades, you know what the town is worth — not in assessed values, but in the deeper sense of what it costs people to come here, what they're betting on, what they hope to find. She knew which families were going to stay. She knew which businesses were going to make it. She knew the difference between someone who loved Haymarket and someone who loved what Haymarket could do for them, and she had learned, over time, that this distinction was the most important one you could make about a person.

The brick sidewalks. The lamp posts. The Disney fight. Thirty years of real estate. A seat on the Town Council. These are the same argument made in different materials: that this place has a character worth protecting, that character requires active maintenance, and that the maintenance is the work of specific people who decided to care.

When Jim Payne called Dottie in the hospital, near the end of her life, she picked up on the second ring. Her voice was the same voice she'd used in Council chambers and at closing tables for years. She thought he might be calling about a listing. She was ready to work it.

Jim said no, Dottie, I just called to talk.

There was a pause. Then she laughed, and they talked.

She died on March 20, 2024. She was seventy-five. Jim and Andrea went to her funeral. They were among the few who stood up and spoke for her — the people she had helped get started in the town she had spent her life making worth getting started in.

That is what community actually is. Not the word, not the Council chambers, not the brick sidewalks, though all of those are part of it. It is the specific fact of who shows up when someone is gone and says what they meant. It is the people she helped being the ones who stood up.

The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 is still there. The trucks still back up the drive-through. The lamp posts are still on Washington Street, and the bricks are still under them, and the town is still the thing the county surrounds but has not consumed.

Dottie Leonard made it worth coming to.

The question of whether the people who came were worthy of it — that question has an answer now, and it is on Fayette Street, and it has the name a child gave it from the back seat of a car, and it opens every morning, and the dogs come in.
