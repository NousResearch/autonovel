# Chapter Two: What Haymarket Is Trying to Be

Haymarket was incorporated in 1799.

The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 is the whole argument. Stand at that corner on a Tuesday morning and watch what crosses it: the flatbeds hauling pipe fittings east toward Gainesville, the commuter sedans headed north for the I-66 ramp, the occasional horse trailer moving at a pace the flatbeds find intolerable. Route 15 wants to go somewhere. Route 55 cuts across and makes it wait. The intersection is unglamorous, signal-timed, flanked on one corner by a McDonald's whose drive-through backs traffic onto the road when the line is long, which is often.

What the town decided to be, over a long time and through the effort of specific people, is visible on Washington Street. The brick sidewalks are not original —  — but they are deliberate.  The lamp posts on Washington Street have the look of something that belongs, which is a quality that requires as much work as anything structural.

Haymarket has the particular self-consciousness of a small place that knows it is being watched. The county wants to absorb it. The developers want to replicate it. The tourists want to photograph it. The Town Council wants to preserve it, though they argue constantly about what preservation actually means — . The incorporated town limits are small. The county presses in from all sides, with its subdivisions named Dominion Valley and Piedmont, its chain restaurants, its logic of commuter convenience. Haymarket is what the county surrounds but has not yet consumed. This is not an accident. It is a condition that has required maintenance.

Dorothy Watts came to Haymarket sometime in the early 1970s, young, and she looked at the place and decided to stay.

She got her real estate license. She served on the Town Council. She sold houses in Haymarket for thirty years. She knew who had sold the Fayette Street corner lot in 1987 and why. She knew which families had come through town and stayed, and which had come through and kept going, and occasionally she could tell you at the first meeting which kind they were. This was not a parlor trick. It was the result of paying attention to people in the specific way that real estate requires — not to what they said they wanted, but to what they kept returning to, which corner of the house they stood in longest, which window they looked out of twice.

Dottie Leonard, which is what she became after she married, understood the arithmetic of a small town: the people who stay are the ones who decide they have something to protect. Everyone else is passing through, and there is nothing wrong with passing through, but you cannot build a place on it.

 She was in the rooms where those decisions got made. She was at the dais when the county wanted something different and the town said no, not here, not this. She understood that saying no to the wrong thing is how you protect the right thing, and she was not afraid to say it.

In 1993, Disney announced plans for a 3,000-acre history-themed park called Disney's America. The proposed site was just outside Haymarket, near the Manassas battlefield. The corporation was confident. It had the land optioned and the renderings prepared and the economic projections ready, and it arrived in Prince William County with the momentum of something that had already decided it was going to happen.

The opposition was national — historians, preservationists, editorial boards that found the idea of a Disney park adjacent to a Civil War battlefield objectionable in ways they were happy to explain at length. But the local fight was local, and Dottie was in it. She confirmed this to Jim Payne personally, in conversations they had before she died. 

Disney withdrew in September 1994. The park was never built. The battlefield is still there.

Jim heard this story from Dottie not as a war story — she did not tell it that way. She told it as a matter of fact, the way you describe the outcome of a decision that was never really in doubt for you, even if it was in doubt for everyone else. They were sitting at her kitchen table, the spring before she went into the hospital, and she had made coffee without asking if he wanted any, which he had come to understand was simply how she operated.

"Were you scared?" he asked.

She considered this. Her hands were around her mug. Outside, a truck went by on Route 15.

"No," she said. "I was angry. That's different."

He looked at her.

"Scared means you think you might lose," she said. "I didn't think we were going to lose. I thought we had to work very hard to make sure we didn't, which is not the same thing."

Jim sat with that. He had his own version of this — the permits, the zoning hearings, the months of public comment periods, the specific friction of trying to build something real in a real place with real rules. He had not been scared either. He had been, at various points, frustrated and tired and certain that the next meeting would be the one where something finally moved. It had always eventually moved.

"You knew," he said. "When you saw the proposal."

"I knew what it would do to the road," she said. "I knew what it would do to the battlefield. I knew what it would do to this town." She set down her mug. "Some things you just know."

The Town Council chambers are a room that seats maybe sixty people. Fluorescent lighting. A dais that makes whoever sits behind it look either important or uncomfortable, depending on the meeting. Most of the meetings are about things that sound small from the outside — dump trucks on Fayette Street, the timing of traffic signals, the question of what kind of signage is permitted on Washington Street and whether the existing ordinance is being enforced. The people on the dais are your neighbors. You see them at the post office. The stakes of speaking to them, in both directions, are different from what they'd be in Arlington or Alexandria, where the Council is an abstraction and the citizens are constituents. In Haymarket, everyone in that room knows everyone else in that room.

Dottie served on that Council. She sat behind that dais and made decisions about this town for years. When she was no longer on the Council, she was still in the room — as a citizen, as a realtor, as the person who had been in Haymarket longer than most of the people currently arguing about dump trucks had been alive. Her presence was a form of institutional gravity. She did not have to say anything to be heard.

She was still looking for the right people when Jim and Andrea Payne came to town with a plan to open a cage-free dog daycare on Fayette Street. She found them. She got behind them. She was right about them.

This is what the next fifty years of Haymarket will know about Dottie Leonard, among other things: she was right about them.

She knew which families were going to stay. She knew which businesses were going to make it. She knew the difference between someone who loved Haymarket and someone who loved what Haymarket could do for them, 

When Jim Payne called Dottie in the hospital, near the end of her life, she picked up on the second ring. Her voice was the same voice she'd used in Council chambers and at closing tables for years. She thought he might be calling about a listing. She was ready to work it.

Jim said no, Dottie, I just called to talk.

There was a pause. Then she laughed, and they talked.

She died on March 20, 2024. She was seventy-five. Jim and Andrea went to her funeral. They were among the few who stood up and spoke for her — the people she had helped get started in the town she had spent her life making worth getting started in.

The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 is still there. The trucks still back up the drive-through. The lamp posts are still on Washington Street, and the bricks are still under them, and the town is still the thing the county surrounds but has not consumed.

Dottie Leonard made it worth coming to.

The question of whether the people who came were worthy of it — that question has an answer now, and it is on Fayette Street, and it has the name a child gave it from the back seat of a car, and it opens every morning, and the dogs come in.
