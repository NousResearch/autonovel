# Chapter Eight

The bell was still in his hands when he got home.

 But he could feel it. The weight of it, the way the metal had been warm from the overhead lamp and cool underneath where it had sat in shadow. His palms remembered the curve. He kept closing his fingers around nothing.

His mother was in the kitchen making something that involved onions. He could smell them from the front door.

"Dinner's twenty minutes," she called.

"Okay."

He went upstairs. His room was the same as he'd left it — bed unmade, headphones on the desk, the small speaker on the windowsill that he used when he wanted sound without the pressure of headphones against his ears. He stood in the middle of it for a moment and then sat on the edge of the bed and looked at his hands.

Three data points. That was what he had.

He'd written them in the notebook during the walk home, stopping twice on the sidewalk to get the words right before they dissolved. The notebook was his father's originally — a proper one, cloth-bound, the pages slightly cream rather than white, the kind his father had used for client notes before he'd switched to the tablet. Cass had found it in the recycling box two years ago and taken it without asking. His father hadn't noticed or hadn't minded; either way, the notebook was Cass's now, and he'd filled about a third of it with things that didn't fit anywhere else.

He opened it to the page he'd written on.

*Bell — cast bronze, medium tenor, C-sharp or D-flat depending on temperature. Struck once, full resonance approximately 4.2 seconds before decay. Under-note: approximately a minor sixth below, audible only during decay phase, begins around 2.8 seconds.*

*Under-note is NOT a casting flaw. Deliberate? Structural? Unknown.*

*Pain: needle behind left eye, onset at approximately 1.5 seconds into under-note. Duration: until resonance ends plus 8–10 seconds. Fades. Does not return until bell is struck again.*

He read it over. The handwriting was worse than usual — he'd been walking fast, and the second stop had been in front of the Garcias' house where their sprinkler was running and he'd had to keep moving before he got wet.

*The ledger. Last entry. "In the faith that they will be useful to someone who can."*

He'd written that separately, at the bottom of the page, and put a box around it. He wasn't sure why. It seemed like the kind of thing that needed a border.

He set the notebook on the bed and lay back and looked at the ceiling.

The pain was gone. That was the thing he kept coming back to. It had been real — specific and located, not the vague headache he sometimes got from too much screen time or from skipping lunch. This had been a needle, and it had been behind his left eye, and it had started at the same point in the bell's resonance every time he'd struck it. Three times. Three strikes, three onsets, same location, same timing. 

His grandfather had not seemed surprised.

That was the other thing.

He'd told Cass to ring it again, and then again, and he'd watched Cass's face during the third ring with the particular attention of a man who was checking something rather than discovering it. Like he'd had a hypothesis and was watching it confirm.

*He knew,* Cass thought. *He knew before I said anything.*

He sat up.

The question was why his grandfather hadn't said so. Why he'd waited for Cass to describe it — the needle, the timing, the fact that it stopped — instead of just telling him what he knew. He could have said: *this bell has an under-note, and people who can hear it feel it here, and here's what that means.* He hadn't. 

He picked up the notebook again and wrote: *Grandfather knew. Why wait?*

---

Dinner was chicken and rice with the onions cooked down into something soft and slightly sweet. His mother asked him how the workshop had been and he said it was good. His father asked if he was getting anywhere with the bell and he said he thought so. Neither of them pushed it. 

His sister Nora had a violin recital in two weeks and talked about it for most of dinner. She was eleven and had strong opinions about her teacher's tempo choices, which she expressed with the confidence of someone who had not yet learned to be uncertain about things. Cass ate and listened and occasionally agreed in the places where agreement seemed expected.

"You're not listening," Nora said.

"I'm listening. You said the Telemann is too slow."

"I said the *Vivaldi* is too slow."

"Right. The Vivaldi."

She looked at him with the expression she used when she suspected he was managing her. He probably was.

After dinner he helped clear the table and then went back upstairs. He had homework — a history essay that was due Friday, which was three days away, which was enough time if he started tomorrow. He opened his laptop and looked at the blank document for a while and then closed the laptop.

He picked up the notebook instead.

*What does the under-note do?*

He wrote the question and then sat with it.  A bell with a deliberate under-note was doing something. His grandfather's great-great-uncle hadn't put it there by accident. The ledger entry proved that — not directly, but by existing. You didn't write about a bell in a ledger unless the bell mattered. You didn't write about the person who would come after you unless you believed the bell would still be doing whatever it did when that person arrived.

He thought about the other entries. The ones he'd read before the last one.

 

The last entry was different. No measurements. No alloys. Just a sentence.

He'd been carrying it around all afternoon like a stone in his pocket, turning it over. *In the faith that they will be useful to someone who can.* His grandfather had said the old man knew it was the last time. Had known and had written not about the bell but about the next person. About Cass, in a way, though Cass hadn't been born yet, hadn't been imagined yet, was just the abstract future shape of whoever would eventually be able to hear what the bell was doing.

He wrote: *The under-note is a signal. But a signal for what?*

Then, underneath: *Who is it supposed to reach?*

He stared at the second question for a long time.

His phone buzzed. Priya, from school, asking if he'd understood the history assignment. He typed back that he thought so and she sent a series of question marks and he sent the assignment sheet photo he'd taken at the end of class and she sent a thumbs up and that was that.

The notebook was open to the questions he'd written, and he was looking at them, and he was thinking —  — that the pain was the point. Not a side effect. Not an unfortunate consequence of being sensitive to certain frequencies. The pain was the signal itself. The bell was doing something and the pain was the confirmation that it was working.

He wrote: *I am the instrument, not the listener.*

He looked at that for a while.

---

He was almost asleep when he heard his father on the phone downstairs.

He lay still and listened to the shape of it through the floor.

His father laughed at something. A real laugh, short, surprised out of him. Then the voice again, slower.

Then silence.

Cass lay in the dark and thought about the bell.

 

*Someone who can.*

He picked up his phone and opened the notes app and typed: *Ask — what did the original maker hear? Did he feel the pain? Is the pain the same for everyone who can hear it, or only me?*

He put the phone down.

The house settled around him, the particular sounds of late night — his parents' voices, lower now, not a conversation exactly, just the presence of each other. The refrigerator cycling on downstairs. Nora's light still on under her door, probably reading past her bedtime, which she did every night and which their mother pretended not to notice.

*The bell wasn't made to ring in the workshop.*

He was awake again.

He sat up in the dark.

The bell had been in the workshop for — how long? His grandfather hadn't said exactly. Decades, at minimum. It sat on the shelf with the other reference pieces, the ones his grandfather kept not to sell but to study, to return to. It had been rung in that room, in that space, with those dimensions and those acoustics, and the under-note had been there every time.

But his grandfather's great-great-uncle had made bells for a living. Had made them to go places. To hang in towers, in churches, in gates. To be struck in open air or in stone chambers with ceiling heights that no workshop could match. The resonance would be different. The decay would be longer. The under-note — if it was audible at all in a workshop — would behave completely differently in the space it had been made for.

He had been hearing a compressed version of the thing. A sketch of it.

He got out of bed and went to his desk and turned on the lamp and opened the notebook.

His grandfather would know where the bell had been made to go.

Whether it was still there was a different question. Whether it was possible to ring it in the right space was another different question. Whether the under-note in its proper acoustic environment would do something he hadn't experienced yet in the workshop was the question underneath all the others, the one he could feel but not quite look at directly.

He turned off the lamp and went back to bed.

Sleep came faster this time. He was in it before he'd finished deciding to be.

---

He woke at six-thirty without the alarm, which happened sometimes when something was waiting for him. He lay in the gray early light and listened to the house — his father already up, the particular sounds of the kitchen, the coffee maker finishing its cycle. He could smell it from upstairs, the dark roast his father bought from the place on Clement Street.

He dressed and went down.

 He looked up when Cass came in.

"Early," he said.

"Couldn't sleep."

"Bell?"

Cass got a glass of water. "Sort of."

His father watched him. Not pushing it, just watching.

"Did you know," Cass said, "that Grandpa's great-great-uncle — the one who made the bell — did you know he went deaf in his left ear?"

A pause. "I knew he went deaf. I didn't know which ear."

"Left. Grandpa told me yesterday." He drank the water. "The pain I get from the under-note is behind my left eye."

His father set the tablet down.

"Grandpa didn't say it was connected," Cass said. "He didn't say it wasn't, either. He just told me."

His father was quiet for a moment. He had the look he got when he was deciding whether to say the thing he was thinking, the small tension around his eyes. Then he said: "What do you think it means?"

"I think it means the man who made the bell could also hear the under-note. And it did something to his left ear over time." He put the glass in the sink. "I think the bell was made by someone who knew what it felt like from the inside."

His father looked at him for a long moment.

"Cass," he said, and then stopped.

"I'm not going to keep ringing it until I go deaf," Cass said. "I'm not an idiot."

"I know you're not."

"I'm going back at eight. Grandpa said we'd keep working."

"I know." His father picked up the tablet again, but he wasn't reading it. "I talked to him last night."

That was what the phone call had been. Cass filed it away. "What did he say?"

"He said you had good ears."

"I'll be back by lunch," he said.

 Cass stepped outside.

The morning was cool and smelled like fog that hadn't fully lifted yet, the particular damp that came off the bay and sat over the streets until the sun got high enough to burn it off. He walked with his hands in his pockets. The notebook was in his jacket, the pen clipped to the cover. His left eye felt fine. His head felt fine.

 But walking now in the gray morning with the fog still in the trees, he thought maybe it was also too small. An instrument was passive. An instrument waited to be played.

He turned the corner onto his grandfather's street.

The workshop light was already on.
