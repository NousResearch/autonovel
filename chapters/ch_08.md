# Chapter Eight: The Dog Whisperer

The new dog's name was Nell. The intake form said two years old, mixed breed, *a little anxious in new situations.* It did not say what the anxiety looked like.

Jim saw it when she came through the door — already pressed low, ears back, a medium-sized dog with a black-and-tan coat and long legs that suggested shepherd somewhere in the line. The owner said, "She might just need some time," and Jim said, "We'll let her set the pace," and unclipped the leash.

Nell moved immediately to the far wall.

The other dogs noticed her the way dogs notice anything new — a brief collective attention, a few heads turning — and then most of them went back to what they were doing. Biscuit trotted over and Jim said, quietly, "Leave her," and Biscuit stopped, looked at Jim, and veered off toward Sergeant instead.

Jim sat down on the floor.

Not near Nell. About eight feet away, off to the side, not looking at her directly. Legs crossed, hands in his lap. He breathed.

The room moved around them. Moose doing his slow patrol of the perimeter. Rosie asleep in her corner — the one she'd claimed sometime in October, circled twice before lying down with the deliberate satisfaction of a woman who has been coming to the same restaurant for years and knows exactly which table is hers. Biscuit and Sergeant in some ongoing negotiation near the water station. Nell's breathing was fast and shallow and she kept her back against the wall.

Jim waited.

At ten minutes, she took three steps away from the wall. Stopped. Took two more, angling toward him. Her tail moved once — not a wag, just a single uncertain flag.

He stayed where he was. She crossed the remaining distance on her own terms, which took another four minutes, and then she was standing next to him with her flank against his knee, and he put one hand on her side and felt the last of the trembling there, and it faded.

---

The phone rang at eleven.

"Hi — this is Karen Whitfield, Rosie's mom? I just wanted to check in, she wasn't eating great yesterday and I just—"

"She's good," Jim said. "She's been in her corner since seven-thirty. Ate her morning biscuit, no problem."

A pause. "Her corner?"

"East wall, under the window. She found it her second week. That's hers now."

"She has a corner," Karen Whitfield said.

"She does. She goes there when she wants to watch the room without being in it. Smart dog."

"I didn't know that. I mean — I knew she liked windows." A beat. "I didn't know you'd noticed."

"She's having a good day. I'll have her ready at four."

After he hung up he stood there a moment with the phone in his hand.

By three o'clock, Nell was lying three feet from Clover — close enough to constitute companionship. When Clover shifted in her sleep, Nell's tail moved twice against the floor.

---

He got home at six-fifteen. Gracie met him at the door before he had the key out, the back half of her already going. Cassie came around the corner from the kitchen at a more dignified pace, arrived at his knee, and stood there while he scratched behind her ears.

Andrea was at the counter. She looked up and said, "There's soup."

"Good," he said.

He fed the dogs first. Cassie's bowl on the left, Gracie's on the right, because Gracie would eat from Cassie's bowl if given the opportunity and Cassie would allow it with an expression of profound martyrdom and then be hungry.

While they ate he sat at the kitchen table. Gracie finished her bowl and put her chin on his knee. Cassie finished hers and walked past them both without acknowledgment, went to her spot on the rug in the living room, turned in a circle, and lay down. She was done with the day.

Jim watched her do this and something in him settled.

Andrea said, "How was Nell?"

"She had a good afternoon. Found a dog she liked."

"Which one?"

"Clover."

Andrea smiled. "Clover's good with the nervous ones."

After dinner, Jim sat in the living room with Gracie pressed against his leg and Cassie on the rug, one eye open, confirming that nothing of interest was happening, then closed again.

The house was quiet in the way houses are quiet when everyone in them is accounted for.

He'd need to be there at six-thirty tomorrow. Three dogs before seven, including Nell for her second day. Second days were often harder — the dog had processed overnight and arrived at conclusions that weren't always useful. He'd need to read her when she came through the door.

Gracie's tail moved once against the cushion without her waking. Jim sat with his hand on her back. Nell on her second step away from the wall, the uncertain flag of her tail. Clover sleeping three feet from her at three o'clock. Rosie in her corner, the same corner, every Tuesday and Thursday, because she'd found it and it was hers and someone had noticed.

He reached over and turned off the lamp.
