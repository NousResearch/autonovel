# Chapter Eight: The Dog Whisperer

The new dog arrived at nine-fifteen.

Her name was Nell. The intake form said two years old, mixed breed, "a little anxious in new situations." It did not say what the anxiety looked like or what made it worse.

Jim saw it immediately. She came through the door already pressed low, ears back —  A medium-sized dog with long legs and a black-and-tan coat that suggested shepherd somewhere back in the line. 

"She's a little nervous. She might just need some time."

"We'll let her set the pace," Jim said.

He unclipped the leash. Nell moved immediately to the far wall.

The other dogs noticed her the way dogs notice anything new — a brief collective attention, a few heads turning — and then most of them went back to what they were doing. Biscuit trotted over and Jim said, quietly, "Leave her," and Biscuit stopped, looked at Jim, and veered off toward Sergeant instead.

Jim sat down on the floor.

Not near Nell. About eight feet away, off to the side, not looking at her directly. Legs crossed, hands in his lap. He breathed.

Nell watched him.

The room moved around them. Moose doing his slow patrol of the perimeter. Rosie asleep in her corner — the one she'd claimed sometime in October, circled twice before lying down with the deliberate satisfaction of a woman who has been coming to the same restaurant for years and knows exactly which table is hers. Biscuit and Sergeant engaged in some ongoing negotiation near the water station. Nell watched all of it and her breathing was fast and shallow and she kept her back against the wall.

Jim waited.

Five minutes. Eight minutes. The fast breathing slowed a degree.

At ten minutes, Nell took three steps away from the wall. She stopped. Assessed. Took two more steps, angling toward Jim, and stopped again. Her tail moved once — not a wag, just a single uncertain flag.

Jim stayed where he was. He let her cross the distance on her own terms, which took another four minutes, and then she was standing next to him, close enough that her flank was against his knee, and he put one hand on her side and felt the residual trembling there, the last of it, and it faded.

 

---

The phone rang at eleven. Jim was in the back, checking water levels, 

"Hi — this is Karen Whitfield, Rosie's mom? I just wanted to check in, she wasn't eating great yesterday and I just—"

"She's good," Jim said. "She's been in her corner since about seven-thirty. Ate her morning biscuit, no problem."

A pause. "Her corner?"

"East wall, under the window. She found it her second week. That's been hers ever since."

Another pause, longer.

"She has a corner," Karen Whitfield said.

"She does. She goes there when she wants to watch the room without being in it. Smart dog."

"I didn't know that. I mean — I knew she liked windows. But I didn't know you'd noticed."

"She's having a good day," Jim said. "I'll have her ready at four."

After he hung up he stood there a moment with the phone in his hand. 

By three o'clock the morning energy had settled. Moose in his corner. Rosie unmoved since morning. Nell — and this was the thing worth noting — lying three feet from Clover, close enough to constitute companionship, and when Clover shifted in her sleep Nell's tail moved twice against the floor.

---

He got home at six-fifteen.

Gracie met him at the door before he had the key out. She'd heard his car — always heard his car — and by the time he stepped inside she was there, the back half of her in motion. He put his hand on her head and she pushed into it.

Cassie came around the corner from the kitchen at a more dignified pace. She arrived at Jim's knee and stood there, and he scratched behind her ears, and she closed her eyes.

Andrea was at the counter working on something for tomorrow. She looked up when he came in and said, "There's soup."

"Good," he said.

He fed the dogs first. Cassie's bowl on the left, Gracie's on the right — same configuration every night, because Gracie would eat from Cassie's bowl if given the opportunity and Cassie would let her do it with an expression of profound martyrdom and then be hungry. He'd learned this in the first week they'd had both of them.

While they ate he sat at the kitchen table. The day moved through him the way a long day does — not in sequence, just as texture. Nell's trembling settling under his hand. Karen Whitfield's voice when she understood that her dog had a corner.

Gracie finished her bowl and came to sit next to his chair. She put her chin on his knee.

Cassie finished her bowl and walked past them both without acknowledgment and went to her spot on the rug in the living room, turned in a circle, and lay down. She was done with the day.

Jim watched her do this and something in him settled.

The soup was good. They talked about tomorrow — a new boarding client, a family coming in for a tour, a question about the outdoor play area that needed a decision before the weather turned. Andy's homework situation, which was ongoing. The supply order.

Andrea said, "How was Nell?"

"She had a good afternoon. Found a dog she liked."

"Which one?"

"Clover."

Andrea smiled. "Clover's good with the nervous ones."

"She is." 

After dinner Jim sat in the living room with Gracie pressed against his leg and Cassie on the rug, apparently asleep, though she opened one eye when he shifted position, confirmed that nothing of interest was happening, and closed it again.

The house was quiet in the way houses are quiet when everyone in them is accounted for.

He'd need to be there at six-thirty tomorrow. Three dogs before seven, including Nell for her second day. Second days were often harder than first — the dog had processed overnight and arrived at conclusions that weren't always useful. He'd need to read her when she came through the door.

The lamp on the side table put a warm light across the room. Gracie's tail moved once against the couch cushion without her waking. Jim sat with his hand on her back and felt the weight of the day —  The floor he'd cleaned. The dog he'd waited for. The woman on the phone who hadn't known her dog had a corner.

He'd known. That was the job. 

Nell on her second step away from the wall, the uncertain flag of her tail. Clover sleeping three feet from her at three o'clock. Rosie in her corner under the window, the same corner, every Tuesday and Thursday, because she'd found it and it was hers and someone had noticed.

Cassie opened both eyes, looked at him, and closed them again.

He reached over and turned off the lamp.
