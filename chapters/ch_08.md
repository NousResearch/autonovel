# Chapter Eight: The Dog Whisperer

The new dog's name was Nell. The intake form said two years old, mixed breed, *a little anxious in new situations.* It did not say what the anxiety looked like.

Jim saw it when she came through the door — already pressed low, ears back, a medium-sized dog with a black-and-tan coat and long legs that suggested shepherd somewhere in the line. The owner, a man in his forties named Paul, said, "She might just need some time," and Jim said, "We'll let her set the pace," and unclipped the leash.

Nell moved immediately to the far wall.

The other dogs noticed her the way dogs notice anything new — a brief collective attention, a few heads turning — and then most of them went back to what they were doing. Biscuit trotted over and Jim said, quietly, "Leave her," and Biscuit stopped, looked at Jim, and veered off toward Sergeant instead.

Jim sat down on the floor.

Not near Nell. Off to the side, not looking at her directly. He breathed.

The room moved around them. Moose doing his slow patrol of the perimeter. Rosie asleep in her corner — the one she'd claimed sometime in October, circled twice before lying down with the deliberate satisfaction of a woman who has been coming to the same restaurant for years and knows exactly which table is hers. Biscuit and Sergeant in some ongoing negotiation near the water station. Nell's breathing was fast and shallow and she kept her back against the wall.

Jim waited.

At ten minutes, she took three steps away from the wall. Stopped. Took two more, angling toward him. Her tail moved once — a single uncertain flag.

He stayed where he was. She crossed the remaining distance on her own terms, which took another four minutes, and then she was standing next to him with her flank against his knee. He put one hand on her side. The trembling faded.

Then a truck on Fayette Street — air brakes, the hard exhale of something heavy stopping — and Nell was two steps back, haunches dropped, ears pinned flat. Not fleeing. Frozen.

Jim didn't move. Didn't say anything. His hand was still out to the side, where it had been, not reaching.

The truck pulled away. The sound changed, diminished, was gone.

Nell stood where she'd gone. Eight seconds, ten. She looked at Jim's hand. She came back.

He let her settle against his knee again and this time kept his hand still on her side for a long moment before he stood. She watched him stand. She didn't retreat.

Paul had been watching from the doorway. "Does she—" he started.

"She's going to be fine," Jim said. "Give her the morning."

---

The phone rang at eleven.

"Hi — this is Karen Whitfield, Rosie's mom? I just wanted to check in, she wasn't eating great yesterday and I just—"

"She's good," Jim said. "She's been in her corner since seven-thirty. Ate her morning biscuit, no problem."

A pause. "Her corner?"

"East wall, under the window. She found it her second week. That's hers now."

"She has a corner," Karen Whitfield said.

"She does. Goes there when she wants to watch the room without being in it. Smart dog."

"I didn't know that. I mean — I knew she liked windows." A beat. "I didn't know you'd noticed."

Jim looked across the floor to where Rosie was sleeping, the rise and fall of her, unhurried.

"She's been off her food at home," Karen said. "Since Monday. I keep thinking — she's ten, and the vet said her levels are fine, but." She stopped. "I guess I'm worried it means something."

"She ate this morning," Jim said. "Full biscuit. No hesitation." He watched Rosie a moment more. "Ten-year-old dog, she knows what she likes and she'll hold out for it. What are you feeding her at home?"

"The same thing I always have."

"Try warming it up. Thirty seconds. See if that changes anything."

A silence that felt like something releasing. "That's it?"

"Might be. She's not in pain — I'd see that. She's comfortable, she's sleeping well, she's got her corner. She's telling you she's fine."

"I didn't know you'd—" Karen started again, and then didn't finish the sentence. "Okay. Thank you."

After he hung up he stood there a moment with the phone in his hand. What Karen had been afraid of was the thing nobody wanted to say out loud, and she hadn't said it, and he hadn't made her. He'd just told her what was true.

By three o'clock, Nell was lying three feet from Clover — close enough to constitute companionship. When Clover shifted in her sleep, Nell's tail moved twice against the floor.

---

He got home at six-fifteen. Gracie met him at the door before he had the key out. Cassie came around the corner from the kitchen at a more dignified pace, arrived at his knee, and stood there while he scratched behind her ears.

Andrea was at the counter. She looked up and said, "There's soup."

"Good," he said.

He fed the dogs first. Cassie's bowl on the left, Gracie's on the right, because Gracie would eat from Cassie's bowl if given the opportunity and Cassie would allow it with an expression of profound martyrdom and then be hungry.

Gracie finished hers and put her chin on his knee. Cassie finished and walked past them both without acknowledgment, went to her spot on the rug in the living room, turned in a circle, and lay down.

Andrea said, "How was Nell?"

"She had a good afternoon. Found a dog she liked."

"Which one?"

"Clover."

Andrea smiled. "Clover's good with the nervous ones."

"Yeah." He picked up his spoon. "She spooked on a truck brake around ten. Went back two steps."

"But she came back."

"She came back."

Andrea looked at him for a moment. She knew what that meant — not the truck, but the part after. The dog choosing to return. "How long did it take her?"

"Ten seconds. Maybe twelve."

She nodded and went back to her soup.

After dinner he sat in the living room with Gracie pressed against his leg. From the rug, Cassie had one eye open. She confirmed nothing of interest was happening and closed it again.

The house was quiet the way it got when everyone in it was accounted for.

Tomorrow he'd need to be there at six-thirty. Three dogs before seven, including Nell for her second day. He'd need to read her when she came through the door — whether she remembered the room as safe or whether the night had rearranged it into something else. Some dogs came back and walked straight to the place they'd found, as if they'd been saving it. Others arrived like it was the first time again. You couldn't know in advance. You had to watch.

He reached over and turned off the lamp.

Gracie's tail moved once against the cushion without her waking. Jim sat in the dark with his hand on her back, and thought about Nell's tail — the uncertain flag of it, the second time, after the truck.
