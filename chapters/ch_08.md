# Chapter Eight: The Dog Whisperer

The new dog's name was Nell. The intake form said two years old, mixed breed, *a little anxious in new situations.* It did not say what the anxiety looked like.

Jim saw it when she came through the door — already pressed low, ears back, a medium-sized dog with a black-and-tan coat and long legs that suggested shepherd somewhere in the line. The owner, a man in his forties named Paul, had the particular careful posture of someone who loved his dog and had learned to announce her before she could announce herself. "She might just need some time," he said.

"We'll let her set the pace," Jim said, and unclipped the leash.

Nell moved immediately to the far wall.

The other dogs noticed her the way dogs notice anything new — a brief collective attention, a few heads turning — and then most of them went back to what they were doing. Biscuit trotted over with the blunt confidence of a dog who had never once doubted his welcome, and Jim said, quietly, "Leave her," and Biscuit stopped, looked at Jim, and veered off toward Sergeant instead.

Jim sat down on the floor.

Not near Nell. Off to the side, not looking at her directly. He breathed.

The room moved around them. Moose doing his slow patrol of the perimeter. Rosie asleep in her corner — the one she'd claimed sometime in October, circled twice before lying down with the deliberate satisfaction of a woman who has been coming to the same restaurant for years and knows exactly which table is hers. Biscuit and Sergeant in some ongoing negotiation near the water station. Nell's breathing was fast and shallow and she kept her back against the wall, and Jim sat where he was and let her measure the distance between them without any help from him.

Paul said, from the doorway, "Should I—"

"You can head out," Jim said. "It's easier."

Paul looked at Nell. Nell was not looking at Paul. She was looking at the floor in front of Jim's left knee.

"Okay," Paul said. He left.

At ten minutes, Nell took three steps away from the wall. Stopped. Her tail moved once — a single uncertain flag.

Jim stayed where he was. She crossed the remaining distance on her own terms, which took another four minutes, and then she was standing next to him with her flank against his knee. He put one hand on her side. The trembling faded.

Then a truck on Fayette Street — air brakes, the hard exhale of something heavy stopping — and Nell was two steps back, haunches dropped, ears pinned flat.

Jim didn't move. His hand stayed where it was, out to the side. Not reaching.

The truck pulled away. The sound changed, diminished, was gone.

Nell stood in the place she'd gone. Eight seconds. Ten. She looked at Jim's hand. She took one step toward him, then stopped, and her head dropped half an inch, the nose working, reading him for something she needed confirmed. Jim sat with his hand still and his breathing even and let her read.

She came back.

He kept his hand on her side for a long moment before he stood. She watched him stand. She didn't retreat.

Paul had come back to the doorway — he'd made it as far as the parking lot, apparently, which was as far as most of them made it. "Does she—" he started.

"She's going to be fine," Jim said. "Give her the morning."

Paul looked at his dog, who was now standing next to Jim with her shoulder against his knee, watching the room. His jaw worked once, like he was going to say something, and then he just nodded. He left again.

---

The phone rang at eleven.

"Hi — this is Karen Whitfield, Rosie's mom? I just wanted to check in, she wasn't eating great yesterday and I just—"

"She's good," Jim said. "She's been in her corner since seven-thirty. Ate her morning biscuit, no problem."

A pause. "Her corner?"

"East wall, under the window. She found it her second week. That's hers now."

"She has a corner," Karen Whitfield said.

"She does. Goes there when she wants to watch the room without being in it. Smart dog."

"I didn't know that. I mean — I knew she liked windows." A beat. "I didn't know you'd noticed."

Jim looked across the floor to where Rosie was sleeping, the rise and fall of her, unhurried.

"She's been off her food at home," Karen said. "Since Monday. I keep thinking — she's ten, and the vet said her levels are fine, but." She stopped. "I guess I'm worried it means something."

"She ate this morning," Jim said. "Full biscuit. No hesitation." He watched Rosie a moment more. "Ten-year-old dog, she knows what she likes and she'll hold out for it. What are you feeding her at home?"

"The same thing I always have."

"Try warming it up. Thirty seconds. See if that changes anything."

A silence that felt like something releasing. "That's it?"

"Might be. She's not in pain — I'd see that. She's comfortable, she's sleeping well, she's got her corner. She's telling you she's fine."

A longer pause this time. When Karen spoke again her voice had changed register slightly, dropped out of the careful brightness she'd started with. "She's slowing down. I know that's — I know that's just what happens. But she used to meet me at the door every time and now sometimes she just lifts her head from wherever she is and I—" She stopped. "Sorry. That's not what I called about."

"It's what you called about," Jim said.

Karen didn't say anything.

"She lifted her head when I came in this morning," Jim said. "Watched me cross the room. She's tracking. She's interested. That's different from what you're afraid of."

The silence had a different quality now.

"Okay," Karen said finally. Then: "Can I ask you something? When she stops tracking — when that changes — will you tell me? Before it gets to — I mean, I'd rather know early. I'd rather have more time with her."

"Yes," Jim said.

"You'll know before I do, probably. That's — I don't know if that's a comfort or not."

"It should be," Jim said. "That's the point."

She was quiet for a moment. "Okay. Thank you. I mean it."

After he hung up he stood there with the phone in his hand. The floor was doing what it did at mid-morning — dogs settled into their arrangements, the energy lower than it had been at eight, the room finding its rhythm. Nell was lying three feet from Clover, close enough to constitute companionship. When Clover shifted in her sleep, Nell's tail moved twice against the floor.

Jim watched that for a moment. Then he put the phone in his pocket and went to get Moose's midday medication.

---

He got home at six-fifteen. Gracie met him at the door before he had the key out — not barking, just there, the whole back half of her already in motion. Cassie came around the corner from the kitchen at a more dignified pace, arrived at his knee, and stood there while he scratched behind her ears.

Andrea was at the counter. She looked up and said, "There's soup."

"Good," he said.

He fed the dogs first. Cassie's bowl on the left, Gracie's on the right, because Gracie would eat from Cassie's bowl if given the opportunity and Cassie would allow it with an expression of profound martyrdom and then be hungry. He'd established this system in the first week and it had held for four years.

Gracie finished hers and put her chin on his knee. Cassie finished and walked past them both without acknowledgment, went to her spot on the rug in the living room, and lay down.

Andrea said, "How was Nell?"

"She had a good afternoon. Found a dog she liked."

"Which one?"

"Clover."

Andrea smiled. "Clover's good with the nervous ones."

"Yeah." He picked up his spoon. "She spooked on a truck brake around ten. Air brakes on Fayette. Went back two steps."

"But she came back."

"She came back. Took about ten seconds." He ate for a moment. "She had to think about it."

Andrea looked at him. She'd been watching dogs with him for long enough to know what that meant — the ten seconds, the thinking. The dog choosing. "Paul seemed nervous at drop-off," she said.

"He came back. Made it to the parking lot."

"They always make it to the parking lot."

"Yeah."

She went back to her soup, and the kitchen was quiet except for Gracie's breathing against his leg and the small sounds of the house settling into evening. Jim sat with the weight of Gracie's chin on his knee and thought about the ten seconds after the truck. The nose working. The thing Nell had needed to confirm before she came back.

You could not rush it. You could not perform your way to it. Either you were the thing the dog needed to read, or you weren't, and the dog would tell you which.

"Karen Whitfield called," he said.

"Rosie's mom. How's Rosie?"

"She's fine. Karen's worried about her slowing down."

Andrea set her spoon down. "She's ten."

"Yeah."

"Is she slowing down?"

"Some. She lifted her head when I came in this morning. Tracked me across the room. She's still there." He looked at his bowl. "Karen just needed someone to tell her the difference between slowing down and something else."

Andrea was quiet for a moment. "Could you?"

"Today I could."

She picked her spoon back up. That was the whole conversation, and it was enough.

After dinner Jim sat in the living room with Gracie pressed against his leg. The house had the particular quiet of a place where everyone in it was accounted for.

Tomorrow he'd need to be there at six-thirty. Three dogs before seven, including Nell for her second day. He'd need to read her when she came through the door — whether she remembered the room as safe or whether the night had rearranged it into something else. Some dogs came back and walked straight to the place they'd found, as if they'd been holding it in mind all night. Others arrived like it was the first time again. You couldn't know in advance. You had to watch.

He reached over and turned off the lamp.

Gracie's tail moved once against the cushion without her waking. Jim sat in the dark with his hand on her back, and thought about Nell's tail — the uncertain flag of it, the second time, after the truck.
