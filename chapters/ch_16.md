# Chapter Sixteen: What Thirty Days Buys

The facility smelled like nothing.

That was the thing Jim kept coming back to, standing in the doorway of the main room on a Tuesday morning in the third week of April. The daycare floor, which on a normal Tuesday smelled like dog and effort and the particular warm-animal smell that had taken him six months to stop noticing, smelled like the cleaning solution he'd used on Friday and nothing else. Bleach-adjacent. Neutral. The smell of a room that was waiting.

He went in anyway.

The folding table he'd set up near the supply closet had his laptop, a legal pad, and the mug of coffee he'd brought from home.  April 22. Eight days in. Twenty-two days remaining on the extension, if you counted from the meeting and not from when the order had actually come down, 

He wrote the date at the top of the legal pad and drew a line under it.

The legal pad had four pages of notes already. Phone numbers, names, the specific questions each family had asked when he'd talked to them. He'd called twelve families in the first week.  The ones he knew best. The ones whose dogs had been coming five days a week, the ones whose situations he understood well enough to have a real conversation.

He looked at the notes from those calls and wrote a new heading on the legal pad: *What we know.*

---

He'd called the Hendersons first, because their dog — a four-year-old lab mix named Biscuit, which was a terrible name for a dog that size but was nonetheless what they called her — had been one of the first dogs through the door when A Pup's Valley opened, and because Jim had known even before he dialed that they would be honest with him. They were that kind of family.

Biscuit was struggling, Mrs. Henderson said. Not in a medical way. In a *she knows something is wrong* way. She'd been waking them up at five in the morning, which she'd never done before, and she'd taken to standing at the front door in the afternoons at the time she used to leave for daycare.

"She's standing there waiting," Mrs. Henderson said. "Like she thinks if she waits long enough it'll go back to normal."

Jim wrote: *Biscuit — door behavior, 4-5pm. Schedule disruption, not medical.*

The Garcias' dog had stopped eating reliably. The Okafor family's corgi had developed a barking problem, which the Okafors found baffling because Wendell had never been a barker. 

By the end of the second call, Jim had stopped feeling like he was conducting welfare checks and started feeling like he was building something. A picture. 

The hardest call had been to the Pattersons, whose golden, a seven-year-old named Archie, had been coming to A Pup's Valley three days a week since the beginning. Jim had watched Archie go from a dog who needed a thirty-minute settling period every single morning to a dog who came in the door and went directly to find his friends, tail already going, completely confident.  And now Archie was at home with Mr. Patterson, who worked from home anyway and was genuinely glad to have the company, but who had also called Jim because Archie had started pacing in the evenings and he didn't know what to do about it.

"He's bored," Jim said. "He's not bored of you. He's bored because the social piece is gone. He needs other dogs."

"I know," Mr. Patterson said. "That's why I'm calling."

Jim wrote: *Archie — pacing, evenings. Social deprivation. Not solvable at home.*

---

Andrea was at the table when he got home that afternoon, her own laptop open, a glass of water she hadn't touched going warm beside her. Gracie was on the floor under the table with her chin on Andrea's foot. Cassie was on the couch, which she was technically not supposed to be on, watching Jim come in the door with the expression she used when she was testing whether today was a day anyone was going to enforce the couch rule.

Today was not that day.

"How many?" Andrea said.

"Twelve total. I've got notes on all of them." He put the legal pad on the table and sat down. "The dogs are struggling."

"I know. I've been getting texts." She turned the laptop to show him. "Three families in the last two days. Their dogs are — it's like they don't know what to do with themselves."

"Because they don't."

She looked at the legal pad. He watched her read it, the way she read things — quickly, going back to certain lines, her mouth moving slightly on the ones she was working through. Gracie shifted under the table and resettled.

"The Okafors' corgi is barking," Jim said.

"Wendell. Yeah, she mentioned that." Andrea set the laptop down. "He needs a job."

"He needs other dogs and a schedule."

"Same thing."

They sat with that for a moment. Outside, a car passed on the street. Cassie rearranged herself on the couch, circling twice before she lay back down, and the couch made a small sound of protest under her weight.

"I've been thinking," Andrea said.

Jim waited.

"We can't do daycare. We can't do boarding. We can't bring the dogs in." She paused, and he could see her working through it, the same way she'd worked through the tour pitch back in the beginning, testing each piece before she said it. "But we can still talk to them. The families. We can do consultations. Virtual check-ins. Someone calls because their dog is pacing and they don't know why — we can answer that call. We can help them understand what's happening and give them something to actually do about it."

Jim looked at the legal pad. Biscuit at the door at five in the afternoon. Wendell barking at nothing. Archie pacing.

"It's not daycare," she said. "I know that. It's not what we built. But it keeps the relationship alive. It keeps them connected to us instead of — instead of going somewhere else. Instead of just waiting and not hearing anything."

"It keeps us connected to them," Jim said.

"Yes."

He picked up his pen. "What would we charge?"

"Nothing. Not right now."

"We'd have to be careful about scope," he said. "We're not veterinarians."

"We know that. They know that. We're not diagnosing anything. We're telling them what we'd tell them if they called us during a normal week and said their dog was acting weird." She paused. "Which they do. All the time. And we answer."

"Yeah." He looked at what he'd written. "Okay. Let's try it."

She pulled the laptop back toward her and started typing. He watched her for a moment — the speed of it, the way she moved through the task once the decision was made — and then he looked down at the legal pad and started building a list of who to call next.

Cassie, from the couch, watched both of them with the patient attention of a dog who was comfortable and had nowhere to be.

---

The thirty days ended on a Tuesday.

Jim was at the facility again — he'd been going in most days, 

He sat at the folding table and looked at the legal pad, which now had eleven pages of notes. Twelve families in the first week, then nineteen more in the second and third, then a different kind of outreach — the virtual check-ins Andrea had proposed, which had started with a call to the Garcias about their dog's appetite and had grown into something that took up most of her afternoons. She had a system now. A spreadsheet. Families who'd had check-ins, families who were on the list, notes from each call that she typed up and added to the same document she'd been building since they opened.

The dogs, by most accounts, were doing better. Not because their situation had changed — they were still home, still separated from the other dogs, still without the structure of the daycare week. But the families had things to try. Wendell the corgi had a new morning routine that involved a forty-minute walk and a training session before Mr. and Mrs. Okafor started work, and the barking had reduced significantly. Biscuit had stopped standing at the front door in the afternoons because the Hendersons had started a five o'clock walk that gave her something to wait for instead.

Jim added a line to the legal pad: *Day 30. Still here.*

He looked at it for a moment, then turned the page and started a new list.

---

That evening, Andrea was on the phone with the Garcias when Jim got home, so he fed the dogs — Cassie first, then Gracie, because that was the order and the dogs knew the order and any deviation from it produced a specific kind of anxious attention from Cassie that Jim found mildly funny and had never bothered to correct. He filled both bowls and set them down and stepped back, and the dogs ate with the complete focus that dogs bring to eating, heads down, entirely present.

He stood in the kitchen and listened to Andrea in the other room.

"The appetite thing is going to keep fluctuating until his schedule stabilizes," she was saying. "That's normal. What you can do is — yeah, consistent feeding times help more than consistent amounts, actually. Try to feed him at the same time every day, even if he doesn't finish it."

A pause.

"I know. I know it's hard to tell." Another pause, longer. "He's going to be okay. He misses the other dogs. That's a real thing and it's not going to fully resolve until he can see them again. But what you're describing isn't a medical issue. It's a dog who's sad. And sad dogs need routine more than anything."

Jim heard her laugh at something the other person said.

"Yeah. Yeah, exactly. Okay. Call me if anything changes."

She came into the kitchen still holding the phone. Gracie, who had finished eating, went to her immediately and pressed the side of her face against Andrea's hip, which was the thing Gracie did when she wanted acknowledgment without making a production of it. Andrea put her hand on Gracie's head without looking down.

"How many today?" Jim said.

"Four calls. Two new, two follow-ups." She put the phone on the counter. "The Garcia dog is doing better. He's still off his food some days, but it's improving."

"Good."

She looked at him. "The extension ran out."

"I know."

"We're still here."

"Yeah."

Cassie had finished her bowl and was now sitting in the middle of the kitchen floor looking between them with the alert expression she used when she thought something interesting might be about to happen. Nothing interesting was about to happen. She sat there anyway.

"I'm going to make dinner," Andrea said.

"Okay."

She opened the refrigerator. He sat down at the kitchen table and opened the legal pad to the new list he'd started at the facility, the one headed *What comes next*, and looked at what he'd written so far. 

He wrote it down: *Dormant, not gone.*

Behind him, he heard Andrea start the water running, the small sounds of dinner beginning, the ordinary machinery of the evening. Gracie had settled near the back door. 

He wrote: *Call the Pattersons tomorrow. Check on Archie.*

Then he wrote: *List of families for Andrea's check-ins — expand to all sixty.*

Then: *Think about what reopening looks like. What we'd need. What the first week back would look like for the dogs.*

The water ran. Something went into a pan and the kitchen filled briefly with the smell of onion and heat. Cassie's weight was warm against his foot.

He kept writing.
