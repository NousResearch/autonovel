# Chapter Sixteen

The line of light under his grandfather's door was still there in the morning.

Cass noticed it because he woke early, before his father was up, before the smell of coffee had reached the second floor, and he lay in the dark for a moment listening to the house breathe. The workshop sounds had stopped sometime in the night. His father had come up. The house was still.

He got up and went to the hall in his socks and stood outside the old man's door and looked at the light. Yellow, not blue — not a phone or a screen, but a lamp. The particular yellow of the lamp on the nightstand, the one with the yellowed shade his grandfather had never replaced because it threw the right color, he said, for reading.

Cass raised his hand and knocked.

Nothing.

He knocked again, two knuckles, quiet.

"Come in," his grandfather said, and his voice sounded like a man who had been awake for hours.

---

The room smelled like the liniment his grandfather used on his hands, and old paper, and underneath both of those, the faint bronze smell that lived in every room of this house, that had soaked into the walls over sixty years of work being brought home on a man's skin. His grandfather was sitting up in bed with a book open in his lap, but the book was face-down and had been for a while — the spine was cracked at an angle that meant he'd set it down and left it. The lamp threw its yellow circle. His grandfather's hands were folded on top of the book.

"Sit down," he said.

Cass sat in the chair by the window. Outside, the sky was going from black to gray, the particular gray of five in the morning in November, when light arrives apologetically.

His grandfather looked at him for a moment. "You heard it."

Not a question.

"Yeah."

His grandfather nodded once, the slow nod of a man confirming something he already knew. He picked up the book and set it on the nightstand without looking at it. "When?"

"Last night. Dad played it for me."

"Played it for you." He said this in a tone that was not quite neutral. "He played it on the laptop?"

"Through the good speakers. The ones in the workshop."

His grandfather was quiet for a moment. Outside, something — a branch, or a bird landing on the gutter — made a small sound and then stopped. "That's not the same," his grandfather said.

"I know."

"The recording doesn't—" He stopped. Pressed his lips together. "There's something the recording doesn't get. The physical dimension of it. You have to be in the room with a bell that size to understand what it's doing to your chest."

Cass had been in the room with bells that size. He knew what his grandfather meant. The recording had gotten something — the under-note had come through clearly enough, the needle behind his left eye had been real — but his grandfather was right that a recording was a translation, and translations always lost something.

"Is that why you went?" Cass said. "To hear it live?"

His grandfather looked at the lamp. "I went because your father asked me to go with him. He was twenty-six. He'd found the record and he wanted to — he wanted to understand it. He didn't want to go alone."

"But you'd already heard it."

"I'd heard it in 1971." His grandfather's hands moved, a small adjustment, fingers interlacing and releasing. "I hadn't heard it in thirty years. I thought maybe I'd been wrong about what I remembered."

"Were you?"

"No."

Cass sat with that. The gray outside the window was getting lighter, or maybe his eyes were adjusting — it was hard to tell, at five in the morning, which was happening.

"What does it do to you?" he said. "After. Does it go away?"

His grandfather turned to look at him directly for the first time since he'd come in. His face in the lamplight was the face of a man in his late seventies, the lines deep, the eyes still sharp in the way that sometimes made Cass feel like he was being read rather than looked at. "What did it do to you?"

Cass thought about how to answer this honestly. The needle behind his eye had faded by the time he'd reached the staircase, but the under-note itself — the ghost frequency, the resolution that didn't resolve — had not faded. It was there when he fell asleep. It was there when he woke up. It was not pain exactly. It was more like the feeling of a word you can't remember, except the word was a sound, and the sound was somewhere in the range where the body felt it before the ear did.

"It's like a splinter," he said. "But in the — I don't know. Behind the ear. Not the ear, but—"

"Yes," his grandfather said. "That's exactly right." He said it with a kind of relief, like a man who had been carrying a description for fifty years and was glad to hear someone else arrive at it independently. "It goes away. Mostly. A few days."

"Has it ever not gone away?"

His grandfather was quiet long enough that Cass thought he wasn't going to answer. Then: "There was a period, after the first time I heard it, when I kept hearing it in other things. In the wrong places. I'd be working on an ordinary bell — a church bell, nothing unusual — and I'd think I heard the under-note starting, and I'd stop, and it wouldn't be there. It was just—" He paused. "It was just my ear looking for it."

"How long did that last?"

"A few months." His grandfather picked up the book again, looked at the cover, set it back down. "Your grandmother thought I was losing my mind. I didn't tell her what it was. I don't know if I could have explained it."

Cass looked at his grandfather's hands on the book. Thick through the knuckles, the skin loose now in the way of old hands, but the tendons still visible when he moved them — still the hands of a man who had worked.

"Why didn't you want Dad to hear it?" he said.

The question came out more directly than he'd intended. His grandfather's expression shifted, something moving through it quickly, and Cass thought for a moment he'd said the wrong thing. But his grandfather didn't look away.

"I didn't say I didn't want him to hear it."

"You said last night—"

"I said I wasn't sure it was something to seek out." His grandfather's voice was careful, the way it got when he was making a distinction that mattered to him. "That's different. I wasn't sure it was something to seek out for myself, either, the first time. But I went. And your father went. And here you are." He looked at Cass steadily. "The three of us have heard it now. What do you do with that?"

Cass didn't have an answer. He hadn't thought of it as a thing to *do* anything with. He'd thought of it as something that had happened, something he was now carrying, but the question of what carrying it was *for* hadn't occurred to him.

"I don't know," he said.

"Good," his grandfather said, which was not the response Cass had expected. "Don't decide yet. You just heard it. Give it time to settle."

"Did you decide what to do with it?"

His grandfather smiled, and it was a real smile, not the polite one he used with strangers or the sharp one he used when he caught Cass making a mistake at the bench. This one went all the way up. "I made bells," he said. "I'd been making bells before I heard it. I kept making bells after. Maybe I listened differently. Maybe I was trying to—" He stopped. "I don't know. Maybe I was trying to answer it."

"Answer it."

"A piece of music asks a question," his grandfather said. "You know this. Every piece of music asks a question, even if it answers it at the end. Most of them answer it. This one doesn't. So." He spread his hands briefly, palms up. "So maybe you spend your life trying to answer it yourself. Or maybe you just live with the question. Both are valid."

Cass looked at his grandfather's hands, still open on the book. The liniment smell was stronger than the bronze smell in this corner of the room.

"Does it bother you?" Cass said. "That it doesn't resolve?"

"Does it bother you that middle C isn't the same frequency on every instrument?"

"That's not—" Cass stopped. Started again. "That's a different thing."

"Is it?"

It wasn't, entirely, and Cass knew it, and his grandfather knew he knew it, and neither of them said anything for a moment. Outside, the gray had shifted to something lighter, a pale wash that was almost silver, the sky doing its slow work.

"Dad didn't say much," Cass said. "After. He just went back to the bell."

"That sounds right."

"He didn't explain it or — he just turned back to the bench."

"What would you have wanted him to say?"

Cass thought about the workshop. The smell of compound and the lamp throwing its circle and his father's back. The sound of the tool moving along the repair line. He'd wanted something, standing in the doorway before he came upstairs. He'd wanted the room to hold him a little longer, but that wasn't the same as wanting his father to speak.

"I don't know," he said. "Nothing, I think. It was fine."

His grandfather nodded. "He's not good at that part," he said, with no particular judgment in it. "The explaining part. He understands it. He just—" He stopped. "He'd rather show you than tell you. He always has been."

"Is that why he played it for me instead of—"

"Yes," his grandfather said, before Cass could finish. "That's exactly why."

Cass sat back in the chair. The first real light was starting to come through the window now, the sky going from silver to a pale cold blue, and the shapes of the trees in the yard were becoming distinct — he could see the bare branches of the oak, the way they divided and divided against the sky, each branch ending in a cluster of smaller ones, the whole thing a kind of frozen argument.

"Granddad," he said.

"Mm."

"When you heard it. The first time. Were you scared?"

His grandfather was quiet for a long moment, long enough that the light outside changed again, a slow brightening. Then he said: "I was twenty-three years old and I had just started working for a man named Forster who had been making bells for forty years, and I thought I understood what a bell was. I thought I understood what sound was. And then I heard that piece and I realized I didn't understand anything." He paused. "So yes. I was scared. But not of the music. Of what the music meant about how much I didn't know."

"And now?"

"Now I know how much I don't know," his grandfather said. "Which is different. You can't be scared of a thing you've made peace with."

Downstairs, Cass heard the particular sound of the kitchen — a cabinet, then the particular sound of his father filling the kettle, the specific pitch of the water hitting the metal bottom, a sound he had been hearing his whole life and could identify from three rooms away. His father was up.

His grandfather heard it too. Something in his face shifted — not softened exactly, but adjusted, the way a room adjusts when someone opens a window.

"Go down," his grandfather said. "Have breakfast with your father."

Cass stood. He was almost to the door when his grandfather said: "Cass."

He turned.

"You asked if it goes away." His grandfather was looking at the lamp, not at him. "The short answer is yes. The true answer is that it changes. It stops being a splinter and starts being—" He seemed to search for it. "Something you know how to work around. The way you work around a crack in a bell. You know where it is. You account for it. You don't pretend it isn't there."

Cass stood in the doorway for a moment. He thought about the bell in the vise downstairs, the repair line along its side, the old wound and the new work both visible under the lamp.

"Okay," he said.

"Go eat," his grandfather said.

---

His father was standing at the kitchen window with a mug in both hands, looking out at the yard. He didn't turn when Cass came in, but his posture changed slightly — a small adjustment of the shoulders that meant he knew Cass was there.

Cass got down a mug. The kettle was still warm. He made tea, the same kind his father drank, the plain black kind that had been in the same tin in this kitchen for as long as he could remember.

He stood next to his father at the window.

The yard was gray and still. The oak tree from his bedroom window was visible from here too, the bare branches against the pale sky. There was frost on the grass, the particular white of early morning frost, each blade coated individually, the whole yard looking like it had been carefully done.

His father drank his tea.

Cass drank his.

After a while his father said: "How'd you sleep?"

"Okay," Cass said. "I went in to see Granddad."

His father turned to look at him, a quick look, then back to the window.

"He was up," Cass said. "The light was on."

"It usually is," his father said. "He doesn't sleep much anymore."

Cass looked at the frost on the grass. The sun was coming up somewhere behind the clouds — you couldn't see it directly, but the sky was getting brighter in the east, the pale blue going warmer at the horizon. "He said it goes away. The — the thing I'm hearing."

His father was quiet for a moment. "It does."

"He said it took him a few months."

"It took me about three weeks." His father looked into his mug. "Everybody's different."

Cass thought about the under-note sitting behind his ear, the splinter that wasn't quite a splinter. Three weeks. He could manage three weeks.

"Dad," he said.

"Yeah."

"Why did you want me to hear it?"

His father was quiet for long enough that Cass thought it might have been the wrong question. Then his father set his mug on the windowsill — just set it there, a deliberate movement, the kind that meant he was doing something with his hands so he could think — and said: "Because I wanted you to know what I knew. What he knew." He paused. "It felt — wrong, somehow, that you didn't know. Like I was keeping something from you that was yours."

Cass looked at the side of his father's face. The gray at his temples. The particular lines around his eyes. He thought about his grandfather at twenty-three, scared of how much he didn't know. His father at twenty-six, not wanting to hear it alone. And now Cass at fourteen, standing at a kitchen window with tea going cold in his hands.

"It's a weird thing to inherit," he said.

His father made a sound that was almost a laugh. Not quite. "Yeah," he said. "It is."

The frost on the grass was starting to melt at the edges, the blades nearest the house going dark and wet while the ones farther out still held their white. The sun was doing its work even without showing itself.

"Granddad said maybe he spent his life trying to answer it," Cass said. "The question the piece asks."

His father picked up his mug again. "He told me the same thing. Years ago."

"Do you think that's true? For him?"

His father considered this seriously, the way he considered things that deserved it. "I think he made bells that were better than they needed to be," he said finally. "Whether that was an answer or just — the way he was — I don't know. Maybe it doesn't matter which."

Cass thought about the bell in the vise. The repair line. The work continuing after midnight.

"What about you?" he said. "Are you trying to answer it?"

His father was quiet for a moment. Then he said, unexpectedly: "I don't think I'm smart enough for that."

Cass looked at him. His father was looking at the yard, at the frost and the bare tree and the pale sky, his face perfectly serious.

"That's not—" Cass started.

"I mean it," his father said. "Not — I'm not being modest. I just think some people hear that piece and it opens something in them and they spend their whole life going through the opening. Your grandfather was like that. I'm not. I heard it and I thought: I need to understand the mechanics of this. How does a bell make that sound. What's the physics of it." He paused. "So I came home and I worked it out. And then I went back to the bench."

"That's not less," Cass said.

His father looked at him then, a real look, the kind that lasted. "No," he said, after a moment. "I suppose it isn't."

They stood at the window until the tea was gone and the frost was mostly melted, the yard going from white to green-gray in the morning light. At some point Cass heard his grandfather's footsteps in the hall above them, slow and deliberate, and then the sound of the bathroom door, and then the footsteps again, and then quiet.

"He's going back to sleep," his father said.

"He said he doesn't sleep much."

"He sleeps in the mornings sometimes. After he's been up all night." His father picked up both mugs and took them to the sink. "You should eat something."

"Yeah."

His father opened the refrigerator and stood looking into it the way he always did, as if the contents might have changed since last night, the cold light on his face. He said, without turning around: "For what it's worth. The three weeks. It's not bad. It's just — present. You'll get used to it being present."

Cass sat down at the kitchen table. The table that had been in this kitchen for thirty years, the wood worn smooth at the edges, the grain still visible in the middle. His grandfather had built it. Cass had done homework at it every visit since he was six years old.

"Granddad built this table," he said.

His father looked over his shoulder. "Yeah."

"Did he build it before or after he heard the piece?"

His father thought about it. "After," he said. "A few years after. Why?"

Cass ran his hand along the grain of the wood. Smooth and warm under his palm, the way wood got when it had been used for a long time by people who touched it.

"Just wondering," he said.

His father turned back to the refrigerator. Outside, a bird landed on the oak tree and the branch moved slightly under its weight, a small perturbation, and then went still. The bird sat there. Cass watched it through the window — small, dark, a shape against the pale sky — and it sat for a moment and then was gone, and the branch moved again and went still again, and you couldn't tell, looking at it, that anything had landed there at all.
