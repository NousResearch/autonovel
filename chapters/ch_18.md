# Chapter Eighteen: Dark Night of the Soul

The morning came in gray, the way November mornings did in Haymarket — not dramatic, not storming, just the particular low light of a sky that had decided not to commit. Jim was at the kitchen table before Andrea came down. He had made coffee. He had not made a list.

Cassie was under the table. Gracie was on the rug by the back door, doing what Gracie did in the mornings, which was to lie very still and watch the yard as though something might happen in it, and when nothing did, to accept this and go back to sleep. The yard was empty. The light was gray. Gracie's eyes closed.

Jim had the mug in his hands. He was looking at the window.

Andrea came down in her gray sweatshirt, the one with the fraying cuffs she kept meaning to throw away. She poured coffee. She sat across from him. She did not say *good morning* and he did not say it either, not from any coldness but because they had been married long enough to know the difference between a silence that needed filling and one that didn't.

The papers from last night were still on the table. Neither of them touched them.

Cassie shifted under the table, her head finding Jim's foot, and settled there.

Outside, a car passed on the street. Then quiet. The refrigerator ran and stopped. Gracie made a small sound in her sleep, some dream-thing, and her paws moved briefly, and then she was still.

"You want eggs?" Andrea said.

"Sure."

She got up. He watched her move around the kitchen, the familiar economy of it, the way she knew where everything was without looking. She cracked eggs into the pan. The smell of butter. The small sounds of it. Jim turned his mug in his hands.

"Cassie slept on the bed again," Andrea said.

"I know."

"You didn't wake me."

"She was already there when I came up."

Andrea looked at him over her shoulder. "You were up late."

"Not that late."

She turned back to the stove. He watched the back of her head, the way her hair was still slightly flattened from sleep on the left side. He thought about saying something and didn't, because there was nothing specific to say — nothing that wasn't already in the room with them, sitting as plainly as the papers on the table.

She put the eggs in front of him. She sat back down with her own plate. They ate.

"Gracie's going to want a walk," Andrea said, after a while.

"I'll take her."

"You don't have to."

"I know."

They ate. Gracie heard her name and lifted her head and looked at them and, when no walk materialized immediately, put her head back down. The light through the window had gotten a little brighter, not much. The sky was still undecided.

---

Jim left the house at half past nine. He did not take Gracie. He put on his jacket and walked out the door and turned right on Fayette Street, heading toward Washington Street, and he did not have a destination. He was just walking.

The cold was the particular cold of a November morning that hasn't fully arrived yet — enough to need the jacket, not enough to be uncomfortable. His breath made small clouds and disappeared. The brick sidewalk on Washington Street was wet from the night, darker than usual, and his footsteps on it had a slightly different sound, more solid, less echo.

He passed the lamp posts. He passed the old buildings with their facades that someone had spent decades fighting to preserve. He walked slowly, not the way he walked when he was going somewhere, but the way you walk when you're letting a place tell you something.

He had chosen this town. That was the thing he kept coming back to, not as a comfort exactly, but as a fact that had weight. He had looked at Haymarket and at the county around it and at the intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 — the old roads, the working roads, the roads that told you what a place actually was before anyone had a chance to dress it up — and he had decided this was where. Not Gainesville with its subdivisions and its chain restaurants. Not the county in its generic sprawl. Here, where the sidewalks were brick and the buildings had been standing long enough to have opinions.

He stopped at the intersection.

Route 15 went north into Maryland, south toward Culpeper. Route 55 went east toward Gainesville and the interstate, west toward the mountains. He stood on the corner and looked at the McDonald's drive-through, which had backed traffic onto the road again, a line of cars inching forward, everyone patient in the way people were patient when they had decided they wanted something and were willing to wait for it.

He turned and looked the other way. The Council chambers were down there, a hundred yards, the building where he had stood at the microphone on a September evening and said *I fully support the hotel* because someone needed to say it and he was someone. The building where Andrea had stood in April, in front of Mayor Leake and the rest of them, and said what had happened to the business since March.

He kept walking.

He passed Dottie's office. The lights were off — it was not quite business hours, or maybe she was out, or maybe she was at the hospital. He did not know, this particular morning, where Dottie was. He knew she was somewhere in the machinery of the town, the way she always was, the way she had been for fifty years. He looked at the dark window of her office and thought: she didn't know either. When she was in the middle of the Disney fight, she didn't know if it was going to hold. She just kept going.

Andrea had told him that. *She said she didn't know if the Disney fight was going to hold, while she was in the middle of it. She just kept going.*

He kept walking.

The town was quiet at this hour in the way small towns were quiet when the work hadn't started yet — not empty, but gathered. A woman came out of the hardware store and nodded at him. He nodded back. He passed the post office. He turned and walked back toward the Council chambers and then past them, up the hill a little, to where you could see the intersection clearly and also the ridge to the west, the Blue Ridge sitting out there in the gray morning, familiar, patient, doing what it had always done.

He stood there for a while.

He was not thinking strategically. He was not making a list. He was just standing in the town he had chosen, looking at the mountains, and letting himself understand something he had been understanding slowly for eight months.

The lie was not that he had been wrong about the town, or wrong about the business, or wrong about the work. The lie was the proportion: the belief, held somewhere below the level of articulation, that if you did the work correctly — the permits, the relationships, the civic engagement, the quality of the facility — the outcome would reflect the effort. That competence was a kind of protection. That love was a kind of protection.

He had known this was not quite true. He had known it the way you know things you have never had occasion to test. He knew it now in a different way, the way you know things after they have happened to you, the knowledge that has moved from your head into the rest of your body and settled there.

The pandemic was not a problem he had failed to solve. It was a thing that had happened to the world. It had happened to him and to Andrea and to the business they had built in a town they loved, and there was no version of doing the work correctly that would have prevented it, and there was no list he could have made that would have been long enough.

He had kept making lists anyway. He understood that about himself. The lists were not a strategy, exactly — they were the thing his hands needed to do when his mind was trying to hold something that didn't fit the containers he had built for it.

A truck went through the intersection, heading west. He watched it until it was gone.

The lie, confronted, did not destroy anything. That was the thing he had not been sure about. He had been afraid, somewhere below the level of words, that if he admitted the proportion was wrong — if he admitted that love and competence were not sufficient protection against the world — something would collapse. The will to do the work, maybe. The reason to keep going.

But it was still there. The town was still there. The building on Fayette Street was still there, with the name on it.

Andy had named it from the back seat of a car, the way kids said things — not as an announcement, just as a fact. *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* And Jim had heard it the way you heard something that was always going to be true, and he had brought it home to Andrea, and that was the name. It was still the name. That had not changed.

He turned and started walking back.

The dogs would come back. He knew this the way he knew that the shepherd mix who had pressed herself against the far wall on her first Tuesday would eventually come to him — not because he had done anything to her, but because he had done nothing to her, and patience was its own argument. The families had not stopped loving their dogs. The relationships were dormant. Dormant was different from dead.

The brick sidewalk was drying now in the pale morning light. He walked on it and listened to his own footsteps and thought about sixty families, sixty dogs, sixty leashes handed to him or to Andrea or to one of the staff, sixty moments of trust extended across a threshold. The threshold was still there. The building was still there. The name was still on the building.

He turned onto Fayette Street and walked home.

---

Andrea was at the kitchen table when he came in. She had cleared the breakfast plates and made more coffee and she had the papers from last night in front of her, not the numbers column — she had turned those face-down — but the margin notes. He could see, from where he stood in the doorway, her handwriting: *sixty families. dormant, not gone.*

She looked up when he came in.

"Cold?" she said.

"Not bad." He took off his jacket and hung it by the door. Gracie lifted her head and looked at him and then at the door, hopeful, and he said, "In a bit," and Gracie sighed and put her head down.

He sat across from Andrea. She had both hands around her mug.

"How far did you go?" she said.

"The intersection."

She nodded. She looked at her mug. "I was thinking," she said, and stopped.

"Yeah."

"When they come back —" She paused again. Not the pause of someone who didn't know what she wanted to say, but the pause of someone making sure she was saying the right version of it. "The first ones back. I know who they'll be."

He waited.

"The Hendersons will be first," she said. "Or the Nguyens. Biscuit's family." She was looking at the margin notes now. "They're not going to have stopped needing somewhere for the dogs to go. They're at home right now, all of them, and the dogs are driving them a little crazy, and the minute there's an order that lets us open, they're going to call."

"They will," Jim said.

"I want to be ready for that." She looked up at him. "I want to have something to tell them. Not just that we're open — that we're the same. That nothing changed."

He thought about what he'd been standing at the intersection thinking, the lie and what was on the other side of it. "Some things changed," he said.

"I know." She picked up her pen and turned it in her fingers. "I don't mean — I know things changed. I mean I want them to know that the part that matters is still here." She set the pen down. "Does that make sense?"

"Yeah," he said. "It does."

Cassie had come out from under the table at some point and was now leaning against Andrea's leg, her big head resting on Andrea's knee. Andrea put her hand on Cassie's head without looking down, the gesture automatic, the way you touched something you had touched ten thousand times.

"I called my mom this morning," Andrea said.

He looked at her.

"While you were out." She shrugged, a small motion. "I just wanted to hear her voice. She asked how things were and I said fine, and that was — I mean, that was a lie, but I didn't want to get into it. She's got her own stuff." She paused. "She asked about the dogs."

"What'd you tell her?"

"I said Cassie had slept on the bed again." The corner of her mouth moved. "She said we were going to regret that."

"We're already past regret on that one."

"I know." She scratched behind Cassie's ear and Cassie's eyes went half-closed, the particular expression of a dog receiving something it had decided it deserved. "I told her we were okay. And I think —" Andrea stopped. She looked at the paper. "I think we are. I think we're going to be."

He watched her. She was looking at her handwriting in the margin: *dormant, not gone.*

"The building's still there," he said.

"Yeah."

"The name's on it."

She nodded, still looking at the paper. "Andy would be annoyed if we let that go."

"He would."

She laughed, a short sound, genuine. Then she was quiet again, but it was a different quiet than the morning's — less weight in it, or the weight was the same but she was holding it differently.

Outside, a car went past. Gracie's ears moved toward the sound and then relaxed. The refrigerator ran. The November light through the window had settled into what it was going to be for the day: gray, steady, not dramatic. The kind of light that didn't ask anything of you.

Jim picked up his coffee mug. It had gone cold. He drank it anyway.

"Thirty days was enough," he said. "The extension. It bought us enough time."

"I know." She had told him this before. She was not contradicting him; she was agreeing with something that was still worth saying. "I know it was."

"We're not going to be the same as before," he said. "When we open. The first month, it's going to look different."

"I know."

"But it's going to look like something." He set the mug down. "It's going to be the start of something, not the end."

She looked at him for a moment. He held her gaze.

"Okay," she said.

Not *I know* again — *okay*. Something settling. He heard the difference.

Cassie moved from Andrea's leg to Jim's, making her slow, deliberate tour of the table, and leaned against his calf. He reached down and put his hand on her back. She was warm. She was entirely present in the moment, the way she always was, the way he had been trying to be all morning on the brick sidewalks of a town that was still there.

"I should take Gracie," he said.

"She's been waiting very patiently."

"She has." He looked at Gracie, who had heard her name and was now sitting up very straight, demonstrating readiness. "Very dignified about it."

"She's a dignified dog."

He stood up. He got the leash from the hook by the door. Gracie was immediately at his feet, the dignified stillness abandoned entirely, her whole body communicating urgency. He clipped the leash and looked back at Andrea.

She was still at the table. She had turned the numbers column face-up again and was looking at it, her pen in her hand, but she wasn't writing anything yet. She was just looking.

He took Gracie out into the gray morning and pulled the door shut behind him.
