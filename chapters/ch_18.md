# Chapter 18

The hall smelled like the hall always smelled — old wood and the particular damp of a house that had been lived in for fifty years without ever being renovated into something else. Cass walked it slowly. The needle behind his left eye was still there, the same faint specific pressure, 

He stopped outside the kitchen doorway.

His father was at the counter with his back to the hall, folding a paper bag flat against his thigh the way he always did before he put it in the recycling — 

"Hey," Cass said.

His father turned. "Hey. Almost ready. I just want to get the cooler loaded before we do the bags."

Cass went to the refrigerator and opened it. There were three bottles of water on the shelf that his grandfather had put there at some point, already cold. He took them out and set them on the counter.

"Those for the road?"

"Probably." His father took the cooler from the corner and set it on the floor, opened the lid, looked inside at the ice situation. "Your grandfather give you his whole lecture on Shostakovich?"

"It wasn't a lecture."

"It's always a lecture." He said it without heat, almost fondly. He went to the freezer and got a tray of ice cubes and cracked it over the cooler, the sound of it sharp in the kitchen. "He played it for me when I was about your age. I sat through the whole thing."

Cass put the water bottles in among the ice. "What did you think?"

His father was quiet for a moment. He put the empty ice tray in the sink. "I thought it was very long."

"He knows I didn't get it," his father said. "We've never discussed it. But he knows."

"He said."

His father looked at him. "He said that?"

"Not in those words."

His father nodded slowly, turned back to the cooler, rearranged the bottles slightly. There was something in his face that Cass couldn't read, some old thing being turned over. He put the lid down on the cooler.

"He loves you," his father said. "He's not always — he doesn't always know how to —" He stopped. Picked up the cooler by the handle. "He loves you. That's the thing."

"I know," Cass said.

"Okay." His father carried the cooler toward the back door. "Grab the bag on the chair."

Cass took the canvas bag from the kitchen chair. It was heavier than it looked — his grandfather had put food in it at some point, wrapped things in paper towels, the way he did. Cass didn't look inside. He followed his father out.

---

The morning had gone gray while they were inside. Not threatening gray, just the flat overcast of a November morning that hadn't decided what it wanted to be yet. The car sat in the driveway with its trunk open, the things they'd brought already loaded from earlier — the overnight bags, the extra jacket Cass had brought and not needed. His father set the cooler in beside them and pressed the trunk lid down until it clicked.

Cass put the canvas bag in the back seat.

His grandfather was on the porch. Cass hadn't heard him come out. 

"You have everything?" his grandfather asked.

"I think so." His father did a slow look at the car, the particular check of a man who has forgotten something before and learned from it. "The records."

Cass's grandfather went still for just a moment. "What records?"

"The ones you were going to let me borrow. The Brahms."

"I said I'd think about it."

"You said you'd think about it last time too."

His grandfather looked at his father with an expression that was not quite amusement and not quite annoyance. "Come back and get them sometime when you're not in a hurry."

"That's what you said last time."

"And yet you keep coming back." His grandfather came down the two porch steps. He moved carefully on the steps, one hand on the railing, and Cass watched this without looking like he was watching. It was new, the carefulness. Or maybe it wasn't new and Cass was only now noticing it.

His father and grandfather shook hands in the way they always did, which was not quite a handshake and not quite an embrace, one hand clasped and the other briefly on the shoulder. 

Then his grandfather turned to Cass.

Cass expected the handshake. Instead his grandfather put both arms around him, briefly, firmly, the wool of the cardigan rough against Cass's cheek. He smelled like the house. Like the record room. Cass stood in it for a moment and the needle behind his eye pulsed once, distinct, and then settled.

His grandfather stepped back. He looked at Cass directly.

"You'll remember it," he said. "The thing you heard. You'll carry it a long time before you understand what to do with it. That's all right."

Cass didn't know what to say to that, so he said, "Okay."

"Good." His grandfather put his hands back in his pockets. "Drive safe."

He went back up the porch steps, one hand on the railing, and through the front door, and that was it. Cass stood in the driveway.

"Come on," his father said. "We'll beat the traffic if we leave now."

Cass got in the car.

His father backed out of the driveway and turned onto the street. Cass watched the house through the window. The porch was empty. The curtains in the front room were the same curtains they'd always been, a dark green that had faded at the edges, and behind them the light was on, the lamp in the corner that his grandfather left on all day, every day, for reasons Cass had never asked about.

The house went behind a hedge and then they were on the main road.

His father turned the radio on low — a station that played classical without announcing the pieces, which was something his grandfather had always objected to, the anonymity of it. *How is anyone supposed to learn anything if they don't know what they're hearing?* Cass could hear the voice of him saying it. He heard it clearly enough that for a second he almost turned to check if his grandfather was somehow in the back seat.

The needle.

He pressed two fingers against his left temple, lightly, not enough to do anything, just to feel where it was. Still there. Specific.

"You all right?" his father asked.

"Headache."

"Dehydrated. Get a water from the cooler."

The cooler was in the trunk. Cass didn't say this. He took his fingers away from his temple.

Outside, the town they'd stayed in gave way to highway, and the highway opened up the way highways do — the particular expansion of space, the sky getting bigger, the trees pulling back from the road. Cass watched it. He was aware of the music on the radio, something with strings, slow and deliberate, not Shostakovich. Something older, maybe. He didn't know enough yet to know.

*You'll carry it a long time before you understand what to do with it.*

His grandfather hadn't said what *it* was, exactly. The music, Cass supposed. Or the thing the music had opened. The two weren't quite the same thing and his grandfather knew the difference and hadn't bothered to explain it, which meant either he trusted Cass to figure it out or he knew that explaining would ruin it. Probably both.

Cass thought about the table. The workbench in the back room, the tools hung above it in their specific places, the way his grandfather's hands moved over a piece of wood the way other people's hands moved over nothing in particular. The table would be his eventually. He didn't know what that meant yet. It felt large and also completely ordinary, the way important things sometimes felt when they first arrived.

His father changed lanes. The car made its small adjustments, the sound of the tires on the road shifting pitch slightly, and Cass felt this more than heard it — the frequency of it, the way the road surface changed under them, rough to smooth to rough again.

"Can I ask you something?" Cass said.

"Sure."

"When you were my age. Did you know what you wanted to do?"

His father was quiet for a moment. The road. "No."

"Did Grandpa?"

"Your grandfather wanted me to study music. I didn't want to. We — it was a thing, for a while." He moved the turn signal for no apparent reason, then turned it off. "He came around. Or I came around. Somewhere in the middle."

"What do you mean, came around?"

"I mean I understood eventually that he wasn't trying to tell me what to be. He was trying to give me something he thought was worth having." His father's hands were easy on the wheel. "I was seventeen before I got that. Seventeen, maybe eighteen. Took me a while."

Cass sat with that.

"Do you regret it? Not studying music?"

His father thought about this for what felt like a genuinely long time, long enough that Cass started to think he wasn't going to answer.

"I regret some of the time I spent being angry about it," his father said finally. "Not the decision. The anger was — it was a waste. Your grandfather and I wasted about three years being stiff with each other over something that didn't end up mattering the way either of us thought it would."

The needle pulsed. Cass kept his hands in his lap.

"He played me the Eighth this weekend," Cass said.

His father glanced over. Quick, then back to the road. "The Shostakovich."

"Yeah."

"What did you think?"

Cass looked at the window. The trees were bare, most of them — November had done its work. The branches against the gray sky had a precision to them, each one distinct, the way things got distinct when there were no leaves to blur the edges.

"I think I heard something," he said. "I don't know what yet."

His father was quiet for a moment. Then: "Yeah."

Just that. One word, and something in it that Cass recognized without being able to name — the sound of someone who understood exactly what you meant and was choosing not to add to it, because adding to it would be the wrong move.

The radio played its anonymous strings. The road ran out ahead of them, straight and gray.

Cass closed his eyes. The needle behind his left eye was still there, a point of pressure that had the quality of a pitch, faint and specific, like a string that had been struck and not yet finished vibrating. He could feel it and he could feel the road through the seat, the small continuous adjustments of the car, his father's hands on the wheel making corrections so small they barely registered.

He thought about the record. The fourth movement, the moment the strings came in under the brass, the particular sound of something being said that couldn't be said any other way. He could hear it in his head and it sounded different now than it had an hour ago. He didn't know why. He didn't know enough to know why.

He opened his eyes.

His father was looking at the road. His hands were at ten and two, the same position they'd been in since they left. He had a coffee from this morning in the cupholder, cold now, and he wouldn't drink it cold but he wouldn't throw it out either — he'd carry it all the way home and set it on the counter and look at it and throw it out there. Cass knew this about his father the way he knew the sound of his footsteps on the stairs, the particular rhythm of them, unhurried, always the same.

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"Thanks for bringing me."

His father looked over. Something crossed his face — not surprise, not quite. More like the expression of a man who had expected to have to wait longer for something and was now recalibrating.

"Of course," he said.

"I mean —" Cass stopped. He wasn't sure what he meant. He tried again. "I didn't know I needed to go until we were there."

His father turned back to the road. He was quiet for a long time, long enough that Cass thought that was the end of it. Then his father reached over and turned the radio off. Not roughly. Just off. The car went quiet.

"He's going to leave you the table," his father said. "Did he tell you?"

"Yeah."

"He told me he was going to." A pause. "I'm glad."

Cass looked at him. His father's face was doing something careful, something held. "You're not — I mean, you don't mind?"

"Mind?" His father shook his head. "No. The table should go to someone who'll use it. You'll use it." He said this with certainty, not as a question. "I wouldn't have known what to do with it."

The needle behind Cass's eye pulsed again, sharper this time — a quick specific pain, like a finger pressed into a bruise and released. He breathed through it.

"How do you know I will?"

His father glanced over. "Because you heard something this morning." He looked back at the road. "People who hear things need somewhere to put them."

The gray sky had started to lighten somewhere ahead of them — not sun, just a brightening, the clouds thinning out over the highway. The road ran toward it.

Cass pressed two fingers to his temple again. The needle was still there. He was beginning to think it might always be there, or at least for a long time, and that this was not necessarily a bad thing. A kind of tuning. A frequency you learned to live with until you understood what it was trying to tell you.

"We'll stop in about an hour," his father said. "Get something to eat."

"Okay."

"You want anything now? There's stuff in the bag your grandfather packed."

Cass reached back and got the canvas bag from the seat and opened it. Inside, wrapped in paper towels, were three pieces of the bread from breakfast and two apples and a small waxed-paper packet that turned out to be slices of sharp cheese. His grandfather had packed it without saying anything, without making a thing of it. Just put it there.

Cass rewrapped the cheese. He held the bag on his lap.

Outside, the highway moved. The bare trees moved with it, the whole world organizing itself into the particular order of a thing in motion, and the needle behind his eye was a constant quiet presence, and somewhere in the back of his skull the fourth movement was still playing, had been playing since the needle came down on the record, would probably keep playing for longer than he could predict.
