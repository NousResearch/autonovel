# Chapter Seventeen

The table had been there before the piece.

Cass sat with his palm flat on the wood and thought about that. A few years after, his father had said. So his grandfather had heard the Scelsi in — what, 1987, 1988 — and then a few years later had come out to the garage with a board and a saw and made this. Had run his hands along this same grain while the sound was still new in him, still being processed, still working its way into whatever it would eventually become.

The grain was tight and even. Oak, probably. Cass pressed his palm flat and felt the warmth of it.

"Eggs?" his father said, from somewhere inside the refrigerator.

"Sure."

The refrigerator door closed. His father moved to the stove, and Cass listened to the sounds of it — the pan, the click of the burner, the brief hiss when butter hit the heat. Outside, the yard was almost fully green now, the frost gone. The oak tree stood still. No bird.

"Dad."

"Yeah."

"Did he talk about it? After he heard it?"

His father cracked an egg against the edge of the pan. A pause. "The Scelsi."

"Yeah."

"Not to me. Not directly." Another egg. The hiss of it settling. "He talked around it sometimes. He'd mention it in the context of other things. You know how he does."

Cass knew. He had watched his grandfather do it all weekend — the way information came out sideways, tucked inside something else, offered as an aside when it was actually the point.

"What other things?"

His father turned the heat down. "He was going through a hard time when he heard it. He didn't say that exactly. But he said the piece found him at the right moment. That he'd been — I don't know. Stopped up, he said. Like something had gone solid in him that was supposed to move." He picked up the spatula. "He said the piece broke it loose."

Cass moved his hand along the edge of the table where the wood had gone smooth from thirty years of people sitting here, from elbows and forearms and the particular friction of daily life wearing the surface down to something almost soft.

"And then he built the table."

"A few years later. Yeah."

"Because the thing that was stopped up was moving again."

His father looked over his shoulder. Something crossed his face that wasn't quite a smile. "I don't know if he'd put it that way. But — maybe."

The eggs came out of the pan onto two plates, and his father set one in front of Cass and sat down across from him with his own. They ate for a minute without talking. The butter had browned a little and the eggs had that slightly crisp edge that meant the pan had been hotter than intended, and Cass ate them anyway because they were good.

"I want to hear it again," Cass said.

His father looked up.

"The Scelsi. I want to hear it again before we leave."

"Okay." His father set down his fork. "Why?"

Cass thought about the needle behind his left eye. It had been there since last night — not sharp, not constant, but present. A low specific pressure that came and went. He had woken up with it and it was still there now, a small insistent point of something.

"I want to know if it happens again," he said.

His father was quiet for a moment. "What happened?"

"There's a note." Cass picked up his fork again. "There's a note in the second movement that — I don't know. It does something. Behind my eye."

His father sat back. The chair made a sound against the floor.

"Left eye," Cass said. "Like a needle. It's not bad. It just — it's specific."

"When did it start?"

"When the note hit. And then it stayed. It's still there a little."

His father looked at him with an expression that was careful and not alarmed, the expression of someone filing information. "Has that happened before?"

"No."

"With any piece?"

"No."

Outside, a car went by on the street, the first one Cass had heard all morning, the sound of it rising and then gone. His father picked up his fork and then set it back down.

"You should tell Granddad," he said.

"I know."

"Before we leave. He'll want to know."

"I know, Dad."

They finished the eggs. His father took the plates to the sink and ran water over them, and Cass stayed at the table with his hand on the wood. The needle was there. Faint, but there. It had a quality to it that wasn't quite pain — more like pressure, like a finger pressed against the inside of his skull just behind the orbital bone. He had tried to locate it exactly last night, lying in bed, and had found that it moved slightly when he moved his eyes, which was interesting and also slightly alarming, which he had decided not to think about until morning.

It was morning now.

He was thinking about it.

---

His grandfather was in the music room when Cass knocked.

The door was open a few inches. Cass pushed it wider and looked in. His grandfather was sitting in the armchair with a book open on his knee, but his eyes were on the window, and the book had clearly not been read in some time. He looked up when Cass came in.

"Sit down," he said.

Cass sat on the bench at the piano, sideways, facing his grandfather. The room smelled like it always did — old paper, the faint metallic smell of the piano strings, something like cedar. Morning light came through the window at a low angle and caught the dust in the air.

"Your father told you," his grandfather said.

"He told me some of it."

His grandfather nodded. He closed the book without marking his place and set it on the arm of the chair. "What do you want to know?"

Cass looked at his hands on his knees. "The table," he said. "He said you built it after. A few years after."

"I did."

"Were you a carpenter before that?"

His grandfather made a sound that might have been a laugh. "No. I was terrible. It took me three attempts to get the legs even." He looked at the window. "I needed something to do with my hands. Something that required attention. The kind of attention that doesn't leave room for anything else." He paused. "You know what I mean."

Cass knew. He had spent four hours once on a single passage in the Brahms intermezzo, not because he couldn't play it but because the playing required something from him that he kept almost giving and then pulling back, and the only way through was to stay in it until he gave it completely. He had come out the other side of those four hours feeling scraped out and clean.

"Yeah," he said.

His grandfather looked at him. "What happened last night?"

So his father had told him. Or maybe his grandfather had simply known — had watched Cass come out of the music room and read it in his face, the way the old man read things. Cass had learned a long time ago that his grandfather noticed more than he said.

"There's a note," Cass said. "In the second movement."

"Which note."

"I don't know the exact pitch. It's the one that comes in about two-thirds through. The low one, the sustained one. It comes in under everything else and then — " He stopped. "It does something behind my eye."

His grandfather went very still.

"Left eye," Cass said. "Like a needle. It started when the note hit and it's still there a little. Right now."

His grandfather looked at him for a long moment. Not alarmed. Something else — the expression of someone recognizing a thing they had been half-expecting.

"I had that," he said.

"When you first heard it?"

"Not the first time. The fourth or fifth. I'd been listening to it repeatedly. The way you do when you're trying to understand something." He shifted in the chair. "It started as pressure. Then it became specific. Like you said — a needle. Left eye."

"Does it go away?"

"Yes. It took about a week, that first time. Then it came back the next time I listened and went away faster. Now I barely feel it."

Cass exhaled. His grandfather watched him do it.

"You're not worried," his grandfather said. It wasn't a question.

"I'm — I don't know. A little. But mostly I want to understand what it is."

"I don't know what it is. Medically." His grandfather picked up the book again and then set it down again. "I asked a doctor, once. He had no useful answer. He thought it might be a specific frequency triggering the trigeminal nerve. The Scelsi works with microtonality — the intervals between the notes, the quartertones. Some of them sit in a range that the ear is not accustomed to processing."

"So it's just — acoustics."

"Maybe." His grandfather looked at the window. "Or maybe it's the piece doing what it's supposed to do. Breaking something loose." He was quiet for a moment. "How did it feel? Not the needle. The piece."

Cass thought about it. The needle was easy to describe because it was physical, located, specific. The other thing was harder.

"Like being inside something," he said. "Like the sound was the room and I was in it. Not listening to it. In it."

His grandfather nodded slowly, the nod of someone confirming something they already knew.

"And then it changed," Cass said, "and the change was — wrong isn't the right word. Necessary. It was necessary and I didn't want it."

"Yes."

"And the note that caused the needle — it was the one that changed everything. The low one. It came in and it made everything else make sense, but the sense it made was — " He stopped again. He was losing the language of it.

"Inevitable," his grandfather said.

The word landed and stayed. Cass sat with it.

"Yeah," he said.

"That's the piece," his grandfather said. "That's what it does. It builds a world and then it shows you that the world was always going to end this way, and you already loved the world, so — " He stopped. "So."

So. The word hung there, doing more work than most sentences.

They sat for a minute. The light moved slightly through the window as a cloud passed. Cass could hear his father somewhere in the house, the sound of a drawer opening and closing.

"I want to hear it again," Cass said. "Before we leave."

"I know." His grandfather stood, slowly, the careful stand of a man whose joints required negotiation in the morning. He moved to the record player and lifted the needle and set the record back to the beginning. "Lie down if you want. The couch."

Cass moved to the couch and lay back and looked at the ceiling. The ceiling was white plaster, slightly uneven, with a crack in one corner that had been there as long as Cass could remember. He looked at it and waited.

The needle touched the record.

The first sound came in — that low, patient, almost inaudible beginning, the sound of something that was not yet a note but was becoming one. Cass felt the familiar strangeness of it, the sense that the piece was not starting so much as arriving, as if it had been there all along and the record was simply making it audible.

His grandfather sat back down in the armchair.

Cass listened.

The first movement did what the first movement did — built its world, slow and specific, the way a room fills with light when the sun comes around the corner of a building. He could feel the piece differently now. Knowing what was coming. He had that thing you got with music you'd heard before, the anticipation of a phrase before it arrived, except with this piece the anticipation was shadowed by something else, by the knowledge of what the second movement would ask of him.

He waited for it.

The second movement began.

He felt his grandfather's eyes on him and then felt them go away, his grandfather looking at the window or the middle distance, giving him the room.

The needle behind his eye arrived before the note did. A faint precursor pressure, like the air pressure change before a storm, and then the note came in — the low sustained one, two-thirds through, exactly where he'd remembered — and the needle sharpened and drove and Cass let out a breath through his nose and held still.

It was pain. Not bad pain, not the kind that required response, but specific pain, a point of it just behind the orbital bone on the left side, and it had a quality that was almost musical, almost like a pitch, like the pain itself was tuned to the note that caused it.

He lay there and listened and hurt and the piece did what it did.

When it ended — the long, patient ending, the world it had built settling into something like acceptance — the needle was still there but softer. He lay still for a moment after the last sound.

"Same place?" his grandfather said.

"Yeah."

"Softer than last night?"

Cass thought about it. "About the same."

His grandfather made a sound. Cass sat up. His grandfather was looking at him with that expression again — recognition, something like it.

"I want to ask you something," his grandfather said, "and I want you to answer it honestly, not the way you think I want you to answer it."

"Okay."

"Does it feel like damage? The pain."

Cass thought about the question. Took it seriously. The needle was still present, faint, the particular pressure just behind his eye. He tried to assess it honestly — whether it felt like something being harmed or something being activated. Whether it felt like a warning.

"No," he said.

"You're sure."

"It feels like — " He searched for the right comparison. "When you hold a note in the upper register for too long and you can feel the resonance in your sinuses. It's not damage. It's just — the body responding to frequency. It's the body saying: I'm in this."

His grandfather looked at him for a long moment. Then he nodded, once, and something in him settled.

"Good," he said.

They sat there. The record had ended and the needle was cycling in the run-out groove, a faint rhythmic tick. His grandfather didn't get up to lift it. Neither did them.

"Granddad," Cass said.

"Mm."

"When you built the table. After you heard the piece." He paused. "Did you know you were building it because of the piece? Or did you just — build a table?"

His grandfather was quiet for long enough that Cass thought he might not answer. The tick of the needle in the groove. The light on the window.

"I built a table," his grandfather said. "I was building a table. I needed to do something with my hands and I had a piece of oak in the garage and I built a table. It was a terrible table the first two times. I built it three times before it was right." He looked at his hands. "It was only afterward, sitting at it, that I understood what I'd been doing. That the thing in me that was moving again — I'd put it into the table. Poured it into the work. Without deciding to."

The needle ticked.

"Does that make sense?" his grandfather said.

"Yeah," Cass said. "It does."

His grandfather got up and lifted the needle from the record and set it in its rest. He stood there for a moment with his back to Cass, looking at the record player.

"Your father was never going to hear it," he said. "I knew that early. He has many gifts but that particular — opening. That's not how he's built." He turned around. "I didn't know if you'd have it. I thought you might. I wasn't sure."

Cass sat with that.

"Is that why you played it for me?"

"I played it for you because you asked me what the most important piece of music I'd ever heard was, and I told you the truth." His grandfather sat back down. "What you do with it is yours."

Outside, Cass heard his father's footsteps on the stairs — the particular rhythm of them, unhurried, coming down toward the kitchen. The sounds of the house. The drawer again. The refrigerator.

"I should go help him pack the car," Cass said.

"Probably."

Cass stood up. He stood there for a moment, not sure what he meant to say, and his grandfather looked at him with the patience of someone who had learned that the important things usually came out sideways, tucked inside something else, if you gave them enough time.

"Thank you," Cass said. "For — all of it. The weekend."

"Come back," his grandfather said. "Not for a holiday. Just come back."

Cass nodded. He went to the door. He was almost through it when his grandfather said, not loudly, just into the room:

"The table will be yours eventually. I'd like you to have it."

Cass turned around.

His grandfather was looking at the window. Not at Cass. The morning light on his face, the old face with its long record of things attended to, worked through, built and rebuilt.

"Okay," Cass said.

He went out into the hall. The needle behind his eye was still there — faint, specific, a point of pressure that had a quality almost like a pitch. He carried it down the hall toward the sounds of his father in the kitchen, toward the ordinary work of leaving.
