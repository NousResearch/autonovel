# Chapter Seventeen

The call came at 9:14 on a Tuesday morning, and Andrea knew before she picked up. 

"Mrs. Payne." It was Gail Ferris. Her golden, Biscuit, had been coming three days a week since November. "I wanted to call and let you know, so you heard it from us directly."

"Of course," Andrea said.

"My neighbor — you probably don't know her, she's on Thornberry — she's retired, and she offered to watch Biscuit during the days. Until things open back up." A pause. "She loves dogs. She's been wonderful with him."

"That's good," Andrea said. "That's really good for Biscuit."

"We love A Pup's Valley. We really do. This isn't — we're not going somewhere else. We just needed something for right now."

"I understand completely."

"We'll be back when you reopen. I mean that."

"I know you do," Andrea said. And she did. She believed it when Gail said it. 

"Thank you for calling," Andrea said. "Tell Biscuit we miss him."

She hung up. She stood in the grooming area with the phone still in her hand, looking at the shelf where the brushes were arranged by size, the shampoos lined up with the labels facing out. 

She set the phone down on the counter.

---

She walked through the facility the way she did every time she came in now — not checking anything, exactly, but moving through it. 

The boarding spaces. The grooming station. The area near the back where the dogs who needed a quieter moment could go, away from the main floor, where someone would sit with them if they needed sitting with.

It still smelled clean. That was the thing that got her, a little — the smell of a facility being maintained for no one.  

She stood in the middle of the daycare floor.

Sixty families. She knew all of them. She knew which dogs came in on Mondays still stiff from the weekend, which ones pushed through the door already looking for their friends, which ones needed five minutes in the quiet room before they were ready for the main floor.  She had learned all of this by paying attention, and now there was nothing to pay attention to.

Biscuit wouldn't be coming back. Not yet. Maybe not at all — she didn't know, and that was the thing she was holding. 

She walked back to the grooming area and picked up her phone and called Dottie.

---

It rang four times. Andrea was about to pull the phone away when Dottie picked up.

"Andrea." Not a question. She had caller ID and she used it.

"Are you busy?"

"I'm between things. What's going on?"

Andrea sat down on the low stool she kept in the grooming area, the one that let her get level with a small dog on the table. "I just got off the phone with one of our client families. They've found another arrangement. A neighbor who'll watch the dog."

"Ah," Dottie said.

"She was very nice about it. She said they'll be back when we reopen."

"They might be," Dottie said.

Andrea almost laughed. 

"I'm sitting in the grooming area," Andrea said. "I came in to clean. There's nothing to clean. I cleaned it Friday."

"You're keeping it up."

"I can't stop." She looked at the brushes on the shelf. "I don't know how to stop doing the work when there's no one to do it for."

Dottie was quiet for a moment. 

"Can I tell you something?" Dottie said.

"Yes."

"When we were in the middle of the Disney fight — this was '93, '94 — there were months when I didn't know how it was going to go. The opposition was national, historians and editorial boards and everyone else, but the local fight was local, and I was in it, and I didn't know if we were going to win." A pause. "I knew what we were fighting for. I knew why it mattered. I didn't know if it was going to hold."

Andrea waited.

"You keep going," Dottie said. "That's all there is. You don't get to know the outcome while you're in the middle of it. You just keep going."

"I know," Andrea said.

"I know you know. I'm saying it anyway."

Andrea looked at the clean floor of the grooming area, the light coming in from the high window, the brushes on the shelf. "Thank you," she said. "I didn't call for advice."

"I know that too," Dottie said. "Call me again if you need to."

After she hung up, Andrea sat on the stool for another few minutes. The facility was very quiet. Outside, somewhere on Fayette Street, a truck passed — the low rumble of it, then gone. She thought about Biscuit, the specific way he came through the door on Monday mornings, already looking for the other dogs before he was fully inside. She thought about Gail Ferris on the phone, being careful, being kind. She thought about the neighbor on Thornberry, retired, who loved dogs.

Then she stood up, put the phone in her pocket, and turned off the lights.

---

Jim was home when she got there. He was at the kitchen table with the legal pad, the one he'd started using after the Council meeting, the pages filling up with lists and questions and the beginnings of plans. Cassie was on the floor beside his chair. Gracie was somewhere in the back of the house — Andrea could hear her moving around, the soft click of nails on hardwood.

Andy was at the table too, doing something on a worksheet, his pencil moving with the particular focused reluctance of a kid who wanted to be done but wasn't.

"How was it?" Jim said.

"Ferris called this morning. Biscuit's neighbor is watching him."

Jim looked at her. "Gail Ferris?"

"She was very kind about it."

He nodded once. He wrote something on the legal pad, and she didn't ask what. She went to the refrigerator and stood in front of it for a moment not really seeing what was in it.

"There's soup," Jim said. "From last night."

"I'm not hungry yet."

She closed the refrigerator and leaned against the counter. Andy had looked up from his worksheet when she came in, assessed the room the way kids do — checking the emotional weather, deciding if it required attention — and gone back to his pencil.

"What are you working on?" Andrea asked him.

"Math."  "It's fractions."

"Are you almost done?"

"Almost." He made a mark on the paper. "There's one I don't get."

"Show me."

He slid the worksheet across the table. She looked at it. The problem wasn't hard — it was a division of unlike denominators, the kind that looks complicated until you see the step. She pulled out the chair across from him and sat down and walked him through it, and he followed, and when he got it his pencil went back to the paper with more confidence.

She sat there while he finished. Jim was still writing. Gracie had come into the kitchen and settled near the back door, in the patch of afternoon light that fell there. Cassie hadn't moved from beside Jim's chair.

---

That night, after Andy was in bed, she sat at the kitchen table with Jim and a piece of paper and they went through the numbers again. 

Jim had his pen. He was not writing. He was looking at the paper.

"The building doesn't stop," Andrea said.

"No."

"I know we know that."

"Yeah."

She looked at the column of numbers. 

Knowing it did not fix the column of numbers.

"Three months," she said. "If it runs three months."

"We don't know that it does."

"No. But if it does."

Jim looked at the paper. He wrote something, crossed it out, wrote it again. She watched his hand. She had watched his hands for years — the way they moved when he was working through a problem, the stillness of them when he'd arrived somewhere.

"It's possible," he said, "that we don't make it."

 She looked at him.

"I know," she said.

"I don't think it's likely. But it's possible."

"I know that too."

He looked at her for a moment, and she looked back, and neither of them said anything else about it.  

 

"I'm not going to tell Andy," she said.

"No."

"He doesn't need to know the worst version of it."

"He knows something's wrong," Jim said.

"He knows something's hard. That's different."

Jim nodded. He looked at the legal pad, at the list he'd been building all day. *Call the Pattersons tomorrow. Check on Archie.* She could read it from across the table. *List of families for check-ins — expand to all sixty. Think about what reopening looks like.*

She reached across and put her hand over his. He turned his hand over and held hers.

The house was quiet. Cassie had come into the kitchen at some point and was lying under the table, near their feet. The back door was dark. Outside, somewhere, Haymarket was doing whatever Haymarket did at ten o'clock on a Tuesday — the Route 15 intersection still running, 

"I went through the whole facility today," she said. "After Gail called. I just walked through it."

"How did it look?"

"Like it's supposed to look when it's full." She paused. "It smelled clean. I keep cleaning it."

"That's not a bad thing."

"I know. I just —" She stopped. She wasn't sure what she'd been about to say. Something about the brushes on the shelf, the labels facing out. Something about maintaining a thing for no one, the particular stubbornness of it. "I talked to Dottie," she said instead.

Jim looked up.

"She said you keep going. That's all there is." Andrea looked at their hands on the table. "She said she didn't know if the Disney fight was going to hold, while she was in the middle of it. She just kept going."

"Dottie would know," Jim said.

"Yeah." Andrea looked at the paper, at the numbers. "She would."

 

She was the person who took care of things. She had always been that person. She did not know, right now, how to be the person who needed taking care of — she didn't have the vocabulary for it, didn't know how to ask for it or what asking would even look like.  

Under the table, Cassie shifted and sighed and went still again.

Jim wrote: *First week back. What does it look like for the dogs.*

Andrea picked up her pen and drew a line under the column of numbers. She wrote, in the margin, the way she sometimes wrote things she wasn't ready to say aloud: *sixty families. dormant, not gone.*

Then she capped the pen and left it there.
