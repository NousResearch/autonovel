# Chapter Six

The bell woke him at 3 AM.

Not the sound of it — the sound was fine, or fine enough, the low sustained note that the practice casting sent through the floorboards like a hand pressing flat against wood. What woke him was the needle. Left eye, socket depth, arriving without warning in the specific way that meant the bell had been ringing for a while before his brain registered it as pain rather than as sleep.

Cass lay on his back and looked at the ceiling and waited for it to pass. It didn't pass. It shifted — lower, steadier, the way a note rings on after the striker leaves the metal, the way bronze holds sound longer than you expect because bronze is dense and the density is the point. He pressed his palm over his left eye. The pressure helped a little. Not much.

Down in the workshop, his grandfather was working.

---

The next morning his grandfather was already at the kitchen table when Cass came down, a mug in both hands, looking out the window at the yard. The frost had come back overnight. The dead grass had a white cast to it, brittle-looking, the kind of cold that wasn't deep yet but was promising.

"You were up late," Cass said.

His grandfather turned. "You heard me."

"I heard the bell."

His grandfather looked at him for a moment, then back at the window. "Come sit down."

Cass sat. His grandfather pushed a second mug toward him — already poured, already the right amount of milk, which meant he'd been expecting this conversation and had been waiting for Cass to start it.

"The alloy," his grandfather said. "I've been going back through the records. Your great-grandfather's records, the ones in the green ledger. The proportion he used for the Aldermast commission." He set his mug down and turned it in a slow circle. "There's a note in the margin. In his handwriting. Says the first pour was off. That he had to melt it back."

Cass waited.

"The proportion he used for the second pour is what's in the ledger. But the first pour — the one he melted back — he wrote the numbers down separately. On the back of the page." His grandfather looked at him. "The crack in the practice casting. I think the alloy is closer to the first pour than the second."

"The one that didn't work."

"The one he melted back, yes."

Cass picked up his mug. The coffee was too hot and he drank it anyway, because the heat was something to focus on that wasn't the question he was about to ask. "So the crack is an alloy problem. Not a casting problem."

"It's both. The crack is in the casting. But why the crack is there — that's the alloy. The tin proportion creates a specific resonance, and that resonance—"

"Creates the wolf tone."

His grandfather was quiet for a moment. Outside, a bird landed on the frost-stiff grass and then lifted off again, apparently deciding against it.

"Your father explained that part," Cass said.

"I know. He told me." His grandfather picked up his mug again. "What I want to know is what you've been doing about it."

"About which part."

"The eye, Cass."

Cass looked at his coffee. The surface had a small circle of steam still rising from it, and he watched it until it was gone. "It's manageable."

"Your father said you said that."

"It's true."

"It may be true and still not be the full answer."

That was such a specific thing to say — the phrasing of it, the precision — that Cass looked up. His grandfather was watching him with an expression he recognized from the workshop: the look that meant *I'm not going to finish this piece for you, but I'm standing here while you finish it.*

"It's worse when the bell rings," Cass said. "Sharper. When it's not ringing it's just — there. Low. Like background noise."

"And last night."

"Last night was both. At the same time."

His grandfather set down his mug. He stood up, slowly, the particular slowness of a man who had spent forty years bent over work that required precision and had paid for it in his hips and lower back. He went to the counter and stood there with his back to Cass for a moment, and Cass thought he was going to say something about the alloy, or about the records, or about the Aldermast commission and what went wrong with the first pour.

Instead he said, "When I was your age, my father had me ring the bells."

Cass waited.

"Not the finished ones. The castings that came out wrong. The ones that were going to be melted back. He had me ring them and listen, and tell him what I heard." His grandfather turned around. "I thought it was a teaching exercise. I thought he was trying to train my ear."

"It wasn't?"

"It was partly that. But mostly he wanted to know if I could feel it."

The needle behind Cass's eye pulsed once, faint, like a string plucked by accident. He put his hand flat on the table.

"My father had headaches," his grandfather said. "Your father had headaches. I had headaches. But what you're describing — the eye, the specific location — that's new. In my experience of this family, that's new."

"You said that. That it was new."

"I know I said it. I'm saying it again because I want you to hear what I mean by it." His grandfather came back to the table and sat down. "New is not necessarily bad. New means we don't have a record of it. New means I can't tell you what it means yet. But it also means I'm paying attention to it, and I need you to pay attention to it, and I need you to tell me when it changes."

Cass said, "Okay."

"Not just okay. Tell me specifically. More pressure or less. More localized or spreading. Whether it's correlated with the bell or whether it's starting to happen on its own."

"It happens a little on its own. Just — low. Constant."

His grandfather wrote something down on the edge of the newspaper that was folded beside his mug. Three words, maybe four. Cass couldn't read them from where he sat.

"Okay," his grandfather said. "Now. Do you want to know what I found in the ledger, or do you want to go look at the casting first?"

"Both," Cass said. "Ledger first."

His grandfather almost smiled. "Good answer."

---

The green ledger was older than Cass had thought. He'd assumed it was his grandfather's — the same generation as the workshop, the same era as the hand tools with the worn wooden handles. But the binding was cracked in a way that meant decades, not years, and when his grandfather opened it to the Aldermast pages, the paper had the particular yellowed brittleness of something that had been pressed flat for a very long time.

His great-grandfather's handwriting was small and vertical, nothing like his grandfather's looser script. The numbers were precise. Cass read them twice.

"The tin proportion," he said.

"Twelve and a quarter percent. That's what's in the ledger. The second pour. The successful pour."

Cass turned the page carefully. On the back, in the same small vertical hand, a column of numbers with a single line drawn under them and two words written below: *melted back.*

"The first pour was closer to fourteen," Cass said.

"Fourteen point one."

"And our practice casting—"

"I had the alloy tested last week. Before I told you about the crack." His grandfather pointed to the column. "Fourteen point three."

Cass looked at the numbers for a long moment. The kitchen was warm and the frost was still on the grass outside and somewhere down the street a car started and drove away, and the sound of it faded in the particular way that sounds fade in cold air, faster than they should.

"So we poured wrong," Cass said.

"The alloy was wrong. Whether the alloy was wrong because of measurement error or because the source material was off — that I don't know yet. I've sent a sample to Hartley."

"How long for results?"

"Week. Maybe ten days."

Cass looked at the ledger. He looked at the words *melted back* in his great-grandfather's handwriting. There was something about the precision of it — not *failed* or *discarded* but *melted back*, as if the metal itself hadn't failed, only needed to return to a prior state and try again — that he found, unexpectedly, reassuring.

"Can we melt back the practice casting?" he asked.

His grandfather looked at him. "Why would we do that?"

"Because if the alloy is wrong, the casting is wrong. If the casting is wrong, we can't learn anything true from it. And if we can't learn anything true from it—"

"We're just ringing a broken bell and hoping the pain tells us something."

Cass said nothing.

His grandfather closed the ledger slowly, both hands flat on the cover. He did this with care, the way he handled anything old, the way he handled anything that held information he might need later. "I want to hear you say the rest of it."

Cass looked at him. "What rest of it?"

"The thought you just had. The one that stopped you."

The needle pulsed again. Cass pressed two fingers to the outside corner of his left eye, not hard, just enough to feel the pressure. "If we melt back the practice casting," he said slowly, "we lose the evidence of what went wrong. The crack. The wolf tone. We lose the thing that showed us the problem."

"Yes."

"But if we keep it, we keep ringing a bell that's hurting me."

His grandfather was quiet.

"Which means," Cass said, and then stopped, because he'd reached the edge of the thought and what was on the other side of it was something he hadn't expected to arrive at in a kitchen on a cold morning over a ledger from before his grandfather was born. "Which means the question is whether the information is worth the cost."

His grandfather said, very quietly, "Yes. That is the question."

The kitchen was completely still. Outside, the frost-stiff grass caught a gust of wind and shivered all at once, every blade of it at the same moment, and then went still again.

"I don't know," Cass said. "I don't know if it is."

"Neither do I," his grandfather said. "That's why I'm asking you."

---

They went down to the workshop after breakfast. The practice casting hung in its frame in the corner, the surface of it dull in the winter light from the single high window. Cass stood in front of it and looked at the crack — hairline, running from the sound bow up toward the waist, maybe four inches long. You had to know it was there to find it. He found it in about three seconds.

His grandfather handed him the striker.

"Just once," his grandfather said. "I want to hear it with fresh ears."

Cass struck the bell.

The note opened up from the metal, full and low, and then the wolf tone arrived underneath it — not a sound exactly, more like a pressure, the way you feel a bass note in your chest before you hear it in your ears. The needle drove in behind his left eye immediately. Sharp, specific, the same spot every time. He held himself still and listened past the pain.

"There," he said.

"Where."

"Under the strike note. About a half-second in. It's—" He reached for the right word. "It's not a sound. It's a feeling. Like the room is slightly the wrong size."

His grandfather was watching him. "What else."

"It beats. Against the main note. Slow — slower than I'd expect. Like—" He thought about the tuning fork demonstration his physics teacher had done last year, two forks almost but not quite the same pitch, the way the sound pulsed between them in a rhythm that made people slightly uneasy without knowing why. "Like interference. Two frequencies that almost match."

"The wolf tone and the fundamental."

"Yeah."

"And the eye."

"Still there. Fading now."

His grandfather made a note. He wrote the same way he did everything in the workshop — completely, without hurrying, as if the note deserved the same attention as the thing being noted.

"The beating pattern," his grandfather said. "How many beats per second? Roughly."

Cass closed his eyes. He replayed the sound in his head, which was a thing he'd discovered he could do — not perfectly, not with perfect fidelity, but close enough to count. "Three. Maybe three and a half."

His grandfather looked up. "You're sure."

"Pretty sure."

"Three beats per second means the frequencies are three hertz apart." His grandfather set down the pen. "That's in the infrasound range. Or the bottom edge of it."

Cass looked at him. "Like the floor of the abbey."

"Like the floor of the abbey." His grandfather picked up the pen again, turned it in his fingers. "Which means the wolf tone isn't just a casting defect. It's generating a frequency that human ears don't fully process as sound." He looked at the bell. "But apparently some people process it differently."

"Through their eye."

"Through your eye."

The bell had stopped ringing. The workshop was quiet — the particular quiet of a space full of metal and wood, a quiet that had texture to it, that absorbed sound differently than a regular room. Cass's eye was a low throb now, fading toward the background level it held all the time.

"My great-grandfather's note," Cass said. "The first pour. Did he ring it before he melted it back?"

His grandfather looked at him.

"I'm asking because if he rang it, and if he had headaches—" Cass stopped. Reformulated. "Was there anything in the ledger about why he melted it back? Not just the numbers. A reason."

His grandfather was still looking at him with an expression Cass couldn't quite read. Not surprise. Something more like recognition, the look of seeing something arrive that you'd been watching for without being sure it would come.

"Turn to page forty-seven," his grandfather said. "The ledger's on the bench."

Cass went to the bench. The ledger was sitting open to the Aldermast pages, and he turned forward carefully, the fragile pages catching the light. Page forty-seven was near the back. His great-grandfather's handwriting, the same small vertical script.

At the bottom of the page, below a column of measurements, a single sentence:

*The bell knows something I do not yet know, and until I understand what it knows, I cannot in conscience offer it to anyone.*

Cass read it twice. He stood there with the ledger in both hands and read it a third time, and the needle behind his eye pulsed once, softly, like a word spoken too quietly to hear.

He carried the ledger back to his grandfather and held it open to page forty-seven. His grandfather read it, or read it again — from his expression, he'd read it before, many times, probably had it memorized, but he read it again anyway.

"He melted it back," Cass said, "because he didn't understand what it was telling him yet."

"Yes."

"Not because it was broken. Because it wasn't finished teaching him."

His grandfather looked at him for a long moment. Then he said something Cass hadn't expected — not a confirmation, not an elaboration, but a question, and a question asked in the voice of a man who genuinely didn't know the answer.

"Do you think you're finished learning from it?"

Cass looked at the bell. The crack caught the light from the high window at a specific angle, thin and bright, a line that was almost beautiful in the way that precise damage is sometimes beautiful, the way a clean fracture in stone shows the grain of the thing.

"No," he said.

"Then we don't melt it back yet."

"Even if it keeps hurting."

"That," his grandfather said, "is your decision. Not mine."

Cass stood with the ledger and looked at the bell and felt the low steady throb behind his eye that was always there now, the string still vibrating from the last note, and he thought about what it meant that his great-grandfather had stood in a workshop probably not very different from this one and decided that a bell that hurt him was still worth listening to.

He set the ledger down on the bench, spine-up, open to page forty-seven.

"I want to try something," he said.

His grandfather waited.

"I want to ring it again, but this time I want to pay attention to the beating. The interference pattern. Not the pain — I know the pain. I want to map the other thing. The frequency itself." He looked at his grandfather. "Can you write down what I say while I listen? I can't hold a pen and track it at the same time."

His grandfather picked up the pen. He pulled a fresh sheet of paper from the drawer under the bench. He positioned the paper and looked at Cass and said, simply, "Ready."

Cass picked up the striker. He held it for a moment, weight of it familiar in his hand, the worn leather of the grip warm from sitting in the workshop all morning. He looked at the crack in the bell, the hairline running from the sound bow to the waist, the thing that was wrong and was also the thing that was telling them something.

He struck it.

The note opened. The wolf arrived underneath it. The needle went in behind his left eye, precise and immediate, and he breathed through it and listened past it, and began to count.
