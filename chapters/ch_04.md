# Chapter Four

The morning came in gray.

Cass lay still for a while after he woke, listening to the rain work its way down from the roof into the gutters. His eye had settled overnight into something low and constant — not sharp, just present, the way a bruise announces itself when you press it. He pressed it anyway, with two fingers, and felt the answering flare. Then stopped.

He got up.

Downstairs, his mother was already in the kitchen. She had her back to him, spooning grounds into the coffee basket, and she didn't turn when he came in. The kitchen smelled like last night's fire and the particular damp of a house that had absorbed too much rain. Cass pulled out a chair and sat.

"How's the eye."

It wasn't quite a question. His mother had a way of asking about his eye that sounded more like she was confirming something she already knew.

"Fine," he said.

She turned then, looked at him, and he saw her read the eye the way she always did — a quick assessment, corner to corner, checking for something she never named. Whatever she saw, she turned back to the coffee basket.

"You were up late."

"I fell asleep," he said. "I just fell asleep late."

She didn't argue with this. The coffee maker made its initial hissing sound, the one that meant it was deciding whether to cooperate. Cass looked at the window over the sink: rain on glass, the yard beyond it reduced to gray shapes, the apple tree at the far end nothing but a dark suggestion.

"Maret sent a note," his mother said.

Cass straightened. "When?"

"This morning. Early." She opened a drawer and came out with a folded square of paper and set it on the table in front of him. The paper was the same cream color Maret used for everything — invoices, receipts, the notes she left for his father when she wanted something ordered. Cass picked it up.

*Come when you're ready. Not before. M.*

That was all. He read it twice, then set it down.

"What did she want?" his mother said.

"She didn't say."

His mother poured two cups of coffee and brought them to the table and sat. She did not ask him anything else.  He wrapped both hands around the cup and felt the warmth come up through his palms.

Outside, the rain was starting to ease. He could hear it changing pitch on the gutters — the difference between water running in volume and water running down to nothing, a distinction he'd learned to make by ear without thinking about it.

"I'm going to the forge," he said.

"I know," she said.

He looked at her.

"You've been going to the forge," she said. "You came home last night and you were going to go back. That's not hard to work out, Cass."

He drank his coffee. It was too hot and he drank it anyway.

"Did he tell you?" he said. "About the bell."

His mother turned her cup on the table, a quarter turn, then back. "He told me your grandfather made something. That it was in the inventory wrong. That Maret found it."

"Did he tell you I rang it."

"He said you rang it."

"Did he tell you what happened."

She looked at him directly. Her eyes were the same color as his father's — a gray that went darker when she was thinking hard — and she was thinking hard now. "He said there was an under-note. He said you heard it."

Cass put his cup down. "Not just heard."

She waited.

"It did something," he said, and then stopped, because he didn't have the right word for what it had done, and he'd learned from his father that saying the wrong word for a thing was sometimes worse than saying nothing. The needle behind his eye made a brief appearance, as if to remind him it was still there. He touched the corner of his eye without meaning to.

His mother watched his hand. "The eye started when you rang it?"

"After. Later." He thought about the moment in his room, the edge of sleep, the tone that arrived somewhere deeper than his ears. "I heard it again last night. Just once. The under-note, or something like it."

She was quiet for a long time. Long enough that the coffee maker finished its cycle and the kitchen settled into the sound of rain on the window.

"Your grandfather," she said, carefully, "had headaches."

Cass looked at her.

"When he was working on something difficult. The left side, usually." She held his gaze. "Your father never told you that."

"No."

"He didn't know if it was relevant." She turned her cup again. "I think he was wrong about that."

Cass sat with this for a moment. 

"Maret will know more," his mother said. "She knew him better than anyone."

He nodded.

She stood, picked up her cup, and then paused with her hand on the back of her chair. "Cass. If it gets bad — the eye — you come home. Whatever she's showing you, you come home first."

"All right."

"That's not negotiable."

"I know," he said. "All right."

She went to the window and looked out at the yard. The rain had nearly stopped. The apple tree was resolving itself back into a tree.

---

The forge in the morning was different from the forge in the afternoon.

Cass had been there at both times and the difference was not just the light, though the light was part of it — morning light came in low through the eastern window and caught the dust in the air and made the whole space look like something suspended, something mid-process. His father was already at the anvil when Cass arrived. He was working on a commission piece, a set of door hinges for the councilor's new house on the hill, and he was in that particular state of concentration that meant he registered Cass's arrival without turning around.

"Maret's in the back," his father said.

Cass hung his wet jacket on the hook by the door. The forge smelled like coal smoke and hot iron and the particular mineral sharpness that came from quenching, the hiss of metal meeting water. He could feel the heat from his father's fire across the room, a wall of warmth that the damp morning made welcome.

He went through.

Maret was at her worktable with the bell in front of her. Not mounted, not hanging — just sitting on a square of cloth, the way you'd set something down to examine it. She had a glass in her hand, the short-handled kind she used for close work, and she was looking at the bell's shoulder, the curve where the waist met the soundbow. She didn't look up when Cass came in.

"Sit down," she said.

He pulled the stool from the corner and sat across the table from her.

"You heard it again last night," she said.

He hadn't told her this. "How do you know?"

"Because you came back this morning and your eye looks like that." She set down the glass and looked at him.  "Tell me what you heard."

"I was almost asleep. It was — it wasn't through my ears. It was the same as yesterday, the under-note, except I was in my bed and the bell was here."

She nodded as if this was expected.

"Is that normal?" he said.

"For this bell?" She picked up the glass again, turned it in her fingers. "I don't know what normal is for this bell. That's why I wanted you here."

He looked at the bell.  His grandfather's work. 

"Tell me what you know about under-notes," Maret said.

"What I know from you or what I know from books."

"What you know."

Cass thought. "Every bell has a strike note and a hum note. The hum note is the one that sustains after the strike fades. And then there are partials — the tierce, the quint, the nominal — and they're supposed to be in specific relationships to the hum note, and if they're off it affects the whole sound."

"And the under-note."

"It's below the hum note. Below what you'd normally tune for. Most bells have one but it's not — it's not usually what you're listening for. It's just there."

Maret set down the glass and folded her hands on the table. "Your grandfather was listening for it."

Cass waited.

"He believed," she said, and then stopped. Started again. "He had a theory. That the under-note in a well-made bell is not a byproduct. That it's the bell's actual fundamental — the tone everything else is built on top of. The thing you hear with your body before you hear it with your ears." She looked at the bell. "He spent three years on this one. Trying to get the under-note to a specific pitch."

"What pitch."

She was quiet for a moment. "He didn't tell me. He said he'd know it when he found it." A pause. "He died before he found it."

The rain had stopped. Cass could hear his father's hammer through the wall, a steady rhythm, iron on iron.

"The note I heard," Cass said. "Last night. Was it—"

"I don't know what you heard last night." She looked at him directly. "I know what the bell sounds like to me. I've rung it. I hear the under-note — I've always heard it, it's why I kept the bell instead of selling it on. But I don't hear it later. I don't hear it in my bed." She paused. "You do."

"My mother told me this morning," he said. "About my grandfather's headaches."

Maret's expression shifted — something moved across it and then was gone. "She told you."

"She said he had them when he was working on something difficult."

"Yes."

"Were they from the bell?"

Maret picked up the glass again, set it down. "I don't know. I know he had them. I know they were worse in the last year, when he was deep into this." She touched the bell's shoulder, a brief contact, her finger on the bronze. "I know he said once that the headaches told him he was close to something."

"What was he trying to do?" Cass said. "Not the under-note — past that. If the under-note was the fundamental, what was he going to do with it?"

Maret looked at him for a long time. Long enough that he started to think she wasn't going to answer.

"He believed," she said, slowly, "that if you got the under-note right — if you found the true fundamental — the bell would do something besides ring."  "He couldn't explain it better than that. I asked him, more than once. He said it was like asking what a key does besides turn. The key turns, and then something else happens. The turn is not the point."

Cass stared at the bell.

A key turns and then something opens.

"He left notes," Maret said. "I've had them in a box since he died. I didn't know what to do with them — your father doesn't have the ear for it, and I wasn't going to send them to some university to be catalogued and forgotten." She stood, went to the shelf on the far wall, and came back with a wooden box, small enough to carry in one hand. She set it on the table next to the bell. "I want you to have them. Not today — take the bell today, take it home, ring it again. But the notes are yours."

Cass looked at the box. Then at her. "Why me."

"Because you heard it last night in your bed." She sat back down. "Your grandfather heard it in his sleep too. He wrote that down. I read it in the notes after he died and I thought — I thought it was just how he wrote, the way some people make everything sound more dramatic than it is. But you didn't know that. You came in here this morning and told me without knowing."

The hammer through the wall had stopped. His father, taking a break or moving to a different stage of the work.

Cass looked at his hands, flat on the table. His grandfather's hands, his mother had said once — the same proportion, the same tendency to go still when he was thinking.

"What if I find the pitch," he said. "What if I get the under-note to whatever he was looking for. What happens."

"I don't know."

"What do you think happens."

Maret was quiet. The forge was quiet. Then: "I think the bell opens something. I don't know what. I don't think your grandfather knew what, exactly. I think that was the point — you don't know what a door opens onto until you open it."

 

"I'll take it home," he said.

Maret nodded.

"Can I ask you something," he said.

"You can ask."

"When you said he was close to something — the headaches telling him that — did he seem—" He stopped. Started over. "Was he scared?"

Maret considered this with the same attention she'd given everything else — no quick answer, no reassurance. "He was excited," she said finally. "And he was careful. Those two things at the same time." She looked at the bell. "Whether that's the same as scared, I couldn't say. I think he thought it was important enough that scared didn't quite — " She paused. "He kept working. Whatever he felt about it, he kept working."

Cass picked up the bell. It was heavier than it looked, the way bronze always was — denser than expectation, the weight of it settling into his palms. He wrapped both hands around the waist and felt the metal, cool and slightly rough, the surface that his grandfather had made.

"The box," Maret said. "When you're ready."

"Not today," he said.

"No," she agreed. "Not today."

He carried the bell back through the forge. His father had started again — the hammer rhythm resumed, steady and particular, the sound of a man working at the pace that was natural to him. Cass stopped in the main room and stood for a moment, holding the bell, watching his father work. His father's back was to him. He was drawing out the hinge plate, moving it on the anvil with the tongs, reading the color of the iron the way Cass had seen him read it a thousand times — the precise orange that meant the metal was ready, the shift toward red that meant you were running out of time.

His father glanced back. Saw the bell in Cass's hands.

He set down the hammer and the tongs and turned around.

They stood there for a moment, the forge between them, the coal fire ticking.

"She gave it to you," his father said.

"She said I should take it home."

His father looked at the bell. His face was doing the thing it did when he was thinking something he wasn't sure how to say — the slight tension around his eyes, the stillness that looked from the outside like nothing but was actually a great deal happening in a small space.

"Your grandfather," he started. Stopped.

"I know," Cass said. "The headaches."

His father looked at him. "She told you."

"Mum told me. This morning."

His father was quiet. He looked at the bell, then at Cass, then at something in between. "I should have told you," he said. "When you first heard the under-note. I should have — I knew about the headaches and I should have said."

"It's all right."

"It's not, really." He said it simply, without making it into more than it was. "I thought — I didn't want to put something in your head that might not be related. And then your eye started and I thought I'd waited too long to say it and it was easier to—" He stopped himself. "That's not a good reason."

"It's all right," Cass said again, and meant it.

His father nodded. He picked up the tongs, then set them back down. "What did Maret tell you."

"About the notes. About what he was trying to do." Cass shifted the bell in his hands. "She thinks the bell is meant to open something. She doesn't know what."

His father looked at the bell for a long time. Then: "That sounds like him."

"You knew him," Cass said. "You knew what he was like."

"I was your age when he died." He looked up. "He was — he was very certain about some things and completely lost about others, and he never seemed to know which was which. He'd be absolutely sure about something that turned out to be wrong, and then he'd be uncertain about something that turned out to be the most important thing he ever did." A pause. "The bell, I think, was the second kind."

Cass held this. His grandfather, certain and lost in the same breath. The bell that had waited.

"I'm going to keep working on it," Cass said. "Ring it. Try to find the pitch he was looking for."

His father picked up the tongs. "I know," he said. He turned back to the fire. "Take care of your eye."

Cass carried the bell to the door and took his jacket from the hook with one hand, the bell cradled in his arm. He stood for a moment with the door half open, the morning air coming in cool and wet after the rain.

His father's hammer started again.

Cass stepped outside. The yard was wet, the path between the forge and the gate still holding puddles from the night's rain, and he picked his way through them with the bell held against his chest. He could feel it through his jacket — the weight of it, the slight cold of the bronze coming through the cloth.

At the gate, he stopped.

The under-note arrived. Not through his ears. The same channel as last night, the same depth, low and specific and gone almost before he registered it. The needle behind his eye flared once, hard, and he pressed his free hand against his eye and breathed until it faded.

He stood there at the gate with his hand over his eye and the bell against his chest, and he waited.

It did not come again.

He took his hand away. His eye throbbed, low and steady, the baseline it had settled into. The street in front of him was empty, rain-washed, the cobblestones dark with water. A starling landed on the fence post ten feet away and looked at him sideways, considered him, and flew off.

 The bell had come to him in the dark and played its single note and then gone quiet, and now he was standing in the wet street outside his grandfather's forge with the bell in his arms and the needle in his eye and no name yet for what he was walking toward.

He went home.
