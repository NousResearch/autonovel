# Chapter Thirteen

The bell Cass had broken was still in the vise.

He stood in the doorway of the workshop and looked at it — the hairline fracture he'd found three weeks ago, the bad repair his grandfather had located the week after, the deeper structural failure that lived between them. The crack ran from the rim up toward the shoulder in a line that was almost invisible until you knew where to look. Then it was the only thing you saw.

His grandfather was at the workbench, doing something with a set of calipers and a notebook. He didn't look up. Cass came in and sat on the stool and waited.

"You're late," his grandfather said.

"Six minutes."

"I know how long six minutes is."

Cass said nothing. The workshop smelled of machine oil and the particular dry-wood smell that came from the old shelving, and underneath both of those, faintly, the smell of bronze — not metallic exactly, more like the inside of a coin, something that had been held by many hands. He had started noticing that smell in the last few months. He wasn't sure if it had always been there.

His grandfather set down the calipers. "What did you do with the interval?"

"Practiced it."

"Where?"

"In my room. I used the recording."

His grandfather looked at him steadily. He had the kind of face that didn't move much when he was thinking — not closed off, just still. "And?"

"I can hold it for about forty seconds before something starts to slip."

"Which part slips?"

"The Aldemar. The silver edge goes flat."

His grandfather picked up the calipers again, turned them over in his hands, set them down in a different place. "That's because you're reaching for it. You're trying to keep it the way you'd hold water in your hands." He paused. "You can't hold water in your hands."

"I know."

"You let it move through you."

"I know," Cass said again, and then, because his grandfather's expression shifted slightly: "I mean — I understand that as a sentence. I don't know how to do it yet."

"Good. That's an honest answer." His grandfather stood up from the bench. "Come here."

---

The cracked bell was a Voss, or had been — Cass had learned to read the stamp on the shoulder, the particular serif of the foundry mark. It was smaller than the other Voss on the shelf, a different casting run, and the alloy was slightly off, his grandfather had said, which was why the repair had gone wrong in the first place. Someone had tried to fix it without understanding what they were fixing.

His grandfather stood beside the vise and looked at the bell the way he always looked at things he was about to work on — not fondly, not clinically, just fully. His full attention, which was a specific thing, different from ordinary looking.

"I want you to strike it," he said.

Cass picked up the mallet from its peg. "It'll damage it more."

"Yes."

Cass held the mallet and looked at the crack. "Then why—"

"Because I want to hear what it does." His grandfather had his arms crossed, not impatiently — it was just how he stood when he was waiting. "I've looked at it. I've measured it. Now I need to hear it. Strike it where I show you."

He pointed to a spot on the rim, two inches from the crack's lower terminus.

Cass struck it there.

The sound that came out was wrong in a way that Cass felt before he heard it — a physical thing, a vibration that went up the handle of the mallet and into his palm and wrist. The bell's voice was split, two tones that should have been one, the fracture turning what should have been a single chord into something that kept trying to resolve and couldn't. The silver edge of the Aldemar interval was in there somewhere, buried under the bad sound, trying to surface. It didn't.

And then the under-note arrived.

It came from the back of the sound, from the frequency that lived beneath the fundamental, and it hit Cass behind the left eye like a needle going in sideways. He'd felt it before — twice, briefly, in practice — but never this sharp. He made a sound he hadn't intended to make. His grandfather looked at him.

"There it is," his grandfather said. Not a question.

Cass pressed two fingers against his temple. The pain had already started to fade, but it left a residue, the way a very bright light left a shape on the inside of your eyelids. "What is that."

"The damaged fundamental. When the fracture interrupts the primary resonance, the sub-harmonic doesn't resolve. It goes somewhere else."

"Into my head."

"Into the auditory nerve, technically." His grandfather picked up a cloth from the bench and held it against the bell's surface, dampening the last of the sound. "Some people feel it as nausea. Some as pressure. For your great-uncle, apparently, it was his left ear — he lost partial hearing in it by the time he was forty. Different ears." He looked at Cass. "Different vulnerabilities."

Cass lowered his hand from his temple. The needle was gone. "He kept working anyway."

"He did."

"Did he know it was the bells? Doing that to his ear?"

His grandfather was quiet for a moment. Outside, a car passed on the street — the sound of it coming and going in the space of four seconds. "He knew," his grandfather said. "He decided it was worth knowing what the bells were telling him."

Cass looked at the cracked bell in the vise. He thought about a man he'd never met, working in a shop that might have looked like this one, losing a little of his hearing every year, keeping the broken bells because they told him things the whole ones couldn't.

"Can you fix this one?" he asked.

"I'm going to try something I haven't tried in about twenty years." His grandfather went to the shelf where the tools were arranged and began selecting things with the deliberateness of someone who had already decided what he needed and was now simply collecting it. "It may not work. The alloy is compromised at the fracture point — the original pour had a flaw, and the repair made it worse, and what I'm going to attempt is essentially reestablishing the resonant integrity without recasting." He set three tools on the bench. "If it works, the bell won't sound the same as it did originally. It will sound different. Better, possibly. Different."

"And if it doesn't work?"

"Then we'll have a bell that sounds worse than it does now, and we'll know something about why."

Cass absorbed this. "That seems like a bad outcome."

"It's an informative outcome."

"Those aren't the same thing."

His grandfather looked at him over his shoulder. Something shifted in his face — not quite a smile, not quite not. "No. They aren't."

---

They worked through the morning. Or rather, his grandfather worked, and Cass watched and handed things over when asked and tried to understand what he was seeing. The process was slow and specific — his grandfather used a tool Cass didn't have a name for to apply something to the fracture that was neither solder nor adhesive, a compound he'd mixed himself from things in unlabeled jars. The smell of it was sharp and mineral, like struck flint.

"Hold this," his grandfather said, and Cass held the bell steady in the vise while his grandfather worked along the crack with something that looked like a dental instrument. The bell was cold against Cass's palm where he braced it.

"How did you learn this?" Cass asked.

"From a man in Bruges. I was twenty-six. I spent three months in his workshop and he let me watch and occasionally handed me things, and by the end I understood about a third of what he was doing." His grandfather didn't look up from the crack. "I've figured out the rest over forty years."

"What happened to the other two-thirds?"

"Some of it I figured out eventually. Some of it I never will. Some of it he took with him when he died."

Cass held the bell and thought about knowledge that died with people. The craft of it, the specific muscle-memory and judgment that lived in one pair of hands and nowhere else. He'd read about that in books — lost techniques, things that couldn't be reconstructed from description alone. He'd thought of it abstractly. Now his grandfather was telling him that he himself carried two-thirds of a dead man's knowledge and one-third of gaps.

"Is that why you're teaching me?" Cass asked.

His grandfather was quiet long enough that Cass thought he might not answer. Then: "Partly." He set down the dental instrument and picked up a small brush. "Also because you hear things."

"Lots of people hear things."

"Not like you do." He applied the brush along the crack in a thin, precise line. "You heard the sub-harmonic in that bell on the second strike. Most people who've trained for years don't hear it until I point it out. You heard it and it hurt you and you asked what it was." He paused. "That's not nothing."

Cass didn't say anything. He wasn't sure what to say. The compliment — if it was one — had a weight to it that made him want to put it down somewhere and look at it later.

"My grandfather taught me," his grandfather said, unprompted. "His grandfather taught him. At some point the chain started — I don't know when. I don't know who the first one was." He set the brush down. "You can let go now."

Cass released the bell. His palm had gone slightly numb from the cold.

"The thing about a chain," his grandfather continued, picking up the dental instrument again, "is that it only works if someone takes the next link. It doesn't pass itself on." He looked at the crack, not at Cass. "You don't have to."

"I know," Cass said.

"I'm not asking you to decide today."

"I know that too."

His grandfather made a sound that was not quite acknowledgment and not quite dismissal, and went back to work.

---

Around noon his grandfather set everything down and went upstairs. Cass heard the refrigerator open and close, the sound of something being poured. He stayed in the workshop and looked at the cracked bell in the vise. The compound his grandfather had applied along the fracture was drying to a color that was almost the same as the bronze — slightly darker, slightly more matte. In a year, if it worked, you might not see it at all.

He picked up the mallet from where he'd set it on the bench. He didn't strike the bell. He just held the mallet and looked at the crack and thought about what his grandfather had said: *it won't sound the same as it did originally. It will sound different.*

Not restored. Changed.

He was still holding the mallet when his grandfather came back downstairs with two mugs. He set one on the bench near Cass without comment. Cass put the mallet down.

"Can I ask you something," Cass said.

"You've been asking me things all morning."

"Something different."

His grandfather sat on his stool and held his mug in both hands. Outside, the midday had gone quiet — that particular stillness of a neighborhood at noon when everyone is either at work or inside. "Go ahead."

Cass looked at the bell. "When you were learning. From the man in Bruges." He stopped, tried to find the actual question. "Did you ever feel like — like you were supposed to be doing something else? Like maybe this wasn't—" He stopped again.

"No," his grandfather said.

"Just no?"

"Just no. I never felt that." He took a drink from his mug. "But I knew people who did. Students of his who had the ability but not the — I don't know what to call it. The pull toward it."

"What happened to them?"

"They became other things. One of them is a very good architect. One of them teaches music theory somewhere in Germany." He paused. "They're not less than me. They just heard something different calling."

Cass picked up his mug. The coffee was too hot to drink yet. He held it anyway. "How do you know which one you are?"

His grandfather looked at him directly — the full-attention look, the one he usually reserved for bells. "You're fourteen."

"I know how old I am."

"I mean — you don't have to know yet. You're allowed to be fourteen and not know." He set his mug down on the bench. "The fact that you're here, though. That you keep coming back. That you heard the sub-harmonic and your first question was *what is that* instead of *can we please stop doing this.*" He picked up the mug again. "That tells me something."

"What does it tell you?"

His grandfather considered this. He had the look of a man choosing between several true things and trying to decide which one to say. "It tells me you might be the kind of person who needs to know what the broken bells are saying."

The refrigerator cycled on upstairs, a sound that traveled through the floor and into the workshop the way sounds always did in this house — through the structure of the building itself, through the wood and concrete that held everything up. Cass heard it. He heard, underneath it, the very faint sound of the Voss bell on the shelf — not struck, just present, the way tuned metal was always present, always faintly resonating with the sounds around it.

He reached out and struck the cracked bell with two fingers. Not a real strike — no mallet, no force. Just a tap.

The damaged sound came out, quieter this time. The split in it, the two tones that couldn't resolve. And underneath, just barely, the beginning of the under-note — he felt it as a flicker behind his left eye, not the full needle, just the suggestion of it.

He held still and listened until the sound was gone.

"Again," his grandfather said. Not a question. Not a command. Just the word, offered, like a door left open.

Cass tapped the bell again. The split sound. The flicker behind his eye. He held the interval — not the Voss-Aldemar interval, but this one, the interval of the broken thing, the sound that kept trying to become something whole and couldn't, not yet, maybe not ever.

His grandfather was watching him.

"I can hear what it wants to be," Cass said. He wasn't entirely sure this was true until he said it.

His grandfather was quiet for a long moment. Then: "Tell me."

"It wants the sub-harmonic to resolve. It keeps — it reaches for it and the fracture cuts it off." He tapped the bell a third time, felt the flicker, stayed with it. "It's like a word you can't remember. The sound is trying to finish itself."

His grandfather picked up the dental instrument from the bench. He looked at the compound drying along the crack. He looked at it for long enough that Cass thought he'd moved on, that the conversation was over.

Then he said: "That's exactly right."

He said it the way Cass's grandfather sometimes said things — quietly, without decoration, in a tone that made the words land with more weight than their size. Not praise exactly. Recognition. Like Cass had named something that had been waiting to be named.

The workshop held the sound of that for a moment.

Then his grandfather said: "The compound needs four hours to cure. Come back at four." He picked up the dental instrument and turned back to the workbench. "Bring the recording of the Aldemar interval. We're going to try something."

Cass stood up from his stool. He put the mug down on the bench, carefully, in the ring it had already made. He looked at the cracked bell in the vise — the bad repair, the deeper break, the compound drying along the fracture line in a color that was almost but not quite bronze.

The sound it wanted to be was still there, somewhere inside the metal. Waiting for the fracture to close, or to be told what it was now that the fracture was part of it.

He picked up his jacket from the hook by the door. His grandfather was already bent over the workbench, writing something in the notebook in his small, even handwriting. Not looking up. Not needing to.

Cass went upstairs, out through the kitchen, into the midday light. He stood on the front step for a moment. Down the street, a dog was barking at something — two short barks and then silence, the sound of a dog who'd made its point and was satisfied.

He took out his phone and set an alarm for four o'clock.

Then he sat down on the step and listened to the neighborhood, and tried to hear what was underneath it.
