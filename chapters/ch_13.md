# Chapter Thirteen: What the Shutdown Does

Jim stood in the middle of the daycare floor and listened.

Nothing.

He stood there for a while.

A working daycare had a sound you could identify from the parking lot — a frequency that came through the walls and settled something in his chest. The room knew what it was for when the dogs were in it.

The room did not know what it was for now.

He walked the perimeter. Checked the water lines, the ventilation, the things that needed checking regardless of whether anyone was here to benefit from the checking. The smell was clean. He stood at the window that looked onto the small fenced yard and looked at the yard.

Nineteen days.

He had done the work.

The room was empty.

He stood there long enough that the silence felt like something he was inside. Then he turned off the lights and locked the door.

---

The spreadsheet was on the kitchen table when he got home. Andrea had the second page open — the projections — which meant she'd been doing the same math and arrived at the same place. Gracie looked up from the corner. Cassie was somewhere in the back.

Jim sat down and pulled the laptop toward him.

The mortgage. The utilities. The insurance. The payroll they were trying to honor because the staff were people who needed to eat. And the BPOL — the Business, Professional, and Occupational License tax — which did not know about the Governor's order and did not care that the floor was empty and the revenue was zero.

He wrote down the number and looked at it.

Andrea came in from the hallway with her phone.

"I called about the BPOL," she said.

"And?"

"Thirty days, maybe. Nothing official." She sat down across from him. "It doesn't solve anything."

"No. But thirty days is thirty days."

She looked at the spreadsheet. He looked at the spreadsheet.

"We're not going to run out," Jim said. "Not yet. But the line is closer than I want it to be."

"I know where the line is."

A moment passed.

"I keep thinking there's something we should be doing," Andrea said. "Some call we should be making or some application we haven't filed yet." She shook her head. "And then I look at the list and we've already done all of it. There's nothing left on the list."

Jim looked at her.

"I know," he said.

She set her phone down. He watched her hands flatten against the table, a small deliberate gesture, like she was steadying something.

"The April fourteenth meeting," he said. "I want you to speak. Not just attend. Get up at the podium and tell them what's happened. The specific numbers."

"You think it'll change anything?"

"The Council can't make good decisions if they're working from abstractions. They need to hear it from someone who's actually on the ground." He paused. "And you're better in that room than I am."

Something passed between them that didn't need words.

"Okay," she said. "April fourteenth."

He called Dottie that afternoon. She picked up on the second ring.

"Jim." Her voice was the same — the voice from Council chambers and closing tables. "Are you calling about the Fayette Street listing?"

He almost laughed. "No, Dottie. I just wanted to talk."

"How are you holding up?"

"We're holding up."

"That's not what I asked."

He looked out the kitchen window. The backyard was bare and bright, the grass still pale yellow-green, not quite committed yet. Gracie had appeared near the fence and was investigating something with the focused attention of a dog who has found a smell and intends to understand it fully.

"It's hard," he said. "The building is empty. I went over there this morning and stood in it."

"You needed to see it."

"Yeah."

"Fifty years I've watched businesses in this town," Dottie said. "Open, close, some of them both more than once. You know what the ones that survive have in common?"

He waited.

"They don't quit. That's the whole list. They don't quit when it's hard and they don't quit when they can't see the other side of it." A pause. "The ones that close — most of them — there was a moment they decided it wasn't worth it anymore. And that was the moment."

"I'm not quitting."

"I know you're not. That's why I'm telling you. Sometimes you need to hear it from someone else." She shifted. "Andrea should speak at the fourteenth. David Leake is a good mayor. Give him something to work with."

"That's what I figured."

"Jim." Her tone went direct without going soft. "You built something for the town. Most people who try to build something in this town are building it for themselves. That's not what you did. It doesn't fix the revenue problem. But it's true."

Out the window, Gracie had found whatever the smell was and was now sitting beside it, satisfied.

"I appreciate that, Dottie."

"Come to the fourteenth. Both of you. Let them see you're serious." He heard her shift again — the sound of someone who'd said what needed saying. "Now. Is there anything I can actually help with, or did you just need to talk?"

"I just needed to talk."

"Well," she said, and he could hear the smile in it, "I've still got that Fayette Street listing, if you change your mind."

---

After he hung up he sat without opening the laptop.

The pandemic wasn't a problem competence could solve. He had built the best facility he knew how to build, had shown up in every room where showing up mattered, and the world had closed it anyway. Not because of anything he'd done. Not because of anything he'd failed to do.

That was the part that kept arriving in his chest like a wrong note. He'd spent his whole working life believing the outcome would be proportional to the effort. The dogs had never believed that. They had always known the food was coming or it wasn't, that the door would open or it wouldn't, and they waited without the particular misery of expecting fairness. He had been less honest than the dog.

He pulled the laptop toward him and opened the projections.

They had enough to operate without revenue for a while, not indefinitely. They had the extension coming, maybe — thirty days on the BPOL, small but real. They had the April fourteenth meeting. They had the building, still there, still smelling like what it was, still with the name on the sign that Andy had given them from the back seat of a car.

Andrea came back into the kitchen. She glanced at the screen.

"Show me," she said.

He walked her through it the way he walked through anything that required clarity: without softening the hard numbers or inflating the optimistic ones. She asked questions. He answered them. Gracie had come back inside and settled under the table, her weight against Jim's foot.

A narrow path. Real, not wishful.

"Okay," Andrea said.

"Okay."

She stood and went to the counter and started the small work of getting dinner together. Jim looked at the screen a moment longer, then closed the laptop.

Gracie shifted under the table. Her tail moved twice against the floor — not a greeting, just the small percussion of a dog who is comfortable and wants you to know it.

Jim reached down and put his hand on her side and felt her breathe.
