# Chapter Fifteen

## April 14, 2020

The drive took eleven minutes.

 Eleven minutes from the house on Fayette Street to the Council chambers, and she and Jim had used all of them.

 The talking was something else — the sound of two people in a car together, doing the thing people do when they are about to do a harder thing.

Jim drove. The Bradford pears were in bloom along Washington Street, that particular smell that nobody could decide whether to call sweet or wrong. Andrea had never resolved the question. Tonight it was just present, the way things are present when you are paying attention to something else.

She had her notes in her lap. She didn't need them.

---

The chambers seated maybe sixty people, but the chairs were arranged for fewer tonight, and half the faces were on a screen. Zoom had become the grammar of the world in the past four weeks, and the Council had adapted with the particular efficiency of a small body that had no choice. Mayor Leake sat at the center of the dais. To his left, the other councilmembers. The fluorescent lights did what fluorescent lights do — made everything look slightly more consequential than it was, or slightly less, depending on the meeting.

Andrea took a seat in the gallery. Jim sat beside her. She knew everyone in the room. She knew which councilmember took notes by hand and which one had a laptop open in front of him. She knew that Councilman Shannon would thank the citizens for coming, because he always did, because he understood that showing up for a town council meeting in a pandemic, or in any other circumstance, was a specific act of faith that deserved to be named.

She had been in this room before. She understood the difference between that and now.

The agenda was the budget. It had been the budget for an hour — the line items, the projections, the machinery of a town trying to understand what the next six months would look like when the last six weeks had already been impossible. The numbers on the screen were the kind of numbers that required careful looking. Andrea looked at them. She did not find them comforting.

Then Mayor Leake paused.

He looked up from the dais, and he looked at Andrea, and he said he'd like to stop there for a moment, if the Council didn't mind, because there was a business owner present who had asked to speak, and he thought it would be worth hearing from her before they continued.

Andrea walked to the podium.

The room was quiet in the specific way of rooms where everyone knows that something real is about to be said.

---

She put her notes on the lectern and did not look at them.

"Since the shutdown order," she said, "we've had zero revenue."

She let that land.

"Zero. Not reduced — zero. The dogs are home with their families, which I understand, and I'm glad they are, but the building doesn't stop costing money because the Governor said stay home. The mortgage doesn't pause. The utilities don't pause. The insurance doesn't pause."

 

"We opened in the fall," she said. "We were building something. We were — I think we were doing it right. The families who brought their dogs to us, the relationships we'd built with them — that doesn't disappear because of a shutdown order. But it goes dormant. And a business can't run on dormant relationships."

She paused. Someone on the Zoom grid shifted.

"I don't know how long we can sustain this. I want to be honest about that. I'm not here to ask you to fix something you can't fix. I'm here because you're our town and you should know what's happening in it."

Mayor Leake thanked her. She walked back to her seat. Jim's hand found her arm for a moment — not a gesture she could have described afterward, just the fact of it.

Councilman Shannon said he wanted to thank her for coming, and for sharing that, and for being part of this community.

She nodded. The budget discussion resumed.

---

What she felt in her body, sitting in that gallery chair while the Council worked through the rest of the agenda, was not relief. It was the specific physical exposure of having said the true thing in a room full of people who knew her name.  

She had not cried.

She had not thought she would cry, but she had not known for certain until she was standing there, and now she knew.

The lights buzzed faintly above the dais. One of the councilmembers on the Zoom grid had a dog visible in the background, a yellow Lab moving across the frame and then out of it, going somewhere with the mild purpose of a dog who lives in a house and has places to be.

Andrea watched it go.

---

The stimulus ordinance came up toward the end.

The discussion was careful and specific — the Council doing what small governing bodies do when they are trying to be responsible about something they cannot fully solve. 

The vote went against it.

 A thirty-day extension on a tax was a small, practical acknowledgment that the town had seen what was happening and had used the tools it had.

She wrote it in her notes: *30 days BPOL.* Underlined it once. The pen made a sound against the paper that was louder than she expected.

Jim looked at the note. He looked at her. She looked back at him.

"Okay," she said quietly.

He nodded.

---

The drive home was eleven minutes.

She had her notes in her lap again, the same notes, the same handwriting, except now there was the underline. The Bradford pears were still doing what they were doing. Route 15 was quiet at this hour, just the occasional truck moving north, the red lights dwindling.

"What do we do with thirty days," Jim said. It wasn't quite a question.

"Figure out what thirty days buys us," she said.

He drove. She looked at the road.

There was a thing she hadn't said at the podium, hadn't said to Jim in the car on the way there, hadn't said to anyone, which was that standing up in that room had cost her something she couldn't fully account for yet. She was someone people trusted with their dogs. She was the person who gave the tours, who learned the names, who sent the photos so the owners would know their dogs were fine. 

She had just stood in front of that town and said: I don't know how long we can sustain this.

Jim turned onto Fayette Street.

---

Jim pulled into the drive.

Through the front window, she could see the dogs — Cassie first, then Gracie, both of them at the glass, their breath fogging the pane for a moment before they disappeared toward the door. She could hear them before Jim had the key out, the specific percussion of two dogs who have been waiting and now understand the waiting is over.

Andy opened the door before they reached it. He was in his socks. He looked at them the way kids look at their parents when they know something important happened and are trying to read whether it went okay.

"How was it," he said.

"Fine," Andrea said.

He looked at her. He looked at Jim. 

"Did they help?"

"Some," Jim said. "They gave us some more time."

"How much time?"

"Thirty days."

Andy processed this. Gracie was pushing against his legs, trying to get past him to the people on the porch. He held her back absently, one hand on her collar, still thinking.

"Is thirty days enough?" he said.

"We'll figure it out," Andrea said. She came inside, past him, and the dogs came with her, Gracie's tail going, Cassie doing the thing she did where she pressed her whole side against Andrea's leg as if checking that Andrea was solid, was real, was actually home.

She was.

Andy let the door close behind Jim. The three of them stood in the entryway of the lit house, the dogs moving between them, and the night was outside and the calendar on the wall still had April 14 circled, and tomorrow it would be April 15.

"I'm going to make tea," Andrea said.

"Yeah, okay," Andy said.

He followed her to the kitchen. Jim followed him. The dogs came last, as they always did, sure of their welcome, sure of the house, sure of nothing else in the particular way that dogs are sure of nothing else.

The kettle went on.

 

She stood at the counter with her back to them and waited for the water.

Jim sat down at the kitchen table. Andy sat across from him. Gracie went under the table. Cassie stayed near Andrea, her chin eventually finding the floor beside the cabinet, her eyes tracking the room with the mild and patient attention of a dog who was comfortable and had nowhere to be.

The kettle ticked. The house was quiet.

Thirty days.

She had not quit.

She was not going to.

The kettle began to sound.
