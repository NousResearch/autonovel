# Chapter One: The House of Bells

The sound hit him three blocks before he reached it.

Not the bells themselves — those came later, deliberate and tuned. This was something else. A pressure at the back of the jaw, low and sustained, like a finger pressed against the hinge where bone met bone. Cass stopped walking and stood on the cobbled street with his satchel cutting into his shoulder and his breath making small clouds in the morning air, and waited to see if it would pass.

It didn't pass.

He shifted the satchel and kept walking.

The House of Bells occupied the corner of Founder's Row and the street the locals called Thread, though no map he'd ever seen used that name. It was a wide, low building of pale limestone that had gone gray with age, three stories under a copper roof that had oxidized to the particular green of old money. Above the main door, suspended from a wrought-iron bracket, hung the sign: a single bell in profile, cast in bronze, no words. You either knew what it meant or you had no business being there.

Cass was not entirely sure he had business being there.

The pain behind his left eye sharpened as he pushed through the door — a needle, quick and precise, then settling into something duller that pulsed in time with his heartbeat. He'd felt it before. Not often, and not this strong. He stood in the entryway and breathed through his nose and waited for his eyes to adjust to the dimmer light inside.

The receiving room smelled like brass polish and old wood and something underneath those things that he couldn't name — a mineral smell, dry and faintly electric, the way air smelled after lightning had come through. The ceiling was high. Along every wall, shelves held bells of every size: finger bells and handbells, tower bells in miniature, bells of bronze and copper and silver and one that appeared to be made of some dark glass he'd never seen before. None of them were ringing. None of them were moving.

The pressure in his jaw said otherwise.

"You're the Dunmore boy."

The woman who said it was standing behind a counter he hadn't noticed, which meant she was good at being unnoticed or he was rattled enough to miss the obvious. Probably both. She was older — sixty, maybe more — with close-cropped gray hair and the straight posture of someone who had decided, at some point, that slouching was a form of dishonesty. She was looking at him with the particular attention of a person taking inventory.

"Cass," he said. "Cass Dunmore."

"I know your name. I asked if you were the boy." She set down the cloth she'd been using on a small handbell. "Your father sent word yesterday. You're to begin today."

"He told me the same."

She studied him for another moment. He had the feeling she was listening to something he couldn't hear.

"I'm Maret," she said. "I run the apprentice workrooms. You'll report to me until Master Veth decides what to do with you, which will take longer than you'd like and less time than you'd expect." She came around the counter. She moved with the efficiency of someone who didn't waste motion. "Come. I'll show you where to put your things."

He followed her through a door behind the counter, down a short hallway that smelled more strongly of that electric mineral smell, and into a long room where four other apprentices were already at work. The room had high windows that let in pale morning light. Each workstation had a small forge, unlit, and a collection of tools arranged with the particular neatness of tools that had consequences if misused. The other apprentices glanced up when he entered. One of them — a girl with dark braids and ink on her left hand — held his gaze for a moment before looking back at her work.

The needle behind his eye pulsed once, hard, and he stopped walking.

Maret turned. "What."

"There's something—" He put his hand to the side of his head before he could stop himself, which was not the impression he'd wanted to make on the first morning. He dropped it. "Nothing. I'm fine."

Maret looked at him the same way she'd looked at him in the receiving room. Listening.

"What do you hear?" she asked.

"I don't—" He stopped. Because the honest answer was complicated, and he'd spent two years learning not to give it. "A pressure. Not exactly sound. Like a note held too long in a closed room."

She was quiet for a moment.

"Which room?"

He looked at her.

"You came in the front," she said. "You've been in the receiving room and this hallway and here. Which room did it start?"

"Outside," he said. "Three blocks away."

Something moved in her expression. Not surprise — she'd decided something, or confirmed something she'd already suspected. She turned and walked to the back of the workroom without explaining herself, and he followed because there was nothing else to do.

The back wall held a door he'd missed at first glance, set flush with the stone and painted the same gray. Maret put her hand flat against it — not the latch, just the surface — and held it there for a moment. Then she opened it.

The room beyond was small and unlit except for what came through the door. In the center, on a wooden stand, sat a bell.

It was roughly the size of a melon, cast in bronze that had gone very dark with age, and it had no decoration — no inscription, no relief work, none of the patterns that covered even the simplest bells in the receiving room. It was plain and very old and it was doing something that bells did not do, which was making the air around it feel thick.

"Don't touch it," Maret said.

"I wasn't going to."

"You were thinking about it."

He had been. His hand had actually moved a few inches toward it before he'd noticed. He pulled it back.

"What is it?"

"A problem," she said. "One we've had for eleven years." She studied the bell from the doorway. "It rings when it wants to. We can't silence it and we can't tune it and we can't determine what it's trying to say. Three master casters have looked at it. Two of them left the House afterward, which tells you something." She stepped back and closed the door. "The fact that you heard it from three blocks away tells me something else."

"What does it tell you?"

"That your father was right to send you here." She walked back through the workroom toward the door to the hallway. "Put your things on the bench by the window. Brennan will show you the tools. We start with alloys today."

He stood there for a moment after she left.

The girl with the ink-stained hand — Brennan, apparently — looked up from her bench. She was maybe fifteen, a year older than him, with a watchful quality about her that reminded him of cats and of people who'd grown up in houses where you learned to watch.

"It's worse on cold mornings," she said. "The ringing." She said it matter-of-factly, the way you'd say *it's worse when it rains* about a bad knee. "You get used to it. Sort of."

"Sort of."

"Well." She shrugged. "You stop noticing it as much. That's not really the same thing as used to it, but it's what we've got." She nodded toward the empty bench. "The tin is in the left drawer. Don't mix it with anything in the right drawer unless Maret tells you to. Last apprentice who did that lost some hair."

He went to the bench and set down his satchel.

The other three apprentices had not looked up again after that first glance. Two boys working together at a shared bench, both with the focused stillness of people running calculations, and another girl in the far corner whose back was to the room. Cass arranged his things the way his father had taught him: tools in order of size, left to right, materials behind. A place for everything. He'd grown up in a workshop. The arrangement was muscle memory.

His father's workshop smelled like woodsmoke and iron, not like this. The smell here was still bothering him — that dry electric quality underneath the polish and the metal. He thought it might be the bell in the back room. He thought it might be something else.

Brennan had gone back to her work, which appeared to involve carefully filing the lip of a small handbell with a tool he didn't recognize. Her movements were small and precise. She worked without looking at what her hands were doing, which meant she'd done it many times.

"How long have you been here?" he asked.

"Two years."

"Do you know what it's trying to say? The bell in the back."

She stopped filing. Set the tool down. Looked at him with an expression he couldn't fully read — not unfriendly, but careful.

"That's not really a first-morning question," she said.

"Okay."

"I'm not saying don't ask it. I'm saying—" She picked the tool back up. "Wait until you've heard it actually ring. Then ask."

He wanted to ask when that would be, but he had the feeling she'd already told him as much as she was going to. He opened the left drawer and looked at the tin.

The morning moved slowly. Maret returned after an hour and walked him through the alloy ratios — tin and copper in what proportions, at what temperatures, what the result of each variation would be in tone. She was a good teacher in the way that people who don't think of themselves as teachers are sometimes good teachers: she explained things once, precisely, and then expected you to have understood. If you hadn't, you could ask. She would explain again. But she would know you hadn't been paying attention the first time, and she would remember.

He asked two questions. Both times she gave him the same look she'd given him in the receiving room, that inventory look, and answered without editorializing.

Around midday, someone brought food into the workroom — bread and a hard cheese and dried fruit, carried in a basket by a young boy who couldn't have been more than ten. He set it on the central table without speaking and left. The apprentices gathered around it with the ease of a routine.

Cass took bread and stood slightly apart, not deliberately but because he didn't know yet how close to stand. Brennan ended up next to him anyway.

"Where are you from?" she asked.

"Dunmore Street." He caught her look. "My father named the street. Or the street named him. I've never been sure which direction it went."

"Aldric Dunmore," she said. Not a question.

"You know him."

"Everyone knows of him. He made the bell in the Hall of Assembly." She broke off a piece of cheese. "The one that rang on its own the night the old Governor died."

"He says that's a story."

"Does he."

Cass thought about the way his father said it — *that's a story, Cass, people need their stories* — and the way he always said it looking at something other than Cass's face. "I don't know. Maybe."

Brennan was quiet for a moment, eating.

"My mother says your father is the best caster in the city," she said. "She also says he should have been made a master here twenty years ago and wasn't, and that it's a shame and a politics, and that the House has been worse for it." She said it with the directness of someone repeating a thing they'd heard often enough to have decided it was true. "I'm not saying that to — I don't know. I'm just saying she says it."

Cass had heard versions of this his whole life. He'd stopped knowing how to respond to it around the time he was eight. "He doesn't seem bothered by it."

"Maybe he's good at seeming."

The needle behind his eye returned, sharp and sudden, and he put his hand to the bread he was holding and squeezed it without meaning to. The bread compressed. He made himself let go.

"It's ringing," Brennan said quietly.

He couldn't hear anything. Not with his ears. But the pressure was there, and underneath it, if he held very still, something that might have been a tone — low and unresolved, the kind of note that wanted to go somewhere and couldn't. Like a question with no grammar.

The other apprentices had gone still too. The two boys at the shared bench had put down their tools. The girl in the corner had turned around, which was the first time Cass had seen her face — she was young, maybe twelve, with large dark eyes that were fixed on the back wall.

Nobody spoke.

The pressure held for perhaps ten seconds. Then it released, all at once, and the room felt suddenly larger, and Cass realized he'd been holding his breath.

"That's the third time this week," one of the boys said. He sounded more annoyed than frightened, which Cass found either reassuring or alarming, he hadn't decided.

"Maret will log it," Brennan said. She picked up her tool again. "She logs every ring. Has a whole book of them."

"What does she do with the book?"

"Looks at it, I suppose." She resumed filing. "Looks for a pattern. Hasn't found one yet, far as I know."

Cass stood with the compressed bread in his hand and looked at the back wall and thought about his father sending him here. His father, who had not been made a master. His father, who made bells that rang on their own on significant nights and then said *that's a story.* His father, who had told him last week, sitting in the workshop after the fire had gone low, that the House of Bells needed someone who could hear what the masters couldn't, and that Cass was going to be that person whether he wanted to be or not.

*Whether you wanted to be or not* was not a thing most fathers said.

He set the bread down and went back to his bench.

The afternoon was alloys and measurements and the particular tedium of getting things exactly right when exactly right was the minimum rather than the achievement. He liked it. He'd grown up with it. The precision was not boring to him the way it was boring to some people — it was the part that mattered, the part that everything else depended on. Get the alloy wrong and the bell would ring wrong. Ring wrong and someone would hear wrong. Hear wrong and whatever the bell was trying to say would not be said.

He thought about the bell in the back room. What it was trying to say.

He thought about eleven years of it saying the same thing to no one who could understand it.

Near the end of the afternoon, Maret came back and looked at his work and said nothing, which he was fairly sure meant it was acceptable. She picked up the ratio sheet he'd been working from and studied it and set it back down.

"Your father taught you the copper-tin ratios," she said.

"Yes."

"He taught you the old ratios. The ones from before the Guild standardized."

He hadn't known there was a difference. "Is that a problem?"

"No." She set the sheet back down. "It's interesting." She looked at him for a moment with that listening expression. "Do you know why he taught you the old ratios?"

"He said they were better."

"He's right. The Guild doesn't think so." A pause. "You'll learn both here. What you do with the knowledge after that is your own business." She turned to go.

"Maret." He said it before he'd decided to.

She stopped.

"When it rang today," he said. "Did you hear it?"

She was quiet for long enough that he thought she wasn't going to answer. Then: "I heard the silence after it. That's all I've ever heard." She looked at him over her shoulder. "You heard the note itself."

"I heard something."

"Yes," she said. "You did."

She left.

He sat at the bench for a moment after the door closed, in the workroom with the other apprentices and the pale late-afternoon light coming through the high windows and the smell of brass polish and that electric mineral quality underneath. Brennan was still at her bench. The two boys had begun cleaning their tools, which apparently signaled the end of the workday. The young girl in the corner had gone back to whatever she was doing, her shoulders rounded, her attention complete.

Cass looked at his hands. His father's hands were larger, more scarred, with the particular calluses of forty years at the forge. His own were still becoming what they would be.

He thought about the bell in the back room. Ten seconds of pressure and something that might have been a note. Eleven years of it, unanswered.

He thought about his father's voice: *whether you wanted to be or not.*

He was not sure yet whether he wanted to be.

He started cleaning his tools.
