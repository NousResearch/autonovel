# Chapter Twelve: March

The news arrived the way bad news usually arrived in Haymarket — 

She read the headlines standing in the kitchen in her coat, Gracie turning circles around her feet.

*Washington State. Italy. A cruise ship.*  She put the phone down and finished with Gracie's harness. The morning was cold and bright. The schedule at A Pup's Valley was full.

She drove to Fayette Street.

---

The first week of March felt almost normal. The Okonkwos brought Biscuit in on Monday. Marcus Webb dropped off Captain on Tuesday morning with the same running commentary he always delivered — Captain had stolen a sock, Captain had discovered the bread drawer, Captain had opinions about the new mail carrier — and Jim had listened and nodded and taken the leash and Captain had gone immediately to the center of the room, where he belonged. The Delgados came Wednesday. Mrs. Delgado was wearing a mask, which Andrea had not seen before, not in this building, and 

But Mrs. Delgado had smiled — Andrea could see it in her eyes — and said Rosie had been sleeping better since they'd started the daycare schedule, and Andrea had said she was glad, and Rosie had pulled toward the floor and that had been that.

By the second week, clients were calling. Not to cancel — just to ask questions. *What are you doing about cleaning? What's your policy if someone gets sick?* Andrea answered them honestly. They were already cleaning constantly. They had always cleaned constantly.  She told them this. Most of them said okay and kept their appointments.

She was on the phone with a client on Thursday afternoon when Jim appeared in the doorway of the small office and held up his phone. She looked at the screen. She kept talking to the client for another minute, said goodbye, said they'd see Moose on Monday.

She looked at Jim.

"Governor's going to order something," he said.

"When?"

"Week, maybe two."

She turned back to her desk. There were fourteen dogs on the floor. She could hear them through the door — the specific sound of the place at mid-afternoon, the way it settled into a particular frequency when the morning energy had burned off and the dogs had sorted themselves into their preferred arrangements. Biscuit was probably by the window. Captain was probably in the middle of everything. The nervous terrier mix who'd come in for her first trial day last week — Penny, her name was Penny — had found a spot near the heating vent and seemed to have decided it was hers.

"Okay," Andrea said.

"We should think about—"

"I know." She picked up her pen. "Let me finish the schedule first."

---

The last day was a Friday.

She knew it was the last day while it was happening, which was worse than not knowing. The Governor's announcement had come two days before — a shutdown order, essential businesses only, effective in seventy-two hours. She had spent Wednesday night going through the client list and sending messages.  She had said: *we're closing as required, we hope to see you and your family soon, thank you for trusting us with your dog.*

Fourteen messages. Fourteen families.

On Friday morning they came, one by one, to pick up their dogs.

The Okonkwos came first, early, Mr. Okonkwo in his work clothes even though there was no work to go to. He took Biscuit's leash and said, "We'll see you soon." He said it like a question.

"Yes," Andrea said.

She watched him carry that small uncertainty out the door.

Marcus Webb came at ten. Captain went to him immediately, tail going, and Marcus stood there for a moment with his hand on Captain's head and said, "Okay, buddy. We're going to be home a lot, so you're going to be sick of me in about four days." He looked up at Andrea. "This is going to be weird."

"It's going to be weird," she agreed.

The Delgados came at noon. Rosie had been sleeping in her corner all morning, and when Mrs. Delgado came through the door Rosie got up and stretched and walked over at a pace that was not quite a run but was not casual either —  Mrs. Delgado crouched down and let Rosie put both paws on her knees, and then she looked up at Andrea and said, "Thank you. For — all of it. These last few months." She stopped. "I mean it."

"I know you do," Andrea said.

By two o'clock the floor was half empty. By three-thirty it was nearly clear. Penny was the last dog left, her owner stuck in traffic, and Andrea sat on the floor next to her and Penny put her head in Andrea's lap with the complete trust of an animal who had decided, in the three weeks since her trial day, that this was a safe place and this was a safe person.

The owner came at four-fifteen, apologetic, coat half-buttoned, and gathered Penny up and said, "I don't know what I'm going to do with her all day," and Andrea said, "She'll be okay," and the owner said, "I know, I know, I just—" and stopped and said, "Thank you," and left.

The door closed.

The building went quiet.

 Andrea stood in the middle of the floor and the quiet pressed in from all sides.

She turned off the lights. She locked the door.

---

The building on Fayette Street did not stop costing money because the Governor said stay home.

This was the specific fact that kept arriving at the kitchen table in the evenings, no matter how many times they'd already looked at it. The lease payment was fixed. The utilities ran whether or not there were dogs on the floor to generate them. The insurance didn't pause. The loan they'd taken to build out the facility did not enter a period of reflection.

The revenue was zero.

Not reduced. Not diminished. Zero was the number, precise and total, and it had been zero for eleven days when Jim spread the spreadsheet across the table on a Wednesday night and they sat down to go through it.

Cassie was under the table. Gracie was on her bed in the corner, chin on her paws, watching them with the mild interest of a dog who has decided that whatever the humans are doing, it is probably not dangerous but might produce food.

"Thirty days," Jim said.

Andrea looked at the column. "Sixty?"

"Not good."

"How not good?"

He turned the laptop so she could see. She looked at it for a while. The numbers were accurate — she knew they were accurate because they had built this spreadsheet together and she knew every line in it. The mortgage on the house. The lease on Fayette Street. The insurance. The loan. The phone. The utilities. The staff — two part-time people who were not there right now because there was nothing for them to do, which meant they were not being paid, which was its own separate problem that Andrea was not going to solve tonight.

The numbers did not improve from looking at them.

"What about the SBA thing?" she said.

"Applied. Waiting."

"How long does waiting take?"

Jim looked at her. "I don't know."

She got up and poured herself a glass of water. Stood at the sink for a moment looking out the window at the backyard, which was dark. Gracie came and sat beside her, not asking for anything, just being there, which was what Gracie did.

Andrea reached down and put her hand on Gracie's head.

She came back to the table.

"Okay," she said. "What else?"

Jim pulled the spreadsheet back. "There's the EIDL. That's separate from the SBA loan. I've been reading about it. The interest rate is better."

"Okay."

"And there's going to be some kind of local thing. I've been watching the Council schedule. They're meeting — there's a special meeting on the fourteenth."

"Of April?"

"Yeah."

She looked at the spreadsheet again. April 14 was twenty-three days away. She did the math without meaning to — twenty-three days at zero revenue, against the column of fixed costs that didn't move.

"We should be there," she said.

"Yeah."

"I mean — we should say something. Not just be there."

Jim was quiet for a moment. He looked at the numbers. He looked at her.

"You should say something," he said. "You're better at it."

"Okay," she said.

Cassie emerged from under the table, stretched, and put her nose on Jim's knee. He reached down without looking and scratched behind her ear. Cassie's eyes went half-closed with the specific satisfaction of a dog receiving exactly what she wanted.

"We're not quitting," Andrea said. She didn't mean it as a statement of resolve. She meant it as a clarification of the facts, the same way she'd told Mrs. Delgado that Rosie was going to be okay. It was true, and it needed to be said, but it wasn't dramatic.

"No," Jim said.

He turned back to the spreadsheet. She pulled it toward her so she could see the sixty-day column again.

They kept going through the numbers.

---

In the mornings, she fed Cassie and Gracie.

She did this because it was the thing to do and because the dogs required it regardless of what the Governor had ordered. She measured out the food — one and a half cups each, which she had been doing so long her hand knew the weight of it before the scoop hit the bag — and the dogs waited with the focused patience of animals who have learned that the food is coming and patience is the correct response.

She set the bowls down and watched them eat.

This was still the work. The building was closed and the revenue was zero and the spreadsheet on the kitchen table said things she didn't want to look at, but this — the bowls, the food, the two dogs eating with complete unselfconscious commitment — this was real.  The fact that a dog needed care and someone was here to provide it.

She thought about Penny. She thought about the way Penny had put her head in her lap on that last afternoon, the weight of it, the trust in it. 

But Andrea was thinking about Fayette Street.

She was thinking about all fourteen of them — Biscuit, Captain, Rosie, Penny, the others, each name attached to a specific animal with a specific personality and a specific way of moving through the room. She knew which ones needed a slow introduction to new dogs and which ones went straight to the center of things. She knew which ones ate too fast if you let them and which ones were indifferent to food but obsessed with the water bowl. She knew their triggers and their preferences and the particular way each one settled when they decided the room was safe.

That knowledge was still in her. All of it. It hadn't gone anywhere.

But they were gone, and the building was closed, and there was nothing to do with the knowledge except hold it.

Cassie finished eating and sat down and looked at her. The look was simple and complete: *I ate. What's next?*

"I don't know yet," Andrea said.

Cassie accepted this and went to her bed.

---

On a Tuesday afternoon in the third week of March, Andrea drove past the building.

She wasn't planning to. She was going to the grocery store on Route 15, which did not require going down Fayette Street, but she turned anyway and drove slowly past and looked at it through the passenger window.

The sign was there. *A Pup's Valley.* Andy's name, in the letters they'd chosen, on the building they'd built. The parking lot was empty. The lights were off inside. The building looked like what it was — closed, quiet, waiting.

She had stood at that sign when they'd first put it up and thought: *this is what we said we would do, and we did it.* The feeling had been specific and clean, not triumphant exactly, more like the satisfaction of a true thing being confirmed. They had said they would build this in this town, and here it was, and the name on it was the name a child had given them from the back seat of a car, which meant the name was right in a way that no amount of strategy could have produced.

The building still looked like that. The sign still said that. None of that had changed.

She drove to the grocery store. She bought what they needed. She drove home.

Jim was at the kitchen table with the laptop open when she came in, and she set the bags down and he looked up and she said, "I drove past."

"How's it look?"

"Like ours," she said.

He looked at her for a moment. 

"Yeah," he said.

She put away the groceries. Gracie supervised from the doorway. Cassie was somewhere in the back of the house, and Andrea could hear her moving around, the soft sound of paws on hardwood, the small ordinary noise of a dog going about her business in a house where someone was home.

The building was closed. The revenue was zero. The kitchen table held the spreadsheet and the numbers and the questions that didn't have answers yet.

But the name was on the building. It was still on the building. And in twenty-three days they were going to go to the Council meeting, 

She closed the cabinet door.

"What time does the meeting start?" she said.

"Seven," Jim said. "April fourteenth."

She nodded. She picked up the pen from the counter and wrote it on the calendar that still hung by the refrigerator, the one with the dog photos that the Okonkwos had given them as an opening gift, a golden retriever grinning in March and a border collie in April and the whole year ahead of it still full of blank squares.

*April 14. Town Council.* She pressed the pen down a little harder than she needed to.

The square was small. The words fit.
