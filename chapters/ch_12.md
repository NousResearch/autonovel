# Chapter Twelve: March

She read the headlines standing in the kitchen in her coat, Gracie turning circles around her feet. *Washington State. Italy. A cruise ship.* She put the phone down and finished with Gracie's harness. The morning was cold and bright. The schedule was full.

---

The first week of March felt almost normal. The Okonkwos brought Biscuit in on Monday. Marcus Webb dropped off Captain on Tuesday with the same running commentary — Captain had stolen a sock, Captain had discovered the bread drawer, Captain had opinions about the new mail carrier — and Jim had listened and taken the leash and Captain had gone immediately to the center of the room, where he belonged. Mrs. Delgado came Wednesday in a mask, which Andrea hadn't seen before, not in this building. But she'd smiled — Andrea could see it in her eyes — and said Rosie had been sleeping better since they'd started the daycare schedule.

By the second week, clients were calling. Not to cancel. Just to ask. *What are you doing about cleaning?* Andrea answered honestly. They had always cleaned constantly. Most of them kept their appointments.

She was on the phone Thursday afternoon when Jim appeared in the office doorway and held up his phone. She looked at the screen. She kept talking to the client, said they'd see Moose on Monday, hung up.

"Governor's going to order something," Jim said.

"When?"

"Week, maybe two."

Through the door she could hear the floor at mid-afternoon — the frequency the place settled into once the morning energy burned off, dogs sorted into their preferred arrangements. Biscuit probably by the window. Captain in the middle of everything. Penny, the nervous terrier mix, had claimed the spot near the heating vent and seemed to have decided it was hers.

"Okay," Andrea said.

"We should think about—"

"I know." She picked up her pen. "Let me finish the schedule first."

---

The last day was a Friday. She knew it while it was happening.

The Governor's announcement had come two days before. She'd spent Wednesday night going through the client list. *We're closing as required, we hope to see you and your family soon, thank you for trusting us with your dog.* Fourteen messages. Fourteen families.

The Okonkwos came first, Mr. Okonkwo in his work clothes even though there was no work to go to. He took Biscuit's leash and said, "We'll see you soon." He said it like a question.

"Yes," Andrea said.

She watched him carry that small uncertainty out the door. Marcus Webb came at ten. Captain went to him immediately, tail going, and Marcus stood there with his hand on Captain's head and said, "Okay, buddy. We're going to be home a lot, so you're going to be sick of me in about four days." He looked up at Andrea. "This is going to be weird."

"It's going to be weird," she agreed.

The Delgados came at noon. Rosie had been in her corner all morning, and when Mrs. Delgado came through the door Rosie got up and walked over at a pace that was not quite a run — Mrs. Delgado crouched and let Rosie put both paws on her knees, and then she looked up at Andrea and said, "Thank you. For all of it. These last few months." She stopped. "I mean it."

"I know you do," Andrea said.

By two o'clock the floor was half empty. By three-thirty it was nearly clear. Penny was the last dog left, her owner stuck in traffic, and Andrea sat on the floor next to her and Penny put her head in Andrea's lap with the complete trust of an animal who had decided, in three weeks, that this was a safe place and this was a safe person.

The owner came at four-fifteen, coat half-buttoned, gathered Penny up and said, "I don't know what I'm going to do with her all day."

"She'll be okay," Andrea said.

"I know, I just—" A stop. "Thank you."

The door closed.

Andrea stood in the middle of the floor. She turned off the lights. She locked the door.

---

The building on Fayette Street did not stop costing money because the Governor said stay home.

This was the fact that kept arriving at the kitchen table, no matter how many times they'd already looked at it. The lease payment was fixed. The utilities ran. The loan they'd taken to build out the facility did not pause. The revenue was zero — precise and total — and it had been zero for eleven days when Jim spread the spreadsheet across the table on a Wednesday night.

Cassie was under the table. Gracie was on her bed in the corner, watching them with the mild interest of a dog who has decided the humans are not in danger but might produce food.

"Thirty days," Jim said.

Andrea looked at the column. "Sixty?"

"Not good."

"How not good?"

He turned the laptop. She looked at it for a while. The mortgage on the house. The lease. The insurance. The loan. The phone. The utilities. The two part-time staff who weren't there because there was nothing for them to do, which was its own separate problem she wasn't going to solve tonight.

The numbers didn't improve from looking at them.

"What about the SBA thing?" she said.

"Applied. Waiting."

"How long does waiting take?"

Jim looked at her. "I don't know."

She got up and poured a glass of water. Stood at the sink looking out at the dark backyard. Gracie came and sat beside her, not asking for anything. Andrea put her hand on Gracie's head, then came back to the table.

"What else?" she said.

"There's the EIDL — separate from the SBA loan, better interest rate. And there's going to be some kind of local thing. There's a special Council meeting on the fourteenth."

"Of April?"

"Yeah."

Twenty-three days at zero revenue against a column of fixed costs that didn't move. She did the math without meaning to.

"We should say something," she said. "Not just be there."

Jim was quiet. He looked at the numbers. He looked at her.

"You should say something," he said. "You're better at it."

"Okay."

Cassie emerged from under the table, stretched, put her nose on Jim's knee. He scratched behind her ear without looking. Cassie's eyes went half-closed.

"We're not quitting," Andrea said. She meant it as a clarification of facts, the same way she'd told Mrs. Delgado that Rosie was going to be okay. True, and it needed saying, but not dramatic.

"No," Jim said.

He pulled the spreadsheet back. She pulled it toward her so she could see the sixty-day column. They kept going through the numbers.

---

In the mornings she fed Cassie and Gracie.

She measured out the food — one and a half cups each, which she had been doing so long her hand knew the weight of it before the scoop hit the bag — and the dogs waited with the focused patience of animals who have learned that the food is coming and patience is the correct response.

She set the bowls down and watched them eat.

All fourteen of them were still in her — Biscuit, Captain, Rosie, Penny, the others, each name attached to a specific animal with a specific way of moving through the room. Which ones needed a slow introduction to new dogs. Which ones ate too fast if you let them. Their triggers, their preferences, the particular way each one settled when they'd decided the room was safe. That knowledge hadn't gone anywhere.

Cassie finished and sat down and looked at her. *I ate. What's next?*

"I don't know yet," Andrea said.

Cassie accepted this and went to her bed.

On a Tuesday in the third week of March, Andrea drove past the building. She wasn't planning to — the grocery store on Route 15 didn't require going down Fayette Street — but she turned anyway and drove slowly past and looked through the passenger window.

The sign was there. *A Pup's Valley.* The parking lot was empty. The lights were off. The building looked like what it was: closed, waiting.

She drove to the grocery store. She bought what they needed. She drove home.

Jim was at the kitchen table with the laptop when she came in. She set the bags down and he looked up.

"I drove past," she said.

"How's it look?"

"Like ours," she said.

He looked at her for a moment. "Yeah," he said.

She put away the groceries. Gracie supervised from the doorway. Somewhere in the back of the house Cassie moved room to room, paws on hardwood, the small ordinary sound of a dog going about her business.

Andrea closed the cabinet. Picked up the pen from the counter. The calendar still hung by the refrigerator — the one the Okonkwos had given them as an opening gift, a golden retriever grinning in March, a border collie in April, the whole year ahead still full of blank squares.

*April 14. Town Council.* She pressed the pen down a little harder than she needed to.

The square was small. The words fit.
