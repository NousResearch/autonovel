# Chapter 19: The Dogs Come Back

The Governor's modified order came through on a Tuesday, and by Wednesday morning Andrea had the door unlocked at six forty-five, fifteen minutes before she'd told anyone to arrive.

She stood in the entry and breathed it in. The building had the particular smell of a place that had been cleaned and not used: floor solution, the faint mineral edge of the washing station, and underneath those, something warmer and older that the cleaning products couldn't reach — the accumulated presence of hundreds of dogs over months of operation, the smell that had no name but that every regular client recognized the moment they walked in, the smell that said *this is a place where dogs have been happy*.

She had kept the building clean all through the shutdown. Every week, sometimes twice a week, she had come in alone and scrubbed and mopped and wiped down surfaces that had nothing on them, because the alternative was letting it go, and she couldn't let it go. Jim had asked her once, in March, why she bothered. She hadn't answered him right away. She'd thought about it for a day and then said: *Because when they come back, I want it to be right*. He'd nodded and hadn't asked again.

The restrictions were real. Capacity limits. Drop-off windows instead of lingering hellos. No tours during active daycare hours, which meant she'd have to schedule them separately, early or late. The Governor's order modified, not lifted — the business could open, but it had to open as a different version of itself, at least for now. She had read the order four times. She understood it. She had built the new intake schedule around it, printed the new protocols, taped the new signage to the door.

She knew which families would come back first.

---

The first car pulled into the lot at seven-oh-three. A white SUV she recognized from the angle of its approach before she could read the plate. The Hendersons. Of course. Their chocolate lab, Biscuit, was a Monday-Wednesday-Friday regular who had been coming since October, two months after they'd opened, and Mrs. Henderson had texted Andrea in April just to say she hoped everyone was okay, not asking about reopening dates, just checking in. That text had cost Andrea something she couldn't have named — the unexpectedness of it, the way it arrived on a morning when the numbers had been particularly bad.

She was at the door when the car stopped.

Biscuit came out of the back seat like he'd been launched. He hit the end of the leash and then turned, immediately, toward the building, and his whole body said *yes, this, here*, and Mrs. Henderson was laughing and apologizing and Andrea was already crouching to let him reach her.

"He's been a menace," Mrs. Henderson said. "I love him, but he's been a complete menace. We walk him twice a day and it's not enough."

"I can see that." Andrea had both hands on Biscuit's sides and he was wriggling in place, not barking, just radiating urgency. "He looks great. He's been well."

"He's been bored out of his mind. So have we, honestly." Mrs. Henderson handed over the leash. "Thank you for — I mean. Thank you."

Andrea straightened up. Biscuit was already pulling toward the interior door, having satisfied himself that the building was real and that he was actually here.

"We're glad to have him back," Andrea said. "We'll text you at noon."

She watched the white SUV pull out of the lot. Then she turned and walked Biscuit inside, and the building was no longer empty.

By nine o'clock she had eleven dogs.

---

Jim had been watching for the shepherd mix.

Her name was Rue. She belonged to a family on the other side of Gainesville who'd found A Pup's Valley through a neighbor's recommendation and started bringing her in January, six weeks before the shutdown. In those six weeks she had gone from pressing herself against the far wall to running the perimeter of the daycare floor with three other dogs like she'd been doing it her whole life. Jim had been thinking about her through April and May and June and into the fall — not with worry, exactly, but with the specific anticipation of an unfinished thing. She had been mid-transformation when the building closed. He wanted to see where she'd landed.

The Garcias' car arrived at ten-fifteen. Mr. Garcia got out and opened the back door and Rue jumped down and immediately looked at the building. Her ears came up. Her tail went straight.

She remembered.

Jim was at the drop-off window. He watched her cross the parking lot on the leash, her gait quick and purposeful, and when she came through the door she made a single circle of the entry and then went directly to Jim and pushed her head against his thigh.

He put his hand on the back of her neck. She was trembling, but not with fear — with the particular vibration of a dog who has been waiting for something and has finally arrived at it.

"She talked about this place the whole drive," Mr. Garcia said. "I know that sounds insane."

"It doesn't." Jim scratched behind her ear. "She was in good shape when we had to close. She's in good shape now. That's good."

"She's been — yeah. She's been fine. We've been working with her at home. But this is different. You can see it."

"I can see it."

Rue went through the interior door and into the daycare floor and the sound that came back was not barking. It was the sound of a dog who knew where she was.

---

By the end of the first week Andrea had given six tours.

Four of them were families she'd never met — people who had found A Pup's Valley during the shutdown through word of mouth, through the virtual check-ins, through the posts she'd kept making on the business page all through the spring and summer even when there was nothing to announce, even when the posts were just photos of Cassie and Gracie in the backyard with a note that said *still here, thinking of you all*. One family had driven past the building every day during their morning walk and finally looked it up when the sign in the window changed. Another had called in April and been told they were closed and called again in September and been told the same thing, and they'd called again in December, and here they were.

The tours were different now. She couldn't walk a family through during active hours — she scheduled them at six-thirty in the morning, before the first drop-off, or at seven in the evening, after the last pickup. The building was quieter then. She walked them through the daycare floor and the grooming area and the boarding spaces and explained the cage-free model the way she always had, the same words in roughly the same order, because the explanation was true and she had found the truest version of it through repetition.

What was different was what she said at the end of the tour, when they were standing back in the entry and the family was deciding whether to sign up.

She used to say: *We know your dog's name. We know what they need. That's the whole thing.*

She still said that. But now she added something she hadn't said before: *We were closed for eight months. We kept this building clean the entire time, because we knew we were coming back, and we wanted it to be right when we did. That's how we operate.*

She didn't plan to say it. It came out during the second tour, on a Thursday morning, to a couple with a border collie mix named Hank, and after she said it she noticed that they both nodded in a way that was slightly different from the ordinary nod of a person processing information. It was the nod of people who had heard something that confirmed what they'd already suspected. She kept saying it after that.

---

The clients who came back noticed the smell.

Not one of them said anything about it. Not directly. But Andrea watched them, the first week — watched them come through the door and pause, just for a moment, and breathe, and then relax in the particular way that people relax when the thing they were slightly worried about turns out to be fine. The building was clean. It smelled like a place where someone had been paying attention. They noticed and they didn't say anything and she understood that their silence was a kind of verdict.

She had mopped that floor alone, in an empty building, on a Tuesday in May when the numbers were at their worst. She had not been thinking about future clients then. She had been thinking about the floor. But the two things turned out to be the same thing, and she had known, on some level, that they would be.

---

Friday of the first week back, Jim came in at four-thirty and found her in the back office with the week's numbers spread on the desk. She had two columns: what they'd had in the last week of February, before the shutdown, and what they'd had this week.

He sat down across from her.

"Forty-three percent," she said.

He looked at the columns. "Of February."

"Of the last full week of February. Which was a good week." She tapped the right column. "But look at the trajectory. Monday was twenty-eight percent. Friday is sixty-one."

"Because people came back mid-week."

"Because people came back mid-week. And some of them brought referrals." She put her pen down. "The Hendersons brought the Nguyens. The Nguyens have a lab mix and a beagle. Both enrolled."

Jim was quiet for a moment, reading the numbers. She watched him do the math she'd already done.

"We're not back," she said. "I'm not saying we're back. Some of the March families — the Hollanders, the Petersons — I don't know yet. I've texted. I haven't heard back. They might have found somewhere else."

"They might have."

"And the capacity restrictions mean we can't take on as many new dogs as we could in February, even if everyone called tomorrow." She looked at the column on the left. "So we're not going to be back in six weeks. That's not what the numbers say."

"What do the numbers say?"

She looked at him. "They say it's the start of something."

He held her gaze for a moment. "Yeah," he said. "That's what they say."

Outside the office window, the last two dogs of the day were still in the daycare floor — a golden retriever named Cooper who always stayed late because his owner worked in Tysons and traffic was unpredictable, and Rue, who Mr. Garcia had picked up an hour ago but whose presence Andrea still felt in the room the way you feel the presence of something that has just left. The sound of Cooper moving around the floor came through the wall, his nails on the clean floor, and the refrigerator in the break room ran, and the building was not empty.

"The Hollanders will come back," Jim said.

"You don't know that."

"No." He looked at the numbers again. "But they will."

She picked up her pen. There was a third column she'd been building, smaller, in the margin — the families who had reached out during the shutdown not to ask about reopening but just to check in. Mrs. Henderson's text. The Garcias' Christmas card with a photo of Rue in a Santa hat. Three other families who had done variations of the same thing. She had not put this column in the formal accounting. It wasn't a financial number. But it was a number.

"We should probably talk about staffing," she said.

"We should."

"Not tonight."

"No," he said. "Not tonight."

She capped the pen and straightened the columns and set them in the folder she kept on the right side of the desk, the one labeled *Current*, and she put the folder in the drawer and closed it.

"Cooper's going to be here another forty minutes," she said.

"I know. I'll stay with him."

She stood up and he stayed seated, and she put her hand on the back of his neck the way she'd seen him put his hand on the back of Rue's neck that morning — not as a gesture she was thinking about, just as the thing her hand knew to do — and then she got her jacket from the hook and her keys from the counter and she left him there with the numbers in the drawer and Cooper on the clean floor and the building around them, smelling like itself again, smelling like the place they had kept clean through all those empty months because they were coming back and they wanted it to be right.

---

The second week, the Hollanders came back.

She was at the front desk when their car pulled in, and she recognized it before she recognized them, and she stood up without deciding to stand up. Their dog was a three-year-old standard poodle named Archie who had been coming since November of the previous year, one of their early regulars, and he had the particular personality of a dog who treats every arrival as a professional engagement — he came through the door and immediately assessed the room with the focused intelligence of an animal who takes his work seriously.

Mrs. Hollander looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with the hour.

"We found another place," she said. "During the shutdown. It was fine. It was — it was fine." She looked at Archie, who was already making his assessment of the daycare floor through the interior door. "It wasn't this."

Andrea said, "We're glad you're back."

"I looked at your page. All those months. You kept posting."

"We did."

"Archie's better here," Mrs. Hollander said. "I don't know how to explain it. He just is."

Andrea took the leash. Archie looked up at her with his professional expression and she looked back at him and said, "Good morning," and he accepted this as appropriate and turned toward the interior door, ready to begin.

---

By the end of the second week they were at seventy-eight percent of the last full week of February.

Jim did not say *I told you so* about the Hollanders. This was not restraint — it simply did not occur to him, because he had not been predicting so much as observing. He had watched how those families had behaved during the shutdown, the ones who texted and called and sent Christmas cards, and he knew what that behavior meant about what would happen when the door was open again. It was not magic. It was pattern recognition.

What he said, on the Friday of the second week, was: "Next year."

She looked up from the desk. "Next year what?"

"Best of Prince William. We should submit."

She was quiet for a moment. The idea had crossed her mind in a different form — not as a plan but as a direction, the way you can feel the direction of something before you can see it. They had been building toward something since they opened, and the shutdown had interrupted the building, and now the building was resuming, and the direction was the same as it had always been.

"We haven't been open long enough," she said. "I mean — technically. I don't know what the submission requirements are."

"I looked. We're eligible."

She looked at him. He had the expression he wore when he had already thought something through and was presenting it as a question because he wanted to hear what she thought, not because he was uncertain.

"Okay," she said.

"Okay."

She turned back to the desk. The numbers for the week were in front of her, and they were better than the numbers for the week before, and the week before had been better than the week before that, and the folder in the drawer said *Current* and the drawer closed cleanly and the floor was clean and Rue had come back and Archie had come back and the Hendersons had brought the Nguyens and the building smelled like itself.

She picked up her pen. She wrote *Best of Prince William — research submission* at the bottom of the yellow pad where she kept the daily to-do list, and then she drew a small box next to it, the way she always did, so she could check it off when it was done.

The box waited. She would fill it in.
