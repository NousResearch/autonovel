# Chapter Nineteen

The diner smelled like the inside of a coffee urn and something that might have been bacon grease from 1987, preserved in the walls.

Cass slid into the booth and looked at the laminated menu without reading it. The laminate had gone yellow at the edges. Outside the window, the parking lot held five cars and a semi with its engine running, and the light was still that flat gray-white that wasn't quite morning and wasn't quite anything else.

His father sat across from him, reading his own menu. The drive had been an hour and a half. Neither of them had talked much after the first twenty minutes, which was fine. The radio had done the talking.

"They have eggs," his father said.

"I see that."

"You want eggs?"

Cass looked at the menu. Scrambled, fried, poached. An omelet with three kinds of cheese he'd never heard of combined. "Maybe. I don't know yet."

His father put the menu down. He'd already decided. He always decided fast.

The waitress came and his father ordered coffee and eggs over easy with toast. Cass asked for the same thing because it was easier than thinking about it, and the waitress wrote it down without looking up and went away, and the diner went back to its own sounds: the semi idling, the coffee machine doing something complicated, two men in a booth near the back talking about a truck that needed a new something.

The needle was still there. Behind his left eye, not sharp now, more like a memory of sharpness. It had been there since the night before, since the record and the headphones and the moment the under-note had arrived and his whole skull had gone tight around it. He pressed two fingers to his temple. The pressure didn't help but it gave him something to do.

"You okay?" his father said.

"Yeah."

"Headache?"

"Kind of. Not bad."

His father looked at him for a moment. Not the worried kind of looking — the assessing kind, the kind that meant he was deciding whether to ask more or leave it. He left it. Cass was grateful.

The coffee came. His father wrapped both hands around the mug, which was thick white ceramic with a small chip in the rim. Cass poured sugar into his from the dispenser on the table and watched it dissolve.

"Grandpa was up before us," Cass said.

"He's always up before everyone. He was probably up before it was dark."

"He packed the bag."

"Yeah." His father drank his coffee. "He does that."

Cass thought about the waxed paper packet, the careful wrapping of the cheese. His grandfather hadn't said anything about it. Hadn't made a point of it. Just put the bag in the back seat the way you put a thing somewhere when you want it to be there without being thanked for it.

"Did he used to do that? When you were a kid?"

His father set the mug down. "Do what?"

"Pack things. Like — when you were leaving."

Something moved in his father's face, not quite a smile. "He used to make sandwiches. Every road trip. Turkey and mustard, always the same. He'd wrap them in foil." He turned the mug in his hands. "I don't think I ever told him they were good."

Cass looked out the window. The semi's engine changed pitch slightly, a low adjustment, then settled.

"They were probably good," Cass said.

"Yeah. They were."

The eggs came. Cass ate. The eggs were fine — not good, not bad, the kind of eggs that exist to be eaten rather than tasted. The toast was white bread gone tan. He ate that too. His father ate steadily and without comment, the way he ate most things, like eating was a task he was competent at and saw no reason to dramatize.

Halfway through, his father said, "Your grandfather wants you to have the Brahms."

Cass stopped.

"The record. He told me last night, after you went to bed. He said — " His father paused, not searching for words exactly, more like making sure he had the right ones. "He said you heard it the way it was supposed to be heard."

The needle behind Cass's eye gave a single hard pulse and then went quiet again. He put his fork down.

"He doesn't have to do that."

"I know he doesn't have to."

"That record is — " Cass stopped. He didn't know how to finish it. The record was old. The record was his grandfather's. The record had a scratch at bar forty-something of the second movement and his grandfather had said *that's just how it sounds now* and Cass had heard it and known exactly what he meant, that a thing could be marked by its history and still be worth hearing.

"You can tell him I said thank you," Cass said. "Or — I'll call him. When we get back."

"He'd like that."

They finished eating. His father paid. The waitress refilled his coffee without being asked and his father thanked her by name, which Cass hadn't even noticed she was wearing a name tag, but of course his father had.

Outside, the air was cold and smelled like diesel and the particular nothing-smell of highway air in November. Cass pulled his jacket tighter. The parking lot asphalt had frost in the low spots, the kind that had given up being frost and was mostly just wet now.

His father unlocked the car but didn't get in right away. He stood by the driver's side door looking at the sky, which was still the same flat gray but maybe slightly lighter in the east, or maybe Cass was imagining it.

"Can I ask you something?" Cass said.

His father looked at him. "Sure."

"When you were learning. Piano." Cass leaned against the car. The metal was cold through his jacket. "Did you ever — did you ever hear something and not know what it was? Like, in the music. Something underneath."

His father was quiet for a moment. Not thinking about whether to answer — thinking about how to answer honestly.

"Once," he said. "I was maybe fifteen. I was playing through a Schubert sonata, one of the late ones, and there was this — " He stopped. Made a small gesture with his hand, like he was trying to shape something in the air that didn't have a shape. "It was like the piece knew something I didn't. Like there was a conversation happening that I could almost hear."

"What did you do?"

"I kept playing." His father opened the car door. "That's usually the answer."

Cass got in. The car was cold inside, not as cold as outside but close. His father started the engine and the heat came on and they sat for a moment while it worked up to something useful.

"The under-note," Cass said. He hadn't meant to say it out loud.

His father glanced at him. "What?"

"That's — that's what I've been calling it. The thing in the Brahms. There's a note underneath, it's like a fundamental, like the whole piece is built on top of it. And when Grandpa played it last night I could — " He pressed his fingers to his temple again, not because it hurt right then but because he needed to indicate the location of the thing. "I could feel it. Here."

His father put the car in reverse but didn't move yet. "Physical?"

"Yeah."

"Like pain?"

"Like — yeah, like pain, but not bad pain. Like when you've been in a cold room and then you come inside and your hands start to warm up. That kind of."

His father backed out of the space. They pulled onto the road that led back to the highway. The frost in the parking lot caught the light for a second — just a second — and then they were past it.

"I had a teacher," his father said, "who used to say that music was the only art form that happened inside your body. Not just to it. Inside it." He merged onto the on-ramp. "She meant it literally. The sound waves. The resonance."

"Grandpa said something like that."

"He taught her."

Cass looked at him. "Seriously?"

"She studied with him for two years. Before she moved to Boston." His father checked the mirror. "He doesn't talk about his students much."

The highway opened up ahead of them. The same highway, the same direction, the same flat gray sky, but something had shifted slightly in the proportions of things. Cass's grandfather had taught his father's teacher. His father had learned from someone who had learned from the same hands that had set the needle down on the Brahms last night and said *this is the one that matters*. The chain of it went back further than he'd understood.

He settled into the seat.

The needle was still there, quiet now, more like a hum than a pain. He thought about what his grandfather had said — not the exact words, he couldn't remember the exact words — something about how some pieces weren't finished when the composer put the pen down. How they kept working on you. He'd said it the way you say something you've been saying for fifty years, without flourish, because the truth doesn't need it.

The road ran east. The trees on either side were bare, their branches making that complicated gray-brown pattern against the sky that looked like something written in a language he didn't know.

His father turned the radio on. Found a station playing something orchestral, too far away to identify, then turned it down low so it was more texture than music.

"He gave me something too," his father said. "Before we left."

Cass waited.

"A letter. From my mother." His father's voice was even. Not performing evenness — actually even, the way you get after you've had a night to sit with something. "She wrote it when I was born. He kept it."

"Did you read it?"

"Last night. After you were asleep."

The semi from the diner passed them on the left, its trailer saying something in large letters about a company Cass didn't know. It moved past and was gone.

"Is it — " Cass started. Stopped. He didn't know what he was asking. *Is it okay* was too small. *What did she say* was too large.

"It's good," his father said. "It's a good letter." He paused. "She knew. That's the thing. She knew she was sick, when she wrote it. She knew she might not — " Another pause. Shorter. "She was thinking about me at forty-three. Who I'd be. Whether I'd be okay."

Cass looked at his hands in his lap.

"Are you?" he said. "Okay?"

His father drove for a moment. The road curved slightly and the sky came into view at a different angle, and there was something on the horizon that might have been the beginning of actual light, not just brightening but color, the faintest possible suggestion of it.

"Yeah," his father said. "I think I actually am."

It wasn't the answer Cass had expected. He'd expected something more careful, more qualified, more adult in the way adults were careful about not claiming too much. But his father said it like it was simply true, like he'd checked and found the thing he'd expected to find.

The needle pulsed once, gently, and Cass pressed his fingers to his temple and held them there.

"The under-note," his father said, not quite a question.

"Yeah."

"Does it happen with other music? Or just the Brahms?"

Cass thought about it. He'd been thinking about it since the night before, since he'd lain in the dark of his grandfather's guest room with the fourth movement still running in his head, trying to locate the sensation, trying to understand its edges. "I don't know yet," he said. "I think it might be — I think it might be specific. Like, not every piece has one. But some of them."

"How do you know which ones?"

"I don't. Not until I'm already in it."

His father nodded slowly. It was the nod of someone who recognizes something, not someone who's just being polite.

"When I was your age," his father said, and then stopped. Started differently. "There was a piece I couldn't finish. I kept getting to the same place — middle of the third movement — and something would stop me. Not technically. I could play the notes. But I'd get to this one moment and my hands would just — " He shook his head. "I couldn't get through it."

"What piece?"

"Beethoven. Op. 109."

Cass knew it. He'd heard it once, at a recital, and had sat very still for the entire duration in a way he hadn't been able to explain to himself afterward.

"What happened?"

"I was eighteen. I played it for your grandfather. The whole thing, straight through, and I got to that place in the third movement and something — it just opened. Like a door I'd been pushing against for two years and it wasn't locked, I just hadn't been ready to go through it."

"Why then?"

His father was quiet for a moment. Outside, a rest stop came and went, its signs promising fuel and food. "I think I'd gotten old enough to know what it was about," he said. "Some music — you have to have lived enough to hear it. And I don't mean that in a pretentious way. I just mean — " He slowed slightly for a truck merging. "The piece is about loss. And I was eighteen and I'd lost things by then. And once I knew what it was about, I could play it."

Cass looked at the road.

He was fourteen. He had not lost things in the way his father meant, not yet, though he understood abstractly that he would. But the under-note had arrived anyway, had found him, had put a needle behind his left eye and kept it there through the night and into the morning, and he didn't know what it meant that it had come this early. Whether it meant he was ready. Whether it meant something else.

"Grandpa was there," his father said. "When it opened. He didn't say anything. He just — when I finished, he sat for a minute, and then he said *now you know where it lives.*"

The color on the horizon was real now. A thin orange line, low and specific, between the cloud base and whatever was below it. It wasn't enough to call sunrise. But it was something.

"Now you know where it lives," Cass said.

"Yeah."

He turned the phrase over. It didn't mean the piece. He was pretty sure it didn't mean the piece. It meant the thing the piece was about — the specific grief or weight or whatever it was that lived inside the music and waited for you to be old enough to find it. His grandfather had told his father. His father was telling him. The chain again, the long chain of it, going back through teachers and students and letters written to children who weren't old enough to read them yet.

The orange line widened, almost imperceptibly, and the gray above it shifted toward something that was not quite blue.

"I'm going to learn it," Cass said. "The Brahms. The whole thing."

His father glanced at him. "The quintet?"

"Yeah."

"That's — " His father paused. "That's a significant piece."

"I know."

"You'd need a quartet to play it with."

"I know."

His father looked at the road. A small sound came from him, not quite a laugh, not quite anything. "Okay," he said.

"What?"

"Nothing. Just — " He shook his head. "You sound like him."

Cass didn't ask which him. He knew.

The orange line was fully real now, a definite color, the kind of color that meant the sun was somewhere behind those clouds doing its work regardless of whether anyone could see it directly. The road ran straight toward it. His father drove. The radio murmured its low orchestral texture. The needle behind Cass's eye held its quiet, steady frequency.

He reached into the back seat and got the canvas bag his grandfather had packed and found one of the apples. It was cold from sitting in the car all night, and when he bit into it the sound was loud in the quiet of the car, and the taste was sharp and specific, and his father reached over without looking and Cass handed it to him and he took a bite and handed it back.

They passed a green highway sign. Eighty miles.

Cass held the apple in both hands. Outside, the orange on the horizon kept widening, kept working its way up through the cloud layers, and the bare trees on either side of the road caught the first of it and held it for a moment — just a moment — before the road curved and they were in shadow again, and then the road straightened and the light was back, and this kept happening, light and shadow, light and shadow, the whole world flickering between the two as they moved through it.

The fourth movement was still running somewhere in the back of his skull.

He didn't mind.
