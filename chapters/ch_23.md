# Chapter Twenty-Three

The waiting room at Dr. Reyes's office smelled like the inside of a new car — . Cass sat in one of the chairs along the wall with his cello case between his feet and his hands in his lap, and the smell was making the needle worse.

It had started on the drive over. A low-frequency hum from somewhere in the wheel well, and then the needle, arriving behind his left eye like something being threaded through bone. His father had the radio on — public radio, a woman talking about interest rates — and Cass had watched the road and breathed through it and not said anything because saying anything would have meant explaining, and explaining would have taken longer than the drive.

The notebook was in his father's jacket pocket. Two years of dates.

His father was at the reception window, talking to the woman behind the desk. Cass could hear the low register of his voice but not the words. He watched a child across the room build a tower of foam blocks and knock it over and build it again, .

The needle pulsed once, hard. He pressed two fingers against his left temple and held them there until it settled back to the baseline hum.

His father came and sat in the chair beside him. He put the notebook on his knee. He looked at the child with the foam blocks and then at the door that led back to the examination rooms.

"She's running about fifteen minutes behind," he said.

"Okay."

His father turned the notebook over in his hands. It was a black Moleskine, soft-covered, the kind his father used for everything — grocery lists, practice schedules, the occasional architectural sketch of a room he was trying to rearrange. This one had a rubber band around it and Cass's name on the front in his father's handwriting. 

"I told her we'd been keeping track," his father said.

"Yeah."

"She may want to take it. To review before we come back."

Cass looked at the notebook. Two years of his father getting up quietly, padding down the hall, writing a date and a time and a duration in that small, even hand. Sometimes a note. *Bad one — lasted 45 min. Preceded by car ride.* Or: *Mild. Strings practice, 1 hr prior.* His father had never mentioned he was doing it. Cass had found out by accident, reaching for a pen in his father's jacket and finding the notebook instead, the rubber band, his own name.

"That's fine," Cass said.

The child knocked the tower down again. Foam blocks scattered across the carpet. The child looked at the wreckage for a moment, then started stacking.

"Does it hurt right now?" his father asked.

Cass considered lying.  But his father had spent two years writing dates in a notebook in the dark, and that deserved something.

"A little," he said. "The hum from the car. It's fine."

His father looked at him. Not the look that meant *I'm worried* — his father didn't make that face, or if he did, he'd learned to keep it off the surface. This was something quieter. The look of a man doing arithmetic.

"How long?"

"Since about Route 9."

His father nodded. He put the notebook back in his jacket pocket.

They sat. The woman on the radio had moved on to something about a wildfire in New Mexico. His father reached over and turned it off. Cass hadn't realized the radio was still on.

---

Dr. Reyes was younger than Cass expected. He'd seen her name on the referral form for three months and built up some picture of her — gray-haired, reading glasses, the kind of doctor who had been doing this for forty years and had opinions about everything. The woman who came through the door and said his name was maybe thirty-five, with dark hair pulled back and .

She shook his father's hand and then Cass's, and she asked him how he was doing in the direct way that meant she wanted a real answer, not *fine*.

"There's one going right now," Cass said. "Started on the way here. Behind the left eye."

She nodded. She had a tablet in her hand and she made a note on it. "Scale of one to ten?"

He thought about the bar 41 night. The needle so bad he'd had to sit down on the edge of the bed and wait it out. He thought about the drive over, the wheel-well hum.

"Three," he said. "Maybe four."

"And when it's at its worst?"

"Eight." He paused. "Eight and a half."

She wrote that down. She asked him to sit on the examination table and he did, the paper cover crackling under him, and she clicked a penlight and looked in his eyes and asked him questions while she did it. When did it start. Whether it woke him from sleep. Whether there was nausea. Whether there were visual disturbances before or after. Whether it got worse with sustained concentration.

"Like playing," Cass said.

"Tell me about that."

He tried to describe it — the way the needle would start during a long practice session, usually in the second hour, usually on sustained high-register work. The way certain frequencies seemed to trigger it faster than others. The preparation before bar 41 of the Tchaikovsky, the specific pitch combination that had made it so bad he'd had to stop.

Dr. Reyes had stopped making notes. She was listening with her whole attention in the way that good teachers did, and Cass felt the particular discomfort of being heard precisely.

"Does it go away when you stop playing?" she asked.

"Usually. Sometimes it takes a while."

"How long?"

"Half an hour. Sometimes more."

She looked at his father. "And you've been tracking this."

His father took the notebook out of his jacket and held it out. She took it and opened it and read for a moment. She turned a few pages. She didn't say anything.

"I started about two years ago," his father said. "When he mentioned it the first time and then didn't mention it again. I thought—" He stopped. "I thought it would be useful to have the record."

Dr. Reyes was still reading. "It is," she said. "It's very useful." She turned another page. "Has he ever been evaluated for hyperacusis?"

"No."

She looked at Cass. "Do loud sounds bother you? Not just the pain — I mean sounds that wouldn't bother most people?"

Cass thought about the cafeteria at school. The particular frequency of the overhead fluorescents. The way a chair scraped across the linoleum floor and the sound went straight through him like something had slipped.

"Yeah," he said. "Some."

"Does it feel different from the needle, or related?"

He thought about this carefully, the way he'd learned to think about the needle — not around it, but directly at it. "Related," he said. "Like the needle is the bad version of the same thing."

She nodded slowly. She closed the notebook and held it in both hands. "I want to run some tests. Audiological first — a full hearing evaluation, not just thresholds. And I'd like to have a conversation with a neurologist colleague of mine about what you've described. The pattern is interesting."

"Interesting how?" his father asked.

She looked at him. "There's a presentation I'm seeing here that could be a few different things. I don't want to speculate until we have more data. What I can tell you is that this is real, it's measurable, and there are interventions that help." She handed the notebook back to his father. "Can I get a copy of this? The dates and notes."

"I'll scan it tonight."

"Good." She turned back to Cass. "The Tchaikovsky you mentioned. Is that for a competition?"

"Concert," he said. "Winter. And there's a competition in March."

"Are you planning to continue playing in the meantime?"

He looked at her. The question sat in the air between them and he understood she was asking it carefully — not *should you stop* but *what are you planning*, which was a different question and a more respectful one.

"Yes," he said.

"Okay. Then let's talk about some things you can do in the short term while we figure out what's going on."

---

On the way home, his father stopped at the Vietnamese place on Clement Street without asking, . They sat in a booth near the window. The restaurant was half-empty, the lunch rush gone, the dinner crowd not yet arrived. Someone in the kitchen was running water.

His father ordered the bun bo hue. Cass ordered the same thing because he couldn't concentrate on the menu.

"She seemed good," his father said.

"Yeah."

"The hyperacusis thing." His father turned his water glass in a slow circle. "I read about it. A little. When I was looking things up."

Cass looked at him. "When?"

"After the first bad one. The one before the spring recital."

So a year ago, at least. Cass did the arithmetic. His father had been reading about it for a year, writing in the notebook for two, and had not said a single word about either.

"Why didn't you—" Cass started.

"Because you weren't ready to talk about it," his father said. "And because I wasn't sure I was right." He picked up his water glass and set it down again. "I'm not a doctor."

The food came. The broth was the color of old brick, fragrant with lemongrass and something deeper underneath, and Cass realized he was hungry in the sudden, urgent way that happened when he'd been holding something tight for a long time and then put it down.

He ate. His father ate. The restaurant made its quiet sounds around them.

"The notebook," Cass said finally.

His father looked up.

"I found it by accident. In your jacket. I wasn't—" He stopped. "I wasn't looking for it."

His father was quiet for a moment. "I know. You left it on the counter."

Cass remembered. He'd been looking for a pen and had pulled the notebook out and set it down to look in the jacket pocket and then gotten distracted by something — his phone, probably — and walked away.

"I was going to tell you eventually," his father said. "When it seemed like the right time."

"When was that going to be?"

His father thought about this seriously, the way he thought about everything — not performing consideration but actually doing it. "When you asked me to come to the appointment," he said. "That was the right time."

Cass looked at his bowl. The bun bo hue was making him feel more himself with every bite, which was a thing he associated with this specific restaurant and this specific soup and his father across the table, and he didn't have words for it so he just kept eating.

"Dr. Reyes said the pattern is interesting," Cass said.

"She did."

"That's not bad interesting or good interesting. That's just—"

"I know." His father picked up his chopsticks. "It's information. We'll get more information."

"And then?"

"And then we'll know more than we know now."

 His father didn't know what came next. He was saying so without saying so.

"Okay," Cass said.

His father nodded. He ate.

After a while his father said, "She asked about the Tchaikovsky."

"Yeah."

"Are you actually going to keep playing it? Between now and the concert?"

Cass thought about bar 41. The way it had opened when he'd under-bowed the preparation. The note ringing in the dark until it was gone. He thought about Margaret Fenn in 1989, the contact in his phone, the thing he'd figured out by accident in the middle of the night.

"I figured something out," he said. "Last week. About the passage that was giving me trouble."

His father waited.

"It was a technical problem. I was over-bowing the preparation. Putting in more than the phrase needed." He paused. "When I let the bow do less, it opened up."

His father was looking at him with an expression Cass couldn't fully read — something attentive and private, the way his father got when he was filing something away.

"What was the phrase?" his father asked.

"Bar forty-one. The beginning of the second theme."

"It's an arrival," Cass said. "After a lot of preparation. The mistake I was making was treating the preparation like it needed to be as big as the arrival. But it doesn't. The arrival is big because the preparation is quiet."

His father looked at him for a moment. Then he said: "Yeah."

Just that. *Yeah.* The way he said it when something landed correctly.

Cass went back to his soup. The water sounds from the kitchen had stopped. Outside, a bus went by on Clement Street, its diesel engine dropping a half-step as it slowed for the stop at the corner.

"Dad."

"Mm."

"How come you never told me you used to play?"

His father set down his chopsticks. He looked at Cass with an expression that was, for just a second, genuinely surprised — the first time in the whole afternoon that Cass had caught him off-guard.

"Who told you that?"

"Nobody. There's a picture. In the hall closet, in the box with the Christmas stuff. You're maybe twelve. You're holding a violin."

His father was quiet. He picked up his water glass and held it without drinking.

"I played for about four years," he said. "I wasn't very good."

"But you played."

"I played." He set the glass down. "I stopped when I was fourteen."

"Why?"

His father looked out the window at Clement Street. A woman was walking a dog, a small terrier mix that was investigating everything on the sidewalk with focused intensity. His father watched the dog for a moment.

"I didn't like how it felt when I played badly," he said. "I could hear the difference between what I was doing and what it was supposed to sound like. And I couldn't close that gap. So I stopped." He paused. "It seemed logical at the time."

Cass thought about this. The needle behind his eye. The two years of dates in the notebook. His father sitting in the dark hallway writing down the time.

"Is that why you never said anything?" Cass asked. "About the—"

"No," his father said. He said it immediately, without hesitation, which meant he'd thought about this before. "I don't think so. I stopped because I didn't have what you have. That's just true. It's not—" He stopped. Started again. "I didn't want to make your thing about my thing. That's all."

The terrier on the sidewalk had found something under a parking meter and was refusing to move on. The woman at the other end of the leash waited with the patient expression of someone who had learned that this was how it went.

Cass looked at his father. His father was still watching the dog.

"You have what I have," Cass said. "You can hear the difference. You just—"

"Cass."

"—you just stopped trying to close the gap."

His father turned and looked at him. He had the expression of a man who has been told something he already knew and was deciding how to feel about hearing it out loud.

Then he said: "Finish your soup."

Cass finished his soup.

Outside, the terrier gave up on whatever was under the parking meter and let the woman lead him down the block. The restaurant settled around them. A family came in through the front door — two parents, a kid who was maybe seven, the kid already asking something urgent about something the parents couldn't quite follow, and one of the parents said *in a minute, in a minute* the way parents said it, not dismissive but genuinely occupied with the business of getting to the table.

His father paid. He always paid. Cass had stopped arguing about it.

They went out into the late afternoon, the fog already coming in from the ocean, the air carrying the cold mineral smell of it. His father's car was half a block up. They walked and Cass carried his cello and the needle was gone — fully gone, for the first time since Route 9 — and the fog was doing something interesting to the sound of the street, softening the edges of the bus noise and the voices and the particular clatter of a city at four in the afternoon.

His father unlocked the car. He opened the trunk and Cass slid the cello case in.

"I'm going to call the audiologist tonight," his father said. "See how fast we can get in."

"Okay."

"And I'll scan the notebook."

"Okay."

His father closed the trunk. He stood with his hand still on it for a moment, not looking at Cass, looking at the car.

"Four years," he said. "I was pretty bad."

"You were twelve."

"I was bad for a twelve-year-old."

Cass looked at him. His father's mouth had done something — not quite a smile, but close. The expression of a man making peace with a small, old thing.

"The arrival is big because the preparation is quiet," his father said. He wasn't asking. He was repeating it back the way he'd repeat back the operative word in a conversation, turning it over.

"Yeah," Cass said.

His father nodded once. He got in the car.

Cass got in the passenger side. The seat was cold. The fog pressed against the windows and the street outside had gone the particular gray of a San Francisco late afternoon, everything muted, everything close. His father started the engine. The wheel-well hum started up and Cass sat with it and waited, but the needle didn't come.

He reached over and turned the radio on. Public radio, different woman, something about a local city council vote. He left it on.

His father pulled out of the parking spot and pointed them toward home, and neither of them said anything else, and the fog came in off the ocean and the city went gray and quiet around them, and the radio woman was talking about a vote that had passed by three votes out of five, which was how most things passed, and Cass watched the streetlights come on one by one as the light went out of the sky.
