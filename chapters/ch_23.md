# Chapter Twenty-Three: What the Dogs Know

The shepherd mix arrived first, as she usually did, because her owner left for the Tysons corridor by six-fifteen and needed somewhere for her to be. Andrea heard the car pull in while she was still refilling the water stations, and she dried her hands on the back of her jeans and went to the door.

The dog's name was Remy. She was three years old and had opinions about strangers and no opinions at all about Andrea, because Andrea was not a stranger anymore. She came through the door already looking for the spot by the window, the long rectangle of morning light that fell across the floor at this hour, and she found it and turned twice and lay down, and that was Remy sorted.

"Good morning," the owner said. Her name was Patricia. She had a travel mug and the particular expression of someone who had been awake since five and was running on the first coffee and the promise of a second. "How are you holding up?"

It was the question everyone asked now, in late March, a week after the funeral. Andrea had been answering it all week.

"I'm good," she said. "Busy morning coming. You okay?"

Patricia looked at her for a moment, and Andrea could see her deciding whether to push on it or let it go. She let it go. She thanked Andrea, said she'd pick up by six, and went back to her car, and the taillights disappeared onto Fayette Street.

Andrea stood in the doorway for a moment. The air outside was cold still, but differently cold than February — there was something behind it now, some quality of almost, the season not yet arrived but clearly on its way. She could smell the ground turning. She went back inside.

---

By eight o'clock there were eleven dogs on the floor.

Jim was in the far corner, sitting cross-legged on the concrete, and next to him was a dog Andrea had not met before today. She had the intake form: male, four years old, a beagle-Lab mix named Cooper, first day. His owner had said he was anxious in new environments, had asked three times whether they were sure about the cage-free setup, had stood at the door for a full minute after handing over the leash before Andrea had gently, firmly, said *he's going to be okay, I'll text you a photo in an hour, you can go.*

Cooper was pressed against the wall six feet from Jim, watching the room with the particular attention of an animal who has decided that the best response to uncertainty is to gather more information before committing to anything. His ears were up. His tail was low but not tucked.

Jim sat with his hands in his lap and did not look at the dog directly. He looked at the middle distance, at nothing in particular, breathing at the pace of someone who has nowhere to be. Around them the other dogs moved and circled and did the ordinary work of a Tuesday morning, and Jim let all of it happen and waited.

Andrea watched from across the room, pretending to check the water stations, which did not need checking.

After seven or eight minutes, Cooper shifted his weight. Then he took a step forward. Then another. He stopped three feet from Jim and stretched his nose out and Jim let him, still not looking, still breathing, and the dog sniffed the back of Jim's hand and then sat down, and Jim's hand moved slowly and scratched behind the dog's ear, and Cooper's tail came up.

That was all. It happened the same way it always happened.

Andrea sent the owner a photo at eight forty-five. The response came back in under a minute: *oh my god he looks so HAPPY thank you thank you thank you.* Three exclamation points and a dog emoji. Andrea looked at the screen for a moment and then set her phone in her pocket and went to check on the ancient beagle, whose name was Franklin and who had arrived at seven-thirty and needed to be carried up the two steps at the entrance because his knees were going. Franklin was asleep under the bench by the grooming area, which was where he always slept, and he was fine.

---

The morning moved the way mornings moved here. A dog got overstimulated near the water stations and Andrea redirected him before it became anything. A client called to ask if she could do an early pickup, and Andrea said of course, and wrote it on the board. One of the staff members — Kelsey, who had been with them since the fall — came to ask about the afternoon grooming schedule, and Andrea told her, and Kelsey nodded and went back to the floor.

The work was not glamorous. It was never glamorous. It was water stations and intake forms and knowing that Franklin needed to be carried and knowing that Remy needed the window spot and knowing that Cooper needed Jim to do nothing for eight minutes before he could do anything at all. It was the text message with the photo. It was the board with the early pickups. It was the floor, which had to be clean, which required someone to have been scrubbing before anyone arrived, which today had been Andrea, at six o'clock, in the quiet before the dogs came.

She thought about Dottie.

Not in a way she could have predicted — not the formal, front-facing grief of the last week, the funeral and the standing up and the speaking. She thought about her the way she sometimes thought about the early days of the business, the way a specific smell or a specific quality of light would drop her back into a moment she hadn't known she'd kept. She was refilling the water station near the window and the March light was doing something particular on the floor and she thought: *Dottie would have liked this.*

Not the awards. Not the write-ups. This. The specific, ordinary Tuesday of it.

She had said something to Andrea once, near the beginning, when Andrea had been in the middle of a stretch of weeks that felt like running uphill without knowing how far the top was. They had been talking — Dottie had a way of arriving in conversation without you quite noticing the conversation had started — and Andrea had said something about not being sure if they were going to make it, not in a dramatic way, just as a fact she was carrying around. And Dottie had looked at her with the particular directness of someone who has been in rooms full of people for fifty years and said: *You keep going. That's all there is to it. You keep going.*

Not a speech. Not a lesson. Just the fact of it.

Andrea set down the water jug and looked out the window at Fayette Street, at the bare trees and the pale sky and the first suggestion of green at the edges of the grass along the fence. The season was turning. She could feel it.

She kept going.

Jim came up beside her. He had Cooper with him, the dog walking loose at his knee, no leash, tail up and swinging. Four hours in and the dog looked like he'd been coming here for years.

"He's good," Jim said.

"I can see that."

"Owner's going to be relieved."

"I already texted her."

Jim looked at the dog, and the dog looked at the room, and Andrea looked at both of them, and for a moment none of them did anything and that was fine.

"You eat anything this morning?" Jim said.

"I had coffee."

"That's not food."

"I know what food is, Jim."

He went and got her a granola bar from the cabinet behind the desk and put it on the counter without saying anything else about it. Andrea picked it up and ate it standing at the window, watching Remy sleep in her patch of light.

---

The early pickup arrived at eleven-thirty. A woman named Gail, collecting her labradoodle, who was named Sprocket, which was a terrible name and also a perfect name, and who came bounding toward the door when he heard Gail's voice from the parking lot because he had been saving up energy all morning and was now prepared to spend it all at once. Andrea got him leashed before he could knock Gail over, which required some doing, and Gail laughed and took the leash and said, "How was he?"

"Fantastic. He played hard this morning and then he crashed around ten, and then he heard your car."

"He always knows."

"They always know," Andrea agreed.

Gail paused at the door, the dog pulling gently toward the parking lot. She was in her mid-sixties, retired, had been bringing Sprocket since they opened. She looked at Andrea for a moment.

"How are you doing," she said. Not a question, quite. More like an acknowledgment.

"I'm okay," Andrea said. "I really am."

Gail nodded slowly. "She was something, Dottie was."

"She was."

"I bought my house through her. 2003. She knew every room of every house on every street in this town." Gail shook her head, the particular motion of someone sorting through something they haven't finished sorting. "She'd have been proud of what you've built here. She told me that herself, you know. A few years back. Said you and Jim were exactly what this town needed."

Andrea did not say anything for a moment. Sprocket was still pulling, patient but persistent, the way dogs are when they can see the car.

"I didn't know that," Andrea said.

"Well." Gail looked at the dog. "She wasn't someone who said things she didn't mean."

She went. Andrea stood in the doorway and watched them go, the dog's tail still going, Gail getting him settled into the back seat, the car pulling out onto Fayette Street and turning toward Washington Street and disappearing.

She hadn't known that. Dottie had said it to a client, to Gail, without telling Andrea. Had just said it because it was true and because someone had asked, or because someone hadn't asked and she'd said it anyway. That was very like her. The support that didn't announce itself.

Andrea went back inside.

---

The afternoon was quieter. Pickups staggered through from four o'clock on, and Andrea did the thing she always did, which was give each owner the specific true report of their dog's day. Not the general reassurance — the actual thing. What time they ate, whether they played hard or took it easy, who they spent time with, what they did when they were tired. The owners always wanted to know. They were leaving a family member with her and Jim for eight or ten hours, and the least she could do was tell them what those hours had held.

She texted a photo to Cooper's owner at four-fifteen — Cooper asleep on the floor near the window, his head on his paws, fully at ease — and the owner called instead of texting back, which Andrea had not expected. She answered.

"I'm sorry," the woman said immediately, "I know you're busy, I just — he looks so *calm*. He never looks like that in new places. He's been to two other daycares and he just — he hates it, he's miserable the whole time, and I've been so worried about leaving him but I have to go back to the office next month and I thought maybe this time—" She stopped. "I'm sorry. I'm rambling."

"Don't be sorry," Andrea said. "That's Cooper. That's who he is here."

"How did you do that? In one day?"

Andrea looked across the room at Jim, who was doing the end-of-day floor check, moving through the space the way he always did, unhurried and thorough. "Jim sat with him this morning. Just sat with him. Didn't push anything. Cooper figured out the rest."

There was a pause on the line.

"That's it?"

"That's it."

Another pause, and then the woman laughed, a small surprised sound. "Okay. Okay. I'll see you tomorrow."

"We'll see Cooper tomorrow," Andrea said. "He'll be fine."

She ended the call and stood with the phone in her hand for a moment. The room was quieter now, half the dogs already gone, the light different, the long late-afternoon gold of it coming through the windows at a different angle than the morning light. Remy was still in her spot, waiting for Patricia, who would arrive by six. Franklin had been picked up at three by his owner, carried back down the two steps, settled into the car with the particular care that old dogs require.

This was the work. This was what it had always been.

She thought about the April meeting, four years ago, standing at the podium in the Council chambers and saying the true thing about what was happening to the business. She thought about the thirty days they'd gotten, the thirty days that had not been a rescue but had been something, a handhold, and what they'd done with it. She thought about the awards, which were on the wall, and the building, which had the name on it, and the name, which had come from the back seat of a car on a Tuesday afternoon when Jim was driving home from school pickup with the question still open.

*A Pup's Valley.*

She hadn't been there for that moment. Jim had told her when he got home, and she had heard it the way you hear something that was always going to be true. She had said, *that's it*, and Jim had said, *yeah*, and that had been the whole conversation about it.

Andy was at school now. He would come in this afternoon, probably, do his homework at the desk in the corner the way he sometimes did, ignore the dogs with the studied indifference of a kid who has grown up around them and therefore finds them unremarkable, which was its own kind of love.

---

Jim came in from the back at four forty-five, drying his hands.

"Cooper's owner called," Andrea said.

"I figured she would."

"She wanted to know how you did it."

He shrugged. Not dismissively — just the shrug of someone who doesn't have a different answer than the obvious one. "He needed time."

"She said he's been to two other daycares."

"I know. She put it in the intake form." He looked across the room at the remaining dogs, doing his quiet inventory. "He'll be easier tomorrow. First day is always the hardest one."

Andrea set her phone on the desk. Outside, a car was pulling into the lot — Patricia, right on time, and Remy's head came up from the window spot before the headlights even crossed the glass.

"Gail told me something today," Andrea said.

Jim waited.

"She said Dottie told her. A few years back. That we were exactly what this town needed."

Jim was quiet for a moment. He looked at the floor, and then at the window, and then at nothing in particular. Outside, Patricia was getting out of her car.

"Yeah," he said finally.

"You knew?"

"I didn't know she said it to Gail. But—" He stopped. Started again. "She wasn't someone who helped people she didn't believe in."

That was true. That was exactly true. Andrea had known it but had not thought about it in those terms, and hearing it said plainly made something settle in her chest, some small remaining tension she hadn't known she was still carrying.

Patricia came through the door and Remy was already up, already moving toward her, tail going, the specific full-body joy of a dog who has had a good day and is now seeing the person she loves most in the world. Patricia laughed and got low and let the dog come to her, and it was exactly what it always was, and it was enough, and it was more than enough.

Andrea watched it happen. The room smelled clean. The floor was clean. The light was going gold and then going orange and then going to the particular blue of a March evening in the Piedmont, and through the window the bare trees were dark against it.

She had learned Remy's name on a Tuesday in November 2019, three weeks after they opened. She had learned Franklin's name the same week. She had learned Cooper's name this morning from an intake form and now she knew it the other way, the real way — the way you know a name when you know the dog behind it, the specific animal, the particular history of how he holds himself in a room.

This was what it meant. Not the awards on the wall, though those were real. Not the name on the building, though that was real too. This. The knowing of the names. The water stations. The photo at eight forty-five. The granola bar Jim put on the counter without making a thing of it. The thirty days they'd gotten and what they'd done with the thirty days. The years of it, accumulating.

*You keep going. That's all there is to it.*

She had kept going. They both had. The dogs had come back when the dogs were allowed to come back, and the families had come back with them, and A Pup's Valley had become what they said it would be, what she had believed it would be even in the weeks when believing it required something she hadn't been sure she had.

Dottie had known. Had told Gail, matter-of-factly, the way she said all true things: *exactly what this town needed.* And then she had died in March, on the twentieth, and Jim and Andrea had gone to the funeral and stood up and spoken, because she was someone worth standing up for, and because the town should know that the people she helped were there, at the end, saying so.

Patricia was leaving, Remy at her side, the dog looking back once at the room before they went out the door. Cooper's owner arrived at five-ten, and Cooper walked to her without being called, tail moving, and she looked at Andrea over the dog's head with an expression that didn't need any words, and Andrea nodded, and that was that.

The last dog left at six-twenty. Andrea locked the front door and stood in the quiet room for a moment. Jim was in the back, doing the last of the cleanup. She could hear the water running, the familiar sounds of the end of a day here.

She got out her phone and looked at the photo she'd sent Cooper's owner that morning. The dog asleep on the floor, his head on his paws, completely at ease. She looked at it for a moment and then she sent it to Kelsey with a note: *good first day.* Then she put the phone in her pocket and went to help Jim with the floors.
