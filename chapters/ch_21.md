# Chapter Twenty-One

He slept four hours and woke with the needle still in place.

It had moved — not gone, but shifted, like a splinter that had worked itself slightly deeper in the night. He lay on his back with his eyes open and let the ceiling tell him where he was. His room. His ceiling. The water stain in the corner that had been there since before his family moved in, shaped like nothing specific. He had looked at it enough times that it had stopped trying to mean anything.

The Brahms was on his stand.

He hadn't put it there. He must have, coming in from the car, setting down the canvas bag, pulling out the score and setting it on the stand out of some muscle habit before he even took off his coat. He had no memory of doing it. The score sat open to the first page, the way it always did when he hadn't decided yet where to start.

He sat up. The needle caught on something — a small, bright flare behind his left eye — and then settled back to its regular frequency. He waited for it. It waited with him. They had an arrangement now.

Downstairs, his father was making coffee. He could tell by the specific rattle of the grinder, which had a loose piece in the housing that made it sound like it was eating itself. His father had been meaning to fix it for two years. The rattle had become the sound of morning.

Cass got up.

---

He came downstairs in yesterday's clothes, which was fine because he'd slept in them. His father was at the kitchen table with the newspaper, which was also a thing his father still did, and the coffee was already made and a mug was out for Cass on the counter. This was the kind of care his father gave: not mentioned, just there.

"Sleep?" his father said, without looking up.

"Yeah."

"The needle?"

"Still there."

His father turned a page. "Dr. Reyes said that might take a week."

"I know."

"Not a week from the competition. A week from when it started."

Cass poured the coffee. "When did it start."

"You tell me."

He thought about it. Not the gas station, not the drive — further back. The Beethoven rehearsal two weeks ago where he had felt it first, thin as wire, and attributed it to the acoustics in the school rehearsal room, which were bad. Then the next rehearsal, and the one after. He had been carrying it for two weeks before he understood what it was.

"Two weeks ago," he said.

His father looked up then. He held the look for a moment, the way he did when he was deciding whether to say a thing.

"Okay," he said. He went back to the paper.

Cass sat down across from him with the coffee. The kitchen was cold — they kept the heat low in the mornings, an old habit from when the heating bill had been a real problem, and now it was just how mornings felt. The cold was specific: the radiator under the window ticked occasionally, giving off just enough warmth to make the cold around it noticeable.

"Dad."

"Mm."

"Did he ever — " Cass stopped. He was thinking about his grandfather, which was what he'd been doing since the car, since the canvas bag, since the score appearing on the stand. He tried again. "Did Grandpa ever have something like this. Something physical."

His father put the paper down. Not folded it, just put it down, which was different.

"His hands," he said.

"What about them."

"The shaking started when he was thirty-eight. He thought it was nerves. He played through it for six months before he saw anyone." His father picked up his mug. "It wasn't nerves."

The radiator ticked.

"What was it," Cass said.

"Essential tremor. He managed it. Changed some things about how he held the bow." His father's mouth did something complicated. "He kept playing until he was sixty-one."

"I didn't know that."

"He didn't talk about it."

Cass held his coffee with both hands and felt the warmth come through the ceramic. Twenty-three years of playing, with his hands shaking. The thought arrived not as something abstract but as a physical sensation — a kind of compression in his chest, like the moment before a difficult passage when the body already knows what the fingers are about to attempt.

"Why are you telling me now," Cass said.

"You asked."

"I mean — why didn't you tell me before."

His father was quiet for a moment. Then: "Because before, you hadn't played the Brahms."

Cass looked at him.

"There are things that make more sense after certain pieces," his father said. He said it simply, not like it was profound, just like it was true. "Your grandfather thought so. I think he was right."

---

He went upstairs after breakfast and stood in front of the score for a long time without picking up the bow.

The cello was out of its case — he had done that much, set it against the stand, the way you set a conversation partner in a chair before you're ready to talk. The morning light came through the window at an angle that made the instrument's varnish look like something had been lit inside it. He knew it was just the wood, just the particular red-brown of the finish catching the winter sun. He knew this. It still stopped him for a second.

The score was open to the beginning of the first movement. He had been through it enough times that he could hear it without playing it — the long opening, the cello finding its way into the key like a hand reaching into dark water and finding the temperature bearable. He had played it badly twice and less badly once and then the competition, which was different from all of those, which was a thing he was still not sure how to think about.

He picked up the bow.

The first note he played was middle C. Not from the piece — just a note. He wanted to hear the room. He wanted to hear the instrument. He played it open, let it sustain, listened to where it went.

The needle did not move.

He played a C major scale, slowly, not thinking about the Brahms. Just listening. The cello in his practice room sounded different from the cello in the competition hall — of course it did, every room was different, but the difference was specific here. Here it was closer. More private. The sound went into the walls and came back slightly changed, the way a conversation sounds different in a small room than a large one.

He was in the second octave of the scale when his phone buzzed on the music stand.

He looked at it. Yusuf.

He finished the scale before he picked up.

"You're home," Yusuf said.

"Yeah."

"How's the head."

"Same."

"The same as yesterday or the same as before the competition."

Cass set the bow down across the stand. "Better than before. Worse than nothing."

"Dr. Reyes said—"

"I know what she said."

A pause. Yusuf did not take offense at being interrupted — he had known Cass long enough that it registered as engagement rather than dismissal. "Okay. I'm calling because Mireille wants to know if you're coming to the thing on Saturday."

"What thing."

"The thing at her place. The thing she has every year in December where she makes her mother's soup and everyone sits around and pretends they're not tired."

"I forgot about that."

"You always forget about it."

"I'll come."

"You don't have to decide now."

"I just decided."

Another pause, shorter. "How was it," Yusuf said. "The competition. Before the—"

"I don't know yet."

"You don't know."

"I don't know how to think about it yet." Cass looked at the score. "I played it. It was — there was a moment in the second movement where it went somewhere I hadn't been before. I don't know what that means yet."

Yusuf was quiet for a moment. When he spoke again, his voice was different — not careful, exactly, but more deliberate. "My father used to say there are performances you understand the next day and performances you understand in five years."

"Your father played chess."

"He also listened to a lot of music." A beat. "He said the ones that take five years are the ones worth having."

Cass picked the bow back up. He turned it in his fingers, feeling the stick, the weight distribution, the place where his thumb sat. It was the same bow it had always been. It felt different today, which was not the bow.

"Yusuf."

"Yeah."

"I'll come Saturday."

"Okay."

"Tell Mireille I'll bring bread or something."

"She'll tell you she doesn't need bread."

"I know. I'll bring it anyway."

He hung up and played the opening of the Brahms, just the first eight bars, to see what it sounded like in his practice room on this specific morning. The needle caught on the third bar — a brief, bright flare — and then subsided. He stopped. He waited. Then he played the three bars again, slower this time, and it was fine.

He stayed at the stand for two hours.

---

His mother came home at noon with groceries and the specific energy of someone who had been out among people and had processed a great deal on the drive back. She came into the kitchen, saw Cass at the table with a glass of water and the Brahms score, and said: "Your father told me about the doctor."

"Okay."

"Not the competition part. The needle part." She put a bag on the counter and started taking things out with the particular efficiency that meant she was working through something. "Has it gotten better."

"A little."

"A little since last night or a little since it started."

"Since it started."

She turned around. She had the expression she wore when she was deciding how worried to be — not the worried expression itself, but the one before it, where she was still gathering information. "Cass. You should have said something."

"I know."

"Two weeks."

"I know, Mom."

She turned back to the counter. He watched her put away a box of pasta, a can of tomatoes, a bunch of parsley that she set in a glass of water the way she always did, stems down, like she was keeping it alive on purpose. She had done this his whole life. Parsley in a glass on the kitchen windowsill. He had never thought about it until right now.

"I was afraid," he said.

She didn't stop moving. But she slowed down.

"Of what," she said.

He wasn't sure how to answer. The fear had not been specific — it had been the kind that lives below the level of language, the body's fear, the musician's fear. The fear that if you named the thing in your head it would become real in your hands. He had spent two weeks not naming it, playing through it, telling himself it was acoustics or nerves or the particular stress of the competition preparation.

"That it was something that would stop me," he said.

His mother was quiet for a moment. She put the last thing away and stood with her back to him, her hands on the counter.

"It didn't stop you," she said.

"No."

She turned around. Her face had settled into something he recognized but couldn't name — not relief, not sadness, something that held both of them at once. "You played the Brahms."

"Yeah."

"With a needle behind your eye."

"Yeah."

She looked at him for a long moment. Then she crossed the kitchen and put her hand briefly on the back of his head — not a hug, just contact, the way she had done when he was small and had hurt himself and she was checking that he was intact. Her hand was cold from the groceries.

"Eat something," she said. "I'm making soup."

He looked at the score. "Can I help."

She made a face that meant she was surprised and trying not to show it. "You can cut the parsley," she said.

So he did. He stood at the kitchen counter with the parsley she'd brought in from the windowsill and a knife and a cutting board, and he chopped parsley while his mother made soup, and neither of them said anything for a while. The kitchen filled with the smell of onion going soft in oil, and then the tomatoes, and then the broth.

The needle was still there. It was quieter in the kitchen than in the practice room — or maybe it was the same and he was just paying less attention to it. He couldn't tell. He cut the parsley finer than it needed to be, the way he did when his hands wanted something to do that wasn't the cello.

"Mom," he said.

"Mm."

"Dad told me about Grandpa's hands."

She stirred the pot without turning around. "He did."

"You knew."

"Of course I knew."

"Why didn't anyone tell me."

She turned down the heat under the pot and finally looked at him. "Because it wasn't a lesson," she said. "It wasn't — your grandfather wasn't a lesson for you to learn. He was a person." She said it without any edge. Just flat, as if it were obvious. "When you were old enough to understand that, your father would tell you."

Cass looked at the parsley on the cutting board. Very fine. Almost dust.

"I think I'm old enough," he said.

"I think so too," his mother said.

---

After lunch he went back upstairs.

He didn't go to the stand. He sat on the floor with his back against the bed and the cello across his lap, not in playing position, just held. He did this sometimes when he needed to think with his hands — not practice, just contact. The instrument's weight was familiar in a way that was below the level of thought.

The afternoon light had moved. The window now caught a lower angle, and the room was dimmer, the varnish on the cello less lit. He could hear his father somewhere in the house — a door, footsteps, the sound of the heating system working. His mother had gone back out. The house had its afternoon sound, which was different from its morning sound.

He was thinking about the second movement.

The moment Yusuf had asked about — the place where it had gone somewhere he hadn't been. He had been trying not to think about it since the competition, the way you sometimes don't look directly at a thing because you're afraid looking will change it. But it was there. It had happened. Whatever it was.

It had been in the transition, the place where the cello drops into the lower register and the melody becomes less a statement and more a question. He had played that transition a hundred times. He knew where it went. And then in the hall, with the needle behind his eye and his grandfather's score under his hands, it had gone somewhere else. Not wrong. Just — further. Like a corridor he had always walked the beginning of and had finally followed to the end.

He didn't know what was at the end. He had been there once. He would have to go back.

He held the cello and let the house make its sounds around him and thought about his grandfather at thirty-eight, playing through the shaking for six months before he told anyone. Twenty-three more years after that. The math of it was simple. The thing behind the math was not.

His phone buzzed. He looked at it.

A number he didn't recognize. Area code he didn't know.

He almost let it go to voicemail. Then something — the same instinct that had made him finish the scale before answering Yusuf — made him pick up.

"Hello."

A pause. Then a woman's voice, older, careful: "Is this Cass Alderman."

"Yes."

"This is Margaret Fenn. I was one of the judges yesterday." Another pause, shorter. "I'm sorry to call directly. I got your number from the festival coordinator. I hope that's all right."

Cass's hand tightened on the cello's neck. He made himself loosen it.

"It's all right," he said.

"I wanted to call because there's something I say to students sometimes, after competitions, and I couldn't say it yesterday because of the — because you had to leave. I thought it would be wrong to let it go unsaid."

He waited.

"What you did in the second movement," she said. "That transition in bar forty-one. I've heard that piece performed by professionals who have never found what you found in that bar. I wanted you to know that I heard it. That it was real." A beat. "It doesn't change the scoring. I'm not calling about the scoring. I'm calling because someone should tell you."

The needle behind his eye caught. A brief, bright flare.

He didn't say anything for a moment. He was aware of the cello in his hands, the floor under him, the window going dim.

"Thank you," he said. His voice came out steadier than he expected.

"You'll lose competitions," she said. "You'll lose ones you should have won. That's the part no one tells you clearly enough." She said it without apology, just as information. "What matters is whether you keep finding things in bar forty-one."

"Yes," Cass said. He wasn't sure what he was agreeing to. He agreed anyway.

"Good luck," she said. And hung up.

He sat on the floor with the phone in one hand and the cello in the other for a long time.

The needle was still there. Quiet, steady. Its single frequency.

He thought about bar forty-one.

He got up, set the cello in the stand, picked up the bow, and found it.
