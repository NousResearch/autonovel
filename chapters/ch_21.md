# Chapter Twenty-One: The Call

The phone was in his hand before he had decided to make the call.

Jim stood at the counter in the kitchen, the coffee he'd poured ten minutes ago gone cold beside him, and looked at Dottie's name on the screen. He had her number from years of calls about listings, about zoning questions, about the particular bureaucratic weather of Prince William County that she had always been able to read better than anyone he knew. He had never called her in the hospital before. He had called her office, her cell, once her home line when the cell went to voicemail during a hearing. Never a hospital room.

He pressed call.

It rang once.

He looked out the kitchen window at the yard. October, and the leaves on the back fence were going yellow at the edges, the slow burn of the season doing what it did whether or not anyone was watching. Cassie was somewhere in the house behind him. He could hear her moving in the other room, the soft percussion of her feet on the hardwood.

The phone rang a second time, and then Dottie picked up.

"Jim." Her voice was the same. He had braced for something diminished, something hospital-filtered, and instead he got the same voice he'd heard across Council chambers and closing tables and a dozen conversations in the parking lot on Washington Street. "Are you calling about the Fayette Street listing?"

He almost laughed. He caught it.

"No," he said. "No, Dottie. I just called to talk."

A pause. Not a long one — just the beat of someone recalibrating, setting down one thing and picking up another.

Then she laughed. A real one, short and dry and entirely herself.

"Well," she said. "All right then."

---

What they talked about, Jim could not have reconstructed afterward in any precise way. That was how it was with the conversations that mattered — you came away with the quality of them rather than the transcript. He remembered the shape of it: the way it moved from the business to the town to the years before the business existed, the years when Haymarket was just the place they lived and not the place they'd built something in.

He sat down at the kitchen table. Cassie came in and put her head on his knee and he put his hand on her without looking down.

Dottie asked how Andrea was doing. He told her. She asked about the boarding numbers going into winter, whether they'd seen the seasonal uptick they'd expected, and he told her that too, and for a few minutes it was almost like talking across a desk somewhere, the ordinary business of two people who had been watching the same town for years.

Then she said something about Fayette Street — not the listing, but the street itself, what it had looked like when she first started working in town, before the light industrial uses had come in, when it was just a road running parallel to Washington that not many people had reason to use. She described it with the specificity of someone who had been keeping track for fifty years, the way you keep track of something you love without announcing that you love it.

Jim listened. Outside, a car passed on the road, and then it was quiet again.

She told him what she had seen in them, when they were going through the approvals. He had never asked her directly — had never needed to, because her support had been present and clear from the beginning, working through the channels it worked through, the word said to the right person at the right moment. But she told him now.

"You two were serious," she said. "Not everyone who comes through that process is serious. You could tell." A pause. "And you loved the town. That matters. You can't fake that in front of people who've been here as long as I have."

Jim looked at his hand on Cassie's head. The fur there was thick and soft, starting to go a little coarse at the muzzle where the years were showing.

"You made it worth building in," he said. "Haymarket. You made it worth the bet."

She was quiet for a moment.

"The town made itself worth it," she said. "I just kept arguing with people who wanted to ruin it."

He knew she meant Disney. She had told him about that, in one of their earlier conversations — confirmed it the way she confirmed things, without drama, as a simple fact of what had happened. *We looked at what they were proposing and we said no.* As if it were obvious. As if saying no to a billion-dollar corporation with a bulldozer and a theme park had been the natural and unremarkable thing to do.

"You did more than argue," he said.

"Maybe," she said. "But the town had to want it too. You can't save something people don't want to save."

The coffee on the counter was cold. The yard through the window was still, the yellow leaves not moving. He could hear Dottie breathing on the other end of the line, the slight effort of it, the thing the hospital room couldn't hide entirely.

He didn't say anything about that. She didn't either.

They talked a little longer — about what the business had become, about the awards, about a family she knew who had recently moved to Gainesville and were looking for somewhere to bring their dog. She gave him the name without being asked. Still working. Still keeping the connections live between the people who needed things and the people who could provide them.

"I'll have Andrea follow up," he said.

"Good," she said.

There was a natural pause then, the kind that comes at the end of a conversation when everything that needed saying has been said and both people know it.

"I'm glad you called," Dottie said. "Not about a listing."

"Me too," Jim said.

He stayed on the line for another second, not because there was more to say but because the moment was complete and he was reluctant to close it. Then he said goodbye and she said goodbye and he set the phone face-down on the table.

---

He sat there for a while.

Cassie lifted her head from his knee and went to her water bowl in the corner. He listened to her drink — the specific sound of it, unhurried, the dog doing what the dog needed to do. Then she came back and lay down under the table, and the kitchen was quiet again.

Jim thought about fifty years.

Not in the abstract. In the particular: fifty years of knowing which properties on Washington Street had changed hands and how, which families had come and which had gone, which buildings had been saved from what and by whom. Fifty years of sitting in the Council chambers and saying what she thought in the precise, efficient way she said things, not wasting the room's time, making the point and sitting down. Fifty years of working listings in a town small enough that every sale was also a story about someone's life.

What did fifty years of that amount to?

He could see it from where he sat, if he thought about it right. Not the individual transactions — the accumulated effect of them. The town that Dottie Leonard had spent fifty years arguing into existence, or arguing for against the forces that would have made it into something else. The brick sidewalks on Washington Street that did not happen by accident. The lamp posts. The historical overlay that made Haymarket look like somewhere with a past because it had one and someone had fought to make sure it showed.

Disney had wanted to build a history-themed park on the edge of a battlefield. She had helped stop it. The battlefield was still a battlefield. The town was still a town. And thirty years later, a couple from Fayette Street had been able to build something in it that the town was glad to have, partly because the town was still the kind of town where that kind of thing was possible.

He thought about the name on the building.

*A Pup's Valley.* Andy in the back seat, not even trying to solve the problem, just saying the true thing because he didn't know he was supposed to be strategic about it. Jim bringing it home. Andrea hearing it and knowing immediately. The name going on the sign and then on the certificates and then on the awards that were framed now on the wall of the front room, catching the light from Andrea's desk lamp at the end of the day.

Andy had been — what, seven? Eight? He hadn't known he was naming something that would last. He had just answered a question the adults forgot to ask him.

Jim turned the phone over on the table. The screen was dark.

He had called Dottie because she mattered to him. That was the whole of it. Not for advice, not for a connection, not for any of the practical reasons he had called her over the years. She was ill and she was in the hospital and she was seventy-five years old and she had spent fifty years making Haymarket worth coming to, and he had wanted to talk to her while he still could.

She had picked up on the second ring.

She had been ready to work a listing.

He sat with that. The specific, exact, impossible-to-editorialize fact of it: a woman in a hospital room, whatever was happening in her body, still oriented toward the work. Not as a performance of toughness. Just because that was who she was and the work was what she did and the town still needed people who knew which families were looking and which properties were coming available and who ought to be connected to whom.

The town would lose that when it lost her. Not the information — someone else would eventually know the listings, the families, the connections. It would lose the particular quality of attention she had brought to it. The fifty years of caring about a place as a place, not as a market, not as a commuter corridor, not as a development opportunity, but as somewhere worth preserving because the people in it were worth something.

Jim looked at the window. The leaves on the fence. The blue of the October sky going toward evening.

He thought about what Andrea had said, years ago now, when they were still in the approval process and the bureaucracy was grinding and they had gone home one night not sure whether the next meeting would go their way. She had sat at the kitchen table — this kitchen table, the same one — and said: *We're building the right thing in the right place. The rest of it will sort itself out.* Not as a comfort. As a conclusion, arrived at by looking at the facts.

She had been right. The rest of it had sorted itself out, slowly and with friction, the way things sort themselves out when the people doing the sorting don't quit.

Dottie had been part of that sorting. She had been the person who knew which doors to knock on and which conversations to have and how to say the thing that needed saying to the person who needed to hear it, without making it complicated, because she had been doing that for fifty years and had gotten efficient at it.

He owed her more than he had said. He knew that. He had said what he could say, which was that she had made the place worth the bet, and she had deflected it in the way she deflected things, and that was fine. That was the right ending for that part of the conversation.

The phone lit up on the table — a text from Andrea, asking if he was heading home soon. He typed back: *On my way in ten.* Then he set the phone down again and didn't move.

Andy had been in the back seat.

That was what he kept coming back to. The back seat — the place where kids sit because they're not driving, not navigating, not responsible for any of the decisions that are being made in the front. Andy had been back there, listening to his parents' problem without being asked to engage with it, and he had just said the thing. *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* A question. Offered as a question. Not knowing it was an answer.

The name was on the building now. It would be on the building tomorrow. Every dog that came through the door came through a door with Andy's name on it — the name a kid had found in five seconds that two adults had been circling for months.

Jim looked at his hand on the table. He had been working with his hands for most of his life — not carpentry, not construction, but the specific physical work of caring for animals, the getting low, the waiting, the reading of a room full of dogs and knowing what it needed. His hands looked like that. Like they had been used.

He thought about the twenty dogs who had had a good day today on Fayette Street. Gerald and Fig. Pepper. Rosie, whose whole back end went when she came through the door. The shepherd mix who had pressed herself against the wall on her first day and was now, months later, one of the regulars, the dog who had found her spot and her people and had no more reason to be afraid.

Dottie had made it possible to build the place those dogs came to. Jim and Andrea had built it. Andy had named it. And the community had come — the families from the new subdivisions, the commuters from Gainesville, the people who needed somewhere for their dogs to go and had found somewhere good — and they had kept coming, and the awards on the wall were accurate, and the business was what it was supposed to be.

He picked up his jacket from the back of the chair.

Cassie got up from under the table, reading the movement, already heading toward the door.

"Not yet," he told her. "I've got one more minute."

She stopped and looked at him. She did not sit. She stood at the edge of the kitchen, watching him with the patient, specific attention of a dog who has learned that *one more minute* means approximately one more minute and is willing to wait.

He stood there in the kitchen with his jacket in his hand and thought about the call. Not about what had been said — about what the call had been. He had called a woman in the hospital because she mattered to him, and she had answered, and she had been ready to work, and they had talked for twenty minutes about Haymarket and the business and the years that had made both of those things what they were. And then they had said goodbye.

That was all it was. That was everything it was.

He put on his jacket.

Cassie moved toward the door, her nails clicking on the hardwood, already into the next moment, the next thing, the present tense she lived in without effort. He followed her to the door and opened it and she went out ahead of him into the October evening, the air cold now and smelling of leaves and woodsmoke from somewhere down the road.

He locked the door behind him.

The Blue Ridge was invisible in the dark, but he knew where it was. He stood on the step for a moment, the keys in his hand, and listened to the quiet of the neighborhood, the distant sound of Route 15, the ordinary sound of a town going about its evening. Somewhere in that town was a building with his son's name on it. Somewhere in a hospital room a woman was still ready to work a listing.

He went to his car.

He sat in it for a moment without starting it, his hands on the wheel. Cassie was inside now, her evening done, already in whatever room she had decided was hers for the night. He thought about calling Andrea to say he was leaving, and then he didn't, because she already knew, because this was how it went, because she'd be there when he got there.

He started the car.

The heater came on. The radio came on, something he didn't register, and he turned it off and drove in the quiet. The road was familiar in the way that roads get familiar when you've driven them enough times that your hands know the turns before your eyes do. He drove through the dark, the headlights picking out the road ahead, the houses set back from it with their lights on, the ordinary fact of people being home.

He would tell Andrea about the call when he got there. Not everything — not the whole shape of it, not the quality of Dottie's voice or the specific way she had laughed when he said he'd just called to talk. He would tell her the practical part: that Dottie was the same, that she'd asked about the business, that she had given him a name to follow up on for boarding. Andrea would know the rest from the way he said it.

That was enough. That had always been enough.

He turned onto his street.

The house was lit from inside, the warm rectangle of the kitchen window visible from the driveway, and he sat in the car for one more moment after he turned off the engine, not because there was anything left to do but because the day had been what it was and the call had been what it was and Dottie was in a hospital room fifty years into a life she had spent making a place worth living in, and Jim was here, in the driveway, with the work still going and the building still standing and his son's name on the sign.

He got out of the car.

He went inside.
