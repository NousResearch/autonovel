# Chapter Twenty-Two

## March 20, 2024

Dottie Watts Leonard died on a Wednesday. She was seventy-five years old.

---

The funeral home was on Sudley Road, a fifteen-minute drive from Haymarket, and Jim had been there once before for someone else's service, years ago, and had not thought much about the building then. He thought about it now as he pulled into the parking lot: the brick facade, the careful landscaping, the way places like this tried to look like homes without quite succeeding. Andrea was already inside. She'd ridden with him but had gone ahead while he sat in the car for an extra minute, not doing anything in particular, just sitting.

Inside, the carpet was the color of old burgundy and absorbed sound the way heavy curtains do. The room they were shown to was not small. Jim had expected small. He had thought of Dottie as a private person — not reclusive, but someone whose work spoke louder than her biography, and he'd assumed the room would reflect that. It didn't. The room was full.

He stood at the back for a moment and counted, roughly, the way you do when you're trying to understand what you're looking at. Sixty people, maybe seventy. A few more arriving behind him. He recognized faces from Town Council meetings — not just Councilmembers but the regulars, the people who showed up because they cared about what happened in that room. He recognized people from Washington Street, from the businesses that had been there twenty years before A Pup's Valley existed and would be there twenty years after. He recognized a woman who had sold him mulch every spring for four years and had never once mentioned that she knew Dottie Leonard, and he understood suddenly that this was probably true of most of the room — that Dottie had been present in everyone's life in Haymarket in ways that hadn't announced themselves as connection until she was gone.

Andrea found his arm and steered him toward two seats near the middle.

"I didn't know there'd be this many," she said quietly.

"I should have."

She looked at him. He was looking at the room.

The service was run by a minister who had known Dottie through her church, and he spoke about her faith and her family in the way ministers do — warmly, generally, with the specific details that families provide in advance and the minister weaves in to make it feel personal. Jim listened. He heard Dottie's name many times. He heard the names of her children, her grandchildren, the years of her marriage. He heard the Disney fight mentioned briefly, almost as an aside, the minister saying she was *never afraid to stand up for what she believed in*, and a murmur moved through the room that was not quite laughter and not quite agreement but something between them, the sound of people who knew exactly what that meant.

Andrea's hand was on his arm.

Jim looked at the photograph on the easel near the front. It was Dottie maybe twenty years ago, standing on what looked like Washington Street — the brick sidewalk visible, the lamp posts behind her — and she was looking at the camera the way she looked at everything: directly, without apology, already thinking about the next thing that needed doing.

---

When the minister finished and invited people to speak, the room went quiet in the way rooms go quiet when everyone is deciding whether to be the first. Jim looked at Andrea. She was already looking at him.

"You first," she said. It was not a question.

He went up.

He stood at the small podium and looked out at the room. He had spoken in public before — Town Council meetings, a few industry events — and the mechanics of it were familiar enough that he could let his attention go somewhere useful instead of worrying about where to put his hands. He looked at the room. He saw the faces of people who were waiting to be given something true.

He said: "My name is Jim Payne. My wife Andrea and I own A Pup's Valley on Fayette Street. Dottie helped us get there."

He paused. Not for effect — he was finding the next thing, the right thing, the specific thing rather than the general one.

"When we were going through the approval process, Dottie knew who we needed to talk to and what we needed to say. She told us: don't make it complicated. You have a good business and a good location and you're good people. Let them see that." He stopped. "I've thought about that a lot. Because she was right, and because the reason she was right was that she already knew the people in those rooms. She'd spent fifty years making sure she knew them. She'd spent fifty years being the kind of person they'd listen to. So when she told us not to make it complicated, what she was really telling us was that she'd already done the complicated part, for decades, and she was handing us the benefit of it. I don't know if she thought of it that way. She probably just thought she was giving us advice."

Someone in the third row laughed, small and genuine.

"There was a phone call," Jim said. "Near the end. I called her at the hospital, just to talk, and when she picked up she thought I might be calling about a listing. She was ready to work it. I said no, Dottie, I just called to talk. And she laughed, and we talked." He stopped again. "That's who she was. That's the whole story, right there."

He went back to his seat. Andrea squeezed his hand once, brief and firm, and then let go.

She went up.

Andrea stood at the podium and took a breath that the room could not hear. She had been in public situations before that required her to say the true thing when the true thing was hard — the April 14 meeting, four years ago, when she'd stood in front of the Town Council and reported the facts of what the shutdown was doing to the business without performing anything, just giving them the information and letting it land. This was different from that. This was not a report. But the instinct was the same: find the specific thing, the true thing, the thing that nobody else is going to say.

"Dottie and I talked about the brick sidewalks once," Andrea said. "She told me how long it took to get them. Not just the money — the years of arguing about what Haymarket was supposed to look like and who got to decide. She said there were people who thought it was a waste of money, that nobody was going to come to Haymarket to look at brick sidewalks, that it was nostalgia dressed up as civic planning." She paused. "She said those people were wrong. And she said it in the way she said things — like the wrongness was a fact, not an opinion, and she'd already moved on to what needed to happen next."

The room was very quiet.

"She cared about this town the way you care about something you made," Andrea said. "Not made by yourself — she'd be the first to say she didn't do it alone. But she put her hands on it. She fought for it. She knew what it cost and she kept paying it anyway, for fifty years. And then she helped us — people she'd just met, people who were new here — she helped us like we were already part of it. Like she'd decided we were worth the investment." Andrea's voice did not break. It went a fraction quieter, which was different. "I don't know what you call that. Generosity, maybe. But it felt like more than that. It felt like she was handing something forward. Like she trusted us with something she'd spent her whole life building."

She came back to her seat.

Jim said nothing. There was nothing to say that the room didn't already know.

Two more people spoke after them — a woman who'd served on the Council with Dottie in the nineties, a man from her church who told a story about a pie at a potluck that made the room laugh properly this time, warm and releasing. The minister said a few more words. There was music, something hymnal and slow. And then it was done, and people began to move, and the room became the ordinary sound of a gathering after a service: low voices, the shuffle of feet on carpet, the particular awkward tenderness of people who have cried in public and are now trying to decide whether to get coffee.

Jim and Andrea spoke to Dottie's daughter for a few minutes. Her name was Patricia and she had Dottie's eyes and her mother's directness, and she said, "She talked about your place. She was proud of what you'd built." Jim said thank you. Andrea said, "She should be. We were following her example." Patricia looked at her for a moment. "She'd have liked hearing that." She would have, Jim thought. She would have moved on quickly to the next thing, but she'd have liked hearing it.

---

Outside, the March air was cold and specific — not the damp cold of February but something drier, with an edge to it, the kind of cold that means the season is changing but hasn't finished changing yet. The parking lot had emptied some. A few people stood in small groups near their cars, not quite ready to leave, doing the thing people do after funerals where you want to stay near each other a little longer without being able to say why.

Jim and Andrea stood near the Subaru. The Blue Ridge was visible to the west, the mountains a long low line of blue-gray against a sky that was pale and cloudless and very bright for March. Jim had lived in sight of those mountains for years and they still did what they did — made the distance feel like something, made the scale of things clear without comment.

Andrea had her coat pulled close. She was looking at the mountains too.

"She picked the right town," Andrea said.

"Yeah."

A minute passed. A car started somewhere in the lot and pulled out slowly.

"The thing she said," Andrea said. "About not making it complicated. You quoted it right."

"I wasn't sure I remembered it exactly."

"You did." She paused. "I remember her saying it. I was standing right there."

Jim looked at her. "I know. I was talking to her, but I was watching you."

Andrea was quiet for a moment. "Why?"

"Because I wanted to see if you thought she was right."

"Did I look like I thought she was right?"

"You looked like you already knew it and were relieved someone else was saying it."

Andrea made a sound that was not quite a laugh. "That's probably accurate."

The cold moved through, a brief gust that stirred the bare branches of the ornamental trees at the parking lot's edge. Jim put his hands in his coat pockets. He was thinking about the room — the sixty or seventy people, the faces he recognized, the murmur when the minister mentioned standing up for what you believed in. He was thinking about Dottie's photograph on the easel. The brick sidewalk behind her. The lamp post.

"She got to see it," he said. "The business. The awards. She got to see it work."

"She did."

"That matters."

"It does," Andrea said. "It really does."

They stood there for another minute. There was nothing left to do in the parking lot — no one else to speak to, no obligation remaining — but neither of them moved toward the car yet. The mountains were still there, the way they always were, indifferent and solid and somehow the right thing to be looking at.

Jim thought about the name on the building. Andy had been nine, maybe ten, in the back seat, and he'd said it like it was obvious, because to him it was. *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* Jim had driven the rest of the way home holding that name in his mind, turning it over, and by the time he'd walked through the door he'd already known it was right. He'd said it to Andrea and watched her face do what it did — that particular stillness that meant she was hearing something true — and that had been that.

The name was on the building. Andy had named it. Dottie had helped them build what went inside. The town had watched them do it, and some of those watchers were now somewhere on the other side of the funeral home's brick facade, getting coffee, telling stories about a woman who had spent fifty years making a place worth caring about.

"We should go," Andrea said. Not urgently. Just noting the fact of it.

"Yeah."

She reached over and took his hand, briefly, the way you touch something to confirm it's still there, and then she dropped it and went around to the passenger side. Jim got in. He started the car. The heater came on.

He pulled out of the parking lot onto Sudley Road and turned toward Haymarket. The mountains were still visible in the side mirror for a mile or so, the long blue line of them, and then the road curved and they were gone, and there was just the familiar drive home through the familiar country, the houses set back from the road, the bare trees, the pale March sky.

The building would be there when they got back. The name would be on it. Tomorrow morning the dogs would arrive, the same ones they always were — the shepherd mix who liked the spot by the window, the ancient beagle who had to be carried up the two steps at the entrance because his knees were going, the young lab who arrived every Monday like she'd been saving up the energy all weekend and was now prepared to spend it all at once. Andrea would know their names. Jim would get low and let them come to him.

The work would be what it had always been.

He drove. Andrea was quiet beside him, looking out the window at the passing fields, the fence lines, the first suggestion of green at the edges of things that meant the season was actually, finally, turning. She had her hands in her lap and her head slightly tilted, and Jim recognized the particular quality of her silence — not sad, not relieved, but something in between those things that didn't have a clean name. He felt it too.

Dottie was gone. The town was still there. The work was still there. The name was on the building, and the building was on Fayette Street, and Fayette Street was in Haymarket, which was still the place they had chosen, still the place they had bet on, still the place that had been worth the bet.

That was the thing. That was the whole of it. It was enough.
