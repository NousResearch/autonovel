# Chapter Twenty-Two

The judge's call came on a Tuesday, four days after the competition.

Cass had been on the floor of his room with the cello between his knees, not practicing — just holding it, his bow arm resting across the upper bout, the instrument warm from his body heat and the afternoon sun coming through the blinds in slats. He'd been working the same eight bars for an hour. Bar thirty-eight through bar forty-five. 

His phone buzzed against the floor. Unknown number.

He almost let it go. 

"Hello."

A pause. Then a woman's voice, older, careful. "Is this Cass Alderman."

"Yes."

"This is Margaret Fenn. I was one of the judges yesterday." Another pause, shorter. "I'm sorry to call directly. I got your number from the festival coordinator. I hope that's all right."

Cass's hand tightened on the cello's neck. He made himself loosen it.

"It's all right," he said.

"I wanted to call because there's something I say to students sometimes, after competitions, and I couldn't say it yesterday because of the — because you had to leave. I thought it would be wrong to let it go unsaid."

He waited.

"What you did in the second movement," she said. "That transition in bar forty-one. I've heard that piece performed by professionals who have never found what you found in that bar. I wanted you to know that I heard it. That it was real." A beat. "It doesn't change the scoring. I'm not calling about the scoring. I'm calling because someone should tell you."

The needle behind his eye caught. A brief, bright flare.

"Thank you," he said. His voice came out steadier than he expected.

"You'll lose competitions," she said. "You'll lose ones you should have won. That's the part no one tells you clearly enough." She said it without apology, just as information. "What matters is whether you keep finding things in bar forty-one."

"Yes," Cass said. He wasn't sure what he was agreeing to. He agreed anyway.

"Good luck," she said. And hung up.

He sat on the floor with the phone in one hand and the cello in the other for a long time.

The needle was still there. Quiet, steady. Its single frequency.

He got up, set the phone on his desk, picked up the bow, and found it.

---

He found it on the third try.

 The discovery in the performance had happened because he'd been exhausted and his arm had given him less than he asked for, and the restraint had been the thing.

He played it through six times. The seventh time the needle sharpened behind his left eye, and he put the bow down.

It wasn't the worst it had gotten. But it was a warning.

He set the cello in its stand, lay back on the floor, and stared at the ceiling. The plaster had a water stain in the corner that looked, from this angle, like a map of a coastline he'd never seen. He'd been staring at it his whole life. He still didn't know what coast it was meant to be.

His father was downstairs. Cass could hear him moving through the kitchen — the particular rhythm of it, the way his father occupied a room through sound before you ever saw him. Cabinet. Drawer. The dull thud of the cutting board. His father cooked the way he did everything: with complete attention and very little noise.

Cass didn't go down.

He was thinking about what Margaret Fenn had said. Not the compliment — the other part. *You'll lose ones you should have won.* She'd said it like it was useful information, like she was handing him a tool he'd need later. He turned it over. Tried to decide if it helped.

It didn't help yet. Maybe it would.

The needle faded to a low hum and then to nothing. He gave it another few minutes anyway, just to be sure.

---

His father knocked at seven.

"Dinner," he said through the door. Not a question.

"Yeah," Cass said. "Coming."

The kitchen smelled like garlic and olive oil and something else — rosemary, maybe. His father had made pasta, the kind with the breadcrumbs on top that Cass had liked since he was eight. He was already at the table with a glass of water, reading something on his phone, and he put the phone face-down when Cass sat. He always did that. Cass had never said anything about it. 

They ate for a while without talking. Outside, the neighbor's dog was barking at something — two sharp barks, then silence, then two more. A pattern without resolution.

"How's the wrist," his father said.

"Fine."

His father looked at him. Not a long look. Just enough.

"A little tight," Cass said. "Not the wrist. The forearm."

"Same side as before?"

"Yeah."

His father nodded. He spun pasta around his fork with a patience that always made Cass think of someone winding thread onto a spool — careful, even, unhurried. "You want me to call Dr. Patel."

"Not yet."

"Okay." He didn't push it.

"I got a call today," Cass said. "From one of the judges."

His father put his fork down. Not dramatically. Just set it on the edge of the bowl.

"Margaret Fenn," Cass said. "She called to — I don't know. To say she'd heard something in the performance. In bar forty-one."

"What did she say about it."

Cass thought about how to describe it. "She said she'd heard professionals play that piece who'd never found what I found in that bar." He looked at his pasta. "She said it didn't change the scoring. She wasn't calling about the scoring."

His father was quiet for a moment. Then: "What did you say."

"I said thank you."

"That's right."

They ate. The dog outside had stopped. 

"She said I'd lose competitions I should have won," Cass said. "She said it like — like she was telling me to write it down. Like it was a fact I should have already."

His father picked his fork back up. "She's not wrong."

"I know she's not wrong."

"Does it help, knowing she's not wrong."

Cass considered this. "Not yet."

"The bar forty-one thing," Cass said. "I found it again today. After she called."

His father looked up.

"I figured out what it was. Why it worked in the performance. I was under-bowing the preparation. My arm was tired and I gave it less than I'd been giving it in practice, and that — the restraint — was the actual thing."

His father was watching him now with the expression he got when Cass said something he wanted to think about. Not impressed, exactly. More like: *pay attention, this is worth keeping.*

"So the mistake was the right answer," his father said.

"Not a mistake. More like — I was too tired to do it wrong."

His father sat with that. "You going to be able to reproduce it."

"I think so. I played it seven times this afternoon. The first six were — getting there. The seventh I had to stop."

"The head."

"Yeah."

His father nodded. He knew the needle. He'd been watching Cass navigate it for two years, since the first time it had shown up mid-rehearsal and Cass had come home with one eye half-closed and his face the color of old wax. The neurologist had said it was probably a tension response. Probably. His father had written down every instance since then in a small notebook he kept in the kitchen drawer. Dates, duration, what Cass had been doing. He'd never mentioned the notebook. Cass had found it once, by accident, and hadn't mentioned it either.

"I want to talk to Dr. Reyes before the winter concert," Cass said.

His father looked at him. "That's three months."

"I know."

"You want to wait three months."

"I want to see if it changes. If it gets worse, I'll call sooner." He met his father's eyes. "I'm not trying to push through something I shouldn't push through. I just want more data."

His father was quiet for long enough that Cass thought he was going to argue. Then: "All right."

"You're going to keep writing it down."

"I'm going to keep writing it down."

"That's fine," Cass said. "That's — yeah. That's fine."

Something shifted in his father's face. Not much. The kind of shift that only happened when something landed that he hadn't been expecting. Cass had acknowledged the notebook. Cass had let him keep it. They had arrived somewhere without discussing how to get there, and his father, who was not a man who showed relief openly, showed a small amount of it.

They finished eating. His father cleared the bowls without asking. Cass got up to help and his father waved him off, not unkindly, the way he did when he needed to do something with his hands.

Cass leaned against the counter.

"She said the work matters even when the outcome doesn't go right," Cass said. "Margaret Fenn. Not those words exactly. But that's what she meant."

His father was running water over the bowls. He didn't turn around. "Sounds like she's been doing this a long time."

"She has. I looked her up after. She was a finalist at the Tchaikovsky Competition in eighty-nine."

His father turned the water off. Stood there a second. "And she called you."

"She called me."

His father turned around. He was holding a dish towel, not using it, just holding it. He looked at Cass for a moment with an expression that Cass couldn't name and didn't try to.

"Okay," his father said. "Good."

That was all. But it was the right amount.

---

Later, Cass went back upstairs and sat with the cello without playing it. Just the weight of it across his lap, the familiar smell of rosin and old wood, the scroll resting against his shoulder. He did this sometimes when the needle had been active — gave his hands the instrument without asking anything of them. Let his body remember what it was for before asking it to do the thing again.

The window had gone full dark. He could see himself in it, faintly, the cello a shadow across his reflection. He looked older than he felt. He usually did, in windows.

Margaret Fenn had heard bar forty-one. She had heard it and thought it was worth a phone call four days later. He turned this over again. He wasn't sure what to do with it — not the praise, which he could set aside, but the specificity. She'd named the bar. She'd known exactly where the thing had happened.

That meant she'd been listening the way he played. Measure by measure. Not for the overall impression but for the actual music, the place where it lived inside the written notes.

He'd assumed, during competitions, that judges heard the shape of a performance — the arc, the technique, the relative ranking. He'd assumed they were listening for error and excellence in a kind of aggregate. That Margaret Fenn had been in bar forty-one with him, had followed the phrase from thirty-nine into forty-one and heard what happened at the turn — that was something he hadn't considered.

Someone had been in the room with him, fully, at the exact moment the thing had occurred.

The needle was quiet. His forearm ached with a low, distant ache, the kind that was just fatigue. He could live with fatigue.

He thought about what his father had said. *So the mistake was the right answer.* Not quite right, but close enough that Cass hadn't corrected him, because the correcting would have taken longer than the conversation had room for. The mistake wasn't the right answer. The constraint was. The tiredness had removed the option of over-playing, and without that option, the only thing left was the truth of the phrase.

He'd have to learn to be that tired on purpose.

His phone was on the desk. He picked it up, opened a new contact, typed *Margaret Fenn — judge — Tchaikovsky finalist 1989 — bar 41.* 

He set the phone down.

He picked up the bow.

He played bars thirty-nine through forty-one one more time, very slowly, at half the marked tempo, under-bowing the preparation the way his arm had done it when it had nothing left to give. The needle stayed quiet. The phrase opened the way it was supposed to open.

He stopped at forty-one. Let the note ring until it was gone.

Then he put the cello in its stand, turned off the light, and lay down on his bed in the dark. His forearm rested across his chest. He could feel his own heartbeat in it, steady and dull, the pulse of a body that had worked today and was ready to stop.

Downstairs, his father was still moving. The soft percussion of a kitchen being put right — a cabinet closing, the click of a light switch, the creak of the third stair from the bottom. His father's footsteps went past Cass's door and down the hall and stopped.

Silence.

Cass stared at the ceiling. The water stain was invisible in the dark. He knew it was there.

Somewhere in the next few months, before the winter concert, he would go back to Dr. Reyes and describe the needle and its frequency and its duration and his father would hand over the notebook with its two years of careful dates and the doctor would look at all of it and say something. Probably something useful. Maybe something that changed what the next year looked like.

He could live with not knowing yet.

He closed his eyes. The phrase from bar forty-one was still sounding somewhere in the back of his skull, the way music did when you'd played something right — not the notes, exactly, but the shape of them. The place where restraint had opened into arrival.

He held onto it until he slept.
