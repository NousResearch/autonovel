# Chapter 24: A Pup's Valley

The sign went up on a Tuesday.

Jim had ordered it months before opening, back when the permits were still pending and the build-out was still a construction site full of sawdust and the particular smell of new drywall — and it had been propped against the back wall for weeks while they waited for the last inspection, for the certificate of occupancy, for the official permission to be what they already were. When the Tuesday came, Jim put it up himself. He did not make a ceremony of it. He got a ladder and he put the sign up and he climbed down and looked at it from the sidewalk.

*A Pup's Valley.*

The letters were clean against the building's face. The morning was clear,  Fayette Street was quiet. A truck went by on Route 15 two blocks over. Jim looked at the sign for a moment and then he went inside, because there was work to do.

---

That was 2019. The sign is still there.

If you drive down Fayette Street today — not Washington Street, not the brick sidewalk stretch that makes the tourism brochures, but Fayette, the working block, the one that doesn't announce itself — you will see it. The building at 6680. The sign on the face of it. The name in clean letters, the same name a kid said from the back seat of a car on the way home from school one afternoon, the kind of afternoon that looks like nothing from the outside and turns out to be the afternoon everything got decided.

The dogs are inside.

You can hear them if you stand close enough — the particular sound of a room full of dogs that know each other and feel safe, which is a different sound from any other kind of dog noise. 

---

Haymarket is still there too.

This should not be taken for granted. The county surrounds it on all sides — Gainesville to the east with its chain restaurants and its subdivisions with their planted names, the I-66 corridor pressing in from the north, the development logic of Northern Virginia doing what development logic does, which is expand until something stops it. The something that has stopped it, here, for this long, is a combination of incorporated town limits and fifty years of people who cared enough to argue about it in public.

The brick sidewalks on Washington Street did not happen by accident. The lamp posts did not happen by accident. The historical overlay that makes Haymarket look like somewhere with a past — like somewhere that was a place before it became a market for places — did not happen by accident. Someone fought for all of it. Several someones, over several decades, in a room with fluorescent lighting and a dais where the stakes were always small enough to be personal and large enough to matter.

Route 15 and Route 55 still meet at the center of town. That intersection is the whole argument, in compressed form: the road running north-south toward the battlefield, the road running east-west toward the sprawl, and the town sitting at the crossing of both and deciding, every few years, what it wants to be. The argument never ends. This is not a failure. This is what a town that is still alive looks like.

Dottie Leonard knew this. She had been in the argument since before most of the current participants were paying attention, which is to say she had been in it for fifty years, which is to say she had been in it long enough to win some rounds and lose some and understand that the winning and losing were not the point. 

She knew. She had the record to prove it.

She moved to Haymarket more than fifty years ago. She served on the Town Council. She sold real estate for three decades, which means she knew every property in the town and every family that had moved through them and every story that attached to the addresses — who had built what, who had left, who had come back, what the place had been before it was what it was now. 

In 1993 and 1994, Disney proposed a three-thousand-acre history-themed park just outside of town, near the Manassas battlefield. The opposition was national — historians, editorial boards, preservation organizations with offices in Washington — but the local fight was local. Dottie was in it. She looked at what was being proposed and at what it would do to the battlefield and to the town and she said no. She said it in the specific, practical way she said all true things, and she kept saying it, and in September of 1994 Disney withdrew. The battlefield is still there. The park was never built.

 It required saying it and meaning it and not stopping. She was good at that. She had been good at it her whole life.

When Jim and Andrea came to Haymarket with A Pup's Valley — with the permits and the zoning hearings and the public comment periods and all the ordinary machinery of getting permission to build something in a real place — Dottie got behind them. Not loudly. That was not her method. Her method was a word said to the right person at the right moment, a name dropped in a room where names mattered, the accumulated credibility of fifty years of showing up applied quietly to the problem of two people who were trying to build something worthy of the town she had spent her life making worth coming to.

She told Gail, later — matter-of-factly, the way she said all true things — that it was exactly what the town needed.

---

Jim called her in the hospital.

She picked up on the second ring. Her voice was the same voice she'd used in council chambers and at closing tables and in the conversations they'd been having for years — clear and ready, the voice of someone who has spent a lifetime in rooms full of people and knows how to be present in them. She thought he might be calling about a listing. She was ready to work it.

Jim said: *No, Dottie, I just called to talk.*

There was a pause. Then she laughed. They talked.

He told Andrea about it that night. He said it the way he said things — precisely, without reaching for what it meant, letting the fact carry its own weight. Andrea listened. She did not say anything for a moment. Then she said: *That's Dottie.*

That was Dottie.

She died on March 20, 2024. She was seventy-five years old. Jim and Andrea went to the funeral, and they stood up and spoke, because she was someone worth standing up for and because the town should know that the people she had helped were there at the end, saying so. They were not the only ones who spoke. They were not even the most prominent ones. But 

The brick sidewalks are still on Washington Street. The lamp posts are still lit in the evenings. The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 is still the argument at the center of everything. Haymarket was here before the Paynes arrived and it will be here after, and what they built in it is part of what it is now — part of the thing that makes it worth arguing about, worth showing up for, worth the fifty years Dottie gave to it and the years Jim and Andrea are still giving.

That is the story.

---

The building at 6680 Fayette Street was empty when Jim and Andrea first walked through it. This is the fact the whole thing rests on: an empty building in a working block of a small town, smelling of old air and potential and the specific quiet of a space that is waiting to become something. Jim walked the floor. He measured. He stood in the middle of the room and looked at what it could hold. Andrea stood beside him and looked at what the families would see when they walked in for the first time, what they would feel when they handed over the leash and drove away.

They saw the same thing. They had always seen the same thing — had seen it at the first facility, years before, in a different town, and had built it there and built it well enough to win awards doing it, and then had chosen to come to Haymarket and build it again because they believed in the place and because the place deserved it. The second bet is always harder than the first. You know more about what it costs. Jim and Andrea made it anyway.

The permits took time. The zoning hearings took time. The build-out took time. The name took a car ride home from school and a kid in the back seat who had been listening to the problem without being asked and who said, the way kids say things when they are being honest and not strategic: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

Jim heard it the way you hear something that was always going to be true. He brought it home to Andrea. She heard it the same way. That was the name. It had taken two adults months to circle what a ten-year-old found in five seconds, because the ten-year-old had no reason to be clever about it. He just said what was true. The name is what it is because a child said so, and the child's name is Andy, and Andy's name is not on the sign but it is in the sign, the way the names of the people who build things are always in the things they build even when you cannot read them directly.

The dogs came in the fall of 2019. First a few, then more. The regulars established themselves — the ones who came every Monday, the ones Jim knew by name and temperament and the specific geometry of how they moved through a room. The families established themselves too. Andrea learned their stories: the family with the anxious lab, the retired couple with the shepherd mix who pressed herself against the wall on her first day and needed two weeks before she trusted the room. The shepherd mix eventually ran to Jim when she came through the door. This took time and patience and the willingness to sit on the floor and wait. Jim sat on the floor and waited.

The business became what they said it would be.

Then March 2020 arrived, and the Governor issued the order, and A Pup's Valley closed, and the floor went empty. The dogs were at home with their families, who were now also at home with their families, all day, every day, in the specific compressed intimacy of a pandemic. The building at 6680 Fayette Street sat quiet. The sign was still on the face of it. The name was still there. Inside, the water stations were maintained and the floors were clean because Jim and Andrea kept them that way, kept the facility ready as if the dogs might come back tomorrow, which was either faith or stubbornness or both.

On April 14, 2020, there was a Special Meeting of the Haymarket Town Council, held partly via Zoom, called to address the budget. Mayor David Leake stopped the budget discussion and invited Andrea Payne to the podium. She went. She told them what had happened to the business since March. She gave them the facts, specific and clean: no revenue, zero, since the shutdown order. The building costs money whether the Governor says stay home or not. She did not cry. She did not ask for sympathy. She reported the situation the way a person reports a situation when they trust the people in the room to understand what the facts mean.

The stimulus ordinance did not pass.

A thirty-day extension on the BPOL tax was approved.

Jim and Andrea went home with that. Thirty days. They looked at what thirty days was worth and they built from it, because that is what they did, because that is what they had always done, because the alternative was a building at 6680 Fayette Street with a sign on it and no dogs inside, and that was not acceptable, and so it did not happen.

The dogs came back when the dogs were allowed to come back. The families came back with them. The shepherd mix who had pressed against the wall on her first day ran to Jim when she came through the door, and kept running to him every morning after that, which is the kind of thing that does not show up in any financial record but is the whole point of the enterprise. Andrea texted photos to the families. The families texted back. The relationships that had gone dormant in March reactivated, one by one, the way a town reactivates after a long winter — not all at once, not with any announcement, just gradually, the ordinary life of the place resuming.

The awards came. Best of Prince William. Best of Virginia.  The point was the dogs on the floor. The point was the families who drove away and trusted. The point was the sign on the building and what it meant that a child's word was on it.

---

Dottie is gone now. The town she made is still here.

This is the thing about places: they outlast the people who shaped them, which is either consoling or terrible depending on how you look at it, and is probably both. The brick sidewalks on Washington Street will be there after everyone who fought for them is gone. The lamp posts will be there. The intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 will still be the argument at the center of everything. Haymarket will still be the town at the edge of the sprawl that has not yet been consumed, the place that looks like somewhere with a past because someone spent fifty years making sure it did.

Dottie made the town. The Paynes built the thing. Andy named it.

The chain of care is complete.  But complete in the sense that every link is present and holds. The town Dottie made was worth building in. The business Jim and Andrea built was worthy of it. The name Andy gave it is honest and specific and exactly right, the way things named by children are honest and specific and exactly right, 

The chain runs forward too. The families who bring their dogs to A Pup's Valley are building something — the daily, unglamorous work of caring for another creature, of showing up, of trusting the people on the other side of the door. Their dogs run to Jim when they come through the door. Their dogs fall asleep on the floor with their heads on their paws. Andrea photographs them and sends the photos to the families who are at work and thinking about their dogs, because she knows they are thinking about their dogs, because she pays attention to what people need before they say it.

This is the business. This is the town. This is what it looks like when people build something worthy of the place they love.

---

The sign is on the building.

There is still work to do. There will always be work to do. That is not a complaint.

Inside, the dogs are there. The sound of them comes through the walls on quiet mornings — not barking, not distress, but the specific sound of animals in their right place. Jim is on the floor with a nervous newcomer, sitting eight feet away, doing what looks from a distance like nothing at all. Andrea is at the front, greeting the family who has just come in for a tour, asking the questions she always asks, learning the name. The floor is clean. The water stations are full. The photo will be taken at eight forty-five.

The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.

The name is on the building. The dogs are inside. The work continues.
