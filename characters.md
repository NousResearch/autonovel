# CHARACTERS.MD
## "Every Dog Has Its Day" — Character Registry

---

> **A note on this registry:** The BUILD prompt was templated for a fantasy novel with invented characters — Cass Bellwright, Maret Corda, a sealed journal, an Academy. None of those people exist in this story. This registry applies the same craft frameworks — wound/want/need/lie, Sanderson's sliders, dialogue distinctiveness — to the real people who are actually here. The frameworks are tools. The people are real. Where a framework doesn't fit, the real person takes precedence.

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Every character entry answers the same questions a fiction writer would ask about an invented character, applied to a documented real person. Where internal life is rendered — wound, lie, need — it is grounded in documented behavior, confirmed events, and the logic of a life as it was actually lived. Nothing here is invented. Everything here is inferred from what is known, clearly labeled as such, and subject to revision when the people themselves provide correction.

The three-slider system (Proactivity / Likability / Competence) is applied as written observation, not judgment. The wound/want/need/lie chain is applied as a craft tool for understanding what drives a person on the page — not as psychoanalysis.

## THE CHARACTERS

---

### 1. JIM PAYNE
**Role:** Co-protagonist, POV character (primary)
**Age at story's opening:** Mid-career adult, established professional

---

#### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Jim has built something before — a successful pet care facility, award-winning, everything he said it would be — and he knows, from that experience, exactly how much work it took, exactly how many times the outcome was uncertain, and exactly what it felt like to be right about something that other people doubted. He carries that knowledge the way a carpenter carries the memory of a difficult joint: as proof of what's possible and as the standard everything after must meet.

**Wound:** The wound is not failure. Jim has not failed in the ways that leave visible scars. His wound is subtler: the particular loneliness of the person who is almost always right about the important things and must wait, repeatedly, for the world to catch up. He sees clearly — the right location, the right model, the right name when Andy says it, the right moment to speak at a Town Council meeting — and then he must navigate the gap between his clarity and everyone else's uncertainty. This is not arrogance. It is the specific exhaustion of the person who shows up prepared to a world that is not.

**Lie:** *If I do everything right, the outcome will be right.* Jim's operating belief — reasonable, earned, mostly true — becomes a liability when the thing that goes wrong is not something he did wrong. The pandemic does not care about his preparation. The shutdown does not care about his permits. The lie is that sufficient competence is sufficient protection.

**Want (external):** To build A Pup's Valley into exactly what he said it would be — the finest dog daycare in the mid-Atlantic — in this specific town, among these specific people, in a way that lasts.

**Need (internal):** To accept that belonging to a place means accepting what the place does to you — that community is not just the meetings you attend and the names you learn, but also the governor's executive orders and the BPOL tax and the moments when the system fails you and you go home anyway.

**Want/Need tension:** Jim wants to build something proof against the world's indifference. He needs to build something that can survive it.

---

#### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 8/10**
Jim does not wait for situations to develop. He researches the councilmembers before he attends his first meeting. He speaks at the September 9 meeting when he has no obligation to. He calls Dottie in the hospital. His proactivity is quiet — he does not dominate rooms — but it is constant. He is always slightly ahead of the situation.

**Likability: 9/10**
The reader empathizes with Jim immediately because he is the rare person who is genuinely good at what he does and genuinely humble about it. He does not claim the title of dog whisperer. The dogs give it to him. His likability is not charm — it is credibility. You trust him because he has clearly earned the right to be trusted.

**Competence: 9/10**
Jim is competent in the specific way that matters most in this story: he knows his trade, he knows his town, and he knows his limits. He does not attempt to be the person who speaks at the April 14 meeting — he understands that Andrea is the right person for that room. This is a form of competence that most people miss: knowing which tool you are.

**Arc type:** Positive arc with a crucible. Jim's belief that preparation is protection is tested by the pandemic and survives — not intact, but deepened. He comes out the other side knowing something he didn't know before: that the community he built relationships with is also the community that can't fully protect him, and that this is not a betrayal.

---

#### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** Precise without being technical. He uses the right word, not the impressive word. *Cage-free* is not jargon to him — it's a description of a philosophy.
2. **Sentence length:** Medium to short. He builds to a point and stops. He does not over-explain.
3. **Contractions/formality:** Contractions in conversation, slightly more formal in public settings — not stiff, just deliberate. At the Town Council microphone, he sounds like someone who prepared what he was going to say.
4. **Verbal tics:** None that call attention to themselves. He is not a person of tics. He pauses before he speaks, which is itself a kind of tic — the pause that means *I'm going to say this right.*
5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** Mostly statements. When he asks questions, they are genuine — he wants to know the answer.
6. **Interruption patterns:** Does not interrupt. Will wait through an uncomfortable silence rather than fill it with something imprecise.
7. **Metaphor domain:** Dogs, buildings, craft. When he reaches for an analogy, it comes from something physical, something made.
8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct. He says what he means. This is not bluntness — it is the absence of performance.

**Example lines:**
- *"I want to be clear about what we're building here. Not the best option in the area. The best option, period."*
- *"He was nervous coming in. Give him a week."*
- *"I'm not calling to ask for anything. I just wanted you to know we made it."*

**No-tags test:** Jim's lines are identifiable by what they don't do — they don't hedge, they don't seek approval, they don't trail off. If a line ends with certainty and doesn't ask for anything in return, it's Jim.

---

#### Physical Appearance and Habits

Jim gets low when he meets a dog — not a crouch exactly, but a lowering of the whole posture, a making-himself-smaller that is not submissive but is clearly intentional. He is still when he's paying attention. Most people fidget; Jim goes quiet in his body. He makes eye contact with dogs the way most people make eye contact with humans — directly, without challenge, long enough to mean something.

He knows the names of everyone in the room before he needs to use them. This is not a trick. It is the practice of a person who believes that knowing someone's name is the minimum requirement for taking them seriously.

**Physical tells:**
- The stillness when he's listening. If Jim goes still, something has his full attention.
- He arrives early to things. Not conspicuously early — just already there when you get there.

---

#### Secrets

1. **The weight of the previous business.** Jim's confidence in the A Pup's Valley model is not abstract — it is built on the specific experience of having done this before, including the parts that were hard. The reader does not immediately know the full shape of that previous experience, what it cost, what it taught him.

2. **The hospital call.** Jim calls Dottie in the hospital without knowing what he's going to say, which is — for him — genuinely unusual. He is not a person who acts without knowing what he's doing. The call is an exception. What it means to him, he does not say out loud.

---

#### Key Relationships

- **Andrea:** The operational partnership is also the marriage. Jim knows which tool he is; Andrea knows which tool she is. They do not compete for the same role. This is rare and it is the reason the business works.
- **Andy:** Jim hears Andy's suggestion from the back seat and brings it home to Andrea without editing it. This is not passivity — it is the recognition that the right answer came from outside his own thinking, and his job is to carry it accurately.
- **Dottie:** Jim understands Dottie the way you understand someone who taught you something important without meaning to teach you anything. He shows up at her funeral and speaks because she spent fifty years showing up. The logic is direct.

---

#### Thematic Role

Jim embodies the question: *What does it mean to be prepared for something you cannot prepare for?* His arc is the arc of competence meeting its limit and finding that the limit is not the end — it is the beginning of a different kind of knowledge.

### 2. ANDREA PAYNE
**Role:** Co-protagonist, POV character (primary, shared with Jim)
**Age at story's opening:** Mid-career adult

---

#### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Andrea has watched people underestimate what dog care actually is — treat it as a service industry rather than a relationship industry, a convenience rather than a trust. She has been in enough rooms where the assumption was that anyone can do this, that the work is simple, that the love is incidental. She has spent her professional life proving otherwise.

**Wound:** The wound is the gap between what she knows the work is and how the world categorizes it. On April 14, 2020, she stands at a podium and explains what the shutdown has done to her business, and the official record reduces it to a clause. This is not a surprise to her. It is the same reduction she has navigated her whole career — the assumption that what she does is not quite serious enough to warrant full attention.

**Lie:** *If I can make them understand, they'll do the right thing.* Andrea's operating belief is that clarity is sufficient — that if she explains the situation well enough, the right outcome will follow. The April 14 meeting tests this directly. She explains it perfectly. The stimulus ordinance does not pass.

**Want (external):** To build A Pup's Valley into a place where every family that walks through the door feels that their dog is known, safe, and loved — and to sustain that place through whatever the world sends at it.

**Need (internal):** To accept that she cannot make everyone understand, and that doing the right thing in the right way is its own justification, independent of whether it produces the right result.

**Want/Need tension:** She wants the world to recognize what she's built. She needs to build it regardless of whether the world does.

---

#### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 8/10**
Andrea is proactive in the domain she controls: operations, relationships, daily decisions. She gives tours without being asked. She learns names before she needs them. When Mayor Leake invites her to the podium, she is ready — not because she anticipated the invitation, but because she has been ready to speak about this every day since the shutdown.

**Likability: 10/10**
Andrea is the emotional center of the novel because she is the character the reader most immediately understands. She is doing something recognizable — building something, loving it, fighting for it — and she is doing it without armor. Her warmth is not performance. The families of Haymarket trust her with their dogs because she is exactly who she appears to be.

**Competence: 9/10**
Andrea's competence is relational. She is the person who knows what day it is, what dog is coming in, what family needs a call back. She does not manage the operation — she is the operation. The distinction matters: a manager can be replaced. Andrea cannot be replaced, because Andrea is the relationship.

**Arc type:** Positive arc with a hard test. Andrea's belief that clarity produces right outcomes is broken at the April 14 meeting and rebuilt into something sturdier: the knowledge that you speak clearly not because it guarantees the outcome but because it is what you owe the situation.

---

#### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** Warm and specific. She uses the dog's name before the owner introduces it. She uses the family's name before they've given her a business card.
2. **Sentence length:** Varies with context. In a tour, she speaks in longer sentences — she is building a picture. At the podium on April 14, her sentences are shorter. She is not building a picture; she is stating facts.
3. **Contractions/formality:** Contractions always. She is never stiff. Even at the podium, she sounds like herself.
4. **Verbal tics:** She tends to begin with the specific before the general — the dog's name, the family's name, the particular situation — before she moves to the principle.
5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** More questions than Jim. She asks because she genuinely wants to know: *How's he doing with the other dogs? Is she eating okay at home?*
6. **Interruption patterns:** Does not interrupt, but she completes sentences — not someone else's sentences, her own. She knows where she's going.
7. **Metaphor domain:** Family. Relationships. She does not reach for abstract metaphors; she reaches for the thing itself.
8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct, but warm. She says hard things without making them harder.

**Example lines:**
- *"Tell me about her. Not the paperwork — tell me about her."*
- *"We're not a facility. That's the wrong word for what we are."*
- *"The stimulus ordinance didn't pass. We went home. We didn't quit."* (narrated, not spoken — but this is her register)

**No-tags test:** Andrea's lines are identifiable by the warmth that is inseparable from the directness. She does not choose between caring and being clear. She is always both.

---

#### Physical Appearance and Habits

Andrea is wherever she's needed, which means she is rarely behind a desk. She gives tours the way someone gives a tour of their home — she knows where the light comes in, she knows which room smells like what, she knows the things that will matter to this particular family. She makes eye contact when she talks to people and she does not look away when the conversation gets difficult.

**Physical tells:**
- She uses the dog's name before she uses the owner's. This is not a strategy. It is the order in which things matter to her.
- She does not fidget at the podium. She is still in the way that people are still when they have prepared not their words but their clarity.

---

#### Secrets

1. **The full weight of April 14.** The official minutes record a clause. What Andrea actually said — the specific words, the specific weight of a specific person speaking about a specific situation — is not in the record. The novel carries it.

2. **The daily cost of running on trust.** Andrea's operation runs on relationships, which means it runs on her. The cost of being the person everyone trusts is that you cannot afford to be uncertain in public. What she carries privately, she does not show at the front desk.

---

#### Key Relationships

- **Jim:** The partnership is expressed in the division of labor — Jim with the dogs, Andrea with the families, both with the town. They do not duplicate each other. This is love made operational.
- **Dottie:** Andrea understands Dottie the way women often understand each other across generations — as someone who built something in a world that did not make it easy, and who built it anyway. Standing up at Dottie's funeral is not a gesture. It is recognition.
- **The families of Haymarket:** These are Andrea's primary relationships outside her own family. She carries them.

---

#### Thematic Role

Andrea embodies the question: *What do you owe a situation that cannot give you what you deserve?* Her answer — you speak clearly, you go home, you do not quit — is the novel's moral spine.

### 3. ANDY PAYNE
**Role:** Supporting character, catalytic
**Age:** Child at the time of the naming scene

---

#### The Function of Andy in This Story

Andy does not need a wound/want/need/lie chain. He is not navigating an arc — he is the pivot point around which an arc turns. His role in the novel is specific and complete: he is the person who named the thing.

The craft requirement for Andy is not psychological depth. It is precision. The scene in the back seat must be rendered with enough specificity that the reader understands why *A Pup's Valley* is the right name and why it could only have come from outside the adult world of branding and permits and BPOL taxes.

**Valley, not daycare.** A valley is a place things come to. It has shelter on the sides and open sky above. Animals know how to find a valley. A child, without agenda, named a dog daycare after a landscape that dogs understand instinctively. This is not a coincidence. It is the logic of someone who has not yet learned to think about businesses as businesses.

---

#### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 5/10** — Andy does not drive the plot. He redirects it, once, permanently.
**Likability: 10/10** — There is nothing not to like. He is a kid who said the right thing.
**Competence: N/A** — Competence is the wrong frame. What Andy has is clarity, which is rarer.

---

#### Speech Pattern

Andy speaks the way children speak when they are not performing: directly, without setup, without awareness that what they're saying might be the thing. *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* There is no buildup. There is no explanation. He says it because it's true and because he doesn't know yet that true things require justification.

On the page, Andy's dialogue should be short, unadorned, and land without comment. The narration does not explain why the name is right. It renders Jim hearing it and knowing.

---

#### Thematic Role

Andy is the novel's thesis made literal: the best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it. He is also the reminder that clarity sometimes requires inexperience — that the adult world of strategy and branding and market positioning can obscure the thing that a kid in a back seat can see plainly.

### 4. DOTTIE WATTS LEONARD
**Role:** Supporting character, connective tissue, historical anchor
**Age at death:** 75 (March 20, 2024)
**Years in Haymarket:** Over fifty

---

#### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Dottie was in the rooms where Haymarket was made — the Town Council chambers, the public hearings, the coalition meetings during the Disney fight. She watched what happened when a community failed to show up and what happened when it didn't. She chose, repeatedly, to be the person who showed up.

**Wound:** The wound of the long-term community builder is invisibility. Not obscurity — Dottie was known, respected, trusted. But the work of building a place's character over fifty years does not produce monuments. It produces brick sidewalks and lamp posts and a town that looks like somewhere worth preserving. People walk on those sidewalks without knowing who fought for them. Dottie knew this and did the work anyway.

**Lie:** *The work is its own reward.* This is not quite a lie — it is true, and Dottie lived it. But it is also the belief that allows a person to accept invisibility as the cost of contribution. The lie is the part where it doesn't cost anything to be uncredited. It does. Dottie paid it.

**Want (external):** To ensure that Haymarket remains a place worth coming to — that its character survives the growth pressure, the development proposals, the people who would turn it into something generic.

**Need (internal):** To be known by the place she spent her life making. Not famous — known. The way Jim calling her in the hospital is a form of being known: someone thought of her, not as a resource or a contact, but as a person.

**Want/Need tension:** Dottie wants to protect the place. She needs the place to protect her — to hold her, to remember her, to send people to her funeral who stand up and speak.

---

#### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 9/10**
Dottie showed up. This is the complete description of her proactivity. She showed up to the Disney fight when it was uncomfortable and nationally visible. She showed up to the Town Council meetings for decades. She answered the phone in the hospital because she was ready to work a listing. Proactivity, for Dottie, was not a personality trait. It was a practice.

**Likability: 9/10**
The reader will love Dottie because she is the person who makes the Paynes' story possible — not by giving them anything, but by having spent fifty years making the town worth building in. She is also, specifically, the person who answers the phone in the hospital thinking it might be a buyer. This is not sad. This is magnificent.

**Competence: 10/10**
Dottie is the most competent person in this story in the domain that matters most to the story: she knows how a community works, what it takes to change one, and what it costs to preserve one. She helped stop Disney. She helped design the historical overlay. She helped the Paynes get their approvals. She did all of this without needing credit, which is the highest form of competence in community work.

**Arc type:** Flat arc (in the structural sense) — Dottie does not change because she has already become what she is. Her arc is the arc of a life fully lived, rendered in the novel as a series of moments that illuminate the Paynes' story by showing them what they are part of.

---

#### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** Professional and precise. She has been in enough rooms to know exactly what words do.
2. **Sentence length:** Efficient. She does not use two sentences when one will do.
3. **Contractions/formality:** Contractions in conversation, formal when the situation requires it. She can shift registers without effort.
4. **Verbal tics:** She answers questions with the answer, not with the preamble. She does not say *that's a great question.*
5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** Mostly statements. When she asks questions, they are tactical — she wants information, not conversation.
6. **Interruption patterns:** Does not interrupt. Does not need to. She waits and then says the thing that closes the discussion.
7. **Metaphor domain:** Real estate, civic process, the town itself. Her metaphors are local and specific.
8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct. Fifty years of public meetings will do this to a person.

**Example lines:**
- *"Are you calling about the listing on Waterfall Road?"* (in the hospital, answering Jim's call)
- *"The historical overlay isn't about nostalgia. It's about what you want the town to be in thirty years."*
- *"Show up. Say what you think. Come back next month."*

**No-tags test:** Dottie's lines are identifiable by their efficiency and their assumption that the person she's talking to can handle directness. She does not soften things that don't need softening.

---

#### Physical Appearance and Habits

Dottie is a woman who has been in enough rooms to know how to occupy one without dominating it. She does not need the center of the room. She has the credibility of someone who has been right about important things for a long time, and this credibility is visible in the way she holds herself — not with performance, but with the ease of someone who has nothing to prove.

She answers the phone. This is a habit that runs so deep it has become character. She answers the phone in the hospital. She was ready to work the listing.

---

#### Secrets

1. **The full cost of the Disney fight.** Dottie confirmed the story to Jim personally. What she carried during those eighteen months — the pressure, the uncertainty, the national attention — is not in the public record. The novel can only gesture at it.

2. **What she thought of the Paynes.** Dottie did not give her support casually. She recognized something in Jim and Andrea that she had spent fifty years looking for: people who understood that a business in a community is not separate from the community. What she said to them privately, in the conversations that don't appear in the official record, is part of the novel's texture.

---

#### Key Relationships

- **Jim and Andrea:** Dottie recognized them as the right people doing the right thing in the right place. Her support was not charity — it was the recognition of quality by someone qualified to recognize it.
- **Haymarket:** Dottie's primary relationship is with the town itself. The Paynes are one chapter in a fifty-year relationship.
- **Jim's call:** The hospital call is the novel's most quietly devastating moment. Jim calls not knowing what he's going to say. Dottie answers thinking it might be a buyer. They talk for a while. He does not tell her it's the last time.

---

#### Thematic Role

Dottie embodies the question: *What does it mean to spend a life making a place worth living in?* She is the answer the novel offers to its own question about community: it is built by specific people doing specific things over long periods of time, without monuments, without guarantees, and often without credit. The brick sidewalks are there because Dottie fought for them. Most of the people walking on them do not know her name.

Jim and Andrea know her name. They stood up at her funeral and said it.

### 5. HAYMARKET AS CHARACTER
**Role:** Setting that functions as participant

---

Haymarket is not a backdrop. It is a participant with its own wound/want/need/lie structure, rendered through its institutions, its history, and the people who show up to its meetings.

**Ghost:** Disney's America, 1993–1994. The town learned that it could be targeted, that its character was not self-protecting, that the fight to remain a specific place rather than a generic one was a fight that had to be actively waged.

**Wound:** The pressure of surrounding growth — the subdivisions named after the things they replaced, the tens of thousands of residents who call themselves Haymarket without knowing who Dottie Leonard was or why the brick sidewalks are there.

**Lie:** *The rules will protect us.* The historical overlay, the Town Council, the approval process — these are real protections, but they are not sufficient protection. The pandemic demonstrates this: the rules cannot protect a small business from a governor's executive order.

**Want:** To remain a place worth coming to — specific, deliberate, self-aware.

**Need:** To extend that specificity to the people who come to it — to make the Paynes, and people like them, genuinely part of what the town is, not just residents who happen to be there.

**Thematic role:** Haymarket embodies the question: *What does a place owe the people who choose it?* The answer the novel offers is: the chance to belong, the chance to fight for it, and the community of people who show up to the same meetings.

**Conflict map:**
- Jim's want (build something proof against the world) vs. the pandemic (the world does not care about preparation)
- Andrea's want (be understood) vs. the official record (which reduces)
- Dottie's want (protect the town) vs. time (which takes everything eventually)
- Haymarket's want (remain specific) vs. growth (which prefers generic)

These are not villain/hero conflicts. They are the conflicts of real life: forces, not enemies. The novel's tension lives in the gap between what people are trying to build and what the world is doing while they build it.

## DIALOGUE DISTINCTIVENESS — NO-TAGS TEST

**Jim:** Ends with certainty. Does not ask for validation. Pauses before speaking. *"He was nervous coming in. Give him a week."*

**Andrea:** Warm and direct simultaneously. Begins with the specific (the dog's name, the family's name) before the general. *"Tell me about her. Not the paperwork — tell me about her."*

**Andy:** Short. Unadorned. No awareness that what he's saying might be significant. *"Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?"*

**Dottie:** Efficient. Professional. Assumes the person she's talking to can handle directness. *"Are you calling about the listing on Waterfall Road?"*

Remove the tags. You can tell who's speaking. This is the test. These four people pass it.

---

## WHAT EACH CHARACTER WOULD NEVER SAY

**Jim** would never say: *I think maybe we should consider...* He considers before he speaks. When he speaks, he has considered.

**Andrea** would never say: *It's fine.* When something is not fine, she says what it is. When something is fine, she says why.

**Andy** would never say anything that sounds like it came from the adult world of strategy. He speaks from outside that world. This is his gift.

**Dottie** would never say: *I don't know if this is the right time to bring this up.* She brings things up when they need to be brought up. The time is always now.

---

*This registry is the ground truth for the characters of this novel. A writer should be able to resolve any question about how a character speaks, what they want, what they're hiding, and how they relate to every other character from what is written here. Where this document is silent, the answer is: go find the real person and ask them. They are still here. That is the privilege and the responsibility of narrative nonfiction.*
