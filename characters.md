# CHARACTERS.MD — Every Dog Has Its Day
### The Definitive Character Registry

---

## A NOTE ON THIS DOCUMENT

The character template that generated this request was built for invented fiction. The character list it specified — Cass Bellwright, Eddan Bellwright, Maret Corda, Rector Suvaine, Torvald Hess — does not exist in this story. This novel has no Academy, no Compact, no sealed journals, no shaking hands connected to fantasy mechanics.

This document has been rebuilt from the ground up to serve the actual characters: the real people of *Every Dog Has Its Day*. The craft frameworks below — wound/want/need/lie, Sanderson's three sliders, dialogue distinctiveness — are applied to Jim Payne, Andrea Payne, Andy Payne, Dottie Watts Leonard, Cassie and Gracie, and the community of Haymarket, Virginia.

These frameworks are tools for understanding real people deeply, not for inventing fictional ones. Applied honestly, they reveal what is already true.

## CHARACTER ONE: JIM PAYNE

**Age:** Mid-fifties (at time of principal events, 2019–2024)
**Role:** Co-founder, A Pup's Valley. Husband. Father. Dog whisperer. Civic participant.
**Arc Type:** Flat arc — Jim does not change his fundamental values. The story tests them. He holds.

---

### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Jim has built something before. He and Andrea ran an award-winning pet care facility before Haymarket. He knows what it costs. He knows what it feels like when it works. He chose to do it again, in a smaller town, with higher stakes — not because the first time was easy, but because he understood what he was capable of and wanted to apply it somewhere it would matter.

**Wound:** The wound is not failure — it is the specific vulnerability of someone who has bet on a place. When you choose a town the way Jim chose Haymarket, you are not just opening a business. You are declaring that this community is worth your best work. The wound is the exposure that comes with that declaration: what if the town doesn't hold up its end? What if the bureaucracy grinds you down, or the pandemic arrives, or the thing you built gets taken from you not by your own failure but by forces entirely outside your control?

**Lie:** *If I do the work correctly — the permits, the relationships, the civic engagement, the quality of the facility — the outcome will be proportional to the effort.*

This is not quite true. Jim knows this intellectually. He does not know it in his body, which is why he keeps showing up to Town Council meetings and learning the names of councilmembers and speaking during Citizens Time about a hotel he has no financial stake in. He is trying to make the world legible by making himself known in it.

**Want:** To build A Pup's Valley into the finest dog daycare in the mid-Atlantic — not for the recognition, but because he knows what excellent looks like and cannot settle for less.

**Need:** To trust that some things are worth doing even when the outcome isn't guaranteed. The pandemic will teach him this. Dottie's death will confirm it.

**Want vs. Need tension:** Jim wants control through competence. He needs to accept that love — for dogs, for a town, for a person — is not a controllable variable. The dogs have always known this. Jim is still learning it.

---

### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 8/10**
Jim does not wait for things to happen to him. He goes to the September 9 Town Council meeting voluntarily. He speaks on behalf of a hotel that doesn't benefit him. He researches the councilmembers, the town planner, the police chief. He builds relationships before he needs them. When the pandemic hits, he does not freeze — he sends Andrea to the podium because she is the right person for that moment, and he knows it.

**Likability: 9/10**
Jim is likable in the most durable way: he is consistent. He is the same person in the Council chambers that he is on the floor with a nervous shepherd mix. He doesn't perform warmth. He demonstrates it, repeatedly, in ways that cost him something — time, attention, the willingness to get low and wait.

**Competence: 10/10**
He has done this before. He knows the industry. He knows the town. He knows the dogs. The awards are not a surprise to Jim — they are confirmation of what he already knew he was building. His competence is the foundation everything else rests on.

---

### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** High but not academic. He uses the right word, not the impressive word. He knows what a BPOL tax is and can explain it without condescension.

2. **Sentence length:** Medium-to-short. He doesn't ramble. He thinks before he speaks and says what he means without circling it.

3. **Contractions/formality:** Moderate contractions in conversation. More formal in civic settings — not stiff, but precise. He treats the Council chambers like they matter, which they do.

4. **Verbal tics:** None that call attention to themselves. He sometimes pauses before he answers a question — not from uncertainty, but from the habit of making sure the answer is right.

5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** Mostly statements. When he asks questions, they are specific and genuine. He is not a rhetorical question person.

6. **Interruption patterns:** He does not interrupt. He waits. This is the same instinct that makes him good with dogs — he lets the other party finish before he responds.

7. **Metaphor domain:** Practical and mechanical. He describes problems in terms of systems: what's working, what isn't, what the fix is. He does not reach for poetic language.

8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct. He says the thing. In emotional moments, he says the practical version of the thing, which is the same instinct.

**Example lines:**

*At the Council microphone:* "I'm Jim Payne, I live on Fayette Street, and I want to say I fully support the hotel. Haymarket needs the tax base and the foot traffic. That's all I've got."

*To a client, about their dog:* "She's doing well. She was cautious the first week — watches the room before she commits. That's a smart dog. She's committed now."

*To Andrea, the night after April 14:* "We got the extension. That's thirty days. We'll figure out what to do with thirty days."

---

### Physical Appearance

Solidly built, the kind of build that comes from work rather than a gym. Dark hair going gray at the temples. He dresses practically — nothing that would look out of place in a Council meeting or on a daycare floor. He looks like someone who has been doing something useful for a long time.

---

### Physical Habits and Tells

- Gets low when meeting a dog. Not a crouch — a genuine floor-level sit, unhurried.
- Stillness. When Jim is thinking, he goes quiet in a way that fills the room rather than empties it.
- He makes eye contact when he's listening, which is more than most people do.
- When he's worried, he gets more organized — lists, logistics, the practical machinery of the next step. The worry doesn't show in his face. It shows in his calendar.

---

### Secrets

1. **The weight of the second bet.** Jim knows that building A Pup's Valley was not a naive first attempt — it was a deliberate choice to do the hard thing again, in a town he loves, with everything on the line. He does not advertise this. The confidence he projects is real, but it is the confidence of someone who has done the math and decided the risk is worth it, not the confidence of someone who doesn't understand the risk.

2. **What the dogs give him.** Jim's gift with dogs is not incidental to his character — it is the truest expression of it. He is, at his core, someone who pays attention without agenda. The dogs respond to this because they can't be fooled by performance. Jim knows this. He does not talk about it because it sounds like a claim, and he doesn't make claims. He demonstrates.

---

### Key Relationships

- **Andrea:** His complement. She is the relational warmth he expresses through action; he is the structural competence she operates within. Their partnership is visible in how the business runs.
- **Andy:** Jim's son named the business. Jim heard it immediately, which says something about the kind of father he is — one who listens to his kid from the front seat.
- **Dottie:** A mentor and ally. The call to the hospital is Jim's most emotionally exposed moment in the novel. He called not because he needed something but because she mattered to him. That is a specific and unusual kind of loyalty.
- **The dogs:** Not pets, not clients, not mascots. Each one is a relationship. Jim keeps track.

---

### Thematic Role

Jim embodies the question: *What does it mean to be competent in a world that doesn't guarantee outcomes?* He is the answer: you do the work anyway, you show up anyway, and you trust that the doing of it is its own justification.

## CHARACTER TWO: ANDREA PAYNE

**Age:** Mid-fifties (at time of principal events)
**Role:** Co-founder, A Pup's Valley. Wife. Mother. The heart of the operation and the novel's emotional center.
**Arc Type:** Positive arc — Andrea moves from someone who absorbs the cost of building something to someone who has learned to speak that cost aloud, in public, without apology.

---

### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Andrea has built something before too. She knows what the early days feel like — the exhaustion, the uncertainty, the gap between what you're trying to create and what currently exists. She has also experienced what it feels like when it works: the families who trust you with their dogs, the dogs who come back Monday morning with their tails already going. She knows what she's doing. She has always known.

**Wound:** The wound is the particular vulnerability of someone whose work is built on trust. When the pandemic hits and the Governor shuts everything down, it is not just revenue that disappears — it is the entire architecture of relationships Andrea has spent months building. Every family she learned. Every dog whose name she knows. Every tour she gave, every follow-up text with a photo. That network of trust goes dormant overnight, and there is nothing she can do to fix it, because the problem is not her.

**Lie:** *If I take care of everyone else — the dogs, the families, the staff, the town — the business will take care of itself.*

Andrea pours herself into the relational work because she is genuinely good at it and because she believes, on some level, that love made practical is sufficient protection against the world. The pandemic proves it isn't. The business can be excellent and still be closed by a Governor's order.

**Want:** To run a facility that families trust completely — not just with their dogs' safety but with their dogs' happiness. She wants to be worthy of the trust people extend when they hand over a leash and drive away.

**Need:** To let herself be seen as someone who also needs something. The April 14 appearance is the moment she does this — she stands at the podium and tells the truth about what is happening, not as a performance of distress but as a factual report. The need is not to be rescued. It is to be witnessed.

**Want vs. Need tension:** Andrea wants to give care. She needs to receive acknowledgment. These are not the same thing, and the tension between them is the emotional engine of her arc.

---

### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 8/10**
Andrea does not wait to be told what to do. She gives the tours. She learns the names. She shows up at the Council meeting when invited and says the true thing. After the shutdown, she does not quit — she rebuilds, relationship by relationship, dog by dog.

**Likability: 10/10**
Andrea is the character readers will love most immediately, because she is the character who loves most visibly. This is not sentimentality — it is the specific, practical love of someone who texts you a photo of your dog because she knows you're worried. That is a skill and a choice, and it is irresistible.

**Competence: 9/10**
Andrea's competence is relational rather than technical, which makes it harder to see and easier to underestimate. She is running the most important part of a trust business: the trust part. Without her, the facility is just a building with dogs in it.

---

### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** Warm and specific. She uses the precise word for a dog's behavior — not "hyper," but "overstimulated." Not "shy," but "needs a slow introduction." She has a professional vocabulary that she wears lightly.

2. **Sentence length:** Variable. Short when she's being direct; longer when she's building a picture for someone, helping them understand something about their dog.

3. **Contractions/formality:** High contraction rate in conversation — she sounds like a neighbor, not a service provider. At the podium, she pulls toward formality not because she's performing it but because the situation requires it.

4. **Verbal tics:** She asks follow-up questions. Not as a technique — as genuine interest. She wants to know the dog's history, the family's schedule, the thing that happened last Tuesday that made the dog anxious.

5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** Higher question ratio than Jim. She gathers information before she concludes.

6. **Interruption patterns:** She doesn't interrupt, but she leans in — physically and verbally — when something interests her. She finishes other people's sentences occasionally, not from impatience but from genuine connection.

7. **Metaphor domain:** Domestic and relational. She describes the daycare in terms of a household — who's getting along with whom, who needs a quieter corner, who's having a rough week.

8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct in professional contexts. In emotional contexts, she leads with the practical and lets the emotional register underneath it.

**Example lines:**

*On a tour:* "We don't use crates. So what that means is your dog is never isolated — but it also means our staff has to actually know what's happening in the room at all times. That's the trade-off, and we think it's the right one."

*To a worried client:* "She ate well, she played for about two hours this morning, and then she found a spot by the window and watched the room for a while. Some dogs do that. She's fine. Here —" *[sends photo]*

*At the April 14 podium:* "Since the shutdown order, we've had no revenue. Zero. The dogs are at home with their families, which I understand, but the building doesn't stop costing money because the Governor said stay home."

*To Jim, after the meeting:* "Thirty days. Okay. What do we do with thirty days?"

---

### Physical Appearance

She looks like someone who has been working since early morning and will work until late evening and has made peace with that. Dark hair. She moves quickly and purposefully — she is not a person who drifts through a room. When she stops, she stops completely, giving whoever she's talking to her full attention.

---

### Physical Habits and Tells

- She knows where every dog is in the room. Even when she's talking to a client, some part of her attention is tracking the floor.
- She makes physical contact with the dogs constantly — not performatively, but the way you touch a shoulder in passing, a gesture of acknowledgment.
- When she's stressed, she gets quieter rather than louder. The energy doesn't disappear — it goes internal.
- She photographs the dogs. Not for marketing, though it serves that too. Because she knows the owners are thinking about their dogs, and a photo is the most direct answer to that.

---

### Secrets

1. **The cost of the trust business.** Andrea knows that being the person everyone trusts is not free. Every family she takes on is a relationship she is responsible for. She carries this weight without complaint, but it is weight. The pandemic makes it visible in a way it wasn't before — suddenly she can't fulfill the promise, not because she failed but because the world changed.

2. **What April 14 cost her.** Standing at that podium and saying the true thing about the business was not easy. It required her to be publicly vulnerable in a town where she wants to be seen as competent and trustworthy. She did it because it was necessary and because Jim was right that she was the right person to do it. She did not find it easy. She has not said this to anyone.

---

### Key Relationships

- **Jim:** Her structural partner. He builds the systems; she builds the relationships. The business is the expression of both.
- **Andy:** Her son named the business. She heard it immediately when Jim brought it home. She knew it was right.
- **Dottie:** A mentor and, eventually, someone Andrea mourned. Dottie understood what it meant to build something in a place you love. Andrea understood Dottie in a way that not everyone did.
- **The client families:** Not customers. Extended family of the business. Andrea holds their stories.

---

### Thematic Role

Andrea embodies the question: *What does it cost to make a place where others feel safe?* She is the answer: it costs you the comfort of your own safety, sometimes. You do it anyway, because the alternative is a world with fewer places like that.

## CHARACTER THREE: ANDY PAYNE

**Age:** Child (elementary-school age during principal events, 2019)
**Role:** Jim and Andrea's son. The person who named the business.
**Arc Type:** Flat — Andy does not need an arc. He needs to be exactly who he is.

---

### The Framework, Applied Honestly

Andy does not have a wound/want/need/lie chain in the way adults do. He has not yet accumulated the self-consciousness that produces lies. This is precisely why he named the business correctly when two adults couldn't.

**What Andy has:** A kid's unselfconscious clarity. He heard the problem — the business didn't have the right name — and solved it the way kids solve things: by saying what seemed true without worrying about whether it was strategic.

**Three Sliders:**
- Proactivity: 6/10 — He solved a problem he wasn't asked to solve, from the back seat, without being invited into the conversation.
- Likability: 10/10 — He is a child who did something genuinely good without knowing he was doing it. This is the most likable thing a person can do.
- Competence: 10/10 — *A Pup's Valley* is a better name than anything a branding consultant would have produced. The competence is accidental and therefore perfect.

---

### Speech Pattern

Andy speaks the way children speak when they are being honest: directly, without hedging, without the adult habit of qualifying everything. He says *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* as a simple suggestion, not a declaration. He does not know he has solved anything. He is just answering a question the adults forgot to ask him.

**The single most important line in the novel:** *"Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?"*

It is a question. It is offered as a question. It is received as an answer.

---

### Physical Presence

A kid in a back seat. The specifics of his appearance matter less than the specifics of the moment: the car moving, Jim driving, the question of the name still open, and Andy — who has been listening to his parents' problem without being asked to — offering the solution.

---

### Thematic Role

Andy embodies the novel's quiet thesis: *The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.* He does not need to be present throughout the novel to carry this weight. The name is on the building. He is present every time someone says it.

## CHARACTER FOUR: DOTTIE WATTS LEONARD

**Age:** 75 at death (1948–2024)
**Role:** Haymarket institution. Former Town Councilmember. Real estate agent. Ally to the Paynes. Connective tissue between the town's past and the Paynes' present.
**Arc Type:** Completed arc — Dottie's arc happened before the novel begins. The novel witnesses its ending.

---

### Ghost / Wound / Want / Need / Lie

**Ghost:** Dottie moved to Haymarket more than fifty years ago and chose to stay. That choice — to invest in a small place, to serve on its Council, to sell its real estate, to fight for its character — accumulated into a life. The Disney fight in 1993–1994 is the most dramatic expression of it: she looked at a very large force moving in a very confident direction and said *not here, not this*, and she was right, and she won.

**Wound:** The wound of someone who has given fifty years to a place is the fear that the place will not hold — that the character she helped build will be erased by the development pressure that surrounds it. This is not paranoia. It is pattern recognition. She has watched what happens to small towns in the path of growth.

**Lie:** *The work is never finished.* Dottie cannot stop. She is still ready to work a listing when Jim calls her in the hospital. This is not a lie in the negative sense — it is the operating belief of a woman who has found her purpose in the work and cannot imagine herself without it. The lie is that stopping would mean something was lost. The truth, which the novel suggests gently, is that she did enough. The work is done. The town is still there.

**Want:** To make sure Haymarket remains worth coming to — that the brick sidewalks and the lamp posts and the historical overlay survive the growth that wants to erase them.

**Need:** To trust that she has done enough. That the people who came after her — people like Jim and Andrea — are carrying it forward.

**Want vs. Need tension:** Dottie wants to keep working because she doesn't trust that the work will continue without her. The Paynes' success is the answer to that fear, and she lives to see it, which is the novel's quiet grace.

---

### Three Sliders

**Proactivity: 9/10**
Dottie does not wait for things to happen. She fought Disney. She served on Council. She got behind the Paynes when they were navigating approvals. She picks up the phone on the second ring, in the hospital, ready to work a listing.

**Likability: 9/10**
Dottie is likable in the way that people who have given fifty years to a place they love are likable: she has earned it. The hospital phone call is the most likable moment in the novel because it is the most purely herself she could be. She is not performing. She is just Dottie.

**Competence: 10/10**
Thirty years of real estate in a small town. A seat on Town Council. The Disney fight. The historical overlay. The accumulated credibility that means when Dottie gets behind something, it moves. Her competence is institutional — it lives in relationships and trust built over decades.

---

### Speech Pattern (8 Dimensions)

1. **Vocabulary level:** The vocabulary of someone who has been in rooms full of people her whole life — precise, clear, unpretentious. She knows the legal language of real estate and the political language of a town council and uses both without showing off.

2. **Sentence length:** Efficient. She does not waste words. She has been making points in public for fifty years and knows how to make one.

3. **Contractions/formality:** Moderate formality — she is warm but not casual. She treats people with a kind of courtesy that is not performance.

4. **Verbal tics:** She tends to ask what you need before she tells you what she thinks. Not from indirection — from the habit of someone who has learned that the most useful thing you can do for someone is figure out what they actually need.

5. **Question vs. statement ratio:** Balanced. She gathers information and then offers conclusions.

6. **Interruption patterns:** She does not interrupt. She listens completely and then responds completely.

7. **Metaphor domain:** Property and place. She thinks in terms of what things are worth, what they cost to maintain, what they look like from the outside.

8. **Directness vs. indirectness:** Direct. She says what she thinks. She has been saying what she thinks in public for fifty years.

**Example lines:**

*On the Disney fight:* "We looked at what they were proposing and what it would do to the battlefield and to this town, and we said no. It wasn't complicated. It just required saying it."

*To Jim, during the approval process:* "Here's who you need to talk to. Here's what you need to say. Don't make it complicated — you have a good business and a good location and you're good people. Let them see that."

*On the phone, in the hospital:* "Jim. Are you calling about the Fayette Street listing?" *(A beat.)* "No? All right then. What's on your mind?"

---

### Physical Appearance

A woman who looks like she has been doing something useful for a long time. She carries herself with the particular confidence of someone who knows her town and knows her place in it. Not imposing — authoritative. There is a difference.

---

### Physical Habits and Tells

- She is always prepared. She picks up the phone ready to work. She walks into a room ready to engage.
- She listens with her whole body — she turns toward the speaker, she does not look at her hands.
- She remembers names. Everyone's name. This is not a trick — it is the result of genuinely caring who people are.

---

### Secrets

1. **The weight of the Disney fight.** Dottie confirmed this to Jim personally, in conversations before she died. What she carried from that fight — the knowledge that she had been part of something nationally significant, that she had looked at a billion-dollar corporation and said *no* — is not something she advertised. She did not need the credit. She needed the outcome, and she got it.

2. **What she saw in the Paynes.** When Dottie got behind Jim and Andrea, she was not doing them a favor in the ordinary sense. She was making a judgment about what they were building and deciding it was worth her credibility. She does not make that judgment lightly. She saw something in them that she recognized from fifty years of watching people either build things or not.

---

### Key Relationships

- **Jim and Andrea:** She helped them get started. They stood up at her funeral and spoke for her. This is the novel's complete statement about what community actually is.
- **Haymarket:** Not a relationship with a place — an identity. Dottie is not someone who lives in Haymarket. She is someone Haymarket is partly made of.

---

### Thematic Role

Dottie embodies the question: *What do we owe to the places that made us, and what do we owe to the people who will come after us?* She is the answer: you fight for the place, you help the people who are trying to do something worthy in it, and you trust that the work continues after you're gone. The Paynes at her funeral, speaking for her, are the proof that it does.

## THE DOGS: CASSIE AND GRACIE

**Role:** The Paynes' two golden doodles. Household members. The original A Pup's Valley residents. The novel's most honest characters.

---

### Framework Note

Cassie and Gracie do not have wound/want/need/lie chains. They do not have sliders in the human sense. They have something more useful for this novel: they are always honest. They cannot perform. They respond to what is actually in the room.

**What they represent structurally:**
- They are the reason the business exists. Jim and Andrea did not build this despite loving dogs. They built it because of it.
- They are the ground truth of every scene they appear in. When the humans are navigating bureaucracy or pandemic uncertainty, the dogs are doing what dogs do: existing in the present tense, requiring care, reflecting the emotional weather without editorializing about it.
- Their absence during the shutdown is its own statement. The daycare floor is empty. The house is full.

**Thematic role:** Cassie and Gracie embody the novel's argument that love made practical — the daily, unglamorous work of caring for another creature — is the most real thing in the story. They are present before the business exists and present after the awards come in. They do not know about the BPOL extension. They know if the food bowl is full and if someone is home.

## HAYMARKET, VIRGINIA: THE COMMUNITY AS CHARACTER

The novel's world bible establishes Haymarket as a participant, not a backdrop. The character registry should honor this by mapping the community's key figures as they appear.

---

### Mayor David Leake

**Role:** Mayor of Haymarket during the April 14, 2020 Special Meeting.
**Function in the novel:** He is the institutional voice that stops a budget discussion to invite Andrea Payne to the podium. This is a small act of civic grace — recognizing that the real subject of the meeting is not the budget but the people the budget is supposed to serve.
**Speech pattern:** Formal, procedural, but not cold. He runs a meeting the way someone runs a meeting when they understand that the people in the room are neighbors.

---

### Councilman Shannon

**Role:** Town Council member, recurring presence in the civic life of the novel.
**Function:** He thanks the citizens every single time for caring enough to show up. This is either a tic or a genuine belief, and the novel should leave that ambiguity intact. Either way, it is the right thing to say, and he says it.
**Speech pattern:** Warm, formal, consistent. He has a phrase and he uses it.

---

### The Town Council as Collective Character

Five people who are also neighbors. They debate dump trucks on Fayette Street with the gravity of a Senate hearing because the dump trucks are real and the street is theirs. They are not a bureaucracy in the abstract sense — they are the specific people you might see at the post office. The stakes of speaking to them, in both directions, are different from what they'd be in a larger city. Jim knows this. Andrea knows this. Dottie knew this for fifty years.

## CONFLICT MAP — HOW WANTS COLLIDE

| Character | Want | In Tension With |
|---|---|---|
| Jim | Build the best facility possible | A world that doesn't guarantee outcomes proportional to effort |
| Andrea | Be worthy of every family's trust | A pandemic that removes her ability to fulfill that promise |
| Dottie | Keep Haymarket worth coming to | Development pressure, time, her own mortality |
| The Town Council | Preserve the town's character | The growth that surrounds and presses in |
| The dogs | Be cared for, now, today | Everything the humans are worrying about instead |

The conflicts in this novel are not between characters. They are between characters and circumstances. Jim and Andrea are not in conflict with each other. Dottie is not the Paynes' antagonist. The antagonist is the gap between what people are trying to build and what the world keeps doing to it.

This is the correct shape for this story. The heroism is ordinary: showing up, speaking up, not quitting. The drama is real: a pandemic, a budget meeting, a phone call to a hospital room. The resolution is quiet: the business is open, the awards are real, the name is on the building, and Dottie is gone, and Jim and Andrea were at her funeral, and they stood up and spoke.

## DIALOGUE DISTINCTIVENESS: THE NO-TAGS TEST

Remove the tags. Can you tell who's speaking?

*"She was cautious the first week. That's a smart dog."* — Jim. Precise, specific, brief.

*"She found a spot by the window and watched the room. Some dogs do that. Here —"* — Andrea. Specific, warm, and she's already sending the photo.

*"Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?"* — Andy. Unselfconscious, direct, a question that is an answer.

*"Are you calling about the Fayette Street listing?"* — Dottie. Ready to work. In the hospital.

*"We got the extension. That's thirty days."* — Jim. The practical version of the emotional thing.

*"The building doesn't stop costing money because the Governor said stay home."* — Andrea. At the podium. The facts, specific and clean.

Yes. You can tell who's speaking. The voices are distinct. The test passes.

*This registry is the foundation. Every scene should be checkable against it. If a character says something that contradicts what we know about them here, the dialogue is wrong. If a scene requires a character not listed here, the solution is research, not invention. The people are real. Write them that way.*
