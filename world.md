# WORLD.MD — "Every Dog Has Its Day"
### The Definitive Reference Document for the Novel of Jim and Andrea Payne

---

> **Writer's Note:** This is a work of narrative nonfiction. Every person named in this document is real. Every place is real. Every event happened. The job of this document is not to invent a world — it is to render the real one with enough precision and depth that a writer can inhabit it fully and honestly. Where dates are confirmed, they are stated. Where texture is inferred from documented fact and reasonable context, that is noted. Nothing is fabricated. Everything is grounded.

---

## The World: Haymarket, Virginia

### Physical Reality

Haymarket sits at the eastern edge of the Piedmont, where the land stops being mountains and starts being suburb. The Blue Ridge is visible on clear days from Washington Street — a long, blue-gray suggestion on the western horizon that reminds you this was once the frontier. The town itself is small enough to walk across in fifteen minutes and old enough to have opinions about it.

Route 15 runs through the middle of town as Washington Street. It is two lanes. It has been two lanes for a long time, and there are people in Haymarket who will tell you it should stay that way, and people who will tell you the traffic is already impossible, and both of them are right. Route 55 intersects at the edge of town, and that is where the McDonald's drive-thru sits, and the McDonald's drive-thru is, in 2019, a genuine political issue. The traffic backs up onto the road. This is not a metaphor. This is a town meeting agenda item.

The Piedmont landscape in this part of Prince William County is rolling, green in spring and summer, brown and copper in fall, and mud-gray in the brief Virginia winters that never commit to being real winters. The soil is red clay under the grass. It stains everything. Old-timers know to check their shoes before they come inside.

**6680 Fayette Street** is the address of A Pup's Valley. Fayette Street runs parallel to Washington Street, one block back, quieter, with less traffic and more parking. The building sits in a commercial strip that is neither charming nor ugly — it is the practical Haymarket, the one that exists behind the brick sidewalks and lamp posts of the historic district. The business is cage-free. The dogs move through it like they own it, because in the way that matters, they do.

**The Town Council Chambers** are in the real municipal building on Church Street. The room is small enough that when citizens come to speak during Citizens Time, they are not addressing a distant dais — they are in the room with the people making decisions. Councilman Shannon thanks citizens for showing up. He does this every time. It is not a performance. He means it.

**The historic district** along Washington Street has brick sidewalks and period lamp posts. These are not accidents. They are the result of deliberate policy work done over decades by people like Dottie Leonard, who understood that a town's character is a choice, not a given, and that if you don't choose it someone else will choose for you.

## Sensory Signatures

**Washington Street on a weekday morning:** Coffee from the small shops, exhaust from the Route 15 traffic, the particular smell of old brick warming in the sun. A dog tied outside a shop, waiting. The lamp posts are on even in daylight.

**A Pup's Valley, any given Tuesday:** The smell of dogs — not bad, not good, just honest. The sound of nails on sealed concrete flooring, the particular percussion of a medium-sized dog running at full speed in an indoor space. Somewhere, a golden retriever is barking at nothing. Jim is low to the ground with a nervous shepherd mix, talking quietly, not moving fast. The shepherd is reconsidering its position on strangers.

**The Town Council Chambers, April 14, 2020:** Half-empty because of COVID protocols. The Zoom participants are visible on a screen at the side of the room. The agenda is budget items. Andrea Payne is in the room. Mayor David Leake stops the budget discussion to invite her to the podium. The minutes will record this in seventeen words.

**The Piedmont in March:** Cold that hasn't decided to leave yet. The trees still bare enough that you can see through them. The red clay mud in the low spots. Everything waiting.

## Timeline: The Events That Matter

### 1993–1994: Disney's America

Disney proposed a 3,000-acre American history theme park on the site of a former Civil War battlefield at Haymarket's doorstep. The proposal was national news. The opposition was fierce and came from multiple directions: historians, environmentalists, preservationists, and people who simply did not want a theme park to define what their community became.

Dottie Watts Leonard was part of that fight. She confirmed this to Jim Payne personally, in the conversations they had before she died. Disney withdrew in September 1994. The land stayed what it was.

This matters to the novel because it establishes what Haymarket is: a town that has, at least once, looked at something large and commercially powerful and said *no, not here, not like that.* It is a town with a history of choosing its own character. Dottie Leonard helped make that choice. The Paynes came to a town worth coming to partly because of what Dottie and others refused to let happen thirty years earlier.

### 2018–2019: The Decision

Jim and Andrea Payne are Haymarket residents. They have run a successful pet care facility before — not a first business, not a learning experience, but an award-winning operation. They know what excellence in this industry looks like from the inside. They choose Haymarket deliberately: they want to live and work in the same community. They want to offer something the town genuinely needs. They want to do it better than anyone else.

They begin navigating the real approval process: zoning hearings, permits, public comment periods. Dottie Leonard is in those rooms. She is a former Town Council member. She is a real estate agent of long standing. She knows everyone. Her support does not come with fanfare. It comes in the way that real support comes from people who have been doing this for fifty years — quietly, through the right conversations with the right people, at the right moments.

The early working name is A Dog's Day Out. It is fine. It does not have the soul of the thing yet.

### The Drive Home: Andy Names the Business

Jim is picking Andy up from school. The business still doesn't have its name. Andy, from the back seat, says: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

Jim hears it immediately. He brings it home to Andrea. That is the name. A Pup's Valley.

This is the quiet thesis of the entire novel: the best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.

### September 9, 2019: Jim at the Podium

At a real Town Council meeting, during Citizens Time, Jim Payne walks to the microphone and says he fully supports the planned hotel. Not because it benefits him directly. Because he believes in what Haymarket is trying to become. He knows the councilmembers by name. He knows the town planner. He shows up because that is what you do in a town you've decided to belong to.

### Late 2019: A Pup's Valley Opens

The business opens. It is cage-free. It offers daycare, boarding, grooming, and training. Jim makes a connection with every dog that walks through the door. Andrea learns the name of every dog and the story of every family. The families understand quickly that this is not a kennel. This is somewhere their dog is actually known.

### March 2020: The World Ends

The Governor issues a shutdown. Revenue disappears overnight. The entire premise of a dog daycare — that you need somewhere for your dog while you're somewhere else — evaporates when no one is anywhere else. Jim and Andrea do not quit. They do not close. They figure out what is still possible and they do that.

### April 14, 2020: Andrea Speaks

This is the emotional center of the novel.

A real Tuesday. A real Town Council Special Meeting, held partly via Zoom, partly in-person with reduced attendance. The agenda is budget items — BPOL tax extensions, stimulus ordinances. Mayor David Leake stops the budget discussion to invite Andrea Payne to the podium.

She speaks about what the shutdown has done to A Pup's Valley. About what it means to run a small business in a town you love when the Governor has told everyone to stay home. About the specific mathematics of a cage-free dog daycare when no one needs daycare.

The official minutes of that meeting record that she "discussed some of the challenges the business has had since the start of the pandemic shut down order from the Governor." Seventeen words for something that cost her something real to say.

The stimulus ordinance does not pass. A 30-day BPOL extension is approved.

Andrea and Jim go home. Andy and the dogs are there. They do not quit.

### The Comeback

The dogs come back. The families come back. A Pup's Valley earns its recognition — Best of Prince William, Best of Virginia. Jim is, by this point, known as the dog whisperer. Not a title he claimed. One the dogs gave him, and the families confirmed.

### March 20, 2024: Dottie Watts Leonard

Dottie Watts Leonard passes away at the age of 75.

Jim had called her in the hospital near the end. When she picked up, she thought he might be a buyer calling about one of her listings. She was ready to work it. That is who she was.

Jim and Andrea go to her funeral. They are among the few who stand up and speak for her.

## The Real Characters

### Jim Payne

Jim is a man who shows up. He is detail-oriented, quietly passionate, and stubborn in the way that dreamers and tech people share — not loud stubbornness, but the kind that outlasts opposition simply by refusing to stop. He knows the names of the councilmembers, the town planner, and the police chief. He reads the agenda before the meeting.

With dogs, he operates differently than most people do. He gets low. He gets quiet. He pays attention. He does not rush the introduction. The nervous ones settle. The wild ones focus. The ones who have never trusted a stranger reconsider. He makes a genuine connection with each dog he meets, and the dogs know it before he says a word. This is not a trained technique. It is a quality of attention.

He is also the person who called Dottie Leonard in the hospital. He is the person who stood up at her funeral. He is the person who drove home from school pickup with Andy's words in his head and knew immediately he had the name.

### Andrea Payne

Andrea is the fire. She is the one who walked to the podium on April 14, 2020, in the middle of a pandemic, and spoke plainly about what was happening to their business and why it mattered. She did not make it abstract. She made it specific.

She runs daily operations. She gives tours. She learns the name of every dog and the story of every family. She understood from the start — understood it bone-deep, not as a business principle but as a fact of life — that people don't drop their dogs off at a facility. They entrust you with their family. That understanding is the difference between A Pup's Valley and everywhere else.

Her relationship with Jim is expressed not in grand gestures but in the daily evidence of two people who have made the same bet and keep showing up to honor it.

### Andy Payne

Jim and Andrea's son. He is the reason the business has the name it does. Five words from the back seat of a car: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

He is a kid who is present in the story the way kids are present in family stories — not as a main actor but as the one who says the true thing without knowing it's the true thing, because he hasn't yet learned to second-guess himself.

### Cassie and Gracie

The Paynes' two golden doodles. Household members. Family. The original A Pup's Valley residents. Jim and Andrea didn't build this despite loving dogs. They built it because of it. Cassie and Gracie are the proof of premise.

### Dottie Watts Leonard (1949–2024)

Dottie is Haymarket. Not a symbol of it — the actual thing.

She lived in this town for over fifty years. She served on the real Town Council. She was instrumental in designing the historical overlay — the brick sidewalks, the lamp posts, the deliberate effort to make Haymarket look like somewhere worth preserving. She worked as a real estate agent for decades, which means she spent fifty years knowing who owned what and who was moving in and who was moving out and what that meant for the town.

She helped stop Disney's America. She helped get the Paynes' approvals through. She told Jim about the Disney fight herself, in the conversations they had before she died — the kind of conversations you have with someone who is still working, still present, still ready to take a call about a listing, right up until the end.

She passed on March 20, 2024. She was 75.

Jim and Andrea went to her funeral. They stood up and spoke. They were among the few who did.

## Themes

**Community as character.** Haymarket is not backdrop. It is a participant. The Town Council debates the color of someone's roof. Councilman Shannon thanks the citizens every time. The McDonald's drive-thru is a political issue. These are not details — they are the texture of a place that takes itself seriously.

**The bureaucracy of dreams.** Every permit is a small battle. Every public hearing is a test of whether you actually believe in what you're doing. The Paynes passed those tests. So did Dottie, thirty years earlier, in a bigger fight.

**Love made practical.** The Paynes' relationship is visible in what they do, not what they say about each other. They built a business together. They survived a pandemic together. They showed up for Dottie Leonard's funeral together. That is the love story.

**The pandemic as crucible.** April 14, 2020 is the hinge point. Everything before it is preparation. Everything after is consequence. Andrea at the podium is the novel's center of gravity.

**Dogs as mirrors.** The dogs in this novel are the only completely honest characters. They reflect the emotional state of the humans around them without any agenda. When Jim walks into a room and the nervous dog settles, that is information about Jim. When the families come back after the shutdown, the dogs' joy at returning is information about what A Pup's Valley actually is.

**The chain of care.** Dottie Leonard spent fifty years making Haymarket worth coming to. The Paynes came and built something worthy of it. Andy named it from the back seat. The chain runs from the Disney fight to the funeral, and it is made of ordinary people doing specific things in a specific place because they believed it mattered.

**A name from the back seat.** The novel's quiet thesis. The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.

## Voice Identity

### Tone

Warm but not sentimental. Specific but not clinical. The heroism in this story is ordinary — showing up, speaking up, not quitting — and the prose should honor that by staying close to the ground. No elevation that the facts don't earn. No irony that undercuts the genuine feeling. The humor is dry and real, the kind that comes from people who take their work seriously but not themselves.

Think: *Friday Night Lights* in its precision and community-depth. *The Bear* in its specificity about what it actually takes to run something. *Educated* in its willingness to sit inside a moment without rushing past it.

### Sentence Rhythm

Long sentences for context and landscape, when the world needs to breathe. Short sentences for decisions, for moments, for the things that matter. The rhythm shifts with the scene. A Town Council meeting has a different tempo than Jim on the floor with a nervous dog. Andrea at the podium has a different tempo than both.

Dialogue is direct. People in this story say what they mean, mostly, because they are people who have chosen to be in a specific place doing a specific thing, and that kind of commitment tends to produce directness.

### Vocabulary Register

Plain American English with specific nouns. Not colloquial to the point of slang, not formal to the point of distance. The word-hoard of someone who reads and works and pays attention to the world around them. Technical when the subject requires it — zoning language, pet care industry terms, Town Council procedural vocabulary — but never as a way of showing off. The specificity is in service of accuracy, not atmosphere.

### POV and Tense

Third-person, close. Past tense. The narration stays close to Jim and Andrea, moving between them as the story requires, occasionally pulling back to the wider Haymarket context. It does not rotate through a large cast — it stays with the people whose story this is.

For Dottie, the narration may occasionally move into a slightly wider historical register, because her story is longer and her connection to Haymarket predates everything else in the book. This is not a different POV — it is the same voice, given more room.

### Exemplar Passages

**One:**
Jim got low. That was the first thing — he always got low, which meant he was on one knee on the sealed concrete floor before the dog had finished deciding whether to trust him. The shepherd mix was a rescue out of Manassas, eight months old, and her owner had warned them she didn't do well with men. Jim had nodded and made a note of it and then, when the moment came, he simply got low and went quiet and waited. The shepherd stood at the far end of her leash and watched him. Thirty seconds. A minute. Jim didn't push it. He had all the time in the world, or at least he was willing to act like he did, which amounted to the same thing. The dog took one step forward. Then another. Jim stayed still. When she finally put her nose against his hand, he didn't make a big deal of it. He just let it happen.

**Two:**
The minutes of the April 14, 2020 Special Meeting of the Haymarket Town Council record that Andrea Payne "discussed some of the challenges the business has had since the start of the pandemic shut down order from the Governor." Seventeen words. The minutes don't record that she had driven to the meeting through empty streets, or that the Zoom participants were visible on a screen at the side of the room like people watching from another dimension, or that she had rehearsed nothing and said everything anyway. The minutes record what was said. They don't record what it cost.

**Three:**
Haymarket is the kind of town where the Town Council debates the color of someone's roof, and this is not a complaint. It means people are paying attention. It means the place belongs to the people who live in it. Councilman Shannon thanks the citizens every time they come to speak — every single time — and it is not a performance. He means it. You can tell the difference, if you've been to enough meetings. Jim had been to enough meetings.

**Four:**
She picked up on the second ring. Jim had known she was in the hospital — everyone knew — but she answered like she was in the office, like she was between appointments, like there was a listing she needed to get back to. He told her he was calling to check on her. There was a pause on her end, and then she said something that made him laugh, and they talked for a while, and when he hung up he sat with the phone in his hand for a moment before he put it down. She had been ready to work it. Right up until the end, she had been ready to work it. That was Dottie.

**Five:**
*Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

Andy said it from the back seat, the way kids say things — not because he'd been thinking about it, but because it was there and it was true and he didn't see any reason not to say it. Jim heard it land. He drove the rest of the way home with it sitting in his chest, that particular feeling of recognizing something you'd been looking for. Andrea heard it and said yes. That was the name. Five words from a kid in a back seat, and the thing had its soul.

### Anti-Exemplars

**Too elevated — this is not the voice:**
The town of Haymarket shimmered in the golden light of late afternoon, its brick-laid streets whispering of centuries past, its lamp posts standing sentinel over a community bound by invisible threads of shared memory and common purpose.

*(The real town doesn't shimmer. It has traffic and a McDonald's problem and a Town Council that argues about dump trucks. Stay on the ground.)*

**Too ironic — this is not the voice:**
Jim, ever the self-styled dog whisperer, performed his ritual of getting low to the ground, as if the universe might reveal its secrets to those willing to kneel before a nervous shepherd mix from Manassas.

*(The irony cuts against what is actually happening, which is real and specific and not a performance. Don't undercut it.)*

**Too vague — this is not the voice:**
The pandemic changed everything for small businesses like A Pup's Valley. It was a difficult time for everyone, and Jim and Andrea, like so many others, had to find a way through.

*(This is what you write when you don't know the specific details. We know the specific details. Use them. April 14. Mayor Leake. The BPOL extension. The Zoom screen.)*

**Too sentimental — this is not the voice:**
Dottie Leonard was a woman who gave everything to her beloved Haymarket, and when she passed, a light went out in the town she had devoted her life to protecting and nurturing.

*(Dottie answered the phone in the hospital ready to work a listing. Let that be the thing. The specific fact is more powerful than the sentiment.)*

## Internal Consistency Rules

**What is real must stay real.** No composite characters. No invented scenes presented as fact. If a detail is inferred from context rather than confirmed, the prose should carry that lightly — "likely," "probably," the slight distance of the honest narrator — rather than asserting certainty.

**Dates are anchors.** September 9, 2019. April 14, 2020. March 20, 2024. These are confirmed dates and they function as structural anchors. Everything else orients around them.

**The minutes say what they say.** The April 14, 2020 minutes record seventeen words about Andrea's appearance. The novel can expand what those seventeen words contain, but it cannot contradict them or invent a different outcome. The stimulus ordinance did not pass. The BPOL extension was approved. Those are the facts.

**The dogs are honest.** Every time a dog appears in this novel, it should be doing something specific and true to dog behavior. Not symbolic, not decorative — actual. The dogs are the emotional truth-tellers of the story. They don't perform.

**Haymarket is specific.** Washington Street. Fayette Street. Route 15. Route 55. Church Street. The Town Council Chambers. The brick sidewalks. The lamp posts. The McDonald's. The hotel that was being planned. The dump trucks on Fayette Street. These are real. Use them.

**Dottie's story is her own.** She confirmed the Disney fight to Jim personally. She answered the hospital phone ready to work. She was 75 when she died. She served on the Town Council. She was a real estate agent. These are the facts. The novel honors her by being specific, not by being generous with what it doesn't know.

**Andy said what he said.** The name came from the back seat. That is the story. Don't over-explain it or make it more dramatic than it was. Its power is in its simplicity.

---

## What This Novel Is

A true story about two people who bet on a town and a dream and each other. About a woman who spent fifty years making a place worth coming to, and the people who came. About a kid who said the true thing from the back seat. About dogs who are honest when humans can't be. About a pandemic that tried to end something and didn't. About showing up — to the meeting, to the podium, to the hospital phone call, to the funeral.

This is the story. Stay in it.
