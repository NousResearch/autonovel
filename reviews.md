# Literary Critic's Review

## "Every Dog Has Its Day" — A Review

**A quiet, deeply felt portrait of small-town civic life and the stubborn grace of showing up**

★★★★ (out of five)

There is a particular kind of American story that concerns itself not with the dramatic arc of triumph or catastrophe but with the slow, accumulative weight of doing the right thing in a specific place over a long period of time. *Every Dog Has Its Day* is that kind of story. It is a novel about a cage-free dog daycare in Haymarket, Virginia, and it is also a novel about what it means to belong somewhere — to choose a town, to build in it, to defend it, and to stay.

Jim and Andrea Payne open A Pup's Valley on Fayette Street in the fall of 2019, five months before a pandemic shuts them down. The novel follows them through the zoning hearings, the build-out, the naming of the business (by their son Andy, from the back seat of a car, in a moment of offhand perfection), the first dogs through the door, the shutdown, the financial terror of zero revenue against fixed costs, and the slow reopening. Braided through this is the story of Dottie Leonard, a real estate agent and former Town Council member who spent fifty years shaping Haymarket into the kind of place worth building in — a woman who helped stop Disney from putting a theme park next to a Civil War battlefield and who, decades later, quietly shepherded the Paynes through the approval process because she recognized them as the right kind of people.

The novel's method is accumulation rather than escalation. Scenes repeat with variation: Jim sits on the floor near a frightened dog and waits. Andrea gives a tour and learns a name. The Town Council meets and Councilman Shannon thanks the citizens for showing up. The Blue Ridge is visible to the west. These repetitions are not redundant; they are structural. The novel is arguing, through its form, that the meaningful things in life are not the ones that happen once but the ones that happen every day — the water stations refilled, the floor mopped, the photo sent at eight forty-five.

The prose is restrained to the point of austerity, and this is mostly a virtue. The sentences are clean and declarative, with a cadence that suggests careful oral storytelling — someone sitting across from you and telling you what happened, without embellishment, trusting the facts to carry their own weight. The best passages achieve a plainspoken lyricism: "The town looked, in that light, like something that had decided to survive." The worst passages — and there are a few — tip into a kind of deliberate withholding that begins to feel like a mannerism, the prose performing its own restraint.

The dog scenes are uniformly excellent. Jim's method with anxious dogs — sitting on the floor, not looking, breathing normally, waiting — is rendered with the specificity of someone who has watched this happen and understood what they were seeing. The novel knows that a dog pressing its shoulder against your thigh is a complete sentence. It knows that a tail at neutral height after three weeks of work is a victory. These scenes carry an emotional weight that the novel earns precisely by not reaching for it.

The pandemic chapters are the novel's structural and emotional center, and they work because the novel has spent its first half establishing exactly what is at stake. We know the dogs. We know the families. We know what the empty floor sounds like because we have heard what the full floor sounds like. When Andrea stands at the podium on April 14, 2020, and says "the building doesn't stop costing money because the Governor said stay home," the sentence lands because the novel has made us understand what the building is and what it costs — not just in dollars but in years of attention and care.

The Dottie Leonard material is the novel's most ambitious element and also its most uneven. At its best, it provides the historical and civic context that elevates the Paynes' story from a small-business narrative into something larger — a meditation on what it means to care for a place across generations. At its worst, it risks hagiography. Dottie is presented as so consistently right, so unerringly perceptive, that she occasionally flattens into an archetype rather than a person. The novel tells us she was angry, not scared, during the Disney fight; it tells us she could identify which families would stay at the first meeting. These are compelling details, but they are always presented from the outside, through Jim's admiring gaze, and the reader occasionally wishes for a moment of Dottie being wrong, or uncertain, or simply ordinary.

The novel's greatest risk is also its greatest achievement: it is genuinely, unironically sincere. In an era of literary fiction that tends toward irony, fragmentation, and moral complexity, *Every Dog Has Its Day* offers a story in which good people build a good thing in a good place and are rewarded for it. This could be cloying. It is not, mostly, because the novel is honest about the cost — the empty months, the zero-revenue column, the moment Jim says "it's possible that we don't make it" — and because the dogs are real. You cannot sentimentalize a dog pressing itself against a wall in fear. You can only describe it accurately and then describe what it takes to change it, and this novel does both.

There are structural questions. The novel is long for what it contains, and certain passages — particularly in the later chapters — repeat information and emotional beats that have already been established. The final chapter attempts a kind of summative grandeur that the rest of the novel has been too modest to claim, and the tonal shift is slightly jarring. The narrator, who has been invisible for most of the book, suddenly steps forward to deliver pronouncements ("The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it") that feel earned by the story but not quite by the prose style that has been telling it.

But these are quibbles against a novel that does something genuinely difficult: it makes you care about a dog daycare in a small Virginia town, and then it makes you understand that caring about a dog daycare in a small Virginia town is not a small thing. It is, in fact, the whole thing — the daily, unglamorous, repetitive work of showing up for a place and the creatures in it, human and otherwise.

The sign is on the building. The dogs are inside. The novel, like the business it describes, does the work.

# Professor of Fiction's Review

## Craft Analysis: "Every Dog Has Its Day"

This is a novel of considerable accomplishment and genuine emotional intelligence. It does several things remarkably well, and the things it does less well are, for the most part, the natural consequences of its ambitions rather than failures of skill. I want to be specific about both.

### What Works

**1. The dog scenes as emotional architecture.** The novel's single greatest craft achievement is its use of animal behavior as a vehicle for emotional meaning. Jim sitting on the floor eight feet from a frightened dog and waiting — this is rendered with such specificity and patience that it becomes the novel's central metaphor without ever announcing itself as one. The prose trusts the image. It does not say "Jim's patience with dogs was a metaphor for his patience with the town's bureaucracy" or "the way he waited for the dog was the way he waited for the business to succeed." It simply shows him sitting, and the reader does the rest. This is first-rate craft.

The novel understands something essential about writing animals: the specificity is everything. "A single uncertain flag" of a tail. The "figure-eight pattern" of Cassie's greeting. Gracie carrying a sock "with the particular dignity of a dog who has done something important and knows it." These details are observed, not invented, and they give the novel a textural authority that grounds even its more expansive passages.

**2. The management of emotional restraint.** The novel knows when not to push. Andrea's speech at the April 14 Council meeting is a masterclass in understatement — the prose gives us the facts she delivers, the silence of the room, Jim's hand on her arm afterward, and then moves on. It does not linger. It does not tell us what she felt. The restraint is the feeling. Similarly, the moment when Jim calls Dottie in the hospital and she thinks he's calling about a listing — the novel lets this land without commentary, and it is devastating precisely because no one in the scene treats it as devastating.

**3. The use of repetition as structure.** The recurring elements — the Blue Ridge on the horizon, the fluorescent lights in the Council chambers, Shannon's "I mean that," the smell of the clean facility — function as a kind of liturgical structure. They create the sense of a life lived in cycles, of work that is never finished, of attention that must be renewed daily. This is formally appropriate to the novel's argument about care as a practice rather than an achievement.

**4. The civic texture.** The novel's portrait of small-town governance is one of its quiet triumphs. The Town Council scenes are rendered with affection and precision — the woman with the diagram, the man asking about noise, the specific patience of elected officials who have read the materials. The novel understands that democracy at this scale is personal in ways that democracy at larger scales cannot be, and it treats this as a source of both strength and vulnerability.

### What Needs Work

**1. Repetition crosses from structural to redundant.** The novel's use of repetition is, as noted, one of its strengths. But there is a line between meaningful recurrence and simple redundancy, and the novel crosses it regularly in the second half.

The phrase "the building doesn't stop costing money because the Governor said stay home" appears, in various forms, at least four times. Andrea's speech at the Council meeting is previewed, delivered, and then reflected upon in ways that cover the same emotional ground. The naming of the business — Andy in the back seat — is retold or referenced in nearly every chapter after Chapter Five. By the final chapter, the reader has encountered the same information and the same emotional beats so many times that the effect is dilutive rather than accumulative.

**Specific suggestion:** Go through the manuscript and mark every instance where a piece of information or an emotional beat is repeated. For each repetition, ask: does this add something new — a new angle, a new context, a deepening — or does it simply re-deliver what the reader already has? Cut or substantially rework every repetition that does not add. The naming scene, for instance, should be fully rendered once (Chapter Five does this well) and thereafter referenced only glancingly, if at all. Trust the reader to carry it.

**2. The Dottie Leonard material lacks interiority and complexity.** Dottie is the novel's most important secondary character, and she is also its flattest. She is presented entirely from the outside — through Jim's admiration, through her own declarative statements, through the narrator's assessment — and she never surprises us. She is wise from her first appearance and wise at her last. She is right about the Disney fight, right about the Paynes, right about the town. She says things like "Some things you just know" and "You keep going. That's all there is," and these are presented as wisdom rather than examined as positions.

The result is that Dottie functions more as a symbol than a character. She is the Spirit of Haymarket, the embodiment of civic virtue, the wise elder who validates the protagonists' choices. This is a legitimate narrative function, but it limits the novel's emotional range. A Dottie who was sometimes wrong, sometimes petty, sometimes tired of being the person everyone relied on — that Dottie would be more interesting and, paradoxically, more moving.

**Specific suggestion:** Give Dottie at least one scene — ideally the kitchen-table conversation with Jim — where she is not dispensing wisdom. Let her be uncertain about something. Let her express frustration, or regret, or ambivalence. Let her say something that Jim disagrees with, or that turns out to be wrong. The novel needs her to be a person, not a monument. One moment of genuine vulnerability would do more for her character than all the assessments of her rightness combined.

**3. The narrator's voice in the final chapters is inconsistent with the rest of the novel.** For most of its length, the novel employs a close third-person perspective that moves between Jim and Andrea, with occasional passages of essayistic narration (Chapter Two, Chapter Fourteen) that provide historical and civic context. This works well. The essayistic passages are clearly marked as a different register, and they earn their place by providing information the close-third sections cannot.

But in the final chapter, the narrator steps fully forward and begins making pronouncements that feel tonally alien to the rest of the book:

> "The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it."

> "The chain of care is complete."

> "This is what it looks like when people build something worthy of the place they love."

These sentences are reaching for a summative grandeur that the novel's own aesthetic has spent twenty-three chapters arguing against. The novel's power lies in its specificity, its restraint, its trust in the concrete detail. When it suddenly shifts into the mode of a commencement address, the effect is jarring. The reader feels the author's hand on the scale.

**Specific suggestion:** Rewrite the final chapter to stay in the novel's dominant register. End with a scene, not a summary. The strongest candidate is already in the manuscript: Jim looking at the sign from the sidewalk, then going inside because there is work to do. That image — the sign, the work, the going inside — contains everything the final chapter's pronouncements are trying to say, and it contains it better because it trusts the reader to feel it rather than be told it. Cut or drastically reduce the summative passages. Let the novel end the way it has lived: in the specific, the concrete, the daily.

**4. Andrea is underserved in the middle chapters.** Andrea is the novel's most compelling character — more emotionally complex than Jim, more articulate about what the business costs her, more interesting in her relationship to vulnerability. The tour scenes are among the novel's best writing. Her speech at the April 14 meeting is the novel's emotional climax.

But in the chapters between the opening and the pandemic (roughly Chapters Four through Seven), Andrea recedes. Jim drives the zoning and permit narrative. Jim meets with David Gill. Jim talks to Dottie. Jim walks through the building. Andrea appears in these chapters primarily as a responder — she asks Jim to walk her through it, she says "okay," she watches him think. The novel seems to recognize this imbalance (it gives her the tours, the client relationships, the emotional labor of the business), but the recognition comes late.

**Specific suggestion:** Give Andrea at least one full scene in the pre-opening chapters where she is the primary actor — not responding to Jim's plan but executing her own. The tour-design work she's doing in Chapter Three ("Andrea thought about the tours she would give") is gestured at but never dramatized. Show her designing the intake process, or making the first call to a prospective client family, or having her own conversation with Dottie independent of Jim. The novel needs to establish her agency before the pandemic chapters, so that her April 14 speech feels like the culmination of a character arc rather than the beginning of one.

**5. The timeline is occasionally confusing.** The novel moves between 2019 (pre-opening), early 2020 (pandemic), and 2024 (Dottie's death) without always clearly marking the transitions. Chapter Two jumps from the present tense of Haymarket's history to Dottie's biography to Jim's conversations with her "before she died" to the present-tense description of the Council chambers. The reader can follow it, but with effort that could be reduced.

More significantly, the novel's pandemic timeline is compressed in ways that obscure the actual duration of the crisis. The shutdown begins in March 2020. The April 14 meeting happens. The thirty-day extension is granted. And then — somewhat abruptly — the dogs are coming back, and it's unclear whether we're in summer 2020 or fall 2020 or later. The novel gestures at the length of the closure ("eight months" is mentioned once) but doesn't dramatize the middle of it — the long, featureless stretch between the initial crisis and the reopening.

**Specific suggestion:** Add a brief chapter or extended scene set in the middle of the shutdown — June or July 2020 — that dramatizes the specific texture of the long wait. Not another crisis, not another financial reckoning, but the particular quality of a Tuesday in July when there is nothing to do and the building is still empty and the families are still at home. The novel has the pandemic's beginning and its end but not its middle, and the middle is where the real endurance lives.

**6. Andy is a device more than a character.** Andy names the business and eats peanut butter and does fractions. That is nearly the entirety of his characterization. He exists in the novel primarily to deliver the name and to be the child whose innocence contrasts with the adults' strategic thinking. This is a legitimate function, but it means that every time the novel invokes him — and it invokes him often, particularly in the later chapters — it is invoking an idea rather than a person.

**Specific suggestion:** This is a smaller issue than the others, and it may not require a major intervention. But if the novel is going to return to Andy as often as it does, it should give him at least one scene where he is doing something other than being the kid who named the business. A scene where he visits the facility and reacts to it — with the "studied indifference" the novel mentions but never shows — would ground him as a character and make the naming scene resonate differently in retrospect.

**7. Some of the dog names are reused or inconsistent.** There appear to be at least two dogs named Biscuit in the novel — the Hendersons' chocolate lab and the Delacroix family's golden retriever (and possibly the Ferris family's golden and the Nguyens' lab mix as well). Similarly, some dogs appear under names that seem to belong to different dogs in different chapters (Rosie is described variously as being in "her corner under the east window" and as "chasing the corgi in a wide arc," which may be the same dog at different moments but reads as inconsistent). The effect is disorienting in a novel that stakes so much on the specificity of individual animals.

**Specific suggestion:** Create a master list of every dog name, breed, owner, and behavioral trait mentioned in the manuscript. Ensure each name belongs to exactly one dog. If the novel intends for multiple families to have dogs named Biscuit (which is realistic), make this explicit — a line about how there are always three Biscuits on the floor, or Andrea's system for distinguishing them. As it stands, the reader cannot tell whether the repetition is intentional or an error, and in a novel about knowing each dog individually, that ambiguity is costly.

### Summary Assessment

This is a novel that knows what it is about and is, for the most part, formally adequate to its subject. Its strengths are considerable: the dog scenes, the civic texture, the emotional restraint, the structural use of repetition. Its weaknesses are the natural consequences of its virtues pushed too far: restraint becomes withholding, repetition becomes redundancy, admiration for Dottie becomes hagiography, and the final chapter's attempt at summation undermines the modesty that has been the novel's defining quality.

The core story — two people building something worthy of the place they love, nearly losing it, and bringing it back — is sound and moving. The novel earns its emotions honestly. With targeted revision addressing the issues above, particularly the redundancy, the narrator's voice in the final chapter, and the development of Andrea and Dottie as fully dimensional characters, this could be a genuinely distinguished piece of work.

The sign is on the building. The dogs are inside. The novel needs one more pass to be worthy of both.
