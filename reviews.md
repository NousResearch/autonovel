

# Literary Critic's Review

## "Every Dog Has Its Day" — A Review

**A quiet, deeply controlled novel about small-town civic life, small business, and the particular courage of showing up**

---

There is a genre of American writing that might be called the literature of the unglamorous good — stories about people who do the right thing at the right scale, without irony, without apology, and without the narrative pyrotechnics that contemporary fiction often uses to justify its own existence. Marilynne Robinson works this territory. So does Wendell Berry. *Every Dog Has Its Day* belongs in their company, not because it reaches their heights — it doesn't, quite — but because it shares their conviction that ordinary decency, rendered with sufficient precision, is enough.

The novel follows Jim and Andrea Payne as they open a cage-free dog daycare called A Pup's Valley in Haymarket, Virginia, a small incorporated town at the intersection of two state highways, pressed on all sides by the suburban sprawl of Northern Virginia. The plot, such as it is, moves through the approval process, the opening, the pandemic shutdown, and the slow recovery — punctuated by the life and death of Dottie Leonard, a local real estate agent and former Town Council member who spent fifty years fighting to keep Haymarket worth living in.

The first thing to say is that the prose is remarkably assured. The sentences are clean, unhurried, and almost entirely free of the kind of decorative language that signals a writer trying to impress. The descriptions of Haymarket — the brick sidewalks, the lamp posts, the intersection of Route 15 and Route 55 — accumulate with the patience of someone who has looked at a place long enough to see it clearly. The author trusts the reader to find the weight in small things: a dog's tail moving twice against a cushion, a woman with flour on her forearm deciding whether to ask a follow-up question, the specific smell of a building maintained for no one during a pandemic.

The dogs are rendered beautifully. The novel understands something that most fiction about animals does not: that the interesting thing about a dog is not what it symbolizes but what it does. Gracie carrying a sock through the kitchen "with the particular dignity of a dog who has done something important and knows it." Cassie putting her chin on a knee "not asking for anything, exactly. Just making contact." The shepherd mix who presses against the wall on her first day and, weeks later, runs to Jim when she comes through the door. These are not metaphors. They are observations, and the novel's refusal to make them into anything more than what they are is one of its great strengths.

The pandemic chapters are handled with restraint that borders on the heroic. In a literary landscape glutted with pandemic novels that mistake their characters' suffering for narrative significance, this book does something harder: it shows the shutdown as a financial and emotional fact, specific to these people and this business, without ever reaching for the universal. Andrea standing in the empty facility, cleaning a building that doesn't need cleaning. Jim on the phone with families whose dogs are pacing. The spreadsheet on the kitchen table. The thirty-day BPOL extension that is not a rescue but is something. The novel earns its emotion by refusing to perform it.

Dottie Leonard is the book's moral center, and the author is wise enough to let her remain slightly opaque. We see her through Jim's and Andrea's eyes — the directness, the fifty years of institutional knowledge, the refusal to confuse fear with effort. Her death arrives with the plainness of fact: "Dottie Watts Leonard died on a Wednesday. She was seventy-five years old." The funeral scene is genuinely moving, not because the prose reaches for feeling but because it has spent twenty chapters earning the right to it.

The novel's weaknesses are the flip side of its virtues. The relentless restraint occasionally tips into monotony. The prose style — declarative, paratactic, built on short sentences and deliberate repetition — is effective for two hundred pages and slightly numbing for the remaining hundred. There are stretches, particularly in the middle chapters, where the accumulation of small domestic details begins to feel less like patience and more like stalling. The reader may find herself wishing for a moment of genuine surprise, a sentence that turns in an unexpected direction, a scene that doesn't resolve into the quiet affirmation the novel has trained us to expect.

The characters, too, are perhaps too consistently admirable. Jim is patient and competent and good with dogs. Andrea is perceptive and hardworking and good with people. Andy says the right thing at the right time. Dottie is wise and brave and right about everything. The novel's commitment to honoring these people — and they are clearly based on real people, or a real family — sometimes works against its obligations as fiction. A novel needs friction, and the friction here is almost entirely external (permits, pandemic, finances). The interior lives of the characters are rendered with great sensitivity but little conflict. Jim and Andrea never disagree about anything that matters. They never want different things. They never fail each other. This is admirable in life and slightly limiting in art.

The structural choice to move between close third person and a more essayistic, narrator-driven mode is ambitious and mostly successful. The chapters on Haymarket's history and Dottie's role in the Disney fight have a different energy — more expansive, more willing to editorialize — and they provide necessary context. But the transitions between modes can feel abrupt, and the narrator's voice occasionally intrudes on scenes that would be stronger without commentary. When the narrator writes, "The reader does" (referring to knowledge the characters don't have about the coming pandemic), the effect is more clever than earned.

The final chapters risk sentimentality and mostly avoid it, though the last pages push close to the line. The repeated invocations of the sign, the name, the chain of care — these are effective as culmination but would be more effective with slightly less repetition. The novel tells us what it means a few too many times. It could trust its images more.

But these are quibbles with a book that does something genuinely difficult: it makes goodness interesting. It finds drama in competence, in showing up, in the daily work of caring for animals and a community. It is a novel about people who build something worthy of the place they love, and it is itself a worthy thing — careful, honest, and built to last.

**★★★★** (out of five)

# Professor of Fiction's Review

## Craft Analysis: "Every Dog Has Its Day"

### What Works

**Voice and Prose.** The prose style is the novel's signature achievement. The short declarative sentences, the careful repetitions, the refusal of ornamentation — this is a voice that has been thought about deeply and executed with discipline. Sentences like "The building was just a building. It was also what he and Andrea had bet on" demonstrate the author's ability to move from the literal to the significant without any visible machinery. The influence is clearly late-period literary realism — I hear echoes of Denis Johnson's restraint, Stewart O'Nan's domestic precision, and the Cormac McCarthy of *The Road* (minus the apocalypse). The voice is consistent across twenty-four chapters, which is no small feat.

**The Dogs.** The animal writing is superb and constitutes the novel's most original contribution. The author clearly knows dogs — not in the sentimental way of most fiction, but in the behavioral, observational way of someone who has spent time reading rooms full of them. The intake scenes (Nell, Cooper, Pepper, the shepherd mix) are the novel's best set pieces. Jim sitting on the floor, not looking at the dog, breathing at a normal rate, waiting — this is rendered with the specificity of lived experience, and it gives the novel an authority that no amount of research could replicate.

**Thematic Architecture.** The novel's central argument — that care is a form of civic participation, that building something good in a specific place is a political act — is developed with patience and intelligence. The parallels between Dottie's fight against Disney and Jim and Andrea's fight to keep the business alive during the pandemic are drawn without being forced. The Town Council scenes function as a kind of democratic liturgy, with Shannon's repeated "I mean that" serving as the novel's quiet refrain.

**The Pandemic Chapters.** These are handled with remarkable discipline. The author resists every temptation to universalize, to editorialize about What It All Meant, and instead stays close to the specific: the spreadsheet, the empty building, the dogs at home pacing. Andrea's speech at the April 14 meeting is the novel's emotional climax, and it works precisely because it is not written as a climax — it is written as a report, and the gap between the plainness of the delivery and the weight of what is being reported is where the emotion lives.

---

### What Needs Work — Specific, Actionable Suggestions

**1. Repetition: Distinguish Between Structural Repetition and Redundancy.**

The novel uses repetition deliberately — the sign, the name, the intersection, Shannon's "I mean that," the phrase "dormant, not gone." This is a valid technique and often effective. However, the novel does not always distinguish between repetition that deepens meaning and repetition that merely restates it. By my count, the origin story of the name (Andy in the back seat) is told or referenced in full at least seven times across the novel. The first three or four instances accrue power. The fifth, sixth, and seventh begin to feel like the author doesn't trust the reader to remember.

**Suggestion:** Go through the manuscript and tag every instance of a repeated motif or anecdote. For each instance after the second, ask: does this occurrence add something the previous ones didn't? Does it arrive in a new context that changes its meaning? If the answer is no, cut it or reduce it to a glancing reference. The Andy-names-the-business story should be told fully once (Chapter Five is the right place), referenced obliquely two or three more times, and then trusted to resonate without being restated. The same applies to the Disney fight, which is narrated in full at least three times (Chapters Two, Seven, and Twenty-Four).

**2. Character Interiority: Give Jim and Andrea Separate Inner Lives.**

Jim and Andrea are well-drawn as a unit but insufficiently differentiated as individuals. They agree on everything. They want the same things. They finish each other's thoughts. They are, in narrative terms, a single character distributed across two bodies. This is a problem because the novel is asking us to spend twenty-four chapters with them, and without internal differentiation, the domestic scenes begin to blur.

**Suggestion:** Identify two or three moments where Jim and Andrea might genuinely disagree — not about the business's mission, but about tactics, timing, or priorities. For example: Jim's instinct to go to the building during the shutdown and maintain it versus Andrea's instinct to focus on the families. Or a disagreement about whether to speak at the Council meeting, or about how much to tell Andy. These don't need to be dramatic conflicts. A scene where one of them says "I think you're wrong about this" and the other has to sit with that would do more for characterization than ten scenes of quiet mutual understanding. The novel's commitment to portraying a strong marriage is admirable, but strong marriages contain disagreement, and showing that would make the harmony more convincing, not less.

**3. The Essayistic Chapters: Earn the Narrator's Authority or Remove It.**

Chapters Two and Twenty-Four shift into a more omniscient, essayistic mode — the narrator steps back from close third person and begins to comment on Haymarket's history, Dottie's significance, and the meaning of the story. This is a legitimate structural choice (it recalls the "Time Passes" section of *To the Lighthouse*, or the historical chapters in *Ragtime*), but it creates a tonal problem. The narrator's voice in these chapters is warmer, more openly admiring, more willing to editorialize than the voice in the close-third chapters. Phrases like "This is what the next fifty years of Haymarket will know about Dottie Leonard" and "The best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them" read as thesis statements rather than narrative. They tell the reader what to conclude rather than letting the scenes do that work.

**Suggestion:** Either commit fully to the essayistic mode by giving the narrator a distinct identity and perspective (who is this narrator? why do they care? what is their relationship to the material?) or pull the narrator back to the same restrained register used in the rest of the novel. The historical information about Haymarket and Dottie can be delivered through scene and dialogue — Jim's conversations with Dottie already do this effectively. The final chapter (Twenty-Four) is the most problematic: it recapitulates the entire novel in summary form, telling us what everything meant. This is the chapter most in need of revision. Consider cutting it entirely and ending on Chapter Twenty-Three, which already provides a satisfying conclusion through scene rather than summary.

**4. Temporal Compression and the Missing Middle.**

The novel covers roughly five years (2019–2024) but distributes its attention unevenly. The pre-opening and opening chapters are detailed and immersive. The pandemic chapters are strong. But the period between reopening and Dottie's death — roughly 2021 to early 2024 — is almost entirely absent. The novel jumps from the first weeks of reopening to the awards on the wall to Dottie's funeral without showing us the years of ordinary operation that constitute the actual life of the business. This creates a structural imbalance: we see the crisis in detail but not the recovery, which means the novel's argument (that persistence pays off, that the work continues) is asserted rather than demonstrated.

**Suggestion:** Add one or two chapters set in the middle period — 2022 or 2023 — showing the business in its mature, operational state. Not a crisis chapter. A Tuesday chapter. Show us what the daily work looks like when the existential threat has passed and the only challenge is doing it well, again, today. This would also be the right place to develop secondary characters (staff members, regular families) who currently exist only as names. The novel tells us Andrea knows sixty families. Show us three of them in enough depth that we know them too.

**5. Andy Is Underwritten.**

Andy is the novel's most structurally important minor character — he names the business, which the novel treats as a foundational act — but he is barely present as a person. He appears in three scenes: the car ride (Chapter Five), the night of the Council meeting (Chapter Fifteen), and briefly doing homework (Chapter Seventeen). In each scene he is essentially a device: he delivers the name, he asks "is thirty days enough," he does fractions. We never see him at the business. We never see him with the dogs in a sustained way. We never learn what he thinks about what his parents have built, beyond the single moment of naming it.

**Suggestion:** Give Andy one full scene — not a chapter, just a scene — in which he is at A Pup's Valley during operating hours, interacting with the dogs and the space in a way that is specific to him. Let us see what the business looks like through a child's eyes. This would also address the novel's tendency to treat Andy's naming of the business as a kind of miracle; showing him as a full person with his own relationship to the place would ground that moment in character rather than leaving it as a narrative convenience.

**6. Sentence-Level Monotony.**

The prose style is effective but risks becoming a tic. The dominant sentence pattern is: short declarative, short declarative, slightly longer declarative with a dependent clause. Repeated across three hundred pages, this creates a rhythmic flatness that works against the novel's emotional peaks. The April 14 speech, the funeral eulogies, the phone call with Dottie — these moments should feel different from the Tuesday-morning-water-station moments, and they don't always, because the prose treats everything with the same measured cadence.

**Suggestion:** Vary the sentence structure more deliberately in the novel's key emotional scenes. Allow yourself one or two longer, more syntactically complex sentences at the moments of greatest feeling. Not purple prose — just a shift in rhythm that signals to the reader's ear that something has changed. Look at how Alice Munro modulates sentence length across a story: the long, winding sentences in her reflective passages versus the short, clipped ones in her dramatic scenes. The contrast is what creates the effect. Right now, the novel is all one register.

**7. The "Specific" Tic.**

The word "specific" (and its variants: "specifically," "the specific") appears with extraordinary frequency — I stopped counting at forty. It is the novel's most overused word and has become a verbal habit rather than a meaningful descriptor. "The specific quality of early mornings." "The specific sound of dogs." "The specific physical exposure." "The particular, specific way." In many cases, "specific" is doing no work — it is a placeholder for the actual specificity the sentence should be providing through detail.

**Suggestion:** Search the manuscript for every instance of "specific," "specifically," and "particular" (which performs a similar function). In each case, ask: is the sentence actually being specific, or is it just claiming to be? If the former, the word "specific" is redundant — the detail does the work. If the latter, replace the word with the actual detail. "The specific sound of dogs who know each other" is less effective than "the sound of dogs who know each other — the loose barks, the play-growls, the silences between." Show the specificity; don't label it.

**8. Point-of-View Drift.**

The novel is primarily in close third person, alternating between Jim and Andrea, but the point of view drifts in ways that seem unintentional. Several chapters begin in one character's perspective and shift to the other's mid-scene without a clear break. Chapter Three, for example, opens in Andrea's perspective (she sees Gracie with the sock) but shifts to Jim's interiority ("Jim had a list of what they knew") without transition. More problematically, the narrator occasionally tells us things neither character could know or think in the moment — "This was not an accident. It was a condition that had required maintenance" (Chapter Two) is narrator commentary, not character thought, but it appears in a chapter that otherwise reads as close third.

**Suggestion:** Decide, chapter by chapter, whose perspective you are in, and stay there. If you want to shift between Jim and Andrea within a chapter, use a section break (you already use these effectively elsewhere). If you want the narrator to comment, set those passages apart — either in their own chapters (as you do in Chapter Two) or in clearly marked interludes. The goal is not rigid consistency but clarity: the reader should always know whose eyes they are seeing through.

---

### Summary Assessment

This is a novel of genuine quality and deep feeling, written by someone who cares about precision and has the discipline to pursue it across a long narrative. Its strengths — the prose, the dogs, the civic argument, the pandemic chapters — are considerable. Its weaknesses — repetition, character flatness, tonal monotony, an over-explaining final chapter — are all correctable. The novel it wants to be is within reach. The primary revision task is one of trust: trusting the reader to remember what has been shown, trusting the scenes to carry their own meaning, trusting the silences between the sentences to do as much work as the sentences themselves.

The dogs, I should note, are perfect. Don't touch the dogs.
