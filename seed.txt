# Novel Seed: "Every Dog Has Its Day" — The Story of Jim and Andrea Payne

---

## Overview

This is the true story of two people who bet everything on a small Virginia town and a big dream — to build the finest dog daycare the mid-Atlantic had ever seen. It is a story about community, bureaucracy, love, grit, and the particular madness of believing in something when the world has other plans. Set against the backdrop of Haymarket, Virginia — a colonial-era town squeezed between old America and the sprawling growth of Northern Virginia — "Every Dog Has Its Day" follows Jim and Andrea Payne from neighbors and citizens to entrepreneurs facing a global pandemic, a skeptical town government, and the magnificent chaos of running a business built on unconditional love.

Jim and Andrea came to this with their eyes open. They were not first-timers stumbling into the pet care industry. They had done this before — built and run an award-winning daycare, boarding, and grooming facility. They knew what excellence looked like. They chose Haymarket deliberately: they wanted to live and work in the same community, to offer something the town genuinely needed, and to do it better than anyone else had. Not just the best in the area. A step above the rest. That was the standard they set for themselves before they ever opened a door.

They brought two golden doodles with them into this life: Cassie and Gracie. The dogs were not props or mascots. They were family — the original A Pup's Valley residents, the ones who made the whole enterprise feel true.

---

## Setting

**Haymarket, Virginia** — Population: small enough that everyone knows your name, large enough that parking on Washington Street is a genuine political issue. It is a town where the Town Council debates the color of someone's roof, where a resident can stand up at a public meeting and change the direction of a conversation, and where Councilman Shannon thanks the citizens every single time for caring enough to show up. It sits at the edge of Prince William County, pressed against the slow-rolling hills of the Virginia Piedmont, and it is the kind of place where you move because you want your kids to recognize the police officers by name.

The year is 2019. A hotel is being planned. A new McDonald's drive-thru is causing traffic chaos on Route 55. The Town Council is arguing about solar panels and dump trucks on Fayette Street. And somewhere on that same Fayette Street, at number 6680, Jim and Andrea Payne are dreaming about dogs.

---

## The Characters

### Jim Payne
Jim is a man who shows up. That's the first thing you need to know about him. On September 9, 2019, in the Council Chambers of the Town of Haymarket, Jim Payne walked up to the microphone during Citizens Time and said, for the record, that he fully supported the plans for the new hotel. Not because it benefited him directly. Because he believed in what Haymarket was trying to become.

Jim is the kind of man who reads the agenda before the meeting. Who lives on Fayette Street and pays attention to what happens on Fayette Street. Who knows the names of the councilmembers and the town planner and the police chief. He is detail-oriented, quietly passionate, and stubborn in the way that only dreamers and engineers can be.

He is also, by reputation and by instinct, the dog whisperer. It is not a title he claimed — it is one the dogs gave him. Every dog he meets, he meets fully. He gets low, he gets quiet, he pays attention. The nervous ones settle. The wild ones focus. The ones who have never trusted a stranger reconsider. Jim makes a connection with each and every dog he encounters, and the dogs know it before he says a word. In a business built on trust, this is not a small thing. It is the foundation.

### Andrea Payne
Andrea is the fire. She is the one who stood up — or rather, was invited to the podium — at a Town Council Special Meeting on April 14, 2020, in the teeth of the COVID-19 pandemic, while the world was shutting down around her, and spoke about what it meant to run a small business in a town you love when the Governor has just told everyone to stay home.

She is the heart of the operation. You'll find her there most days — not in an office, not behind a desk, but wherever she's needed. Helping out. Giving tours. Learning the name of every dog and the story of every family. She understood, bone-deep, that people don't just drop their dogs off at a facility. They entrust you with their family. Andrea treated every dog accordingly.

### Andy Payne
Andy is Jim and Andrea's son, and he is the reason the business has the name it does.

It happened on a school pickup — one of those ordinary moments that turns out to matter. Jim was driving, and the question of what to call the new business was still unsettled, still floating. Andy, from the back seat, said: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

Jim heard it immediately. The warmth of it. The way it felt like a place rather than a brand. He brought it home to Andrea, and that was that. A Pup's Valley. Named by a boy who grew up watching his parents build something, who absorbed their love for dogs and community and gave it back to them in five words on the way home from school.

The name carries that origin. It sounds like somewhere you'd want to go. Somewhere the dogs know they're going to have a great day.

### Cassie and Gracie
The Paynes' two golden doodles are not supporting characters — they are proof of concept. Jim and Andrea didn't build A Pup's Valley despite loving dogs. They built it because of it. Cassie and Gracie are the household's original residents, the ones who made the whole enterprise feel true before a single permit was filed or a single client walked through the door.

### Dottie Leonard
Dottie Watts Leonard is Haymarket. Not a symbol of it — the actual thing. She lived in this town for over fifty years. She served on the Town Council. She helped design the historical overlay that gave Haymarket its character — the brick sidewalks, the lamp posts, the deliberate effort to make this place look and feel like somewhere worth preserving. She worked as a real estate agent for decades, which meant she understood, better than almost anyone, what land means and what development costs.

She was a woman who showed up to meetings and changed things. When Disney — the Walt Disney Company, with its billions and its political allies and its governor-backed plan — came to Haymarket in 1993 with designs to build a massive theme park on the rolling Piedmont landscape just outside of town, Dottie Leonard was one of the people who stood up and said no. This is not speculation or inference. She told Jim Payne herself, in conversations they had over the years. The fight was national news — historians, conservationists, and a broad grassroots coalition pushed back against what David McCullough called a "commercial blitzkrieg." In September 1994, Disney withdrew. The Piedmont stayed the Piedmont. Dottie stayed in Haymarket. She had helped make sure of it.

The novel should treat this as the fact it is: Dottie Leonard helped save the town that the Paynes would later choose to build their lives in. Without her, Haymarket might have become something else entirely — a tourist corridor, a sprawl zone, a place hollowed out by the gravitational pull of a $650 million theme park. Instead it remained a town where a woman could spend fifty years knowing her neighbors, shaping her streets, and eventually helping a couple named Jim and Andrea get their dog daycare approved because she believed in what they were building.

By the time the Paynes were navigating permits and zoning hearings, Dottie Leonard was a known quantity — a woman whose opinion carried weight, whose roots ran deep, and who understood that a business isn't just a business. It's what a community becomes. She got behind the Paynes. She helped get the town behind them. When the approvals came through, her fingerprints were on them in the way that only someone trusted by a community can leave fingerprints — quietly, completely, without needing credit.

Dottie Watts Leonard passed away on March 20, 2024, at the age of 75.

Jim called her in the hospital near the end. When she picked up, she thought he might be a prospective buyer calling about one of her listings. She was ready to work it. That is who she was — a woman who was selling real estate from a hospital bed because the idea of not working, not being useful, not being engaged with the town she loved, was simply not something she entertained. She worked until the end. Haymarket was her business, in every sense.

Jim and Andrea went to her funeral. In a room where this woman's half-century of work for Haymarket should have filled every seat, Jim and Andrea were among the only ones who stood up and spoke for her. They said what needed saying about a woman who had shaped the town they'd chosen to build their life in — who had, in ways both direct and indirect, made A Pup's Valley possible.

The novel should honor her fully. Not as a secondary figure, but as the connective tissue between the Haymarket of the past and the Haymarket the Paynes arrived to build something in. She kept this town worth coming to. They came to it. That is not a coincidence — it is a chain of care, passed from one generation of believers to the next.

## The Arc of the Story

### Act One: The Dream Takes Root (2018–2019)

Jim and Andrea are Haymarket residents. They live on Fayette Street. They go to meetings. They watch the town grow. And somewhere in all of this small-town American ordinariness, they decide: *we are going to build something here*.

They have done this before. The award-winning facility in their past is real — they know what it takes, what it costs, what it feels like to get it right. This is not naivety. This is a second act, deliberate and informed. They want to be in Haymarket specifically. They want to live where they work and work where they live. They want to offer a service the town needs and deliver it at a level the town has never seen.

They navigate permits, zoning, approvals — the careful dance with the Town Planner and the Council and the ARB. The Town of Haymarket does not do anything casually. There are procedures. There are public hearings. There are people who show up to comment. Everything is entered into the minutes. And in those rooms, Dottie Leonard's decades of goodwill open doors that might otherwise stay closed.

The early name is A Dog's Day Out. It is fine. It does the job. But it doesn't have the soul of the thing yet.

### Act Two: The Name (The Drive Home)

Jim is picking Andy up from school. The business is open, or nearly open, and the name question is still alive in the way unfinished things stay alive. Andy, from the back seat, says it: *A Pup's Valley*.

Jim goes still for a moment. Then he smiles.

That night, he tells Andrea. She hears it the same way he did — immediately, completely. A Pup's Valley. That's the name. That's what this place is. Their son gave them the words for the thing they had already built.

### Act Three: Open for Business — Then the World Ends (2019–2020)

By late 2019, they are open. A Pup's Valley is real. The town is growing — the hotel is being approved, new businesses are coming, the Mayor reflects on a good year. There is momentum. There is optimism. Jim is making friends with every dog that comes through the door. Andrea is giving tours and learning names and making people feel like they found the right place, because they did.

Then March 2020 happens.

The Governor of Virginia issues a shutdown order. Businesses close. People stop dropping their dogs off because people stop going to work. The entire premise of a dog daycare — that you need somewhere for your dog to be while you are somewhere else — evaporates overnight. Andrea watches the revenue disappear. Jim watches Andrea. They both watch the calendar.

### Act Four: April 14, 2020 — The Night Andrea Spoke

This is the emotional center of the novel.

It is a Tuesday. The Town Council is holding a Special Meeting via hybrid format — some members in the Chambers, Councilman Day on Zoom from his home. They are discussing a Business Stimulus Package. Whether to give restaurants a percentage of their meals tax back. Whether to extend BPOL deadlines. Councilmen are negotiating the arithmetic of compassion.

And then Mayor Leake stops the meeting.

He invites Andrea Payne — owner of A Pup's Valley — to the podium.

She is not a restaurant. The stimulus being discussed is not for her. But the Mayor wants the Council to understand what the shutdown actually means, in human terms, to a small business owner in their town. Not in percentages. In a person's face.

She speaks. The minutes record only that she "discussed some of the challenges the business has had since the start of the pandemic shut down order from the Governor." But imagine what she said. Imagine standing in those chambers — the same chambers where your husband spoke eighteen months ago because he believed in this town — and telling the people who govern that town that you are in trouble. That what you built is in jeopardy.

The stimulus ordinance is ultimately not passed. What does pass is a 30-day extension on business license payments, no penalties, no late fees. It is not nothing. It is not everything.

Andrea and Jim go home. Andy is there. Cassie and Gracie are there.

They do not quit.

### Act Five: The Comeback

The turning point is not dramatic. It does not arrive with fanfare. It arrives the way good things usually do — gradually, then all at once.

The first client who comes back after the shutdown. The dog who runs through the door like no time has passed — because for the dog, no time has. Dogs do not understand pandemics. They understand: *this is the place I love*. And they show it completely, without reservation, the way only dogs can.

Jim is there, getting low, making the connection. Andrea is there, giving the tour to a new family who heard about this place and drove over from the next town because someone told them it was a step above the rest. The business finds its footing. Then it finds its stride.

A Pup's Valley becomes what Jim and Andrea always said it would be: not just the best dog daycare in Haymarket, not just the best in Northern Virginia, but the kind of place that earns awards and keeps earning them. The kind of place where dogs know — before they're even out of the car — that they are going to have a great day.

Welcome to the upgrade.

## Themes

**Community as character.** Haymarket is not a backdrop — it is a participant. The Town Council meetings, the Citizens Time comments, the Planning Commission debates — these are the texture of the world Jim and Andrea live in. The novel should feel like you know this town.

**The bureaucracy of dreams.** Every permit, every public hearing, every zoning question is a small battle in the war of making something real. Use it as dramatic tension, as obstacle, as proof of how much the Paynes wanted this.

**Love made practical.** Jim and Andrea's relationship is expressed not in grand gestures but in showing up — to meetings, to the business, to each other's hard moments. The love story is quiet and load-bearing.

**The pandemic as crucible.** April 14, 2020 is the hinge of the story. Everything before it is preparation; everything after it is consequence.

**Dogs as mirrors.** Dogs throughout the novel reflect the emotional state of the humans around them. A frightened dog at drop-off. A dog who runs to Andrea the moment he sees her. A dog who waits by the door. In a story about people navigating uncertainty and fear, the dogs are the only ones who are completely honest.

**A name from the back seat.** The naming of A Pup's Valley by Andy is the novel's quiet thesis: the best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.

**The chain of care.** Dottie Leonard spent fifty years making Haymarket worth coming to. The Paynes came to it and built something worthy of it. Jim standing up at her funeral — one of the only ones to speak — closes a circle the novel should make visible: the people who protect a place and the people who fill it with new life are part of the same long project.

---

## Tone

Warm. Specific. Grounded. The heroism is ordinary: showing up, speaking up, not quitting. The humor is dry and real, the kind that comes from actually attending a Town Council meeting and watching grown adults debate the color of someone's roof. The emotion earns its moments because the rest of the novel does the unglamorous work.

Think: a Southern Virginia version of *Gilmore Girls* crossed with *The Bear* — community-deep, detail-rich, and quietly devastating in the moments that matter.

---

## Key Story Beats

- **1993–1994** — Dottie Leonard and the Haymarket community help turn back Disney's America, preserving the Piedmont landscape and the character of the town.
- **2018–2019** — Jim and Andrea, experienced operators of a previous award-winning pet care facility, choose Haymarket deliberately and begin building A Pup's Valley. Dottie Leonard helps them navigate the approval process and gets the town behind them.
- **September 9, 2019** — Jim speaks at Town Council in support of the planned Haymarket hotel.
- **The drive home** — Andy, on a school pickup, names the business from the back seat.
- **Late 2019** — A Pup's Valley opens. Jim is the dog whisperer. Andrea is the heart of daily operations.
- **March 2020** — COVID-19 shutdown. Revenue disappears.
- **April 14, 2020** — Mayor Leake stops a Special Meeting budget discussion to invite Andrea to the podium. She speaks about what the shutdown has done to her business.
- **The comeback** — Gradual, earned, real. Dogs return. Families arrive from neighboring towns. Awards follow.
- **Today** — A Pup's Valley is exactly what Jim and Andrea said it would be.
