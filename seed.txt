# Novel Seed: "Every Dog Has Its Day" — The Story of Jim and Andrea Payne

---

## CRITICAL DIRECTIVE — READ BEFORE WRITING ANYTHING

This is a work of narrative nonfiction — a true story about real people, a real place, and real events. Every character in this novel is a real person. Every location is a real place. Every event described happened.

**DO NOT invent fictional characters.** Every person in this novel must be drawn from the characters described in this seed file: Jim Payne, Andrea Payne, Andy Payne, Dottie Leonard, and the real people of Haymarket, Virginia.

**DO NOT invent fictional settings.** The setting is Haymarket, Virginia — a real town in Prince William County. The business is A Pup's Valley at 6680 Fayette Street. The Town Council chambers are real. The Piedmont landscape is real.

**DO NOT invent fictional plot lines.** The story beats are drawn from real events: Town Council meetings, the COVID-19 shutdown, Andrea's appearance before the Mayor, Andy naming the business, Dottie Leonard's history and death. Do not substitute fantasy, allegory, or invented drama for the real story.

**The model for this novel is narrative nonfiction in the tradition of Friday Night Lights, The Orchid Thief, or Educated** — true stories told with novelistic depth, emotional honesty, and specific detail. It reads like a novel because the writing is good, not because the facts are invented.

Write about dogs, daycare, community, bureaucracy, love, the pandemic, and what it means to build something real in a real place. That is the story. Stay in it.

## Overview

This is the true story of Jim and Andrea Payne, who bet everything on a small Virginia town and a big dream — to build the finest dog daycare the mid-Atlantic had ever seen. It is a story about community, bureaucracy, love, grit, and the particular madness of believing in something when the world has other plans.

Set in Haymarket, Virginia — a colonial-era town squeezed between old America and the sprawling growth of Northern Virginia — "Every Dog Has Its Day" follows Jim and Andrea from neighbors and citizens to entrepreneurs facing a global pandemic, a skeptical town government, and the magnificent chaos of running a business built on unconditional love.

Jim and Andrea came to this with their eyes open. They were not first-timers stumbling into the pet care industry. They had done this before — built and run an award-winning daycare, boarding, and grooming facility. They knew what excellence looked like. They chose Haymarket deliberately: they wanted to live and work in the same community, to offer something the town genuinely needed, and to do it better than anyone else had. Not just the best in the area. A step above the rest.

They brought two golden doodles with them: Cassie and Gracie. The dogs were not props or mascots. They were family.

## Setting — Real Place, Real Town

**Haymarket, Virginia** is a real town in Prince William County. Small enough that everyone knows your name, large enough that parking on Washington Street is a genuine political issue. The Town Council debates the color of someone's roof. Councilman Shannon thanks the citizens every single time for caring enough to show up.

The year is 2019. A hotel is being planned. A McDonald's drive-thru is causing traffic chaos on Route 55. The Town Council is arguing about dump trucks on Fayette Street. At 6680 Fayette Street, Jim and Andrea Payne are dreaming about dogs.

A Pup's Valley is cage-free. It offers daycare, boarding, grooming, and training. This is where the story lives.

## The Real Characters

### Jim Payne
Jim is a man who shows up. On September 9, 2019, in the real Council Chambers of Haymarket, he walked up to the microphone during Citizens Time and said he fully supported the planned hotel. Not because it benefited him. Because he believed in what Haymarket was trying to become.

He is detail-oriented, quietly passionate, and stubborn in the way only dreamers and tech people can be. He knows the names of the councilmembers, the town planner, and the police chief.

He is also, by reputation and instinct, the dog whisperer. Not a title he claimed — one the dogs gave him. Every dog he meets, he meets fully. He gets low, gets quiet, pays attention. The nervous ones settle. The wild ones focus. The ones who have never trusted a stranger reconsider. Jim makes a connection with each and every dog he encounters, and the dogs know it before he says a word.

### Andrea Payne
Andrea is the fire. She stood up — or rather, was invited to the podium — at a real Town Council Special Meeting on April 14, 2020, in the teeth of COVID-19, and spoke about what it meant to run a small business in a town you love when the Governor has told everyone to stay home.

She is the heart of the operation. You'll find her at A Pup's Valley most days — not behind a desk, but wherever she's needed. Giving tours. Learning the name of every dog and the story of every family. She understood bone-deep that people don't just drop their dogs off at a facility. They entrust you with their family.

### Andy Payne
Andy is Jim and Andrea's son, and he is the reason the business has the name it does.

It happened on a real school pickup. Jim was driving, the question of what to call the business still unsettled. Andy, from the back seat, said: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?*

Jim heard it immediately. He brought it home to Andrea, and that was that. Five words from a kid in a back seat.

### Cassie and Gracie
The Paynes' two golden doodles are real — household members, family, the original A Pup's Valley residents. Jim and Andrea didn't build this despite loving dogs. They built it because of it.

### Dottie Watts Leonard
Dottie Watts Leonard is Haymarket. Not a symbol of it — the actual thing. She lived in this town for over fifty years. She served on the real Town Council. She helped design the historical overlay that gave Haymarket its character — the brick sidewalks, the lamp posts, the deliberate effort to make this place look like somewhere worth preserving. She worked as a real estate agent for decades.

She was instrumental in stopping Disney's America — a massive proposed theme park — from being built outside Haymarket in 1993-1994. This is a real historical event. Disney withdrew in September 1994. The fight was national news. Dottie told Jim herself, in conversations they had before she died.

Dottie got behind the Paynes when they were navigating the approval process. She helped get the town behind them. When the approvals came through, her fingerprints were on them in the way only someone deeply trusted by a community can leave fingerprints — quietly, without needing credit.

Jim called Dottie in the hospital near the end. When she picked up, she thought he might be a buyer calling about one of her listings. She was ready to work it. That is who she was — a woman who worked until the end.

Dottie Watts Leonard passed away on March 20, 2024, at the age of 75. Jim and Andrea went to her funeral. They were among the only ones who stood up and spoke for her.

## The Real Arc of the Story

### Act One: The Dream Takes Root (2018–2019)
Jim and Andrea are Haymarket residents on Fayette Street. They go to meetings. They know their neighbors. They have run a successful pet care facility before and know exactly what they're doing when they decide to build A Pup's Valley. They navigate real permits, real zoning hearings, real public comment periods. Dottie Leonard is in those rooms, and her support matters.

The early name is A Dog's Day Out. It is fine. It doesn't have the soul of the thing yet.

### Act Two: The Name
Jim is picking Andy up from school. Andy says: *Dad, why don't you call it A Pup's Valley?* Jim tells Andrea. That's the name. A Pup's Valley.

### Act Three: Open for Business — Then the World Ends (2019–2020)
A Pup's Valley opens. Jim is making connections with every dog that walks through the door. Andrea is giving tours and learning names. Then March 2020 happens. The Governor issues a shutdown. Revenue disappears overnight. The entire premise of a dog daycare — that you need somewhere for your dog while you're somewhere else — evaporates.

### Act Four: April 14, 2020 — Andrea Speaks
This is the emotional center of the novel. A real Tuesday. A real Town Council Special Meeting, held partly via Zoom. Mayor David Leake stops a budget discussion to invite Andrea Payne to the podium. She speaks about what the shutdown has done to her business. The official minutes record only that she "discussed some of the challenges the business has had since the start of the pandemic shut down order from the Governor." The stimulus ordinance does not pass. A 30-day BPOL extension is approved. Andrea and Jim go home. Andy and the dogs are there. They do not quit.

### Act Five: The Comeback
The dogs come back. The families come back. A Pup's Valley earns its awards — Best of Prince William, Best of Virginia. Jim is the dog whisperer. Andrea runs daily operations. The business becomes exactly what they said it would be.

### Woven Throughout: Dottie Leonard
Dottie's life and legacy runs through the entire story. Her fight to preserve Haymarket. Her support for the Paynes. Her death on March 20, 2024. Jim's call to her in the hospital — she thought he might be a buyer. Jim and Andrea at her funeral, among the few who stood up to speak. She is the connective tissue between the Haymarket of the past and the Haymarket the Paynes came to build something in.

## Themes

**Community as character.** Haymarket is not a backdrop — it is a participant. Real Town Council meetings, real Citizens Time, real debates.

**The bureaucracy of dreams.** Every permit, every public hearing is a small battle in the war of making something real.

**Love made practical.** Jim and Andrea's relationship is expressed not in grand gestures but in showing up.

**The pandemic as crucible.** April 14, 2020 is the hinge. Everything before is preparation; everything after is consequence.

**Dogs as mirrors.** Dogs reflect the emotional state of the humans around them throughout the novel. They are the only completely honest characters.

**The chain of care.** Dottie Leonard spent fifty years making Haymarket worth coming to. The Paynes came and built something worthy of it.

**A name from the back seat.** Andy naming the business is the novel's quiet thesis: the best things are built by families, named by children, and given to communities that were waiting for them without knowing it.

---

## Tone

Warm. Specific. Grounded. Narrative nonfiction with novelistic depth. The heroism is ordinary: showing up, speaking up, not quitting. The humor is dry and real.

Think: *Friday Night Lights* crossed with *The Bear* — community-deep, detail-rich, and quietly devastating in the moments that matter. Real people. Real place. Real story. No invented characters, no fictional settings, no fantasy.
